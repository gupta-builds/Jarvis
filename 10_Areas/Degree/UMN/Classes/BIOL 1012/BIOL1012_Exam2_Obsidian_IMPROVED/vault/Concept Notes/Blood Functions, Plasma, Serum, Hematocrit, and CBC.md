# Blood Functions, Plasma, Serum, Hematocrit, and CBC

## Blood 1 — Functions of RBCs, WBCs, Platelets, and Serum

### Red Blood Cells (Erythrocytes)
- Carry **oxygen** to all tissues; help return **carbon dioxide** to lungs.
- Packed with **hemoglobin**, which binds O₂.
- Tissues need O₂ for **cellular respiration → ATP production**.
- Low RBCs = less O₂ to tissues → fatigue, shortness of breath, pallor.

### White Blood Cells (Leukocytes)
- Carry out **immune defense** — both innate and adaptive.
- Different types: phagocytes, NK cells, B cells, T cells, mast cells.
- High count often signals active immune activity (infection, leukemia, rejection, vaccine response).

### Platelets (Thrombocytes)
- Cellular **fragments** (not full cells).
- Main job: initiate **blood clotting** at sites of vessel damage.
- Low platelets → bleeding risk; high platelets → excess clot risk.

### Serum
- **Plasma minus clotting proteins**.
- The gold standard for **biochemistry/chemistry tests**.
- Key distinction: serum is used heavily for lab diagnostics because the clotting-related proteins are removed.

## Blood 2 — Plasma, Serum, and Hematocrit

### Plasma
- The **liquid portion** of blood.
- Contains water, dissolved solutes, **proteins**, and **clotting factors**.
- Used for blood transfusions.
- **Plasma has clotting proteins; serum does not.**

### Serum
- Plasma minus its proteins (especially clotting factors).
- Gold standard for biochemistry tests.

### Hematocrit
- **Percent of blood volume occupied by cells** (almost all are RBCs).
- Hematocrit ≈ another measure of RBC abundance.
- Normal range: **35–55%**
- Low hematocrit → low RBC count → possible anemia or oxygen transport problem.
- High hematocrit → high RBC proportion.

> **Quick comparison:**
> - Plasma: everything fluid, including clotting proteins
> - Serum: plasma without the proteins → used for lab tests
> - Hematocrit: % of blood that is cells (mostly RBCs)

## Blood 3 — Signs and Symptoms for Abnormal Cell Counts

### Too Few RBCs / Low Hematocrit
- Less oxygen delivered to tissues.
- Signs/symptoms: **fatigue, weakness, shortness of breath, pale skin, dizziness, poor exercise tolerance**.

### Too Many RBCs / High Hematocrit
- Blood becomes more viscous.
- Risk of clot formation.
- Signs/symptoms: headache, high blood pressure.
- Connected to **blood doping** (see below).

### Too Few WBCs
- Weakened immune defense.
- Signs/symptoms: **frequent infections, hard-to-clear infections, slow recovery**.

### Too Many WBCs
- Usually signals active immune response.
- Possible causes: **infection (bacterial or viral), leukemia or lymphoma, organ rejection, vaccine response, autoimmune disease**.
- Signs/symptoms: fever, swelling, body aches, fatigue, flu-like symptoms.

### Too Few Platelets (Thrombocytopenia)
- Poor clotting ability.
- Signs/symptoms: **easy bruising, bleeding gums, nosebleeds, prolonged bleeding after cuts, petechiae (tiny red/purple skin spots)**.

### Too Many Platelets
- Greater tendency to form inappropriate clots.
- Risk of blocked vessels.

## ⚠️ Important Clarifications from Lecture

### Vitamin K Deficiency vs. Low Platelets
- **Vitamin K** (found in leafy greens, eggs, nuts) helps your body make proteins needed for clotting.
- A **Vitamin K deficiency** reduces clotting **factors** (proteins in plasma) — **not platelet numbers**.
- So Vitamin K deficiency → bruising/bleeding, but **normal platelet count**.

### Hemophilia
- A condition involving **low or nonfunctional plasma clotting proteins**, with **normal platelet numbers**.
- Key point: platelet count is normal, but clotting still fails because the protein factors are missing.

### Sickle Cell Anemia
- RBCs are abnormally shaped (sickle-shaped) and **less efficient at transporting oxygen**.
- CBC would show: abnormal RBC morphology, often low hematocrit.
- Signs/symptoms: **fatigue, pale skin, difficulty with oxygen delivery, episodes of pain**.

## Blood 4 — Interpreting a CBC

### What a CBC Reports
- Number of RBCs, WBCs, platelets per mL of blood.
- Hematocrit (%).
- Often also: RBC size, hemoglobin amount per RBC, hemoglobin concentration per RBC, variation in RBC size.
- **You do not need to memorize normal ranges — the exam will tell you if a value is H/N/L.**

### Interpretation Steps
1. **Which value is abnormal?** (RBC, WBC, platelet, hematocrit?)
2. **What does that cell type do?** Connect abnormality to function.
3. **What conditions/symptoms follow?**

### Practice Patient Interpretations (from Lecture)

| Patient | Abnormal Value | Possible Conditions | Possible Symptoms |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Patient 1 | All normal | Baseline check, screen for blood cancers, check before surgery, investigate fatigue/SOB | None (or reason was screening) |
| Patient 2 | **High WBC** (26 × 10³) | Infection (bacteria/virus), leukemia or lymphoma, organ rejection, vaccine response | Flu-like symptoms, tiredness, nausea, muscle pain, swelling, SOB |
| Patient 3 | **Very low platelets** (2 × 10³) | Thrombocytopenia, clotting disorders (hemophilia-like, but NOT Vitamin K deficiency) | Excessive bleeding after small cuts, bruising, petechiae |
| Patient 4 | **Low RBC/hematocrit** (low hemoglobin) | Sickle cell anemia (cells not efficient at transporting O₂) | Fatigue, pale skin, easy bruising |

> **Exam tip:** Patient 1 with all-normal values might have had a CBC ordered for: infection screening, pre-op check, blood cancer screening, or investigation of fatigue/SOB/bruising.

## Blood Doping (Lecture Emphasis)

- All blood cells develop in the **bone marrow** (inside long bones).
- **Erythropoietin (EPO)** is a hormone that stimulates RBC development.
- **Darbopoietin** is a synthetic form of EPO → using it artificially increases RBC count.
- Effect on CBC: **higher RBC count, higher hematocrit** → more oxygen to tissues → enhanced athletic performance.
- This is cheating: it gives an unfair advantage and is banned in sport.
