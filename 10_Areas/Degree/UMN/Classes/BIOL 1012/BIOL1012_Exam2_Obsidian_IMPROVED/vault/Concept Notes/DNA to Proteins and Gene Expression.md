# DNA to Proteins and Gene Expression

## DNA 1 — The Central Dogma

**DNA → RNA → Protein**

- **DNA** stores the instructions (in the nucleus).
- **RNA** carries and uses those instructions.
- **Proteins** carry out most cellular functions and create the observable phenotype.

---

## DNA 2 — Key Definitions

### Transcription
- Making **RNA from DNA**.
- RNA polymerase reads the DNA **template strand** and synthesizes a complementary **mRNA** strand.
- Happens in the **nucleus**.

### Translation
- Making a **protein from mRNA**.
- Ribosomes read mRNA codons; tRNAs bring matching amino acids; a polypeptide chain is built.
- Happens at **ribosomes** in the cytoplasm (or on rough ER).

### Gene Expression
- The process of using the information in a gene to produce a functional product (usually a protein).
- Includes transcription + translation.
- Different patterns of gene expression create different cell types even though they have the same DNA.

---

## DNA 3 — Steps in Transcription and Translation

### Transcription Steps
1. DNA at the gene region is **unwound** by RNA polymerase.
2. RNA polymerase reads the **template strand** (3'→5' direction).
3. Complementary **RNA nucleotides** are added one by one.
4. The resulting **mRNA transcript** is released.
5. mRNA leaves the nucleus and enters the cytoplasm.

### Translation Steps
1. mRNA binds to a **ribosome**.
2. Ribosome reads mRNA in **codons** (triplets of 3 bases).
3. **tRNA** molecules bring the matching amino acid to each codon (via anticodon).
4. Ribosome links amino acids into a growing **polypeptide chain**.
5. Translation always **starts at AUG** → first amino acid is **Methionine (Met)**.
6. Translation **stops** at a **stop codon** (UAA, UAG, or UGA) — no amino acid added.

---

## DNA 4 — Functions of Each Molecule

| Molecule | Function |
|----------|----------|
| **mRNA** | Carries the coding message from DNA; sequence of codons |
| **tRNA** | Adapter: matches mRNA codon with the correct amino acid via its anticodon |
| **rRNA** | Structural and catalytic component of the ribosome |
| **Ribosome** | Site of protein synthesis; reads mRNA and links amino acids |
| **RNA polymerase** | Synthesizes RNA during transcription; reads DNA template |

---

## DNA 5 — Where, When, and Why

| | Transcription | Translation |
|--|--------------|-------------|
| **Where** | Nucleus (eukaryotes) | Ribosomes in cytoplasm or on rough ER |
| **When** | When a cell needs a particular protein | After mRNA reaches ribosomes |
| **Why** | To copy gene instructions into a portable format | To build the protein the cell needs |

Not every gene is active in every cell at every time — cells regulate which genes are expressed.

## DNA 6 — Using the Codon Table (Exam Skill!)

### Base Pairing Rules: DNA → RNA (Transcription)
| DNA base | mRNA base produced |
|---------|-------------------|
| A | U |
| T | A |
| C | G |
| G | C |

### Reading the Codon Table (Translation)
- Read mRNA in **triplets (codons)**: first base = row, second base = column, third base = right column.
- Translation **always starts at AUG = Methionine (Met)**.
- Translation **stops at UAA, UAG, or UGA** (stop codons — no amino acid added).

### Worked Example from Practice Questions
**mRNA sequence:** `CGA AUG CCU GCU AUA AGA GGU UAA UCU`

- CGA → **skipped** (before start codon)
- **AUG** → Met (START)
- CCU → Pro
- GCU → Ala
- AUA → Ile
- AGA → Arg
- GGU → Gly
- **UAA** → STOP ← translation ends here

**Protein sequence: Met – Pro – Ala – Ile – Arg – Gly (then STOP)**

> The first amino acid is always **Met** because all translation begins at AUG.

### mRNA Base Pairing (Transcription direction)
- C pairs with G
- G pairs with C
- T pairs with A
- A pairs with U

## DNA 7 — How DNA Codes for Amino Acids

- Order of **DNA bases** → order of **RNA bases** (via base pairing in transcription).
- mRNA bases are read in **triplets = codons**.
- Each codon specifies **one amino acid** (or a stop signal).
- So: DNA base sequence → codon sequence → amino acid sequence → protein structure → protein function.

---

## DNA 8 — Gene Expression, Not DNA Differences, Explains Cell Differences

- Most cells in one person have the **same DNA** (same genome).
- A muscle cell and a nerve cell look and work differently because they **express different genes**.
- The proteins produced differ → cells differ in structure and function.
- **Phenotype differences among cells = differences in gene expression**, not differences in DNA.

---

## DNA 9 — Ways Gene Expression Is Regulated

- Genes can be **turned on or off** (transcription factors, signals from hormones).
- Amount of **transcription** can increase or decrease.
- mRNA can be **processed differently** (alternative splicing).
- **mRNA stability** can change (some mRNAs last longer = more protein made).
- **Translation rate** can be modified.
- Proteins can be **modified or degraded** after synthesis.
- Signals from the **environment or hormones** can trigger changes in expression.

---

## High-Yield Distinctions

### Gene vs. Chromosome
- **Gene:** a short segment of DNA that contains the code (blueprint) for one or more proteins.
- **Chromosome:** a rod-shaped bundle of tightly coiled DNA and proteins found in the nucleus. Contains many genes.

### Genotype vs. Phenotype
- **Genotype:** an organism's particular set of genes (alleles).
- **Phenotype:** the observable physical and functional traits resulting from genotype + gene expression + environment.

---

## Memory Anchors
- **DNA stores → RNA carries → Protein performs**
- **Transcription = DNA to RNA** (in nucleus)
- **Translation = RNA to Protein** (at ribosome)
- **AUG = Start = Met**
- **UAA, UAG, UGA = Stop** (no amino acid)
- **Same DNA in all your cells → different proteins expressed = different cell types**
