## CP 1.2 Human Relatedness to Other Species
- **Phylogeny = a hypothesis (best-supported “map” of relatedness)** based on evidence like fossils, comparative anatomy/physiology, and DNA comparisons.
- **How to tell “who is more closely related” on a tree:** compare *most recent common ancestor (MRCA)*. The pair with the *more recent MRCA* is *more closely related* and tends to share *more DNA and inherited traits*. 
- **Common ancestor:** a past organism/population that gave rise to both lineages; “recent” common ancestors = closer relationships.
- **Humans and chimpanzees:** humans did *not* come from modern chimps; humans and chimps are *separate lineages* that share a *common ancestor* (now extinct / not directly observed).
- **Hominins (human lineage in this activity):** the shared trait across the hominin group shown is *bipedal walking*; other traits (bigger brains, tool use) appear later on the tree.
- **Species boundaries idea (humans vs Neanderthals):** some interbreeding can occur between distinct species; in this class framing, Neanderthals are typically treated as a *different species* because they’re consistently distinguishable by DNA/morphology and interbreeding seems uncommon.
## CP 1.3 - Human Genetic Diversity
> [!NOTE] Go through the CP by yourself. 
- **Gene/allele/locus**: the packet’s HLA example is literally “gene at a locus with many alleles.”
- **SNPs/variation**: differences are often small, spread across many positions; that’s why ancestry tests use lots of loci.
- **Key claim** you may be tested on: **Most genetic variation is within populations, not between them**, so race is not a good biological classifier.
## CP 1.4 - Lactase Persistence
> [!NOTE] Review the graph question
- **Human diversity:** most variation is **within** regions/groups, not between.
- **Race vs genetics:** DNA can estimate **ancestry**, but race is a **social category** and not detectable as a clean genetic boundary.
- **Lactase persistence:** a clear natural selection case: mutation → heritable variation → dairying as selective agent → allele frequency increases in those populations.
