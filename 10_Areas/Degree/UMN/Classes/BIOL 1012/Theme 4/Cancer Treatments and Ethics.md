# Cancer Treatments and Ethics
## Exam Objectives Covered
- CA9: describe surgery, radiation, chemotherapy, and immunotherapy; explain how they work, advantages, and limits.
- E1-E6: apply four pillars to STI prevention, intersex development, transgender children, cancer genetic testing, cancer treatment access, puberty blockers, and informed consent.
## 30-Second Version
Cancer treatment is a tradeoff problem. Treatments try to remove or kill cancer cells, but cancer cells are still human cells, so treatments can damage healthy tissues too. Ethics questions ask: Who benefits? Who is harmed? Who decides? Who gets access?

## Abby Case Anchors
From lecture/course packet 4.6:
- Abby has an ovarian mass and abnormal CA-125 tests.
- PET scan uses radiolabeled glucose.
- Cancer cells often appear bright because they are metabolically active and rapidly dividing.
- If cancer is localized, surgery may be enough.
- If cancer has spread, surgery plus chemotherapy may be needed.
- Removing both ovaries can lower risk of cancer in the other ovary but affects fertility and ovarian hormone production.
- Loss of ovaries lowers estrogen/progesterone and can cause premature menopause unless treated with hormone replacement.

## Cancer Treatment Comparison
| Treatment | How it works | Advantages | Limitations / side effects |
|---|---|---|---|
| Surgery | Physically removes tumor/cancer tissue. | Can fully remove localized tumor; more targeted than whole-body drug treatment. | May not work if cancer has spread; invasive; can leave cells behind; can damage nearby tissue. |
| Radiation | Uses targeted radiation to damage DNA and kill cancer cells in a local area. | Useful for localized tumors; can reduce need for major surgery; local rather than whole-body. | Can damage nearby healthy cells; fatigue/low blood count; radiation itself can cause DNA damage. |
| Chemotherapy | Drugs/toxins kill or inhibit rapidly dividing cells. | Can reach cells that surgery/radiation miss; useful after spread; often combined with other treatments. | Hits healthy rapidly dividing cells too: hair, skin, gut lining, immune cells; nausea, hair loss, infection risk, fatigue; expensive. |
| Immunotherapy | Uses or modifies immune system to target cancer. Includes engineered T cells, antibodies, checkpoint inhibitors, cytokines, vaccines, or viruses. | Can be specific to cancer markers; can work when other treatments fail; uses immune targeting. | Requires information about the cancer; can be individualized/expensive; immune side effects; not all cancers respond. |

Exam trap: chemotherapy does not "know" cancer cells by identity. It mainly targets rapidly dividing cells, which is why side effects happen.

## PET Scan Logic
PET scan reasoning:
1. Radioactive glucose is injected.
2. Cells take up glucose.
3. Rapidly dividing/metabolically active tissues take up more.
4. Scan shows bright regions.
5. Doctors compare expected bright regions, like brain or bladder/urine signal, with abnormal bright regions.

PET scan trap: bright bladder signal can be urine containing tracer, not necessarily cancer.

## BRCA and Genetic Testing Ethics
### Biology
- BRCA1 and BRCA2 are tumor suppressor genes.
- A mutation raises risk of breast/ovarian and some other cancers.
- Risk is not certainty.
- Genetic relatives may want testing because they may share inherited mutations.

### Limits of genetic testing
- Most cancers do not have one clear genetic cause.
- A positive result gives risk information, not a guaranteed diagnosis.
- A negative result does not mean "no cancer risk."
- Testing can affect family members who did not choose to know.

### Ethical pillars
| Pillar | Cancer genetic testing question |
|---|---|
| Autonomy | Does the person understand what the test can and cannot tell them? Can they choose whether to know? |
| Beneficence | Could testing help with screening, prevention, or treatment planning? |
| Nonmaleficence | Could testing cause anxiety, discrimination, privacy harm, or unnecessary procedures? |
| Justice | Who can afford testing and follow-up care? Are benefits available only to some groups? |

## Ethics Map
### Four pillars
| Pillar | Short meaning | Exam question to ask |
|---|---|---|
| Autonomy | Self-rule; ability to make decisions | Who gets to decide, and was consent informed? |
| Beneficence | Doing good | What benefit is expected? |
| Nonmaleficence | Avoiding harm | What harm could this cause or fail to prevent? |
| Justice | Fairness in benefits/burdens | Who gets access, who carries risk, who is left out? |

### STI prevention
| Pillar | Application |
|---|---|
| Autonomy | People need accurate information and voluntary choices about testing, disclosure, vaccines, and barrier methods. |
| Beneficence | Prevention reduces infection, infertility risk, cancer risk for HPV, and community spread. |
| Nonmaleficence | Withholding prevention information or access can cause avoidable harm. |
| Justice | Condoms, vaccines, testing, and treatment should be accessible, not only available to people with money/insurance. |

### Intersex development
| Pillar | Application |
|---|---|
| Autonomy | Infants cannot consent to medically unnecessary body-altering procedures. |
| Beneficence | Care should improve the person's health and well-being, not just make anatomy fit expectations. |
| Nonmaleficence | Early unnecessary surgeries can cause physical and psychological harm. |
| Justice | Intersex traits are part of human diversity; care should not be shaped by stigma. |

### Transgender children and puberty blockers
Biology of puberty blockers:
- Puberty blockers are typically **GnRH agonists** (e.g., leuprolide). They initially stimulate, then **desensitize** GnRH receptors on the anterior pituitary through continuous (non-pulsatile) exposure.
- Result: anterior pituitary stops responding to GnRH → FSH and LH drop → gonads are not stimulated → gonadal sex hormones fall.

**Effects in a person with testes (Person 2):**
- Testosterone production decreases.
- Puberty changes delayed/paused: less voice deepening, less facial/body hair growth, less muscle mass increase, less genital growth, reduced/no sperm production.

**Effects in a person with ovaries (Person 1):**
- Estrogen and progesterone production decreases.
- Puberty changes delayed/paused: delayed breast development, no menstrual cycles, delayed hip widening, delayed ovulation.

**Key points:**
- Effects are largely **reversible** if blockers are stopped (puberty resumes).
- Blockers buy time for the adolescent to mature and make decisions about further treatment.
- They do NOT provide cross-sex hormones — that is a separate treatment step.

Ethics:
| Pillar | Application |
|---|---|
| Autonomy | The child/adolescent's assent, guardian consent, and clinical understanding all matter. |
| Beneficence | May reduce gender dysphoria distress by pausing unwanted puberty and buying time for decision-making. |
| Nonmaleficence | Must weigh medication risks (bone density effects, delayed growth plate closure, unknown long-term effects), and also weigh harm of forcing unwanted puberty without intervention. |
| Justice | Access should not depend only on geography, politics, cost, or family resources. |

### Informed Consent (Objective E6)
**Informed consent** means a person agrees to a medical intervention after receiving and understanding adequate information. It is not just signing a form.

**Components of valid informed consent:**
1. **Disclosure** — provider explains the treatment, its purpose, expected benefits, risks, side effects, and alternatives (including doing nothing).
2. **Understanding** — the patient/guardian comprehends the information in a meaningful way.
3. **Voluntariness** — the decision is made freely, without coercion or undue pressure.
4. **Capacity** — the person has the cognitive ability to make the decision (for minors, guardian consent + child's assent).
5. **Right to withdraw** — consent can be revoked at any time.

**Applied to puberty blockers:**
- The adolescent and guardians must understand what blockers do and don't do, what is reversible, what is uncertain, what alternatives exist, and that they can stop treatment.
- Informed consent is ongoing, not a one-time event — as the adolescent matures, their capacity to participate in decisions increases.

### Cancer treatment in the United States
| Pillar | Application |
|---|---|
| Autonomy | Patients need understandable options, risks, costs, and likely outcomes. |
| Beneficence | Treatment aims to remove cancer, extend life, reduce symptoms, or improve quality of life. |
| Nonmaleficence | Treatments can cause serious side effects; overtreatment can harm. |
| Justice | Underinsurance, high out-of-pocket costs, and unequal access shape who gets timely care. |

### AI in cancer treatment
Lecture 4.6 framed AI cancer planning as an ethics issue.

Key concerns:
- AI is trained on human-generated data, which can contain bias or errors.
- AI recommendation may differ from an oncologist's recommendation.
- Some AI systems may be hard to explain.
- Legal responsibility can be unclear.
- New technology may be expensive and unevenly available.

Pillar application:
- autonomy: patient should know whether AI is being used and what role it plays
- beneficence: AI may improve detection or treatment planning
- nonmaleficence: bad/bias-driven recommendations can harm patients
- justice: AI should not improve care only for wealthy or well-resourced groups

## Exam-Style Applications
- "Why would hair be affected by chemotherapy?" -> hair follicle cells divide rapidly.
- "Advantage of removing both ovaries?" -> reduces risk of cancer in the other ovary, especially with high genetic risk.
- "Disadvantage of removing both ovaries?" -> infertility using own eggs and loss of ovarian estrogen/progesterone.
- "AI treatment plan differs from oncologist; what ethical issue?" -> autonomy, nonmaleficence, responsibility, and justice all matter.
- "Puberty blocker effect?" -> suppresses puberty-driving gonadal hormone pathway, delaying secondary sex characteristics.
