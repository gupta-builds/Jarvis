# Fertilization Pregnancy and Birth

## Exam Objectives Covered
- PB1: explain how contraceptives prevent fertilization and pregnancy, especially hormonal contraception preventing ovulation.
- PB2: identify contraceptives that reliably prevent pregnancy and/or STI spread.
- PB3: describe source, targets, and responses/actions of hCG.
- PB4: identify location of fertilization and implantation.
- PB5: describe differentiation and germ layer derivatives.
- PB6: describe basic processes in each trimester.
- PB7: describe positive feedback in childbirth.

## 30-Second Version
Fertilization usually happens in the **fallopian tube/oviduct**. One sperm fuses with one egg to form a diploid **zygote**. The early embryo divides by mitosis/cleavage, becomes a blastocyst, and implants in the **endometrium**. hCG keeps the corpus luteum alive so progesterone stays high and the uterine lining is maintained.

## Fertilization and Early Development
### Fertilization
- Usual location: **fallopian tube / oviduct**.
- Sperm and egg are haploid.
- One sperm fertilizes one egg.
- Fusion creates a **zygote**, which is diploid and one cell.

Exam trap: many sperm may reach the egg, but normally only one fertilizes it.

### Cleavage
- Early mitotic divisions of the zygote.
- The embryo increases cell number without much overall growth at first.
- Cleavage leads toward the blastocyst/blastula stage.

### Implantation
- Usual location: **uterine endometrium**.
- The blastocyst attaches/implants after reaching the uterus.
- In the CP 4.4 timing model, fertilization happens around ovarian cycle day 14 if it occurs at ovulation; the embryo reaches the uterus several days later.

## hCG and Early Pregnancy
| Hormone | Source | Target | Response/action |
|---|---|---|---|
| hCG | Early embryo/trophoblast/chorion, later associated with placenta development | Corpus luteum in ovary | Maintains corpus luteum so progesterone and estrogen stay high |

Why hCG matters:
- Without hCG, the corpus luteum would degenerate.
- If corpus luteum degenerates, progesterone drops.
- If progesterone drops, the endometrium is shed.
- hCG prevents that early loss of the uterine lining.

Pregnancy tests detect hCG.

## Contraception
### How contraceptives prevent pregnancy
Contraceptives can work by:
- preventing ovulation
- blocking sperm from reaching egg
- reducing sperm survival/movement
- preventing fertilization
- preventing implantation or changing uterine/cervical conditions depending method

### Hormonal contraception
Lecture phrasing: hormonal birth control keeps hormone levels **constant medium-ish** instead of allowing the normal cyclical surge.

Mechanism:
1. Synthetic estrogen and/or progesterone provides steady feedback.
2. GnRH/FSH/LH patterns are suppressed or flattened.
3. No big estrogen/LH surge.
4. No ovulation.
5. No egg released, so fertilization cannot occur.

Other common effects:
- cervical mucus can become thicker
- endometrium may become less favorable for implantation

### Reliability pattern
- Most reliable pregnancy prevention: long-acting reversible contraceptives such as hormonal implants and IUDs, plus sterilization. These work well because they do not depend on perfect use each time.
- Most reliable STI prevention among contraceptive methods: barrier methods, especially condoms, because they reduce exchange of body fluids and direct contact.

Exam trap: hormonal contraception can be very effective for pregnancy prevention, but it does **not** reliably prevent STI spread.

### Fertile window
If trying to get pregnant, the best timing is around ovulation and the days before ovulation. Practice key anchor: roughly days 8-15 in a typical 28-day cycle, especially days 13-15 closest to ovulation.

## Differentiation and Pregnancy
### Differentiation
**Differentiation** is the process where cells become specialized in structure and function.

**When it occurs:** Differentiation begins during **gastrulation** (around week 3 of embryonic development, first trimester). This is when the three germ layers form and cells commit to different fates. Differentiation continues throughout development and into adult life (stem cells still differentiate in bone marrow, gut lining, etc.).

Mechanism:
- All cells in an organism carry essentially the same DNA.
- Different cell types activate different subsets of genes.
- Different gene activity → different proteins → different cell structures and functions.
- Signals from neighboring cells, position in the embryo, and chemical gradients determine which genes get turned on.

### Germ layers
During gastrulation, cells organize into three primary germ layers. Each gives rise to specific adult tissues:

| Germ layer | Major derivatives |
|---|---|
| **Ectoderm** (outer) | Epidermis (outer skin), hair, nails, sweat glands; entire nervous system (brain, spinal cord, nerves); lens of eye; tooth enamel |
| **Mesoderm** (middle) | Skeletal muscle, smooth muscle, cardiac muscle; bone and cartilage; blood and blood vessels; kidneys and urinary structures; most reproductive organs; connective tissue; dermis (inner skin layer) |
| **Endoderm** (inner) | Lining of digestive tract (stomach, intestines); lining of respiratory tract (lungs, trachea); liver; pancreas; thyroid; bladder lining |

**Memory anchor:** Ecto = outside (skin + nerves that sense the outside). Endo = inside (gut/lung linings). Meso = everything in between (muscle, bone, blood, kidneys).

Exam trap: differentiation is NOT caused by cells losing DNA. Nearly all cells keep the full genome. It is differential gene expression that makes cells different.

## Trimesters
### First trimester (weeks 1–12)
- Fertilization, cleavage, implantation, early placenta formation.
- **Gastrulation** (~week 3): three germ layers form; differentiation begins.
- **Organogenesis** (weeks 3–8): all major organ systems begin forming — heart starts beating, neural tube closes (becomes brain/spinal cord), limb buds appear.
- Embryo is **most vulnerable to teratogens** (drugs, alcohol, infections, radiation) during organogenesis because body plan and organs are being established.
- After week 8, the embryo is called a fetus.
- hCG peaks around weeks 8–10, maintaining the corpus luteum until the placenta takes over progesterone production.

### Second trimester (weeks 13–26)
- Rapid growth in size; fetus goes from ~3 inches to ~14 inches.
- Organs continue maturing: kidneys produce urine, liver produces bile.
- **Quickening**: mother can feel fetal movement (usually weeks 16–20).
- Bones harden (ossification); fingerprints form.
- Sex organs become distinguishable on ultrasound.
- Fetus is not yet viable outside the womb for most of this period (viability threshold ~24 weeks with intensive care).

### Third trimester (weeks 27–40)
- Rapid weight gain and fat deposition.
- **Lung maturation**: surfactant production increases (critical for breathing after birth).
- **Brain growth**: massive neural development, myelination.
- Fetus moves into head-down position for birth.
- Immune antibodies transfer from mother to fetus.
- Braxton-Hicks contractions may occur (practice contractions, not true labor).
- Ends with labor and delivery.

## Childbirth Positive Feedback
Positive feedback means the response increases the original stimulus until a clear endpoint occurs.

Childbirth loop:
1. Fetus presses/stretchs cervix.
2. Stretch signals cause oxytocin release.
3. Oxytocin stimulates uterine contractions.
4. Contractions push fetus harder against cervix.
5. More cervical stretch causes more oxytocin.
6. Loop continues until fetus is expelled and stretch stimulus ends.

Key contrast:
- Negative feedback stabilizes hormone levels.
- Childbirth positive feedback escalates contractions until birth.

## Exam-Style Applications
- "Where does fertilization occur?" -> fallopian tube/oviduct.
- "Where does implantation occur?" -> uterine endometrium.
- "What does hCG do?" -> maintains corpus luteum, keeping progesterone high.
- "How does hormonal contraception prevent ovulation?" -> steady hormones suppress normal FSH/LH/LH surge.
- "Which method prevents STI spread?" -> condoms/barrier methods, not hormonal birth control.
