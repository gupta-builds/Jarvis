## Figure 1: Uterine and Ovarian Cycles
What the figure tests:
- ovarian phase: follicular vs luteal
- uterine phase: menses vs proliferative vs secretory
- hormone pattern: LH, FSH, estrogen, progesterone
- what is happening in ovary/endometrium on a given day

Fast reading method:
1. Find the day.
2. Ask if it is before, at, or after ovulation.
3. If day is around 14, look for LH surge and ovulation.
4. If progesterone is high, it is luteal/secretory.
5. If bleeding/endometrium shedding is shown, it is menses.
6. If endometrium is rebuilding before ovulation, it is proliferative.

High-yield anchors:
- Day 3: low hormones; menses/early follicular.
- Day 14: LH surge; ovulation.
- Day 23: progesterone high; luteal/secretory.

## Figure 2: Hormones of Spermatogenesis
What the figure tests:
- hypothalamus -> GnRH
- anterior pituitary -> FSH and LH
- FSH -> Sertoli cells -> sperm production
- LH -> Leydig cells -> testosterone
- testosterone -> sperm support, secondary sex characteristics, negative feedback

Prediction method:
1. Start at the changed hormone/structure.
2. Move downstream to target response.
3. Move upstream through feedback if testosterone changes.

Examples:
- Low testosterone -> less negative feedback -> GnRH/FSH/LH increase.
- High testosterone -> more negative feedback -> GnRH/FSH/LH decrease.
- No FSH -> Sertoli stimulation drops -> sperm production decreases.
- Damaged Sertoli cells -> sperm count low even if LH/FSH/GnRH are normal.

## Table 3: Hormone Levels on Three Days
Given estrogen, progesterone, and LH:

| Pattern | Likely point in cycle |
|---|---|
| High progesterone, moderate estrogen, low LH | Luteal/secretory phase |
| High estrogen and high LH | Around ovulation |
| Low estrogen, low progesterone, low LH | Menses/early follicular |

Reasoning:
- Progesterone high means corpus luteum is active.
- LH high means ovulation window.
- Estrogen high before ovulation helps trigger LH surge.

## Figure 5: Cell Cycle
What the figure tests:
- G0, G1, S, G2, M
- checkpoint logic
- p53/G1 checkpoint

Fast map:
- G0: not actively dividing
- G1: growth/function before DNA replication
- S: DNA replicated once
- G2: prepare for mitosis after replication
- M: mitosis/cell division

Checkpoint anchors:
- G1 checkpoint: DNA damage before replication; p53 matters here.
- G2 checkpoint: DNA replicated correctly and not damaged.
- M checkpoint: chromosomes lined up/attached before separation.

## Figure 6: Cancer Progression
What the figure tests:
- normal growth vs uncontrolled growth
- tumor vs cancer
- in situ vs malignant
- metastasis

Reasoning chain:
1. Mutation in growth-control genes.
2. Cell divides when it should not.
3. Tumor forms.
4. More mutations accumulate.
5. Cancer invades local tissue.
6. Cancer enters blood/lymph.
7. Metastases form elsewhere.

Key definitions:
- tumor = mass of rapidly dividing cells
- malignant = invasive cancer
- metastasis = spread to distant tissues/organs
- carcinogen = mutation-causing cancer-risk factor

## Practice Question Patterns
### 1. Anatomy diagram questions
Study structure function, not just names.

High-yield paths:
- sperm: seminiferous tubules -> epididymis -> vas deferens -> ejaculatory duct -> urethra -> penis
- ova if not fertilized: ovary -> fallopian tube -> uterus -> cervix -> vagina -> vaginal opening

### 2. Homologue table questions
Build the answer by precursor:
- gonads: testes / ovaries
- genital tubercle: penis / clitoris
- labioscrotal swellings: scrotum / labia majora
- internal duct structures: seminal vesicles vs fallopian tubes/uterus/part of vagina

### 3. Endocrine pathway questions
Always identify:
- stimulus
- source
- hormone
- target
- receptor
- response

Example testosterone development pathway:
- stimulus: Y chromosome/SRY-related developmental signaling
- source: testes
- hormone: testosterone/androgens
- target: bipotential genital/gonadal tissues
- receptor: androgen receptor
- response: development toward typical male structures

### 4. Haploid/diploid questions
Fast rule:
- body cells, oogonia, spermatogonia, zygote = diploid
- sperm and egg before fertilization = haploid

### 5. Feedback questions
Use testosterone as the feedback signal:
- testosterone low -> GnRH/LH/FSH up
- testosterone high -> GnRH/LH/FSH down

### 6. Uterine/ovarian day questions
Use hormone clues:
- LH surge = ovulation
- progesterone high = luteal/secretory
- endometrium shedding = menses
- estrogen rebuilding endometrium = proliferative

### 7. Hormonal birth control questions
Synthetic estrogen/progesterone:
- keeps hormone levels steady
- prevents normal LH surge
- prevents ovulation

Exam trap: it prevents pregnancy better than STI spread. Condoms/barriers are the STI prevention answer.

### 8. Cell cycle questions
Match phase to action:
- G1 = growth before DNA replication
- S = DNA replication
- G2 = preparation after replication
- M = mitosis
- G0 = resting

p53:
- tumor suppressor
- G1 checkpoint
- stops division after DNA damage

### 9. Cancer genetics questions
Gas/brake model:
- proto-oncogene = normal gas pedal
- oncogene = stuck gas pedal
- tumor suppressor = brake
- mutated tumor suppressor = broken brake

Multiple mutation logic:
- one mutation can raise risk
- cancer usually develops after multiple genes/pathways fail

### 10. Treatment questions
| If question says... | Think... |
|---|---|
| localized tumor | surgery/radiation may work |
| spread/metastasis | chemo/systemic treatment may be needed |
| rapid dividing healthy cells hurt | chemotherapy side effects |
| immune system used | immunotherapy |
| nearby healthy tissue harmed by local DNA damage | radiation |

## Mini Practice Answers
### Why is LH surge the best ovulation predictor?
LH directly triggers ovulation and corpus luteum formation. Estrogen rises before it, but LH is the cleanest "ovulation is happening now" clue.

### Why does chemotherapy cause hair loss?
Chemotherapy targets rapidly dividing cells. Cancer cells divide rapidly, but so do hair follicle cells, skin/gut lining cells, and some immune-related cells.

### Why does a BRCA mutation increase risk but not guarantee cancer?
BRCA genes are tumor suppressors. A mutation weakens a brake, but cancer usually needs additional mutations and failures in other growth-control systems.

### Why can an XY embryo develop female-like external genitals?
Typical male external genital development requires androgen signaling. If testosterone is not produced or androgen receptors do not work, target tissues do not receive/respond to the signal.

### Why can an XX embryo develop intersex external genitals?
Typical female external genital development depends on very low androgen exposure. Higher-than-typical androgen levels during development can shift external genital development.
