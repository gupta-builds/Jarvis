# Uterine and Ovarian Cycles

## Exam Objectives Covered
- CYC1a-CYC1d: use the provided 28-day chart to identify phases, hormone levels, and ovarian/uterine processes.
- CYC2: identify source, stimulus, target, and response for FSH, LH, estrogen, progesterone.
- CYC3-CYC5: explain estrogen/progesterone functions and ovarian/uterine phase events.
- CR1: compare GnRH, LH, and FSH in persons with testes vs ovaries.

## 30-Second Version
There are two linked cycles:

- **Ovarian cycle**: what happens in the ovary.
  - follicular phase -> ovulation -> luteal phase
- **Uterine cycle**: what happens to the endometrium.
  - menses -> proliferative phase -> secretory phase

High estrogen helps trigger the **LH surge**, which triggers ovulation. After ovulation, the corpus luteum makes **progesterone**, which maintains the endometrium. If progesterone drops, menses happens.

## How to Read the 28-Day Figure
The exam gives the figure. You do not need to redraw it from memory, but you do need to reason from it.

### Ovarian cycle timing
| Days in typical 28-day cycle | Ovarian phase | What is happening |
|---|---|---|
| Days 1-14 | Follicular phase | Follicle grows; estrogen rises. |
| Around day 14 | Ovulation | Oocyte is released; LH surge is the key clue. |
| Days 15-28 | Luteal phase | Corpus luteum secretes progesterone and some estrogen. |

### Uterine cycle timing
| Days in typical 28-day cycle | Uterine phase | What is happening |
|---|---|---|
| Days 1-5-ish | Menses | Endometrial lining is shed. |
| Days 6-14 | Proliferative phase | Endometrium rebuilds/thickens under estrogen. |
| Days 15-28 | Secretory phase | Endometrium becomes secretory and maintained under progesterone. |

Exam trap: **follicular/luteal** are ovarian phases. **menses/proliferative/secretory** are uterine phases.

## Hormone Pathway Table
| Hormone | Stimulus (what triggers release) | Source | Target | Response/action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| GnRH | Low gonadal hormone feedback (or positive estrogen signal near ovulation) | Hypothalamus | Anterior pituitary | Stimulates FSH and LH release |
| FSH | GnRH binding receptors on anterior pituitary cells | Anterior pituitary | Ovarian follicle cells (granulosa cells) | Promotes follicle growth; follicle cells secrete estrogen |
| LH | GnRH binding receptors on anterior pituitary cells; massive release (surge) triggered by high sustained estrogen positive feedback | Anterior pituitary | Mature follicle → corpus luteum | Triggers ovulation; causes luteinization (follicle → corpus luteum); corpus luteum secretes progesterone |
| Estrogen | FSH stimulation of follicle growth (follicular phase); LH maintenance of corpus luteum (luteal phase) | Granulosa cells of growing follicle; corpus luteum | Uterus/endometrium, hypothalamus/pituitary, bone, breast tissue | Rebuilds endometrium (proliferative phase); at high sustained levels triggers positive feedback → LH surge; at moderate levels contributes to negative feedback |
| Progesterone | LH-driven corpus luteum activity after ovulation | Corpus luteum (later placenta if pregnant) | Uterus/endometrium, hypothalamus/pituitary | Maintains thick secretory endometrium; strong negative feedback on GnRH/FSH/LH (prevents new follicle development during luteal phase) |

## Phase Logic
### Follicular phase (Days 1-14)
- Multiple follicles begin growing in the ovary (recruited by FSH).
- Usually only one follicle becomes dominant.
- FSH supports follicle development by stimulating granulosa cells.
- Growing follicle secretes **increasing estrogen**.
- Estrogen causes the endometrium to proliferate (rebuild after menses).
- Early/moderate estrogen provides **negative feedback** on hypothalamus/pituitary (keeps FSH/LH from spiking too early).
- As the dominant follicle grows larger, estrogen rises higher and higher.
- When estrogen reaches a **high sustained threshold** (typically after ~10-12 days of rising), it **switches to positive feedback**.
- Positive feedback → hypothalamus increases GnRH pulse frequency → anterior pituitary releases massive LH (and some FSH).

### Ovulation (Around Day 14)
- **LH surge** is the direct trigger for ovulation.
- The mature follicle ruptures and releases the oocyte.
- Oocyte enters the fallopian tube (swept in by fimbriae).
- The oocyte is viable for ~24 hours after release.
- The body shifts from follicular phase to luteal phase.
- The ruptured follicle transforms into the corpus luteum.

### Luteal phase (Days 15-28)
- The corpus luteum forms from the collapsed follicle.
- Corpus luteum secretes **high progesterone** and moderate estrogen.
- Progesterone maintains the endometrium in the secretory phase (thick, vascular, glandular, ready for implantation).
- Progesterone provides **strong negative feedback** on GnRH/FSH/LH → prevents new follicle recruitment → prevents a second ovulation in the same cycle.
- If no pregnancy: corpus luteum degenerates after ~10-12 days → progesterone and estrogen drop → endometrium cannot be maintained → menses begins → cycle restarts.
- If pregnancy: hCG from embryo maintains corpus luteum → progesterone stays high → no menses.

### Menses (Days 1-5)
- Day 1 is defined by the start of menstrual bleeding.
- The endometrium is shed because progesterone and estrogen have fallen.
- The functional layer of the endometrium breaks down and exits through the cervix and vagina.
- Hormone levels are at their lowest point.
- FSH begins to rise slightly at the end of menses, starting the next follicular phase.

### Proliferative phase (Days 6-14, uterine cycle)
- Estrogen from the growing follicle stimulates endometrial cells to divide and thicken.
- Blood vessels regrow in the endometrium.
- Endometrium goes from thin (post-menses) to thick.
- This phase ends at ovulation.

### Secretory phase (Days 15-28, uterine cycle)
- Progesterone from the corpus luteum transforms the endometrium.
- Endometrial glands secrete glycogen-rich fluid (nutrients for potential embryo).
- Blood vessels become coiled and extensive.
- Endometrium is at maximum thickness and receptivity.
- If no implantation → corpus luteum dies → progesterone drops → secretory endometrium cannot be maintained → menses.

### Estrogen: Two Different Jobs Depending on Level
| Estrogen level | Effect on hypothalamus/pituitary | Effect on uterus |
|---|---|---|
| Low/moderate (early-mid follicular) | **Negative feedback** — keeps GnRH/FSH/LH from spiking | Stimulates endometrial proliferation |
| High sustained (late follicular, ~day 12-13) | **Positive feedback** — triggers LH surge → ovulation | Endometrium is thick; cervical mucus becomes thin/stretchy (sperm-friendly) |
| Moderate (luteal phase, from corpus luteum) | Contributes to negative feedback along with progesterone | Helps maintain endometrium alongside progesterone |

## Hormone Level Anchors
Use these as quick pattern recognition.

| Day | GnRH | LH | FSH | Estrogen | Progesterone | Phase clue |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Day 3 | Low | Low | Low | Low | Low | Menses / early follicular |
| Day 14 | High | High surge | High-ish/surge | High | Low | Ovulation |
| Day 23 | Low | Low | Low | Middle/moderate | High | Luteal / secretory |

From practice key: Day 14 is the high-hormone ovulation day; Day 23 is progesterone-high luteal/secretory.

## Feedback Logic
### Negative feedback (most of the cycle)
Negative feedback returns a variable toward a stable level.

- **Progesterone** in the luteal phase strongly inhibits GnRH → FSH and LH stay low → no new follicle recruitment → no second ovulation.
- **Moderate estrogen** (early-mid follicular phase) also contributes mild negative feedback on GnRH/FSH/LH.
- This is the same logic as testosterone negative feedback in spermatogenesis.

### Positive feedback (unique to the ovarian cycle — key exam distinction from spermatogenesis)
Positive feedback moves farther from the starting point until a dramatic event occurs.

**Mechanism of the LH surge (step by step):**
1. Growing follicle secretes increasing estrogen throughout the follicular phase.
2. Estrogen rises steadily for ~10-12 days.
3. When estrogen reaches a **high sustained threshold** (not just a brief spike), it switches from inhibiting to *stimulating* the hypothalamus/anterior pituitary.
4. Hypothalamus increases GnRH pulse frequency.
5. Anterior pituitary releases a massive burst of LH (and some FSH).
6. LH surge triggers ovulation.
7. After ovulation, the follicle becomes the corpus luteum, progesterone rises, and negative feedback resumes.

**Why this matters for the exam:**
- In spermatogenesis, testosterone **always** negatively feeds back. There is no positive feedback switch.
- In the ovarian cycle, estrogen **switches** from negative to positive feedback depending on concentration and duration.
- This switch is what makes ovulation possible. Without it, no LH surge, no ovulation.

Exam trap: estrogen does not always do the same thing. Low/moderate estrogen = negative feedback. High sustained estrogen = positive feedback → LH surge.

## Male vs Female Hormone Comparison
| Hormone | Persons with testes | Persons with ovaries |
|---|---|---|
| GnRH | Stimulates FSH/LH from anterior pituitary | Same |
| FSH | Targets Sertoli cells; supports sperm production | Targets follicles; supports follicle growth and estrogen production |
| LH | Targets Leydig cells; stimulates testosterone | Triggers ovulation and corpus luteum formation |

Same upstream wiring. Different gonadal target cells and different outputs.

### Key feedback difference (high-yield exam distinction)
| Feature | Spermatogenesis | Ovarian cycle |
|---|---|---|
| Dominant gonadal hormone | Testosterone | Estrogen (follicular) / Progesterone (luteal) |
| Feedback type | **Always negative** — testosterone inhibits GnRH/FSH/LH at all concentrations | **Switches** — moderate estrogen = negative; high sustained estrogen = positive → LH surge |
| Result | Steady-state testosterone; continuous sperm production | Cyclical hormones; one ovulation per ~28-day cycle |
| Cycling? | No monthly cycle | ~28-day cycle with distinct phases |

This is why persons with testes produce sperm continuously while persons with ovaries ovulate once per cycle.

### Primary vs Secondary Sex Characteristics — Side-by-Side
| Category | Person with testes (Person 2) | Person with ovaries (Person 1) |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary** (present at birth / directly for reproduction) | Testes, penis, scrotum, epididymis, vas deferens, prostate, seminal vesicles | Ovaries, fallopian tubes, uterus, cervix, vagina, labia, clitoris |
| **Secondary** (develop at puberty, not required for reproduction) | Facial/body hair, voice deepening, increased muscle mass, broader shoulders | Breast development, widened hips, body fat redistribution, pubic/axillary hair |
| **Driving hormone** | Testosterone | Estrogen |

## Exam-Style Applications
- "Which hormone best predicts ovulation?" -> LH surge.
- "High progesterone, moderate estrogen, low FSH/LH: uterine phase?" -> secretory phase.
- "Corpus luteum is in the ___ and releases ___." -> ovary; progesterone.
- "If progesterone cannot bind its receptor in the uterus..." -> endometrium will not be maintained normally.
- "If estrogen cannot positively regulate the hypothalamus..." -> LH surge/ovulation may fail.
