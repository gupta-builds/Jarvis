# Concept - Levels of organizational culture and how to read them
## Core idea
Organizational culture works on more than one level.
Some parts are easy to see.
Some parts are easy to hear.
The deepest parts are often invisible but still shape behavior every day.
To understand a culture well, you have to move through three levels:
1. artifacts
2. espoused values
3. underlying assumptions
This model matters because many people judge culture too quickly from surface clues.
A company may look creative, friendly, intense, or modern, but those visible clues do not automatically tell you what behavior is actually rewarded.
## 1. Artifacts: what you can observe
Artifacts are the visible parts of culture.
A newcomer can usually notice them right away.
Examples:
- office layout
- dress style
- logos and branding
- slogans and recurring phrases
- rituals and ceremonies
- stories about heroes
- how meetings feel
- how people interact across levels
- how formal or informal the communication seems
Artifacts matter because they give first impressions.
They answer questions like:
- What seems normal here?
- What kind of image is the organization presenting?
- How much hierarchy, energy, or openness is visible?
But artifacts are also dangerous to overread.
Why?
Because the same artifact can mean different things in different places.
Example:
- an open office could reflect collaboration
- or it could reflect surveillance and lack of privacy
So artifacts are useful clues, not final proof.
## 2. Espoused values: what the organization says it believes
Espoused values are the stated principles of the organization.
These may appear in:
- mission statements
- value statements
- hiring materials
- onboarding messages
- executive speeches
- internal brand language
Examples might include:
- customer first
- integrity
- innovation
- teamwork
- accountability
- inclusion
This level matters because organizations try to define what they stand for and what behavior should follow from those values.
Espoused values help members answer:
- What does this place claim to care about?
- What kind of behavior is supposed to be admired?
- What is considered right and wrong here?
But there is still an important problem:
> Stated values are not always lived values.
A company may say:
- “we value transparency”
but punish bad news.
A company may say:
- “we value innovation”
but reward risk avoidance.
So values must be tested against behavior.
## 3. Underlying assumptions: what is deeply taken for granted
Underlying assumptions are the deepest layer of culture.
They are:
- mostly invisible
- emotionally powerful
- treated as obvious truth
- often not discussed directly
These assumptions form over time when certain ways of thinking or acting repeatedly solve problems and become accepted as “the way things are.”
Examples:
- conflict is dangerous
- speed matters more than perfection
- customers always come first
- leaders should never show uncertainty
- loyalty matters more than criticism
- failure is shameful
- data matters more than intuition
- hierarchy should be respected
This layer is the most important because it quietly shapes:
- decisions
- communication
- feedback
- risk-taking
- cooperation
- ethics
- reactions to change
Once assumptions become normal, people stop noticing them.
They no longer feel like choices.
They feel like reality.
## 4. Why this three-level model matters
The model matters because it explains why culture is often misunderstood.

A person sees artifacts and assumes they understand the culture.
But the real culture may be very different.

Example:
- the office has creative decor and flexible seating
- the company says it values experimentation
- but employees quietly know that failed experiments damage careers

In that case:
- the artifacts suggest innovation
- the espoused values claim innovation
- the underlying assumptions punish innovation

The deepest layer wins.

That is why culture analysis must always ask:
1. What is visible?
2. What is claimed?
3. What is actually taken for granted?

## 5. How to read a culture well
A good culture reader does not stop at slogans.

Ask:
- What stories get repeated?
- Who gets promoted?
- What behavior gets rewarded?
- What behavior gets ignored?
- What happens when someone disagrees?
- What happens when a mistake is made?
- What happens under pressure?
- What do leaders actually react strongly to?

These questions help move from surface to substance.

## 6. Leadership connection
Leaders often focus too much on the visible layer and not enough on the hidden one.

They may redesign offices, publish new values, or improve branding.
Those moves can matter, but they do not change deep culture unless daily behavior changes too.

A leader must ask:
- What assumptions are we reinforcing?
- What do people actually learn from our actions?
- Are our systems teaching the same values our words claim?

The strongest leadership insight here is:
> Culture is taught more by repeated consequence than by repeated speech.

## 7. Entrepreneurship connection
Founders create culture very early through small repeated choices:
- how they respond to bad news
- whether they welcome disagreement
- what they praise
- who they hire
- how they handle mistakes
- whether urgency becomes panic or focus

Those choices become assumptions quickly.

This means an early-stage startup already has culture, even if it has no handbook.

## 8. Speaking connection
This concept is also helpful for speaking skill.
To communicate well in an organization, you need to know:
- what language people trust
- what kinds of arguments carry weight
- whether direct disagreement is accepted
- whether stories matter more than metrics
- whether caution or boldness is respected

Understanding culture makes communication sharper because you are not just speaking into a room.
You are speaking inside a value system.

## 9. Fast comparison
### Artifacts
- visible
- easy to notice
- hard to interpret fully

### Espoused values
- officially stated
- gives norms and ideals
- may or may not match behavior

### Underlying assumptions
- hidden
- deeply influential
- strongest driver of behavior

## 10. Quick memory line
> Artifacts show the surface. Values show the claim. Assumptions show the truth people live by.
# Concept - Culture strength, culture change, and leadership signals
## Core idea
A culture becomes powerful when people not only know the values, but feel them strongly and act on them consistently.

That is why culture strength matters.
But it is also why culture change is difficult.
The stronger the shared patterns become, the harder they are to shift.

This concept connects three big ideas:
1. culture strength
2. the dark side of strong culture
3. how leaders spread and change culture through signals and systems

## 1. What makes a culture strong
A strong culture has two major features:
- high agreement
- high intensity

### High agreement
People largely share an understanding of what matters.

### High intensity
People care strongly about those values and act on them with energy.

So strong culture is not just:
- clear values
or
- passionate people

It is both at once.

This usually creates:
- faster coordination
- stronger identity
- clearer expectations
- less need for constant supervision
- more predictable behavior

A strong culture can therefore improve performance because employees do not have to guess the rules every day.

## 2. Why strong culture can help performance
Strong culture helps when it supports the right behaviors.

Benefits include:
- alignment around goals
- role clarity
- faster decision patterns
- shared standards
- greater commitment
- easier socialization of newcomers

In a strong culture, people often know:
- what matters most
- what quality looks like
- what behavior will be respected
- what kind of effort is expected

This reduces confusion and increases consistency.

## 3. The dark side of strong culture
Strong culture is not automatically healthy.
It can become dangerous when people stop questioning it.

Possible risks:
- conformity pressure
- reduced dissent
- weak critical thinking
- rigidity during change
- ethical blind spots
- loyalty to the group over truth

A strong culture can make people feel united while still pushing them toward poor decisions.

This is especially risky when:
- the environment is changing fast
- the old assumptions no longer fit reality
- leaders confuse unity with quality
- questioning the culture feels disloyal

So the real goal is not “strong culture at any cost.”
The real goal is:
> strong enough for clarity, open enough for correction.

## 4. Subcultures and competing forces
Large organizations rarely have only one culture.
They often have subcultures based on:
- department
- profession
- geography
- product line
- leadership style

Subcultures can be useful because they fit local work demands.
But they can also create friction.

Example:
- operations may value reliability
- design may value experimentation
- finance may value control
- sales may value speed

None of these are automatically wrong.
The issue is whether the organization can coordinate across different local logics.

A leader must often manage not only one culture, but the relationship between several subcultures.

## 5. How culture is spread
Culture spreads through repeated signals.
The lecture provides several strong mechanisms.

### Formal statements
Mission, vision, and values communicate the official identity of the organization.

### Physical space
The design of space can suggest openness, hierarchy, creativity, speed, or control.

### Role modeling
People learn from what leaders repeatedly do.

### Reward systems
Rewards teach employees what the organization truly values.

### Stories and legends
Stories turn culture into memorable examples.

### Measurement and control
What is tracked shapes what people prioritize.

### Leader reaction in crisis
Crisis shows what values remain real under pressure.

### Organization design
Reporting lines, access, and structure shape daily behavior.

### Hiring and promotion
Who enters and who advances are some of the strongest culture signals in any organization.

The key idea:
> Culture spreads through repeated reinforcement, not occasional inspiration.

## 6. Why leader signals matter so much
Employees watch leaders closely because leaders define consequences.

A leader teaches culture by:
- what they notice
- what they ignore
- what they reward
- what they punish
- how they respond to bad news
- how they handle mistakes
- whether they invite voice or fear

This means leader behavior is never neutral.
Even silence communicates.

If a leader says:
- “We want honesty”
but punishes the messenger,
the real culture becomes caution and silence.

If a leader says:
- “We value teamwork”
but rewards only individual stars,
the real culture becomes competition.

## 7. Why culture change is difficult
Culture change is hard because culture is not only an idea.
It is a pattern that has been reinforced over time.

People are used to:
- certain habits
- certain assumptions
- certain informal rules
- certain emotional expectations
- certain power structures

That is why changing culture is not just changing a sentence on a website.

Real culture change requires shifts in:
- systems
- routines
- rewards
- hiring
- leadership behavior
- decision processes
- what gets celebrated
- what gets corrected

Until those change, the old culture usually survives underneath the new words.

## 8. What successful culture change usually requires
To shift culture, leaders usually need to:
1. define what must change and why
2. identify which current assumptions are getting in the way
3. align systems with new values
4. model the new behavior repeatedly
5. reinforce the change through hiring, promotion, and reward decisions
6. expect resistance and inconsistency during the transition

This matters because people often underestimate how long culture change takes.
Culture is sticky because it is social and repeated.

## 9. Culture in crucial moments
The chapter notes make an important point about major transitions:
- joining a new organization
- mergers and acquisitions
- crises
- major strategic shifts

These are culture-testing moments.

### Joining a new organization
New employees try to decode:
- what behavior is safe
- what matters here
- what gets rewarded
- how people really communicate

### Mergers and acquisitions
Cultural clash can damage integration because the two organizations may have different assumptions about:
- speed
- hierarchy
- risk
- communication
- customer focus
- accountability

### Crisis
Crisis reveals whether values survive pressure.
It often exposes the difference between stated values and actual values.

## 10. Leadership connection
This concept matters for leadership because culture is one of the leader’s strongest indirect tools.

A leader may not supervise every moment.
But culture influences thousands of moments when the leader is absent.

That means the best leaders do more than set goals.
They shape the environment that influences how people pursue those goals.

## 11. Entrepreneurship connection
Founders often wait too long to think about culture.
But culture is being built from the beginning.

Early startup signals include:
- how honestly people speak
- whether speed destroys quality
- whether mistakes become learning or blame
- whether the founder wants loyalty or truth
- whether hiring is based on talent only or talent plus fit

Those patterns become harder to change later.

## 12. Speaking connection
Culture and speaking are closely linked because culture determines:
- who feels safe to speak
- how direct people can be
- whether feedback is welcomed
- whether leaders actually listen
- whether storytelling, data, or confidence carry more weight

A strong communicator should be able to ask:
- What message style fits this culture?
- What language will sound credible here?
- Is this a culture where challenge is welcomed or resisted?
- How can I speak honestly while still fitting the setting?

## 13. Quick memory line
> Strong culture creates clarity. Weak culture creates confusion. Rigid culture creates danger.
