# Business-Level Strategy & Sustainable Advantage

## Core idea
Business-level strategy answers one question:
> **How should a firm compete inside a specific industry?**

A good strategy connects **who** is being served, **what** is being offered, and **how** the firm delivers value better than rivals. Without clear answers to all three, the firm has no real position.

---

## The three fundamental strategy questions

| Question | What it defines |
|----------|----------------|
| **Who** do we serve? | Target customer segment — broad or narrow |
| **What** do we provide? | Range and type of products or services |
| **How** do we provide it? | Unique processes, capabilities, or delivery methods |

Firms gain competitive advantage when their answers to these three questions are coherent, differentiated, and hard to copy.

---

## Mission, Vision, Goals, and Plans — the hierarchy

These four terms are often confused. They operate at different levels of abstraction:

| Term | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Mission** | Why the organization exists; its fundamental reason for being | "To organize the world's information and make it universally accessible" |
| **Vision** | A picture of the desired future state the organization aspires to reach | "To be Earth's most customer-centric company" |
| **Goals** | Specific desired outcomes the firm wants to achieve, derived from the vision | "Grow market share by 10% in three years" |
| **Plans** | Concrete actions and resource allocations designed to accomplish goals | "Launch in two new markets; hire 50 engineers; invest $5M in R&D" |

**Exam tip — how these connect:**
- Mission explains WHY
- Vision explains WHAT the future looks like
- Goals define WHAT success looks like in measurable terms
- Plans specify HOW resources will be deployed to reach goals

A firm with a mission and vision but no goals has inspiration without direction. A firm with goals but no plans has ambition without action.

## Sustainable competitive advantage

A firm has a **sustainable competitive advantage** when it creates more value than rivals in a way that is **hard to match or replace over time**.

The textbook identifies four conditions (often called the VRIN framework):

| Condition | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Valuable** | The resource or capability helps the firm create value for customers |
| **Rare** | Few or no competitors possess it |
| **Inimitable** | Difficult for rivals to copy, replicate, or acquire |
| **Non-substitutable** | There is no equivalent alternative that rivals can use instead |

**Sources of sustainable advantage:**
- Proprietary technology or patents
- Brand reputation built over decades
- Unique organizational culture or talent
- Economies of scale that newcomers can't easily match
- Protected distribution networks or customer switching costs
- Human capital that is hard to recruit away

## External analysis: Porter's Five Forces

Used to assess **industry attractiveness** — how much profit potential exists in an industry and where the pressure on profitability is coming from.

### The five forces:

**1. Threat of New Entrants**
New competitors reduce existing firms' profits by increasing supply and competing for customers.

Barriers that reduce this threat:
- Supply-side economies of scale (high volume → low unit cost)
- Demand-side network effects (more users → more valuable product, e.g. Microsoft Windows)
- High customer switching costs
- Large capital requirements to enter
- Incumbency advantages (brand, technology, regulatory knowledge)
- Restricted access to distribution channels
- Government policy (licenses, patents, regulations)

**2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers**
Strong when suppliers are concentrated, have no substitutes, or when switching suppliers is expensive.

**3. Bargaining Power of Customers (Buyers)**
Strong when buyers purchase in large volumes, products are undifferentiated, or switching is easy. Powerful buyers force prices down.

**4. Threat of Substitutes**
A substitute is a *different product* that serves the *same need* (e.g., Uber for taxis, streaming for cable). Substitutes cap prices and reduce industry profits.

**5. Rivalry Among Existing Competitors**
Most intense when:
- Products are undifferentiated (price is the main battleground)
- Fixed costs are high (firms must compete to fill capacity)
- Products are perishable
- Exit barriers are high (firms can't leave easily, so they keep fighting)

**Important limitation:** Five forces is a snapshot. It must be revisited as markets change. The goal is not only to understand pressure but to identify which forces a firm might try to *modify* in its favor.

## Internal analysis: Resource-Based View (RBV)

The RBV argues that **internal resources and capabilities** — not just industry structure — explain competitive advantage.

**Three categories of resources:**

| Category | Examples |
|----------|---------|
| **Tangible** | Real estate, equipment, inventory, cash, manufacturing plants |
| **Intangible** | Brand names, patents, proprietary processes, culture, technology |
| **Human** | Employee skills, knowledge, leadership capability, institutional memory |

**Core competencies** emerge when multiple resources and capabilities combine in ways rivals cannot easily replicate.

## Value chain analysis

A systematic tool for identifying **where in the firm's operations** value (or cost) is created.

**Primary activities** (directly create and deliver the product):
- Inbound logistics — receiving and storing raw materials
- Operations — converting inputs into finished goods
- Outbound logistics — delivering products to customers
- Marketing and sales — helping customers find and buy the product
- Service — maintaining value after the sale

**Support activities** (enable primary activities):
- Procurement — purchasing inputs
- Technology development — improving products and processes
- Human resource management — recruiting, training, developing
- Firm infrastructure — management, finance, legal

**How to use it:** Compare your value chain to competitors'. Where do you outperform? Where do you fall short? Where can you differentiate?

## SWOT Analysis — the bridge between external and internal

SWOT links the internal scan (Strengths, Weaknesses) with the external scan (Opportunities, Threats).

| Category | Source | Question |
|----------|--------|---------|
| **Strengths** | Internal | What do we do especially well? What resources or capabilities give us an edge? |
| **Weaknesses** | Internal | Where are we vulnerable? What do rivals do better? |
| **Opportunities** | External | What trends, market gaps, or competitive voids can we exploit? |
| **Threats** | External | What forces could undermine our position? |

**How SWOT creates strategy:**
- Match **Strengths → Opportunities** (leverage what you're good at where the market rewards it)
- Use **Strengths → to counter Threats** (use advantages to defend against risk)
- Address **Weaknesses** before **Opportunities** that require them
- Avoid **Weaknesses + Threats** combinations (most dangerous quadrant)

**Critical exam point:** SWOT is only useful when it leads to *action*. A SWOT that stays on paper is just a list.

## Generic competitive strategies (Porter)

Three ways to achieve competitive advantage:

### Cost Leadership
- Compete through the **lowest price** across a broad market
- How: exploit economies of scale, efficient operations, tight cost controls, standardization
- Risk: rivals find a way to get equally low costs; technology disrupts production

### Differentiation
- Compete through **uniqueness** valued by customers, justifying a premium price
- How: product features, service quality, brand, design, customer experience
- Risk: rivals copy the differentiation; customers stop valuing the difference

### Focus
- Serve a **narrow segment** — a specific geography, customer group, or product type
- Can be a **cost focus** (lowest cost in a niche) or **differentiation focus** (most distinct in a niche)
- Example: Genzyme focused on rare "orphan" diseases where large competitors had no presence
- Risk: the niche shrinks; a broad competitor enters the niche with more resources

### Stuck in the Middle
Firms that fail to commit to a clear strategy end up with:
- Higher costs than cost leaders
- Less distinction than differentiators
- No coherent position that customers recognize

This leads to weak financial performance and confused brand identity. The lesson: **strategic trade-offs are necessary**. Choosing one direction means intentionally not pursuing others.

---

## Strategy and employees

A firm's chosen strategy shapes the culture it needs:
- **Cost leadership** strategy → tight controls, efficiency focus, disciplined culture, preference for process-followers
- **Differentiation** strategy → risk tolerance, innovation, creative autonomy, preference for creative and independent thinkers
