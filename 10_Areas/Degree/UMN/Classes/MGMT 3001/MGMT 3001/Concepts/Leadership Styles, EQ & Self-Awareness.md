# Leadership Styles, EQ & Self-Awareness

## Core idea
Leadership effectiveness depends on **style** (how you lead), **emotional intelligence** (how you read and manage people), and **self-awareness** (understanding your own defaults, strengths, and blind spots).

These three work together: knowing yourself allows you to choose a style deliberately instead of defaulting blindly.

---

## Fundamental leadership debates (exam context)

Before applying any theory, recognize these ongoing debates:

| Debate | Two positions |
|--------|--------------|
| Born vs. Made | Are leaders born with innate qualities, or can anyone develop leadership through practice? |
| Universal vs. Contingent | Is there one transcendent leader for all situations, or does effectiveness depend on fit? |
| Satisfaction vs. Performance | Should leaders prioritize follower well-being or results? |

**Exam tip:** These debates help you frame answers. If asked "what makes leadership effective?" — lead with the *contingent* view (fit with context matters), not just traits.

## Task-oriented vs. relationship-oriented leadership

### Task-oriented leaders focus on:
- Short-term planning and goal setting
- Role clarity — making sure everyone knows what they are supposed to do
- Monitoring performance and progress
- Correcting work that falls below standard

### Relationship-oriented leaders focus on:
- Emotional support and coaching
- Developing subordinates' skills over time
- Recognizing and praising good work
- Building trust and morale

**Key insight:** These are **two independent dimensions**, not opposites on one scale. A leader can be high or low on both simultaneously.

## The Managerial Grid

The Managerial Grid (Blake and Mouton) maps **concern for production** (task) against **concern for people** (relationship):

| Style | Concern for Production | Concern for People | Result |
|-------|----------------------|-------------------|--------|
| Impoverished | Low | Low | Minimal effort; team drifts |
| Country Club | Low | High | Pleasant but low performance |
| Produce or Perish | High | Low | Results but burnout/resentment |
| **Team Leader** | **High** | **High** | **Best all-around performance** |

**The Team Leader style** is considered the strongest general pattern because it delivers results while maintaining commitment and morale. Most strong leaders aspire to this quadrant.

## Transformational vs. Transactional leadership

### Transactional leadership
Leaders use **exchanges** to get work done. If you do X, you get Y.

Key mechanisms:
- **Contingent reward** — "Meet the sales target and you get the bonus"
- **Management-by-exception (active)** — Constantly monitor for deviations and correct them
- **Management-by-exception (passive)** — Only intervene after a mistake has happened

**Best for:** Stable environments, routine work, clear expectations

### Transformational leadership
Leaders raise motivation and performance by connecting people to vision and meaning, not just rewards.

Four elements (the "4 I's"):
1. **Idealized Influence (Charisma)** — Leader becomes a role model others want to emulate
2. **Inspirational Motivation** — Articulates a compelling future that followers believe in
3. **Intellectual Stimulation** — Challenges followers to think differently, question assumptions, innovate
4. **Individualized Consideration** — Treats each follower as an individual; coaches and develops them personally

**Best for:** Change, growth, commitment, creative work

**Transformational vs transactional on exams:**
- Transactional = keeps operations stable using exchanges + standards
- Transformational = raises performance by adding emotion, meaning, and a compelling direction

## The Charismatic Leader
Charismatic leaders arouse strong emotional attachment through compelling vision and personal attributes.

**Characteristics:**
- Extremely self-confident
- Willing to take personal risks for the cause
- Uses powerful imagery, symbols, and metaphors to communicate vision
- Creates a strong sense of meaning and mission

**Important nuance:**
- Charisma is partly a social construction — followers *grant* it based on their perceptions
- Charisma has a **dark side**: narcissistic or exploitative leaders can use it to serve personal agendas rather than the organization's good

## Leader–Member Exchange (LMX) Theory
Leaders do not build the same relationship with every follower. Instead, they develop **differentiated relationships** that create:

- **In-group:** High-quality relationships — more trust, autonomy, challenging assignments, support
- **Out-group:** Lower-quality relationships — more formal, rule-based, transactional

**Relationship life cycle:**
1. **Stranger phase** — formal/transactional, limited trust
2. **Acquaintance phase** — testing and role-making, growing familiarity
3. **Mature Partnership phase** — strong mutual trust, often transformational in quality

**Practical implication:** In-groups often get more opportunity, which can drive performance — but also creates fairness concerns if in-groups form along demographic or personal-preference lines.

## House's Path–Goal Theory
The leader's job is to **make the path from effort to valued reward as clear and achievable as possible**.

Leaders help by:
- Reducing ambiguity about what to do (clarifying)
- Increasing confidence that effort will lead to success (motivating)
- Removing obstacles from the path (supporting)
- Connecting task completion to meaningful rewards (linking)

**Four leadership styles in Path–Goal:**
- **Directive** — tells followers exactly what to do (best when task is ambiguous)
- **Supportive** — builds relationships and reduces stress (best when work is demanding or stressful)
- **Participative** — involves followers in decisions (best when followers are skilled and value autonomy)
- **Achievement-oriented** — sets challenging goals and expects excellence (best when followers want to grow)

**Exam hook:** Path–Goal says leaders supplement what is *missing* in the task or the follower to make success feel achievable.

## Emotional Intelligence (EQ)
Emotional intelligence is the ability to recognize and manage emotions — in yourself and in others.

**Four domains:**
1. **Self-awareness** — understanding your own emotions, triggers, and tendencies
2. **Self-management** — regulating emotions instead of reacting blindly under pressure
3. **Social awareness** — reading other people's emotions and group dynamics accurately
4. **Relationship management** — using emotional insight to communicate, influence, coach, and resolve conflict

**Signals of high EQ in a leader:**
- Reads the room before speaking
- Regulates reactions before escalating conflict
- Listens actively instead of dominating
- Adapts tone to the person and situation
- Challenges others without humiliating them
- Gives feedback that lands as helpful, not threatening

**Signals of low EQ:**
- Reacts defensively to feedback
- Misreads team morale
- Lets personal frustration damage professional relationships
- Fails to adjust style across very different contexts
