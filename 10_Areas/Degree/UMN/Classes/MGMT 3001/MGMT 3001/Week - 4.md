# Week 4 — Corporate-Level Strategy, Vertical Integration & Internationalization

## What you must be able to do
- Distinguish business-level from corporate-level strategy — these answer different questions.
- Explain single-business, related diversification, and unrelated diversification — and when each makes sense.
- Apply the diversification test (three prongs) to evaluate whether an expansion move makes sense.
- Explain vertical integration, distinguish backward from forward integration, and weigh its pros and cons.
- Distinguish partnership, outsourcing, and full ownership as alternatives.
- Explain why and how firms internationalize.
- Distinguish intended, emergent, and realized strategy — and explain why the gap between plan and reality matters.

## Key ideas
- Corporate strategy is about portfolio scope and fit across businesses.
- Related diversification usually creates more value than unrelated diversification (inverted U-shape).
- Ownership is a choice: own, partner, or contract — each has a different cost structure.
- Vertical integration trades control for flexibility; the right answer depends on transaction vs. administrative costs.
- No strategy executes exactly as planned — the realized strategy is always a hybrid.

## Lecture

### 1. Business-level vs. corporate-level strategy — the distinction

| Business-level strategy | Corporate-level strategy |
|------------------------|------------------------|
| How to compete inside *one* industry | What industries should the firm be in? |
| Focus: competitive positioning | Focus: portfolio scope |
| Chapter 5 | Chapter 6 |

**Corporate-level strategy is about three decisions:**
1. **Scope** — which industries and markets to compete in
2. **Organizational design** — how to coordinate resources across business units
3. **Ownership** — should the firm own activities, partner, or outsource?

**Corporate advantage** = the value created because the firm owns these businesses together that wouldn't exist if they were independent. Without this, the portfolio destroys value instead of creating it.

---

### 2. Single-business strategy
- Full focus on one product category or market
- Deepens core competence and expertise
- Highest vulnerability: if the market shifts, the firm has nowhere to pivot
- Best for firms with a clear, strong, and durable position

**McDonald's as a Corporate Strategy Case Study (from lecture):**
McDonald's is the lecture's primary example for demonstrating how all five corporate-level strategies can be used *simultaneously* by a single firm:

1. **Single business** → Fast food, mass production (core identity)
2. **Related diversification** → Real estate (McDonald's owns the land under most of its franchise locations and charges franchisees rent — a related business using the same geographic footprint)
3. **Unrelated diversification** → Historically moved into other product lines
4. **Vertical integration** → Long-term supplier contracts and performance management (backward integration); real estate ownership and charging it back to franchisees (forward control)
5. **Partnership** → CITIC Group partnership in China to increase Chinese branches (joint venture to navigate a foreign market)
6. **Internationalization** → Localization of products — e.g., serving regional menu items in different countries to adapt the core offering to local tastes

**Why this matters:** McDonald's shows that a large firm doesn't use just one corporate strategy — it uses a portfolio of strategies simultaneously across different dimensions.

---

### 3. Diversification — three approaches

#### Related diversification
- Own multiple businesses that **share resources or capabilities**
- Goal: **economies of scope** — the cost of operating two businesses together is less than operating them separately
- Performance tends to be strongest here
- Example: Disney (films, theme parks, merchandise, streaming all share the entertainment brand)

**Economies of scope sources:**
- Shared distribution or retail presence
- Shared technology or manufacturing
- Shared brand or customer relationships
- Shared management expertise

#### Unrelated diversification
- Own businesses with **no operational connection**
- Goal: **financial economies** — spread capital risk, use financial discipline across many businesses
- **Lecture example: Amazon** → Retail + Hardware manufacture (Kindle, Echo) + Grocery (Whole Foods) + IMDB + Video (Prime Video). These divisions share almost no operational capabilities — Amazon's corporate advantage is capital allocation and data/logistics infrastructure
- Research finding: performance typically peaks with related diversification, then **declines** as diversification becomes more unrelated

**The inverted U-shape:** Performance increases from single-business to related diversification, then decreases as firms move into increasingly unrelated industries. This is one of the most testable concepts in Week 4.

#### Why firms diversify:
- Synergy (combined cost savings or capability gains)
- Growth (new revenue beyond the core)
- Risk management (reduce single-market dependence)
- Economies of scope
- International expansion
- Sometimes: managerial self-interest (compensation, prestige) — a governance concern

---

### 4. The diversification test — three prongs

Before entering any new business, all three tests must pass:

| Test | Question |
|------|---------|
| **Industry attractiveness** | Is this industry's structure favorable for high returns? (Use Five Forces) |
| **Cost of entry** | Is the cost to enter lower than the expected long-term benefit? |
| **Better-off** | Will this business be better off because the firm owns it — and will the firm be better off for owning it? |

**Bonus — ownership test:** Does the firm actually need to *own* this, or would a partnership or contract achieve the same result with less commitment?

### 5. BCG Growth-Share Matrix — portfolio tool

Classifies business units by market growth rate and relative market share:

| Quadrant | Growth | Share | Strategy |
|----------|--------|-------|---------|
| **Stars** | High | High | Invest to maintain; future cash cows |
| **Cash Cows** | Low | High | Extract cash to fund other units |
| **Question Marks** | High | Low | Choose: invest to become a star or divest |
| **Dogs** | Low | Low | Typically divest |

### 6. Vertical integration — owning more of the chain

**Definition:** Owning business units that supply inputs OR handle the distribution/sale of outputs.

| Type | Direction | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Backward** | Own upstream inputs | Candy company buys a sugar plantation |
| **Forward** | Own downstream distribution | Apple owns Apple Stores |

#### The cost-based decision rule:

**Integrate when:** Transaction costs (buying from outside) > Administrative costs (managing internally)

**Transaction costs include:**
- Writing and enforcing contracts
- Risk of opportunistic behavior by suppliers
- Monitoring quality and delivery
- Negotiating at each transaction

**Administrative costs include:**
- Coordination overhead across business units
- Internal management of activities that could be externally specialized
- Risk of inefficiency without market competition

#### Benefits of vertical integration:
- More control over quality and delivery
- Lower transaction costs (no repeated negotiation or contracts)
- Easier coordination and differentiation
- Protection of proprietary processes or technologies
- Capture more of the value chain margin

#### Costs of vertical integration:
- Higher administrative burden
- Lower flexibility (locked into internal processes)
- Internal suppliers may become inefficient without competitive pressure
- Risk of getting locked into outdated technology
- Reduces ability to respond quickly to market shifts

### 7. Alternatives to full ownership

| Alternative | Description | Best when | Real example (from lecture) |
|-------------|-------------|----------|-----------------------------|
| **Outsourcing** | Contract with an outside firm for activities previously done internally | Activity is not core; external specialists are more efficient | — |
| **Spot contracts** | Buy commodities at market price; no long-term commitment | Commodity goods with stable, competitive markets | — |
| **Strategic alliance** | Collaborate without merging; share knowledge or access | Neither firm can afford to fully own; complementary capabilities | Du Nord & Delta |
| **Joint venture** | Create a new shared entity to pursue a specific opportunity | Both firms have assets to contribute; neither wants to absorb the other | Toyota and Mazda Plant (joint US manufacturing) |
| **Preferred supplier** | Long-term exclusive or near-exclusive relationship | Stability needed but full ownership is too costly | Microsoft and Dell |
| **Acquisition** | One firm buys another outright | Full control and integration needed | Amazon and Whole Foods |
| **Merger** | Two firms combine into one | Equal footing; combined strength needed | Disney and Pixar |

**The partnership spectrum (from lecture):** Ranges from low collaboration (preferred supplier arrangements) to high organizational combination (joint ventures, mergers, acquisitions). The higher the collaboration level, the more committed — and the harder to exit.

### 8. Internationalization

**Why firms expand internationally:**
- **New markets** — grow revenue beyond a saturated home market
- **Economies of scale** — spread fixed costs over more units globally
- **Location advantages** — cheaper labor, raw materials, specialized talent
- **Risk diversification** — reduce dependence on one country's economy

**What implementation requires:**
- Aligning organizational structure with international scope (e.g., divisional structure by region)
- HR systems that function across cultures and regulations
- Information and control systems that work across time zones

### 9. Intended, Emergent, and Realized Strategy — critical exam concept

Strategy rarely executes as planned. Three terms describe the gap:

| Type | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Intended strategy** | What managers deliberately designed and planned to do |
| **Emergent strategy** | Unplanned patterns that develop through real-world learning and adaptation |
| **Realized strategy** | The actual strategy the firm ends up executing — a mix of intended and emergent |

**Formula:** Realized = (Intended − Abandoned) + Emergent

**The ESPN case study (from lecture) — the clearest example of emergent strategy:**

| Stage | What happened |
|-------|--------------|
| **Intended strategy** | Scott and Dan Rasmussen were fired from the New England Whalers. They built a cable network to air Connecticut sports — that was the whole plan |
| **Emergent strategy** | The station became popular and started airing less regional content — eventually even Roller Derby — because viewers responded to anything sports-related |
| **Realized strategy** | ESPN stations, ESPN The Magazine, and eventually the Disney/ABC partnership — a global sports media empire that nobody planned from the start |

**Why this matters:** ESPN's founders didn't set out to build the world's dominant sports network. The realized strategy emerged from what worked — and they recognized and invested in it. This is emergent strategy working at its best.

**Why it matters for exams:**
- Most firms abandon parts of their intended strategy because conditions change
- Some of the best strategies emerge from experiments, customer feedback, and unexpected opportunities
- The danger: firms that can't distinguish between "this isn't working" and "this is hard" may abandon good strategies too early

**Exam application:** If a case study shows a firm doing something different from its original plan, ask: is this emergent strategy that should be recognized and built on, or is it strategic drift that should be corrected?

Forward integration::Owning or controlling downstream distribution or customer-facing stages.

Intended strategy::What managers originally planned to do.
Emergent strategy::Patterns that develop through adaptation and learning — unplanned, but potentially valuable.
Realized strategy::The actual strategy executed — a combination of planned and emergent elements.
BCG Matrix::A portfolio tool classifying business units as Stars (high growth/share), Cash Cows (low growth/high share), Question Marks (high growth/low share), or Dogs (low growth/share).
