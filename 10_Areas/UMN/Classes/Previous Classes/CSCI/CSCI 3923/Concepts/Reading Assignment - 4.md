# Chapter - 6
Chapter 6 of _Weapons of Math Destruction_, titled **"INELIGIBLE TO SERVE: Getting a Job,"** explores how big companies use computer programs to decide who gets hired, often creating unfair "walls" that keep good people out.
1. *The Story of Kyle Behm (Personality Tests)*: The chapter starts with a young man named Kyle who was very smart but had **bipolar disorder**. When he applied for a simple, part-time job at a grocery store, he was **"red-lighted"** (rejected) by a computer test before he even got to talk to a human.
	- **The Test:** It was a personality test that measured things like how "outgoing" or "agreeable" someone is.
	- **The Problem:** Because Kyle had a mental health condition, the test saw him as "broken," even though he was perfectly capable of doing the job.
	- **The Unfairness:** Kyle’s father, a lawyer, realized that these tests might be illegal because they act like secret **medical exams**, which companies aren't allowed to use to pick workers.
2. *From "Who You Know" to "What the Robot Thinks"*: In the old days, people got jobs through friends or family (the "insider game"), which was often unfair to women and minorities. To fix this, companies like **Kronos** built software to make hiring "scientific".
	- **The Scale:** Today, these automated systems are used on **60 to 70 percent** of people applying for jobs in the U.S..
	- **Proxies:** Since the computer doesn't know how you will actually work in the future, it uses **proxies** (stand-in data) like your personality score to guess if you’ll be a good employee.
3. *Why Personality Tests are "Sloppy"*: The author explains that while these tests are sold as high-tech science, they are actually **poor predictors** of how well someone will do at work.
	- **The Goal:** The primary goal of these tests isn't to find the _best_ worker; it’s to **exclude as many people as possible as cheaply as possible**.
	- **The "Damned if You Do" Questions:** Some tests force you to choose between two bad options, like "I am lazy" or "I am high-strung," with no way to explain yourself.
	- **Unique vs. Orderly:** A grocery store might ask if you are **"unique"** or **"orderly"**. If you pick "unique," the computer might label you a **narcissist** (someone who only cares about themselves) and reject you.
4. *Managing "Herds" vs. Individuals*: O'Neil compares hiring to **professional basketball**.
	- **Basketball Teams:** They use data to find "stars." If they miss a good player, they study what they got wrong and fix their model.
	- **Big Corporations:** They manage **"herds"** of people for low-wage jobs. They don't care if the computer accidentally rejects a "star" because there are millions of other people to choose from. They have **no reason to fix their mistakes**.
5. *Resume Robots (The "Emily vs. Lakisha" Study)*: Even if you pass the personality test, your **resume** is usually read by a robot first—about **72 percent** of resumes are never seen by a human.
	- **Name Bias:** A famous study showed that resumes with "White-sounding" names (like Emily) got **50 percent more callbacks** than identical resumes with "Black-sounding" names (like Lakisha).
	- **Robot Rules:** To pass the robot, you have to use "safe" fonts like Arial and use specific **keywords**. This means people with money to pay for "resume experts" get the jobs, while the poor get left behind.
6. *The St. George’s Medical School WMD*: The author shares a story from a London medical school in the 1970s that tried to use a computer to screen students.
	- **The Glitch:** The computer was taught using data from past human bosses who were biased.
	- **The Result:** The computer started **automatically rejecting** anyone with a "foreign-sounding" name or women, because that’s what the humans had done in the past. It was a "Weapon of Math Destruction" because it made the school's old prejudices run faster and more efficiently.
7. *Churn and Commutes*: Companies hate **"churn"** (when workers quit quickly) because it costs money to train new ones.
	- **The Xerox Example:** Xerox used a model to find people who would stay a long time. The math found that people with **long commutes** were more likely to quit.
	- **The Choice:** Because people with long commutes often lived in **poor neighborhoods**, Xerox realized that using "distance from work" would unfairly hurt poor people. To their credit, they **removed that data** to be fair.
8. *The Hunt for "Superstars" (Gild)*: For high-paying tech jobs, a company called **Gild** looks at "social capital".
	- **Social Capital:** This means looking at how much you help other people online or if you are "friends" with famous tech leaders like the founders of Google.
	- **Hidden Talent:** Gild even found that high-quality coders often visit certain **Japanese manga** (comic book) websites.
	- **The Blind Spot:** If you are a mom taking care of kids or have a hobby that isn't online, your "social score" might be lower, even if you are a great worker.
9. *Conclusion: Digital Phrenology*: The author ends by calling these hiring models a new kind of **phrenology** (a fake science from the 1800s where people felt bumps on your head to guess your personality). These modern "weapons" use **untested assumptions** to lock people out of the workforce, creating a cycle where the "losers" stay losers simply because a computer said so.
# Chapter - 7
Chapter 7 of _Weapons of Math Destruction_, titled **"Sweating Bullets: On the Job,"** looks at how mathematical models treat workers once they actually have a job. It explores how computers treat humans like replaceable machine parts to save companies money.
1. *The "Clopening" Nightmare*: The author introduces a new word: **"clopening"**.
	- **What it means:** This is when an employee works late at night to close a store and then has to come back just a few hours later, before the sun is up, to open it.
	- **Why it happens:** Big companies like Starbucks and Walmart use data to see when they are busiest. If the computer sees they need someone at 11:00 PM and again at 4:00 AM, it might schedule the same person for both.
	- **The Human Cost:** This makes workers very tired and ruins their lives because they can’t plan for sleep, meals, or taking care of their children.
2. *From "Hunches" to "PhDs"*: In the past, scheduling was a "guessing game". A store owner might notice it’s slow on Tuesday mornings and busy on Saturdays and change the schedule based on that observation.
	- **The Big Data Change:** Now, companies hire "legions of PhDs" with powerful computers to calculate exactly how many workers are needed every single hour.
	- **Irregularity:** These programs look at things like the weather or Twitter traffic. If it’s raining, the computer knows people will go to a café, so it tells a worker to come in right now. Workers often only get a day or two of notice for their shifts.
3. *"Just-in-Time" Humans*: The author explains a concept called **"Just-in-Time"** manufacturing.
	- **The Factory Idea:** In the 1960s, car companies like Toyota realized they could save money by ordering parts (like steering wheels) only right when they needed them, so the parts wouldn't sit around in a warehouse.
	- **The People Problem:** Scheduling software treats **people like those car parts**. The company wants workers to show up "right on cue" so the company doesn't "waste" money paying someone during a slow hour.
4. *Why This is a WMD (Weapon of Math Destruction)*: This software meets the three rules of being a WMD:
	- **Opacity (Mystery):** Workers have no idea how the computer picks their hours; they are just summoned by an "arbitrary program".
	- **Scale:** These programs are used at massive companies, affecting millions of low-wage workers.
	- **Damage:** It keeps people in poverty. For example, a mom named Jannette Navarro had to stop going to college because her Starbucks schedule was so crazy she couldn't plan her classes.
5. *The "Idea Generator" (Cataphora)*: The author talks about a company called Cataphora that made software to rate office workers.
	- **Digital Phrenology:** It scanned every email and message to see who was "sharing ideas".
	- **The Blind Spot:** Computers can’t measure "soft skills," like a worker who makes everyone laugh or gives good advice over lunch. If those things aren't in an email, the computer thinks that worker is "useless".
	- **No Feedback:** If the computer incorrectly labels a worker as a "loser" and they get fired, the system never learns that it made a mistake.
6. *The Teacher Evaluation (Tim Clifford)*: The chapter also discusses "Value-Added Models" used to grade teachers.
	- **The Flip-Flop:** A teacher named Tim Clifford got a terrible score of 6 out of 100 one year, but the next year he got a 96 out of 100, even though he taught the exact same way.
	- **Statistical "Noise":** Because teachers only have about 25–30 students, the math is "random". One "bad" or "good" kid can change the whole score, making the model a "statistical farce".
	- **Simpson's Paradox:** This is a math error where a whole group shows one trend (like SAT scores going down), but when you look at subgroups (like different income levels), every single group is actually doing better. This error was used to unfairly blame teachers for failing schools.
7. *The Ethical Conclusion*: The author argues that the "objective" of these models is the problem.
	- **Profit over People:** These models are built for **efficiency and profitability**, not for justice or helping people.
	- **A "Captive" Workforce:** Because these programs are everywhere, workers can’t just quit and find a better job—every other company is using the same unfair math.
	- **The Cycle:** The math makes the rich richer while keeping the poor "bleary-eyed" and struggling.
# Extra Readings
## Ethical Frameworks (short but in-depth)
> [!NOTE] Fast way to use frameworks in this class
> 1) Name the framework.  
> 2) State its “main question.”  
> 3) Apply it to the case (who is affected + what the framework says).  
> 4) Name the weakness/edge case (what it misses).
### Cultural relativism
- **Core idea:** What counts as “right” depends on the norms of the culture you’re in.
- **Main question:** *What does my society/culture treat as acceptable here?*
- **Strengths:** Reminds you that moral views are shaped by social context; avoids assuming your norms are “default.”
- **Limits:** Can’t easily criticize harmful practices if a culture permits them; cultures can conflict; a single culture can be split (no single “answer”); brand-new tech may not have settled norms yet.
### Moral subjectivism
- **Core idea:** Each individual decides what is right for themselves.
- **Main question:** *What do I personally believe is right in this situation?*
- **Strengths:** Forces you to own your reasoning; recognizes individual context.
- **Limits:** If “I think it’s okay,” then almost anything can be justified; gives weak protection to others who are harmed; breaks down when people’s views clash.
### Moral relativism
- **Core idea:** No objective moral standard exists; therefore we should be cautious about judging others.
- **Main question:** *Do I have grounds to judge someone else’s action as wrong?*
- **Strengths:** Encourages humility and awareness of bias.
- **Limits:** We still *must* judge in real life (work, policy, safety); harm spills across groups—“don’t judge” doesn’t stop damage.
### Act utilitarianism (consequentialism)
- **Core idea:** The morally right act is the one that produces the best overall outcomes.
- **Main question:** *Which option produces the most overall well-being (and least suffering)?*
- **Strengths:** Takes harm seriously; makes you list stakeholders and consequences.
- **Limits:** Consequences are hard to predict and compare; can sacrifice minorities if “net good” is larger; can hide injustice behind totals.
### Rule utilitarianism
- **Core idea:** Judge the rule, not the one-off act: if everyone followed the rule, would outcomes improve?
- **Main question:** *What if this became a general rule—would that make society better or worse?*
- **Strengths:** Helps prevent “special pleading”; highlights trust, stability, and long-run effects.
- **Limits:** The “right” rule framing is tricky; still risks majority domination; still faces prediction/measurement problems.
### Kantian (rule) deontology (duty-based)
- **Core idea:** Morality is about duties and respect for persons, not outcomes.
- **Main question(s):**
  - *Am I treating people as ends (not tools)?*
  - *Could I will my action as a universal rule without contradiction?*
- **Strengths:** Strong protection for individuals; flags exploitation quickly (e.g., coercion, deception, using people as a tool).
- **Limits:** Doesn’t weigh outcomes well; duties can conflict (which duty wins?).
### Act deontology
- **Core idea:** Duties matter, but context can change which duty has priority.
- **Main question:** *Which duty is most binding in this specific context?*
- **Strengths:** Handles conflicting duties better than strict rule-only versions.
- **Limits:** Still may under-weight consequences; disagreements remain about which duty dominates.
### Social contract theory
- **Core idea:** Morality helps people live together; rules are justified if reasonable people would accept them as the “deal” for living in society.
- **Main question:** *Would this rule/practice be acceptable if I could be in any role affected by it?*
- **Strengths:** Connects ethics to legitimacy, fairness, and enforceable norms; good for policy/standards.
- **Limits:** People don’t literally consent; the “minimal deal” may ignore deeper inequities.
### Rights-based approaches
- **Core idea:** Evaluate whether anyone’s rights are violated (property, privacy, speech, due process, etc.).
- **Main question:** *Whose rights are involved, and are any being infringed?*
- **Strengths:** Strong baseline protection; good for privacy, consent, and procedural fairness.
- **Limits:** Rights can conflict; disagreement about what counts as a “right” (and how strong it is).
### Legalism (not a full ethical theory)
- **Core idea:** “If it’s legal, it’s moral.”
- **Main question:** *Is it allowed by the rules/law?*
- **Strengths:** Rules matter for coordination and accountability.
- **Limits:** Laws can be unjust; law doesn’t cover every moral case (especially new tech); legality ≠ ethical justification.
### Virtue / character ethics
- **Core idea:** Focus on what a good person (with good character) would do; habits and moral perception matter.
- **Main question:** *What would a just/honest/courageous person do here, and what kind of person does this choice make me?*
- **Strengths:** Explains why “seeing the moral issue” matters; highlights integrity, courage, care, humility.
- **Limits:** Hard to translate into a single “correct” action; virtues can conflict; people disagree on ideal character.
