# Research Ethics, Consent, IRB, IACUC, and the 4 Pillars

## Ethics 1 — IRB and IACUC Roles

### IRB — Institutional Review Board
- Reviews research involving **human participants**.
- Composition: administrators, scientists, and **community members** (not just scientists).
- Ensures proposed research meets ethical standards **before** it begins.
- Reviews:
  - Risks and benefits to participants.
  - Whether informed consent is properly obtained.
  - Privacy and confidentiality protections.
  - Fair selection of participants (justice).
  - Whether deception is justified and if debriefing is planned.

### IACUC — Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee
- Reviews research involving **non-human animals**.
- Composition: administrators, scientists, veterinarians, and community members.
- Ensures animals are treated humanely.
- Also conducts **regular inspections** of animal facilities and research protocols.

| | IRB | IACUC |
|--|-----|-------|
| **Oversight of** | Human subjects research | Animal research |
| **Core concerns** | Consent, risk/benefit, fairness, privacy | Humane treatment, minimize pain/suffering |
| **When it acts** | Before research begins; reviews protocols | Before research begins; inspects facilities |

## Ethics 2 — The 3 Rs of Animal Research

### Reduce
- Use **fewer animals** while still obtaining valid results.
- Maximize the information obtained **per animal** (e.g., use one group for multiple measures).

### Refine
- **Modify procedures** to minimize pain, stress, or suffering.
- Improve: handling methods, anesthesia, housing conditions, surgical technique.

### Replace
- **Avoid animal use** when an alternative method can answer the research question.
- Examples of replacements: cell culture (in vitro), computer modeling, simulations, organ-on-a-chip technologies, previously collected datasets.

### Ethical vs. Unethical Use of the 3 Rs

| Ethical | Unethical |
|---------|-----------|
| Testing in cell culture before moving to animals | Using far more animals than the experiment requires |
| Using pain control (anesthesia, analgesics) appropriately | Allowing avoidable pain or distress |
| Designing experiments efficiently to minimize animal numbers | Using animals when a validated non-animal method exists |
| Choosing species with least sentience capable of answering the question | Selecting convenient animals without justification |

## Ethics 3 — Consent in Human Subjects Research

### Core Principle
- Participation must be **voluntary**.
- Participants must **understand** what they are agreeing to before they agree.

### Informed Consent
- Participants are told and must understand:
  - What the study is about.
  - What will happen to them.
  - Possible **risks**.
  - Possible **benefits**.
  - That they can **withdraw** at any time without penalty.
- Then they agree (or refuse) based on this understanding.

### Ethically Obtained Consent
- Information is **understandable** (appropriate language, reading level).
- Risks are fully **disclosed**.
- Participation is **voluntary** — no coercion or undue pressure.
- Person has **decision-making capacity**.
- No deception, or if deception is used, it is justified and participants are **debriefed** afterward.

### Unethically Obtained Consent
- Important risks are **hidden or minimized**.
- Misleading participants in unjustified ways.
- Coercion or pressure to participate (e.g., withholding treatment, offering excessive rewards).
- Consent forms are **too complex** for participants to understand.
- Participants are **not truly informed** before agreeing.

## Ethics 4 — Specific Consent, General Consent, and Informed Consent

| Type | Scope | What it means |
|------|-------|--------------|
| **Informed consent** | Quality of consent | Participant understands risks/benefits and freely agrees — describes the *ethics* of permission |
| **Specific consent** | Narrow scope | Participant agrees to use of their data/tissues for *specific* question(s), *specific* time period, *specific* researchers only |
| **General consent** | Broad scope | Broader permission for unspecified future use; more flexibility for researchers, but less control for the participant |

> **Key distinction:** "Informed" describes the *quality/ethics* of consent (did they understand?). "Specific" vs. "general" describes the *scope* of what they agreed to (how broadly can data/samples be used?).

### Why This Matters in Transplant and Research Contexts
- In organ donation: does "donor" status automatically consent to all uses of tissue?
- In research: if a patient donates a blood sample for one study, can researchers use it for something else?
- The Henrietta Lacks case (pre-HIPAA): tissue used without any consent → violated autonomy and justice.

## Ethics 5 — The 4 Pillars Applied to Research Cases

### Autonomy
- Central to human subjects research.
- People must be able to **make free, informed decisions** about their participation.
- Question to ask: Did participants truly understand? Were they free to refuse?

### Beneficence
- Research should aim to produce **meaningful benefit**:
  - New knowledge that improves health.
  - Better treatments, prevention strategies, or care.
- The benefit should be proportionate to the risks taken.

### Non-Maleficence
- Harm must be **minimized**.
- Risks should not be disproportionate or unnecessary.
- Applies to both the research subjects AND to third parties who might be harmed by the research outcomes.

### Justice
- **Benefits and burdens** of research should be distributed fairly.
- Vulnerable populations (prisoners, low-income groups, children) must not be exploited as research subjects simply because they are convenient.
- Historical failures: **Tuskegee syphilis study** — Black men with syphilis were denied treatment without their knowledge; violated autonomy, justice, beneficence, and non-maleficence.

## Applying Pillars to Case Studies (Exam Style)

### Human study case — Questions to ask:
- Did participants understand what was happening?
- Was deception used? Was it justified? Was there debriefing?
- Were the risks reasonable given the potential benefit?
- Were participants free to refuse?
- Was a vulnerable population exploited?

### Animal study case — Questions to ask:
- Could animals have been **replaced** with a non-animal method?
- Was **suffering minimized** (Refine)?
- Was the **smallest valid number** of animals used (Reduce)?
