# Week - 1
## 1.1 Structural Organization of the Human Body
To understand the human body, we must view it as a hierarchical masterpiece where complexity is built from the bottom up.
- **The Chemical Level:** This is the most basic stage. It begins with **subatomic particles** (protons, electrons, neutrons) which form **atoms**. Atoms are the smallest units of pure substances called **elements** (such as hydrogen, oxygen, carbon, and nitrogen). When two or more atoms bond, they form **molecules** (like water, proteins, and sugars), which are the chemical building blocks for everything that follows.
- **The Cellular Level:** The **cell** is the smallest independently functioning unit of life. Even single-celled bacteria have this structure. In humans, cells consist of **cytoplasm** (a water-based fluid) enclosed by a flexible membrane and containing various **organelles**—tiny units that perform specific jobs.
- **The Tissue Level:** A **tissue** is a community of similar cells that work together to perform a specific task.
- **The Organ Level:** An **organ** is an anatomically distinct structure made of two or more tissue types (e.g., the bladder or the heart), each performing one or more physiological functions.
- **The Organ System Level:** This is a group of organs working together to meet major physiological needs. **Figure 2** in your text identifies 11 major systems:
	- **Integumentary:** Encloses internal structures (skin).
	- **Skeletal:** Supports the body and enables movement.
	- **Muscular:** Enables movement and helps maintain body temperature.
	- **Nervous:** Detects and processes sensory information.
	- **Endocrine:** Secretes hormones to regulate processes.
	- **Cardiovascular:** Delivers oxygen and nutrients to tissues.
	- **Lymphatic:** Returns fluid to the blood and defends against pathogens.
	- **Respiratory:** Removes carbon dioxide and delivers oxygen.
	- **Digestive:** Processes food and removes wastes.
	- **Urinary:** Controls water balance and removes wastes from the blood.
	- **Reproductive:** Produces sex hormones and gametes.
- **The Organism Level:** This is the highest level—a living being that can independently perform all functions necessary for life.
## 1.2 DNA Overview
All living things pass on genetic material to their offspring in the form of **DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid)**, which acts as a "blueprint" for building each unique individual.
- **Location and Genome:** In humans, DNA is protected inside a membrane-bound organelle called the **nucleus**. Every cell in your body contains a complete copy of these instructions, known as your **genome**(unique).
- **Cellular Differentiation:** While every cell has the same DNA, a cheek cell looks different from a muscle cell because each cell only uses a *subset* of those instructions to make the specific proteins it needs.
- **Chemical Structure:** DNA is a macromolecule composed of units called **nucleotides**. Each nucleotide has three parts: *a sugar, a phosphate group, and a base*. There are four bases: *Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Cytosine (C), and Guanine (G).
- **The Genetic Code:** The *order* of these bases creates the code that instructs cells on how to build proteins.
- **Organization:** DNA is organized into *23 pairs of chromosomes*. Before a cell divides, it replicates its DNA so each new cell gets a full copy.
- **Mutations:** Occasionally, errors occur during replication. *Uncorrected errors are called **mutations***. If a mutation occurs in a *sex cell* (sperm or egg), it can be passed to offspring, creating the variation we see in populations.
## 1.3 Human DNA and Genetic Diversity
While humans are 99.9% identical, the remaining 0.1% accounts for our unique traits.
- **Genes and Alleles:** A *gene* is a specific segment of DNA that codes for a trait (like a protein for blood type). Different versions of the same gene are called *alleles*. The location of a gene on a chromosome is its *locus*.
- **The Human Genome Project:** From 1990 to 2003, researchers mapped the 3 billion base pairs of the human genome, identifying approximately *22,300 protein-coding genes*.
- **SNPs (Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms):** Most human variation comes from these single-base-pair differences (e.g., having an 'A' where someone else has a 'C').
- **Distribution of Variation:** Human genetic variation is *continuous*. It is very difficult to identify genetic "groups" or races because variation in most traits is distributed throughout the global population. Humans are essentially one large, interbreeding group.
- **Sources of Variation:** Diversity arises from *mutations*, *genetic recombination* (==the shuffling of genes during sexual reproduction==), and *gene flow* (==migration==).
## 1.4 The Genetic Basis of Evolution
Evolution is defined as a **change in the frequency of traits within a population over time**. Biologists distinguish between **micro-evolution**, which involves *small changes in trait frequencies from one generation to the next*, and **macro-evolution**, which describes *larger changes such as one species diverging into two over many generations*. The **theory of evolution** serves as a unifying framework for biology, based on the principle that all life has diversified from a **common ancestor**. The American geneticist *Theodosius Dobzhansky* famously stated that "nothing makes sense in biology except in the light of evolution".
- **Natural Selection:** Independently conceived by *Charles Darwin and Alfred Russell Wallace*, this is a primary mechanism of evolution based on three principles:
	    1. *Inheritance:* Characteristics are passed from parents to offspring.
	    2. *Competition:* More offspring are produced than resources can support, leading to a struggle for survival.
	    3. *Variation:* Offspring vary in their inherited traits.
 - **Descent with Modification:** Individuals with traits (==adaptations==) that allow them to compete better for resources will survive and have more offspring, making those traits more common in the next generation.
 - **Evidence in Nature:** Peter and Rosemary Grant demonstrated this in Galápagos finches; after a drought, birds with *larger beaks* survived better because they could eat hard seeds, leading to an evolved population with larger average beak sizes.
 - **Patterns of Evolution:**
	 - *Divergent Evolution:* Two species evolve in different directions from a common point.
	 - *Convergent Evolution:* Distantly related species independently evolve similar traits (analogous structures) because of similar environmental pressures (e.g., flight in both bats and insects).
- **Structures:**
	- *Homologous:* Shared similarities due to a ==common ancestor== (e.g., the bones in a whale's flipper and a human arm).
	- *Analogous:* Similar in function but ==different evolutionary origins== (e.g., wings of a butterfly and a bird).
	- *The Modern Synthesis:* This is the coherent 20th-century theory that integrated Darwin’s natural selection with Mendel’s genetics. In **population genetics**, evolution is defined as *a change in the **frequency of a specific gene** (allele) in a population*. All gene frequencies in a *gene pool* (the sum of all genes in a population) must add up to 100%. Beneficial genes may become *"fixed,"* meaning every individual carries them, while *detrimental genes* are eliminated. *Figure 5* shows a classic example: *peppered moths* shifting from light to dark coloration as the Industrial Revolution caused soot to darken tree trunks, providing better camouflage for darker individuals.
## 1.5 Mechanisms of Evolution
While natural selection is the most well-known force, four factors can change gene frequencies.
1. **Natural Selection**: This mechanism increases the frequency of beneficial alleles that aid in *survival to sexual maturity and reproduction* while selecting against deleterious ones.
2. **Mutation**: Though its effect on frequency is small in a single individual, mutation is the *ultimate source* of new alleles and genetic variation in all populations.
3. **Genetic Drift**: *Genetic drift* is the effect of *random chance* on a population’s genetic structure. It is most significant in *small populations*. In a population of ten, the death of one individual represents the loss of 10% of the gene pool. Frequencies may undergo a *"random walk"* until an allele is fixed or lost (*Figure 1*).
	- *Bottleneck Effect:* A disaster (like a hurricane) kills a large portion of the population for reasons unrelated to traits, leaving a random, unrepresentative sample of survivors (*Figure 2*).
	- *Founder Effect:* A small group leaves a large population to start a new one, with a genetic structure matching only the "founding fathers and mothers". This is seen in the **Afrikaner population** of Dutch settlers, who have high incidences of **Huntington’s disease (HD)** and **Fanconi anemia (FA)** because the original colonists carried these mutations.
4. **Gene Flow:** *Gene flow is the movement of alleles in and out of a population due to the **migration** of individuals or **gametes** (like wind-blown pollen)*. *Figure 3* demonstrates how an individual traveling between geographic locations can introduce a new allele (like the brown allele) into a different population (the green population).
## 1.6 Introduction to Phylogenies
Evolution is the gradual change in traits within a population over generations, which can eventually lead to the formation of new species. A **phylogeny** is the description of these evolutionary relationships, such as which groups are most closely related or which diverged most recently from a common ancestor. Biologists use the term **taxa** (singular: taxon) to refer to any level of these groups, whether they are populations, species, or entire kingdoms.
- **Phylogenetic Trees:** These diagrams act as hypotheses of the evolutionary past.
	- **Rooted Trees:** These have a single ancestral lineage at the base representing a common ancestor for all organisms in the diagram. **Figure 1a** shows how the three domains—Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya—diverge from one single point.
	- **Unrooted Trees:** These show relationships among species but do not attempt to identify a single common ancestor (**Figure 1b**).
- **Branch Points:** A **branch point** is a node where a single lineage splits into distinct new ones. Rotating the tree at these points does not change the information because each taxon's evolution from that point is independent.
- **Limitations:** It is a common mistake to assume more closely related organisms always look more alike; however, if a lineage develops a major new adaptation, it may look very different from its close relatives. **Figure 2** illustrates this: lizards and rabbits both have amniotic eggs, yet lizards and frogs might appear more similar to a casual observer even though the lizard is more closely related to the rabbit.
- **Taxonomy:** This is the science of classifying organisms into a hierarchical system of increasingly specific groups. The levels, from most inclusive to most specific, are: **Domain, Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, and Species**. **Figures 3 and 4** demonstrate this hierarchy using a dog (_Canis lupus familiaris_), showing how it shares broad categories like "Animalia" with many creatures but becomes more isolated as the categories narrow toward its specific subspecies.
## 1.7 How Phylogenies are Made
To build these trees, scientists use **morphologic** (form and function) and **genetic** evidence.
- **Homologous Structures:** Features that overlap both morphologically and genetically because they were inherited from a common ancestor are **homologous**. **Figure 1** shows that the bone arrangements in a bird wing and a bat wing are homologous, indicating a shared evolutionary past.
- **Analogous Structures:** When similar characteristics occur due to similar environmental pressures rather than a close evolutionary relationship, they are **analogous** (or a **homoplasy**). For example, a honeybee wing and a bird wing serve the same function but have completely different embryonic origins and structures (**Figure 2**).
- **Molecular Systematics:** This field uses DNA technology to identify relationships. While powerful, it can be tricky; a single mutation like an insertion or deletion can shift the entire genetic code, making two closely related sequences look unrelated.
- **Cladistics and Parsimony:** Scientists organize homologous traits into **clades** (or **monophyletic groups**), which include a single ancestor and all its descendants. To determine the most likely tree, they apply **maximum parsimony**, assuming that evolution followed the simplest path with the fewest major changes. **Figure 4** illustrates this by grouping lizards, rabbits, and humans into the clade **Amniota** because they all share the ancestral trait of an amniotic egg.
## 1.8 The Evolution of Primates
Humans belong to the order **Primates**, which also includes lemurs, monkeys, and apes.
- **Primate Characteristics:** All primates descended from tree-dwellers and share adaptations for climbing, known as **brachiation**. Key traits include:
	1. **Rotating shoulder joints**.
	2. **Opposable thumbs** (and big toes in most) for gripping branches.
	3. **Stereoscopic vision**, where two overlapping fields of vision allow for depth perception and gauging distance.
	4. **Large brains**, flattened nails instead of claws, and a trend toward holding the body upright.
- **Prosimians vs. Anthropoids:** **Prosimians** (e.g., lemurs, bush babies) are often smaller, nocturnal, and have smaller brains than **anthropoids** (monkeys, apes, and humans).
- **Apes:** Evolving roughly 25 million years ago, apes are larger than monkeys, lack a tail, and are more intelligent. The **great apes** include the genera _Pan_ (chimpanzees and bonobos), _Gorilla_, _Pongo_ (orangutans), and _Homo_ (humans) (**Figure 2**).
- **Hominins:** This term refers to species more closely related to humans than to chimpanzees.
	- **Australopithecus:** These early hominins (like "Lucy") lived 4 million to 2 million years ago and were the first to show definitive evidence of **bipedalism** (walking upright), though they still spent time in trees.
- **Homo erectus:** Appearing 1.8 million years ago, this was the first hominin to migrate out of Africa, use fire, and hunt (**Figure 6**).
	- **Homo sapiens:** Modern humans likely arose in Africa about 160,000 to 200,000 years ago, as supported by mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) and Y chromosome evidence.
## 1.9 Cells of the Nervous System
The nervous system is built from two types of cells: **neurons** and **glial cells**.
- **Glial Cells (Glia):** These outnumber neurons ten to one and provide physical/metabolic support, insulation, and waste transport.
- **Neuron Structure:** Neurons are information processors (**Figure 1**).
	- **Dendrites:** Tree-like branches that receive signals.
	- **Soma:** The cell body containing the nucleus.
	- **Axon:** A major extension that sends signals toward other neurons
	- **Myelin Sheath:** A fatty layer produced by glia that coats the axon, acting as an insulator to increase signal speed. Loss of this (as in Multiple Sclerosis) causes signal interference.
	- **The Signal:** Communication is an **electrochemical event**.
	- **Resting Potential:** Between signals, the membrane is in a state of readiness, with sodium (Na+) concentrated outside and potassium (K+) inside, creating a negative internal charge (**Figure 3**).
	- **Action Potential:** When a signal is received, sodium gates open, and Na+ rushes in. If it reaches the **threshold of excitation**, a massive electrical spike (the action potential) moves down the axon like a wave (**Figure 4**). This is an **all-or-none phenomenon**—it either fires at full strength or not at all.
	- **The Synapse:** The electrical signal triggers the release of chemical **neurotransmitters** from **synaptic vesicles** in the **terminal buttons**. These cross the **synapse** (the small gap between neurons) to bind to **receptors** on the next neuron in a "lock-and-key" relationship (**Figure 2**).
	- **Reuptake:** After the signal is delivered, excess neurotransmitters are pumped back into the original neuron to clear the synapse (**Figure 5**).
	- **Drugs:** **Agonists** mimic neurotransmitters to strengthen their effects, while **antagonists** block them. For example, **SSRIs** treat depression by preventing the reuptake of serotonin, leaving more of it in the synapse.
## 1.10 The Brain and Spinal Cord
The **Central Nervous System (CNS)** includes the brain and spinal cord.
- **Spinal Cord:** Acts as a relay station and handles automatic **reflexes**
- **Cerebral Cortex:** The uneven outer surface characterized by bumps (**gyri**) and grooves (**sulci**). A very deep groove is a **fissure**.
- **Hemispheres:** The brain is divided into left and right hemispheres by the **longitudinal fissure**. They are connected by a thick band of fibers called the **corpus callosum**, allowing them to communicate (**Figure 2**).
- **The Four Lobes ():**
    1. **Frontal:** Involved in reasoning, motor control, and language. It contains **Broca’s area** (language production). Damage here can cause personality changes and loss of impulse control, as seen in the famous case of **Phineas Gage**.
    2. **Parietal:** Processes body senses like touch, temperature, and pain via the **somatosensory cortex**. This area is organized topographically, mirroring the physical layout of the body (**Figure 6**).
    3. **Temporal:** Associated with hearing, memory, and emotion. It contains **Wernicke’s area**, essential for understanding speech.
    4. **Occipital:** Contains the primary visual cortex for interpreting sight.
- **Limbic System:** Involved in emotion and memory.
	- **Thalamus:** The sensory relay where all senses except smell are processed before going to the cortex.
	- **Hippocampus:** Essential for learning and memory.
	- **Amygdala:** Tying emotional meaning to memories.
	- **Hypothalamus:** Regulates homeostatic processes like temperature and appetite
	- **Hindbrain:** Includes the **medulla** (autonomic processes like breathing), **pons** (bridge to the spinal cord), and **cerebellum** ("little brain" for balance and procedural memory) (**Figure 11**).
	- **Imaging:** Modern techniques like **CT** (x-rays), **PET** (radioactive tracers), **MRI/fMRI** (magnetic fields), and **EEG** (electrical activity) allow us to see the brain's structure and function.
## 1.11 How Memory Functions
Memory is an information processing system similar to a computer.
- **Three Functions:** **Encoding** (inputting info), **Storage** (retaining info), and **Retrieval** (getting info back out) (**Figure 1**).
- **Encoding Types:** We process info best through **semantic encoding** (meaning), especially the **self-reference effect**, where we remember info better if it relates to us personally.
- **Atkinson-Shiffrin (A-S) Model:** Info passes through three stages (**Figure 2**):
    1. **Sensory Memory:** Very brief storage of sensory events.
    2. **Short-Term Memory (STM):** Temporary "working memory" that holds about 7 items (plus or minus 2) for about 20 seconds. Moving info from STM to LTM through repetition is *memory consolidation*.
    3. **Long-Term Memory (LTM):** Continuous storage with limitless capacity.
- **LTM Types:**
	- *Explicit (Declarative):* Memories we consciously try to recall, including **Semantic** (facts/knowledge) and **Episodic** (personal events/autobiographical).
	- *Implicit (Non-declarative):* Memories not part of our consciousness, primarily **Procedural memory** (how to do tasks like riding a bike).
	- *Retrieval:* This happens via **recall** (accessing info without cues), **recognition** (identifying previously learned info), or **relearning**.
## 1.12 Parts of the Brain Involved with Memory
Researchers once searched for the **engram** (a single physical trace of memory) but now believe memories are distributed.
- **Equipotentiality Hypothesis:** If one area of the brain is damaged, another part of the same area can take over its memory function.
- **Key Brain Regions (****):**
	- **Amygdala:** Regulates emotions and helps deeper encoding of emotionally arousing events.
	- **Hippocampus:** The "save button" for new **declarative memories**. If damaged, like in the case of **H.M.**, a person can remember the past but cannot form new facts or even.
	- **Cerebellum:** Processes implicit/procedural memories, like motor learning.
    ◦ **Prefrontal Cortex:** Involved in semantic tasks and the retrieval of information.

• **Arousal Theory:** Strong emotions trigger the release of neurotransmitters and hormones that strengthen memory, creating **flashbulb memories**—exceptionally clear recollections of important, emotional events (**Figure 2**).

1.13 Problems with Memory

Memory is not a perfect recording; it is a **construction** and **reconstruction**.

• **Flexibility:** When we retrieve a memory, we can alter and modify it, adding new events or distortions.

• **Suggestibility:** External misinformation can lead to the creation of false memories. This was seen in the DC sniper case, where public focus on a "white van" (based on faulty eyewitness tips) delayed the capture of suspects in a blue sedan.

• **Eyewitness Misidentification:** This is the leading cause of wrongful convictions (**Figure 1**). Police procedures, like giving confirming cues ("Good choice"), can turn a witness's uncertainty into false confidence.

• **Misinformation Effect:** Exposure to incorrect info can cause a person to misremember the original event. In a famous study, using the word "smashed" instead of "hit" caused participants to remember cars going faster and falsely recall seeing broken glass (**Figure 2**).

• **False Memory Syndrome:** The recall of false autobiographical memories, often surrounding traumatic events that may never have occurred.
