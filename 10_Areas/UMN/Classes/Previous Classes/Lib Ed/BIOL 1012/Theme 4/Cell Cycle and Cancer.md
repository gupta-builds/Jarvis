# Cell Cycle and Cancer
## Exam Objectives Covered
- CD1: describe G1, S, G2, and M in the normal cell cycle.
- CD2: compare mitosis and meiosis.
- CD3: connect cell cycle/differentiation to embryonic development and cancer.
- CA1-CA8: define cancer, identify causes, explain proto-oncogenes/tumor suppressors/p53/BRCA, describe progression, define metastasis, malignant tumor, and carcinogen.
## 30-Second Version
The cell cycle normally makes accurate new cells. Cancer happens when cells lose control of division. The big genetic logic is:
- **Proto-oncogenes** are the gas pedal: they promote growth when conditions are right.
- Mutated proto-oncogenes become **oncogenes**: gas stuck on.
- **Tumor suppressor genes** are brakes/checkpoints: they stop growth when something is wrong.
- Mutated tumor suppressors remove the brakes.
Cancer usually needs **multiple mutations**, not one single mutation.
## Cell Cycle Basics
| Phase | What happens                                                                           |
| ----- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| G0    | Resting/non-dividing state; cell is not actively preparing to divide.                  |
| G1    | Major growth and normal cell function; cell gets energy/materials for DNA replication. |
| S     | DNA is replicated once.                                                                |
| G2    | More growth and preparation for mitosis; checks replicated DNA.                        |
| M     | Mitosis and cell division/cytokinesis.                                                 |
Exam trap: DNA is replicated **once** in S phase, not repeatedly throughout the cycle.
## Checkpoints
Checkpoints prevent damaged cells from moving forward. Think of them as quality-control gates.

| Checkpoint | Location in cycle | What is checked | What happens if check fails |
|---|---|---|---|
| G1 checkpoint (restriction point) | End of G1, before S | Cell size adequate? Growth signals present? DNA undamaged? Nutrients available? | Cell enters G0 (stops dividing) or triggers repair. If damage is irreparable, apoptosis (cell death). |
| G2 checkpoint | End of G2, before M | DNA replicated completely and correctly? No replication errors? Cell large enough? | Cell pauses for repair. If repair fails, apoptosis. |
| M checkpoint (spindle checkpoint) | During M, before anaphase | All chromosomes attached to spindle fibers? Chromosomes aligned at metaphase plate? | Cell pauses until attachment is correct. Prevents unequal chromosome distribution. |

### How to use checkpoints on the exam
**Question pattern:** "A cell has DNA damage. At which checkpoint should it be stopped?"
- If damage exists BEFORE DNA is copied → **G1 checkpoint** (prevents copying damaged DNA)
- If damage occurred DURING copying → **G2 checkpoint** (prevents dividing with bad copies)
- If chromosomes aren't lined up properly → **M checkpoint** (prevents unequal division)

**Question pattern:** "Both daughter cells have the same mutation. When did the error likely occur?"
- Before S phase (the error was copied during replication) → G1 checkpoint should have caught it.

**Question pattern:** "Only one daughter cell has a mutation. When did the error likely occur?"
- During S phase (replication error in one copy) → G2 checkpoint should have caught it.

### p53
- **p53** is a tumor suppressor gene (often called "guardian of the genome").
- It acts mainly at the **G1 checkpoint**.
- Normal p53 function:
  1. Detects DNA damage or abnormal signals.
  2. Halts the cell cycle at G1 (prevents entry into S phase).
  3. Activates DNA repair machinery.
  4. If repair succeeds → cell can proceed.
  5. If repair fails → p53 triggers **apoptosis** (programmed cell death).
- Mutated p53:
  - Cannot detect damage or cannot stop the cycle.
  - Damaged DNA gets copied in S phase.
  - Mutations accumulate in daughter cells.
  - Cancer risk increases dramatically.
  - p53 is mutated in **over 50% of human cancers** — it's the most commonly mutated gene in cancer.

Exam wording: p53 does **not** accelerate cell division. It helps stop unsafe division. It is a brake, not a gas pedal.
## Mitosis vs Meiosis
| Feature           | Mitosis                                                | Meiosis                                 |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------- |
| Used for          | Growth, repair, embryonic development, replacing cells | Producing sperm/eggs                    |
| Chromosome number | Maintained                                             | Halved                                  |
| Output            | Genetically similar body cells                         | Haploid gametes                         |
| Theme 4 risk      | If uncontrolled, contributes to cancer                 | Errors can affect gametes/fertilization |
Early embryo cleavage is mitosis. Gamete production is meiosis.
## Differentiation and Development
Embryonic development requires:
- controlled cell division
- cells staying alive when needed
- cells dying when needed
- differentiation into specialized cell types
Differentiation mechanism:
- cells mostly share the same genome
- different genes are active in different cells
- different proteins create different cell structures/functions
Cancer connection: cancer is not just "cells dividing." It is cells dividing when they should stop, ignoring checkpoints, accumulating mutations, and sometimes invading/spreading.
## Cancer Genetics
### Definition
**Cancer**: uncontrolled cell growth caused by loss of normal cell-cycle control.
Cancer cells differ from normal cells because they can:
- divide without the usual signals
- ignore stop signals/checkpoints
- accumulate mutations
- avoid normal cell death
- invade nearby tissues
- sometimes spread to distant sites
### Proto-oncogene vs oncogene
| Term           | Normal or damaged?             | Role                                                           |
| -------------- | ------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Proto-oncogene | Normal                         | Promotes cell growth/division when conditions are appropriate. |
| Oncogene       | Mutated/damaged proto-oncogene | Tells cells to grow/divide too much or at the wrong time.      |

Lecture analogy: proto-oncogene = gas pedal. Oncogene = gas pedal stuck down.
Examples from lecture:
- HER2
- MYC

### Tumor suppressor genes
- Normal role: stop cell growth when conditions are not right.
- If mutated, cells may keep dividing with damage.
Lecture analogy: tumor suppressor gene = brake or parking brake.
Examples:
- p53
- BRCA1
- BRCA2
### BRCA1 and BRCA2
- BRCA1 and BRCA2 are tumor suppressor genes.
- Mutations increase risk of certain cancers, especially breast and ovarian cancers.
- Risk is not certainty.
- A person with a BRCA mutation often needs additional mutations in the other copy of the gene and/or in other growth-control genes for cancer to develop.
Exam traps:
- A mutated BRCA gene does not mean the cell will always become cancerous.
- Most breast cancers do not have one clear BRCA-only cause.
- People of any sex can inherit and pass on BRCA mutations.
- Preventive surgery can reduce risk but cannot make cancer impossible everywhere in the body.

## Causes of Cancer
Cancer mutations can be:
- **inherited** through germ-line DNA
- **spontaneous** during DNA replication
- **environmentally caused** by carcinogens

**Carcinogen**: something that can cause mutations and increase cancer risk.

Lecture examples:
- tobacco smoke
- diesel exhaust
- pesticides
- UV light
- alcohol
- some viruses
- asbestos
- radon
- DNA replication errors over time

## Cancer Progression
| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Tumor | Mass of rapidly dividing cells. Can be benign or malignant. |
| Benign tumor | Abnormal growth that stays contained; does NOT invade surrounding tissue or spread. Often encapsulated. |
| Malignant tumor | Cancer cells invade surrounding tissue and can enter blood/lymph. This IS cancer. |
| In situ | Growth remains where it first formed; has not broken through basement membrane. |
| Angiogenesis | Tumor stimulates growth of new blood vessels to supply itself with nutrients/oxygen. Required for tumor to grow beyond ~1-2mm. |
| Metastasis | Cancer cells spread to distant organs or tissues via blood or lymph. |
| Metastases | Distant cancer sites formed by spread. |
| Stage | Describes extent/spread; higher stage usually means poorer prognosis. |

Typical progression:
1. Cell gets mutation(s) in growth-control genes (proto-oncogene → oncogene, or tumor suppressor loss).
2. Cell divides more than it should; ignores checkpoints.
3. Additional mutations accumulate (typically **5-7 mutations** in different growth-control genes needed for full cancer).
4. Tumor forms (initially may be benign).
5. Tumor triggers **angiogenesis** — new blood vessels grow to feed it.
6. More mutations → cancer becomes malignant, invades nearby tissue (breaks through basement membrane).
7. Cancer cells enter blood or lymphatic vessels.
8. Metastases form at distant sites (liver, lungs, brain, bone are common destinations).

Exam trap: 
- Tumor does NOT automatically mean cancer. Benign tumors are not cancer.
- Malignant = invasive = cancer.
- Metastasis specifically means spread to DISTANT tissues/organs, not just local invasion.

## Prevention and Screening Anchors
Lecture 4.6 listed ways to limit cancer risk:
- screen for breast, cervical, colon, and prostate cancer when recommended
- avoid tobacco
- maintain healthy weight
- exercise regularly
- eat a healthy diet and limit processed foods
- limit alcohol
- limit sun exposure
- get vaccines for viruses linked to cancer risk, such as HPV and hepatitis B

## Exam-Style Applications
- "Which statement about cell division is true?" -> oncogenes increase cell division and can cause cancer.
- "What checkpoint does p53 affect?" -> G1 checkpoint.
- "Why does chemotherapy affect hair/skin?" -> those cells divide rapidly too.
- "Why does age increase cancer risk?" -> more cell divisions over time means more chances for mutations.
- "Why can BRCA testing help but not predict perfectly?" -> BRCA is one risk factor; cancer requires multiple changes and environment/lifetime context matter.
