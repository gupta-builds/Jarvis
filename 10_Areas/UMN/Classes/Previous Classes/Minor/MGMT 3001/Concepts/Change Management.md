# Change Management
## ==Definition==
- Organizational change is the set of processes and activities through which an organization adapts, innovates, or transforms to stay aligned with its environment and strategy.
- Change management is the deliberate work of moving change from idea to execution.

- Textbook: [[50_Archive/Previous Classes/Minor/MGMT 3001/Textbook/Chapter - 11|Chapter - 11]].
## Why it matters
- Organizations change to compete, improve performance, or survive.
- The textbook connects change to globalization, regulation, technological innovation, strategy, and environmental fit.
- A firm that refuses to change can lose viability even if it was once strong.
## Triggers, origination, and magnitude
- **Proactive change:** initiated because leaders anticipate an event or opportunity.
- **Reactive change:** initiated in response to a known threat or opportunity.
- **Planned change:** predetermined and rolled out from the top down.
- **Organic change:** emerges lower in the organization as people solve problems or innovate.
- **Incremental change:** small improvements during stable times.
- **Disruptive change:** forces significant change.
- **Transformative change:** radical change, usually because of a major threat or major opportunity.

## Change process equation
- Change is more likely when:
  - dissatisfaction with the status quo,
  - multiplied by a new model for the organization,
  - multiplied by a process for change,
  - is greater than resistance to change plus cost of change.
- If any of the first three pieces is weak, the product is weak.

## Components of change
- **Dissatisfaction:** energy for breaking inertia. It must extend beyond a few scattered people.
- **Model for change:** description of what is being changed and why.
- **Process for change:** implementation plan, communication, progress measurement, and embedding behavior so the organization does not fall back.
- **Resistance:** individual resistance that can amplify in groups.
- **Cost:** social, psychological, emotional, political, or financial.

## Change leaders
- Commitment to a better way.
- Courage to challenge existing power bases and norms.
- Personal initiative to move beyond defined boundaries.
- The best change leaders embrace tensions, hold people accountable, invest in capabilities, and emphasize continuous learning.

## Change tensions from lecture
- Revitalization vs. normalization.
- Globalization vs. simplification.
- Innovation vs. regulation.
- Optimization vs. rationalization.
- Digitization vs. humanization.

## Skills of change managers
- Clear communication and active listening.
- Emotional intelligence.
- Strong organization.
- Attention to detail.
- Problem solving and decision making.
- Delegating without micromanaging.

## Common traps
- Treating change as an event rather than a process.
- Announcing a new model without a real implementation process.
- Assuming resistance is irrational. Resistance can reflect real social, political, emotional, or financial cost.
- Underestimating the power of old habits and old incentives.

## Course examples
- Netflix shows survival through business-model change away from DVDs and into streaming/content.
- British Airways is used as reactive change.
- Domino's is used as organic change because pressure and ideas emerged from lower levels before leadership bought in.
- Alan Mulally, Peter Cuneo, Richard Clark, and Steve Jobs are lecture examples of change leadership.

## How to apply
> [!TIP]
> Use the change equation as a checklist: raise dissatisfaction honestly, make the new model concrete, build an implementation process, and lower real resistance and cost where possible.
> [!WARNING]
> Common trap: calling an announcement a change. If roles, routines, measures, incentives, and behavior do not move, the organization has only heard a message.
> [!SUMMARY]
> Change is a system problem: strategy creates the reason, culture shapes resistance, power moves resources, and leadership keeps the process alive.

## Entrepreneurship use
- Startups change constantly, but that does not remove the need for process.
- A pivot needs dissatisfaction, a new model, and a change process. "We need to pivot" is not enough.
- Founder courage includes challenging the old plan when evidence says it no longer fits.
