# Entrepreneurship Management Playbook
## Purpose
This is the MGMT 3001 source-of-truth file for your entrepreneurship journey. It combines the course into founder-facing operating principles. The goal is not to make entrepreneurship sound inspirational. The goal is to turn the course into decisions you can reuse while building a company.
## ==Definition==
- ==Entrepreneurship management is the practice of turning uncertainty into coordinated action by choosing a strategy, designing the work, leading people, building culture, making decisions, negotiating constraints, and adapting responsibly.==
- *This file is not a separate theory from the course.* It is a founder-facing synthesis of the textbook topics and lectures already linked below.

## 1. Start with fit, not imitation
- The contingency view is the founder's first rule: there is no one best way to manage.
- Do not copy Google culture, Amazon structure, or Apple strategy because they sound successful. Ask what your task, people, environment, and strategy require.
- Early company design should fit the work: uncertainty requires learning cycles; execution work requires clarity; creative work requires psychological safety; compliance-heavy work requires discipline.
## 2. Strategy is what you refuse
- Use the who/what/how questions:
  - Who do we serve first?
  - What problem do we solve?
  - How do we solve it differently or better?
- Strategy is not a list of aspirations. It is a pattern of tradeoffs.
- If you cannot name what you will not build, not sell, and not chase, your strategy is still foggy.
## 3. Choose an advantage you can actually build
- Cost leadership needs an operating system that lowers cost.
- Differentiation needs something customers value enough to choose and pay for.
- Focus needs a narrow segment where the company can win before expanding.
- Sustainable advantage requires resources or capabilities that are valuable, rare, difficult to imitate, and difficult to substitute.
## 4. Read the market before falling in love with the product
- Use five forces before overcommitting:
  - Are entrants easy?
  - Can suppliers squeeze you?
  - Can buyers demand everything?
  - Are substitutes already solving the job?
  - Is rivalry price-based and brutal?
- A large market is not automatically attractive.
- Re-run the analysis because the lecture treats five forces as a snapshot, not a permanent truth.
## 5. Build only what should be owned
- Corporate strategy appears earlier than founders expect. Even a small company chooses what to own, outsource, partner for, or integrate.
- Vertical integration is useful when control over inputs, quality, data, delivery, or customer experience matters enough to justify complexity.
- Partnership is useful when you need speed, distribution, credibility, or complementary capability without the burden of ownership.
## 6. Treat the first team as the first product
- A startup team needs more than talent.
- Use the IPO framework:
  - Inputs: purpose, structure, people, tools.
  - Processes/states: norms, trust, psychological safety, coordination, feedback.
  - Outputs: results, satisfaction, viability, learning.
- If the team cannot surface bad news, the market will surface it later at a higher cost.
## 7. Culture forms before you name it
- Culture is built through repeated behavior, not posters.
- The first hiring decision, first customer promise, first crisis, first firing, first reward, and first missed deadline all teach the organization what is real.
- Artifacts matter, but underlying assumptions matter more.
- If the company says "quality" and rewards speed at any cost, speed becomes the culture.
## 8. Decide under bounded rationality
- You will not have perfect information, perfect criteria, or unlimited time.
- Use the rational model for structure:
  - define the problem,
  - identify criteria,
  - weight criteria,
  - generate alternatives,
  - evaluate alternatives,
  - choose.
- Use experiments to improve information.
- Watch for availability bias, anchoring, confirmation bias, status quo bias, framing, and escalation of commitment.
## 9. Change is a process, not a vibe
- A pivot needs dissatisfaction, a new model, and a process.
- If people are not dissatisfied, they will not move.
- If the new model is vague, they will not know where to move.
- If the process is weak, they will fall back into old behavior.
- Resistance can be rational because change has social, emotional, political, and financial costs.
## 10. Use power without poisoning trust
- Founders hold power because they control vision, equity, roles, information, and access.
- Authority is not the same as influence. Early influence usually depends on expertise, referent power, credibility, and consistency.
- Coercive power can force action fast, but it spends trust.
- Rational persuasion works when facts are the issue. Inspirational appeal works when values are the issue. Consultation works when commitment is the issue.
## 11. Negotiate from interests, not ego
- Positions are what people ask for. Interests are why.
- Founder negotiations include cofounder equity, investor terms, vendor agreements, salaries, customer pricing, and role boundaries.
- Know your BATNA before the meeting.
- Know your reservation value before the meeting.
- Watch anchoring, framing, overconfidence, and escalation.
## 12. Conflict is data if you keep it about the work
- Task conflict can improve decisions.
- Relationship conflict damages effort and attention.
- Build norms that let people challenge ideas without making disagreement personal.
- Psychological safety matters because silence hides risk.
## 13. Ethics is operating infrastructure
- Ethics is not only law.
- CSR is not only image.
- Early shortcuts compound into culture.
- Use the four ethical frameworks as lenses:
  - utilitarianism asks about consequences,
  - Kantianism asks about duty and universal rules,
  - virtue asks about character,
  - justice asks about fairness and process.
- Strong founder question: would this decision still make sense if customers, employees, investors, and future hires could see the reasoning?
## 14. Global thinking starts before global expansion
- Globalization affects suppliers, competitors, customers, technology, standards, labor, and regulation.
- Multinational operations bring opportunity and complexity.
- Hofstede-style social dimensions help notice differences, but do not use them as stereotypes.
- International management requires global mindset, emotional intelligence, long-range perspective, motivation skill, negotiation skill, and cultural humility.
## How to apply this playbook
> [!TIP]
> Use this file as a decision stack. Start with strategy, then design the work, then build the team and culture, then choose the decision/conflict/power tools that fit the problem. Do not jump to hiring, branding, or fundraising before the strategic choice is clear.
> [!WARNING]
> Common founder trap: copying another company's culture or operating model without checking task, environment, people, and strategy. The course's contingency logic says fit beats imitation.
> [!SUMMARY]
> MGMT 3001 turns entrepreneurship into a management problem: choose where to compete, build the capability to compete there, lead people through uncertainty, and keep ethics inside the operating system.

## Course-grounded examples
- *Netflix-style change logic:* when the old model no longer fits the environment, the founder problem is not just product change; it is change management.
- *Zappos-style culture logic:* hiring, onboarding, and rewards teach people what the organization truly values.
- *BATNA logic in founder negotiations:* cofounder equity, investor terms, salary offers, and vendor contracts all require a walk-away point before the conversation starts.

## Founder checklist
- What is the actual strategy, including what we will not do?
- What market force can kill us?
- What capability are we building that gets stronger over time?
- What should we own, and what should we partner for?
- What behavior are we rewarding?
- What is the decision process for high-stakes choices?
- Where are we escalating commitment because we want to be right?
- Where is power being used without trust?
- What conflict are we avoiding?
- What ethical line would be costly to cross later?
## Common founder traps
- Mistaking ambition for strategy.
- Hiring smart people without designing team norms.
- Copying another company's culture.
- Treating investor pressure as proof of market truth.
- Confusing speed with learning.
- Avoiding conflict until it becomes personal.
- Using authority when influence would work better.
- Calling a pivot when there is no change process.
- Treating CSR as marketing instead of operating logic.
