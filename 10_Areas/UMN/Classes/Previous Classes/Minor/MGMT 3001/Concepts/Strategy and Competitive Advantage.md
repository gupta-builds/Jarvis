# Strategy and Competitive Advantage
## ==Definition==
- **Business-level strategy** asks how a firm competes in a particular market.
- The course frames strategy with three questions: who do we serve, what do we provide, and how do we provide it.
- **Sustainable competitive advantage** means a firm performs better than competitors over time because its resources, capabilities, activities, or position are difficult to copy or replace.

## Why it matters
- Strategy is not just a goal. It is a set of choices and tradeoffs.
- Porter shows up in the lecture because strategy requires choosing what not to do.
- A firm with no tradeoffs can drift into "stuck in the middle": not low cost enough, not differentiated enough, and not focused enough.

## Strategic management process
1. Current state evaluation.
2. External analysis.
3. Internal analysis.
4. Strategy formulation.
5. Strategy implementation.
6. Strategic control.

## Current state evaluation
- Clarify products and services.
- Clarify customers.
- Clarify process or approach.
- Examine mission, goals, and plans.
- Examine stakeholder expectations.
- Verify the firm's reason for existence.

## External analysis: Porter five forces
- **Threat of new entrants:** high when barriers are low. Barriers include economies of scale, capital needs, switching costs, distribution access, and regulation.
- **Supplier power:** high when suppliers are concentrated, differentiated, hard to switch from, or able to integrate forward.
- **Buyer power:** high when buyers are concentrated, purchase in volume, face low switching costs, or see the product as undifferentiated.
- **Threat of substitutes:** high when customers can solve the same need in a different way.
- **Rivalry:** intense when competitors fight on price, growth is slow, fixed costs are high, or products are similar.
- Lecture warning: five forces is a snapshot. Revisit it before, during, and after entering a market.

## Internal analysis
- **Resources:** assets the firm controls.
- **Capabilities:** what the firm can do with those resources.
- **Core competence:** a capability central to advantage.
- **Value chain:** the connected activities that create and deliver value.
- **Value system:** the broader network of suppliers, distributors, partners, and customers around the firm.

## SWOT as fit
- **Strengths and weaknesses** are internal.
- **Opportunities and threats** are external.
- SWOT is useful only when it leads to action. The point is fit: use strengths to exploit opportunities, fix weaknesses that expose threats, and adjust as the environment changes.

## Generic strategies
- **Cost leadership:** win by producing at lower cost and often competing on price.
- **Differentiation:** win because customers value something distinct enough to pay for it.
- **Focus strategy:** serve a narrower segment especially well. Focus can be cost-based or differentiation-based.
- The digital economy can let some firms blend cost and differentiation in a narrow setting, but the trap remains: if the activity system is incoherent, the firm is stuck in the middle.

## Common traps
- Calling every goal a strategy.
- Listing a SWOT without saying what to do.
- Treating five forces as timeless.
- Assuming differentiation means "better at everything." It means meaningfully different to a target customer.
- Assuming low cost means cheap quality. Cost leadership is an operating system, not just a lower price.

## Course examples
- Starbucks is used to apply five forces: substitutes include tea, soda, and other coffee shops; entry at Starbucks scale is harder than opening one coffee shop; buyer preferences shape the force.
- Burger King vs. McDonald's and Coke vs. Pepsi show rivalry when competition centers on price and close substitutes.
- Spirit Airlines is a cost leadership example because activities reinforce low cost.
- Apple is a differentiation example because product design, ecosystem, brand, and user experience support premium pricing.

## How to apply
> [!TIP]
> Start with the who/what/how choice, then test it against five forces and the firm's resources. A good answer names the target customer, the value proposition, the pressure from the industry, and the activity system that makes the position believable.
> [!WARNING]
> Common trap: writing a goal as if it were a strategy. "Grow fast" or "be innovative" is not a strategy unless it explains where the firm competes and what tradeoffs it accepts.
> [!SUMMARY]
> Strategy is fit between external pressure and internal capability. Five forces reads the market; RBV and value chain explain what the firm can actually defend.

## Entrepreneurship use
- Startups often fail strategy by trying to serve everyone. Use who/what/how to narrow the first wedge.
- Use five forces before committing to a market. A big market with brutal buyer power or no differentiation path can still be a bad first target.
- A founder's real strategy is visible in tradeoffs: what the company refuses to build, sell, or chase.
