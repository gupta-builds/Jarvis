# Teams and Team Effectiveness

## ==Definition==
- A team is a group of people with interdependent work, shared goals, and mutual accountability.
- Team effectiveness is not just output. The course treats effectiveness as results, member satisfaction, team viability, and learning/adaptation.

## Source map
- Lecture: [[50_Archive/Previous Classes/Minor/MGMT 3001/Week - 5|Week - 5]].
- Textbook: [[Chapter - 17|Chapter - 17]].

## Why it matters
- Organizations use teams when work requires multiple skills, perspectives, and coordinated action.
- Teams can outperform individuals, but only when the team design and process support the work.
- A bad team can be worse than a strong individual because coordination costs, conflict, loafing, and groupthink can destroy effort.

## IPO framework
- **Inputs:** what the team starts with.
- **Processes and states:** how the team works and what the team becomes like internally.
- **Outputs:** what the team produces and whether it can keep performing.

## Inputs
- **Goal or purpose:** what the team exists to accomplish.
- **Structure:** roles, authority, task design, and decision rules.
- **People:** skills, diversity, personality, motivation, and availability.
- **Tools:** communication channels, shared documents, technology, resources, and physical or virtual work setup.

## Team states
- **Norms:** shared expectations about behavior.
- **Identity:** whether members see themselves as part of the same group.
- **Trust:** willingness to be vulnerable because others are expected to act responsibly.
- **Psychological safety:** belief that people can speak up, ask questions, and admit mistakes without punishment.
- **Team efficacy:** belief that the team can succeed.
- **Empowerment:** belief that the team has control and meaningful work.
- **Cohesion:** attachment to the team.

## Team processes
- **Coordination:** aligning timing, roles, handoffs, and dependencies.
- **Peer monitoring and feedback:** noticing gaps and helping each other improve.
- **Conflict management:** keeping task conflict constructive and preventing relationship conflict.
- **Backup behavior:** stepping in when teammates need help.
- **Communication:** sharing information early enough for others to act.

## Types of teams
- **Advisory teams:** recommend but do not usually implement.
- **Task force or project teams:** temporary teams solving a specific problem.
- **Skunkworks or special initiative teams:** separated enough to pursue innovation.
- **Virtual teams:** rely heavily on digital coordination.
- **Departmental teams:** ongoing teams inside a function.
- **Production/service/leadership teams:** execute recurring work.
- **Self-directed teams:** own more decisions about how the work gets done.

## Team development
- **Forming:** orientation and uncertainty.
- **Storming:** disagreement over roles, goals, or methods.
- **Norming:** shared expectations settle.
- **Performing:** the team can execute well.
- **Adjourning:** the team closes or transitions.

## Conflict inside teams
- **Task conflict:** disagreement about ideas, methods, or work. It can improve decisions when managed well.
- **Relationship conflict:** personal tension, anger, or personality friction. It usually damages attention and effort.
- Task conflict can spill into relationship conflict if people do not manage tone, trust, and process.

## Common traps
- Calling any group a team.
- Focusing only on output and ignoring viability.
- Treating storming as failure. Some storming is the work of becoming a team.
- Confusing cohesion with quality. Cohesion can become groupthink.
- Assuming virtual teams need less structure. They usually need more explicit norms.

## Course examples
- HealthCare.gov is useful as a coordination failure: many contractors and agencies, unclear requirements, incomplete testing, and weak oversight.
- A shared task board works because it makes progress visible and creates accountability.
- The course connects team conflict to psychological safety: people need enough safety to disagree about the work.

## How to apply
> [!TIP]
> Use IPO as the first diagnostic. If the team has the wrong skills or task design, fix inputs. If the team avoids hard conversations, fix processes and states. If output is weak, check both the result and whether the team can work together again.
> [!WARNING]
> Common trap: assuming a team is always better than an individual. The textbook uses task complexity and interdependence to decide whether a team is worth the coordination cost.
> [!SUMMARY]
> Teams need structure and trust at the same time. Psychological safety supports learning, but norms and accountability keep the work from becoming vague.

## Entrepreneurship use
- Early founders often hire for skill but forget team process. The first team needs norms before pressure exposes the gaps.
- Psychological safety is not softness. It is infrastructure for surfacing bad news before customers do.
- In a startup, viability matters: if the team wins one sprint but burns trust, the company pays later.
