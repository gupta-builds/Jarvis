# Week 6 — Decision Making: Rational Model, Bounded Rationality & Bias

## What you must be able to do
- Explain the six steps of the rational decision-making process.
- State the key assumptions of the rational model and explain why each often fails in practice.
- Explain bounded rationality and satisficing.
- Identify and explain the major heuristics: availability, representativeness, anchoring.
- Identify and explain the major biases: confirmation bias, escalation of commitment, status quo bias, framing.
- Explain how emotions and social situations affect decision quality.
- Distinguish programmed from non-programmed decisions.
- Name the four organizational decision-making models and explain when each applies.

## Key ideas
- Decision making is the heart of organizational life — success depends on making good choices consistently.
- The rational model is a useful benchmark, not a description of how decisions actually get made.
- Heuristics save time but introduce predictable, systematic errors called biases.
- Bias management is more realistic than pretending bias can be eliminated.
- Better processes improve the quality of decisions under real-world constraints.

## Lecture

### 1. The rational decision-making process — step by step

The rational model provides the gold standard for structured decision making. It assumes complete information, clear preferences, and sufficient time.

**Six steps:**

| Step | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **1. Define the problem or opportunity** | Clearly state what organizational goal or issue needs to be addressed |
| **2. Identify criteria and objectives** | Determine what a good outcome looks like (e.g., cut costs, grow revenue, reduce risk) |
| **3. Weight the criteria** | Rank objectives by importance — not all criteria matter equally |
| **4. Generate alternatives** | Brainstorm a full range of possible courses of action |
| **5. Evaluate each alternative** | Rate how well each option achieves each weighted criterion |
| **6. Choose the optimal decision** | Calculate weighted scores and select the highest-scoring option |

**Strengths of the rational model:**
- Forces criteria to be explicit before evaluating options
- Reduces post-hoc rationalization ("I knew that was the right choice all along")
- Creates a defensible, auditable decision trail
- Prevents anchoring on the first solution that comes to mind

---

### 2. Assumptions of the rational model — and why they fail

The model assumes:
- The problem is clearly defined
- All alternatives are known
- Preferences are stable and consistent
- Full information is available
- There is no time pressure
- The decision maker is fully objective

**Reality check:** In practice, managers face unclear problems, incomplete information, conflicting goals, extreme time pressure, and their own cognitive limits. This gap between ideal and real motivates the next concept.

---

### 3. Bounded rationality and satisficing

**Bounded rationality** (Herbert Simon): Managers *intend* to be rational but are constrained by:
- Incomplete and uncertain information
- Limited cognitive processing capacity
- Time pressure and competing priorities
- Conflicting organizational goals

Because of these constraints, managers typically **satisfice** — they choose the first option that meets minimum acceptable criteria, rather than searching indefinitely for the perfect answer.

**Is satisficing bad?** Not always. In a fast-moving environment, an 80% decision made quickly can outperform a 100% decision made too late. The key is knowing when a decision is important enough to invest more time in.

---

### 4. Decision-making conditions

| Condition | Goals | Alternatives | Outcomes |
|-----------|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Certainty** | Clear | Known | Fully predictable |
| **Risk** | Clear | Known | Probabilistic |
| **Uncertainty** | Clear | Incomplete | Unknown |
| **Ambiguity** | Unclear | Unknown | Unknowable |

Most real management decisions occur under **risk or uncertainty**. Ambiguity is the most difficult — it's hard to even frame the problem, let alone solve it.

---

### 5. Intuitive decision making — when and how it works

When speed is critical or analytical time is unavailable, managers rely on **intuition** — rapid judgments based on pattern recognition from experience.

**How it works:** The brain synthesizes years of past experience into subconscious "pattern matches." When a new situation resembles a known pattern, a rapid response emerges.

**Thin-slicing** (Malcolm Gladwell): The ability to read patterns from narrow "slices" of experience and make accurate snap judgments.

**When intuition works:** When the decision maker has deep relevant experience AND the current situation genuinely resembles past situations.

**When intuition fails:** When the pattern being recognized is actually a stereotype rather than a real signal, or when the situation is genuinely novel.

---

### 6. Heuristics and biases — the full exam list

**Heuristics** are mental shortcuts that save cognitive effort. They are efficient but introduce **systematic bias** — predictable, not random, errors.

#### The three primary heuristics:

**Availability Heuristic**
- Judge probability by how easily examples come to mind
- Vivid, recent, or emotionally intense events *feel* more probable than they are
- Example: After seeing a plane crash in the news, people overestimate air travel risk
- Managerial example: A manager recalls a past project failure vividly and consistently underestimates new initiative potential

**Representativeness Heuristic**
- Judge by how closely a case resembles an existing stereotype or category
- Often ignores actual statistical base rates
- Example: Hiring a well-dressed, articulate candidate over a more qualified one because they "look the part"
- Leads to: stereotyping, underweighting of statistical information, false pattern-matching

**Anchoring and Adjustment Heuristic**
- Start from an initial reference point (anchor) and adjust — but don't adjust far enough
- The anchor biases the final estimate even when it's arbitrary
- Example: First salary offer in a negotiation becomes the anchor; final agreement clusters near it
- Managerial example: Starting a budget review with "last year's number" as anchor leads to only incremental changes when radical rethinking was needed

---

#### Additional critical biases:

| Bias | Definition | Managerial example |
|------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Confirmation bias** | Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs; discounting contradictions | Manager believes the launch will succeed and dismisses all warning data |
| **Escalation of commitment** | Continuing to invest in a failing course because of prior investment (sunk cost fallacy) | Pouring more money into a failing product line "because we've already spent so much" |
| **Status quo bias** | Favoring the current state and resisting change even when evidence supports it | Keeping an underperforming supplier because changing is uncomfortable |
| **Framing** | Same facts feel different depending on how they're presented | "90% success rate" vs. "10% failure rate" are identical but feel very different |

**Framing in depth:** People are **risk-averse when choices are framed as gains** (prefer certain smaller gain over risky larger gain) and **risk-seeking when framed as losses** (prefer risky option to avoid certain loss). This is why how a manager frames a problem shapes what decision their team makes — even with the same facts.

### 7. Emotions and social situations

**Emotions directly affect judgment:**
- Good mood → more optimistic, more risk-tolerant
- Fear/anxiety → more risk-averse, narrow focus
- Anger → overconfidence, less information-seeking
- Sadness → more analytical, more likely to make changes

**Social conformity (Asch experiments):**
- Subjects agreed with an obviously wrong majority answer to avoid social rejection
- Implication: Group settings can produce bad decisions that feel normal because the group collectively validated them
- This is the social basis of groupthink

**Appropriateness framework:**
Rather than calculating utility, people ask: *"What does a person like me do in this situation?"* Decisions become identity-based, not purely analytical.

---

### 8. Four organizational decision-making models

| Model | Core assumption | Best fit |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| **Classical** | Managers can rationally identify the optimal decision | Decisions with clear goals, good information, limited alternatives |
| **Administrative** | Managers satisfice using bounded rationality and intuition | Most real-world management decisions |
| **Political** | Decisions emerge from bargaining among managers with different goals | High-stakes decisions with multiple competing stakeholders |
| **Garbage Can** | Problems, solutions, and people flow randomly; solutions sometimes precede problems | Highly ambiguous, loosely organized settings |

**Political model exam point:** In most large organizations, "the right answer" is insufficient — you also need to build coalitions, manage stakeholders, and negotiate. Strategic decisions are rarely made by one rational actor alone.

---

### 9. Programmed vs. non-programmed decisions

| Type | Definition | Response |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Programmed** | Recurring decisions; rules or SOPs exist | Apply existing procedure |
| **Non-programmed** | Novel, unstructured; requires unique judgment | Deliberate analysis needed |

**Managerial error:** Treating a non-programmed decision as if it were programmed — applying an old rule to a genuinely new situation. This is how organizations get blindsided.

---

### 10. Improving decision quality

Practical strategies:
- **Slow down on high-stakes choices** — most bias operates in fast, automatic thinking
- **Make criteria explicit before** evaluating options — prevents post-hoc rationalization
- **Actively seek disconfirming evidence** — assign someone to steelman the opposing view
- **Take the "outsider's view"** — how would someone with no stake evaluate this?
- **Pre-mortem analysis** — before committing, imagine the decision has failed and explain why
- **Use statistical models** where base rates or probabilities exist
- **Build in accountability** — decisions that must be explained to others are more carefully made
