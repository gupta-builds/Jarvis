## Discussion - 3
1. Review of Present Status and Change in Approach: Adobe
One firm showing a clear change in direction is Adobe. Years went by with Adobe offering its design software through one time purchases - like Creative Suite. Slowly, things began moving toward ongoing payments tied to online services via Adobe Creative Cloud. 
What changed (the “shift”)? Older model meant buying software once. That came with fewer updates over time. Slower rollout of new features happened back then. Upgrades showed up less often compared to now. Today’s setup runs on subscriptions, tied to online platforms that keep improving through regular upgrades while linking features together smoothly.
Evolved products: A single plan gives access to design software like Photoshop, Illustrator, then Premiere Pro through Creative Cloud. Updates appear automatically. Files live in the cloud by default. Expansion of AI-supported creation capabilities inside core products.
Evolved processes: A steady stream of improvements arrives regularly, not waiting on major upgrades rolled out after long waits. Subscriptions shape Adobe's business more than anything else. Growth now means holding onto subscribers.
Evolved customers: Finding steady footing among creatives like designers, yet holding ground with photographers too - video editors stick close by. Now covers more options like: young learners, teams/organizations using shared assets and collaboration, and business users doing marketing tasks.
2. SWOT Analysis
Strengths: Powerful names show up alongside trusted software. A web of connected items makes moving away harder. Subscription model supports continuous product improvement.
Weaknesses: Pay plans might upset those watching their budget. Complexity, the product suite can feel overwhelming for beginners.
Opportunities: Growth in creator economy + digital content demand. Faster tools powered by artificial intelligence.
Threats: Newer tools like Canva and Figma. Some tasks seek less demand for classic editing when using new AI driven tools. Piracy/gray market alternatives.

Five Forces Analysis (Creative Software/Design Tools)
Threat of New Competitors (Medium): delivery happens online, but getting pros to switch isn’t fast because tools fit tightly together.
Supplier Power (Low - Medium): cloud setup is pretty standard; talent matters but doesn’t drive the whole market.
Buyer Power (Moderate - High): casual users have lots of options; heavy users stick because workflows and file expectations lock them in.
Threat of Substitutes (High): layout apps, template tools, and AI-based tools can replace parts of traditional editing.
Rivalry (High): many niche apps compete on price, UX, and collaboration.

How does Adobe derive competitive advantage?
Adobe's edge comes from software integration, ecosystem lock in, and recurring revenue models. Tools that fit together make life smoother for people who create stuff, and switching gets messy once users are in. Year after year, fresh features arrive without pause because subscriptions keep changes flowing.
