# Chapter 16 - Conflict and Negotiation
## Textbook anchor - conflict and negotiation
- ==Conflict is a process where one party perceives its interests are being opposed or negatively affected by another party.==
- *Mechanism:* conflict becomes useful when it stays cognitive/task-focused and becomes costly when it turns affective/relationship-focused.
- Conflict styles trade off assertiveness and cooperativeness:
  - competing protects your outcome,
  - accommodating protects the relationship,
  - avoiding postpones or exits,
  - compromising splits difference,
  - collaborating searches for a better joint solution.
- Negotiation works best when parties separate positions from interests, know their BATNA, define reservation values, and identify whether a bargaining zone exists.
> [!WARNING]
> The biggest negotiation trap is arguing over positions before discovering interests. The second is negotiating without knowing your BATNA and walk-away point.


## Conflict and negotiation
- Conflict is an emotional or cognitive response when one person's or group's interests, perspectives, or behaviors differ from another's.
- Negotiation is the process by which two parties attempt to reach agreement by offering and reviewing positions or courses of action.

## Why conflict matters
- Conflict is inevitable in organizations because people depend on each other while holding different goals, incentives, and views.
- Avoiding all conflict is not good management. Suppressed conflict can hide serious risk.
- Managed conflict can improve decision quality; unmanaged conflict can damage relationships, effort, and attention.

## Types of conflict
- **Task or cognitive conflict:** issue-focused disagreement about ideas, plans, and work.
- **Relationship or affective conflict:** personal tension, anger, friction, or personality-based conflict.
- The key is to keep conflict focused on the issue before it becomes personal.

## Conflict management styles
- Competing/forcing.
- Collaborating/problem solving.
- Compromising.
- Avoiding.
- Accommodating.
- Style choice depends on issue importance, relationship importance, relative power, and time constraints.

## Negotiation basics
- **Issue:** subject of the discussion.
- **Position:** what a person wants.
- **Interest:** the underlying need, goal, value, or belief behind the position.
- **BATNA:** best alternative to a negotiated agreement.
- **Reservation value:** walk-away point.
- **Bargaining zone:** range where agreement is possible.
