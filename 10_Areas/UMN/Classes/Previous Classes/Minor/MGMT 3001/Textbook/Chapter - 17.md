# Chapter 17 - Leading Teams
## Textbook anchor - team effectiveness
- ==A team is a small number of people with complementary skills, shared purpose, mutual accountability, and interdependent work.==
- *Mechanism:* teams outperform individuals only when the task requires interdependence, diverse information, coordinated effort, and shared commitment. Otherwise, teams can add coordination cost without adding value.
- Use the IPO logic:
  - *Inputs:* task design, team composition, size, interdependence, resources, leadership, and context.
  - *Processes and states:* norms, trust, psychological safety, coordination, participation, conflict management, and shared mental models.
  - *Outputs:* results, member satisfaction, learning, and future ability to work together.
- Psychological safety does not mean comfort or agreement. It means people can raise concerns, admit mistakes, and challenge ideas without fear of punishment.
> [!TIP]
> In a team scenario, ask whether the problem is an input problem, a process/state problem, or an output problem before recommending a fix.

## Textbook
Chapter 17 covers team dynamics from basic definitions to the complex dynamics of team effectiveness and boundary management.
### 1. Teams vs. work groups

The chapter begins by distinguishing between two types of groups. While people often use the words interchangeably, they function differently in a business setting.

- **Work Group:** Characterized by a **clearly focused leader**, individual accountability, and a purpose that is the same as the organization’s overall mission. They typically handle straightforward, independent tasks where work is pre-determined and results in individual work products.
- **Team:** A group of **two or more people** with **complementary skills** committed to a specific objective. Teams feature shared leadership roles, individual _and_ mutual accountability, and a purpose specific to the team. They are essential for complex, interdependent tasks that result in collective work products.

### 2. When are teams appropriate?

Teams are not always the right choice; they are "appropriate" based on two factors:

- **Task Complexity:** This refers to the amount of information to be processed, uncertainty of outcomes, and the presence of subtasks requiring diverse skills. When a task is too large or complex for one person, a team is needed.
- **Task Interdependence:** The degree to which members must rely on one another to produce the group's work. For example, product development requires the integrated expertise of marketing, manufacturing, and R&D.

### 3. Team characteristics and design

A well-designed team must have a clear formal organization to support collaboration.

- **Team Composition and Size:** Effective teams need members with the right mix of **technical skills** (to solve the problem) and **interpersonal skills** (to work well together). While size varies, smaller teams often find coordination easier, but complex tasks might require more members.
- **Manager-Led vs. Self-Directed Teams:**
    - **Manager-Led:** The manager acts as the leader, defining goals and methods. These can be **Vertical** (manager and subordinates in a formal chain of command) or **Horizontal/Cross-Functional** (members from the same hierarchical level but different departments).
    - **Self-Directed:** These teams determine their own objectives and methods. They empower workers but can struggle without clear leadership if an impasse is reached.
- **Collocated vs. Geographically Distributed (Virtual) Teams:**
    - **Collocated:** Members work in close proximity and rely on **face-to-face communication**, which helps build trust and commitment through social cues like body language.
    - **Geographically Distributed:** Members are dispersed and rely on **electronic tools** (e-mail, video calls). They face greater coordination and interaction problems.
- **Leveraging Diversity:** Diversity includes differences in gender, race, function, and even decision-making styles.
    - **Advantage:** Diverse teams produce more creative and innovative solutions over the long term because they draw on broader networks and complementary information.
    - **Challenge:** They often struggle with group identity and conflict in the short term due to a lack of shared experiences.

### 4. Team development and norms

Teams generally evolve through a five-stage process known as **Tuckman's Model**:

1. **Forming:** Defining the task, setting ground rules, and learning about each other's skills.
2. **Storming:** Experiencing conflict over interpersonal issues and different perspectives.
3. **Norming:** Establishing new standards for collaborative behavior and creating group cohesion.
4. **Performing:** Bridging differences to complete the task and adopting roles that enhance the group.
5. **Adjourning:** Disbanding after the task is complete and taking stock of the experience.

**Midpoint Transition:** Many teams undergo a shift at the halfway point toward their deadline, where they abandon old perspectives and push to finish on time.

**Team Norms:** These are expected behaviors that should be established early to prevent conflict. They include:

- **Meeting Norms:** When and how often to meet.
- **Working Norms:** Deadlines, work review, and accountability.
- **Communication Norms:** How to share information and discuss feelings.
- **Leadership Norms:** The leadership structure to be used.
- **Consideration Norms:** Treating others with mutual respect.

### 5. The team process

The "team process" is how members interact to get work done.

- **Purposeful and Rigorous Decision Making:** Teams should move from **divergent** (many ideas) to **convergent** (one solution) perspectives. This involves a 5-step process: (1) Identify problem, (2) Prioritize criteria, (3) Generate solutions, (4) Review solutions, (5) Complete task.
- **Effective Participation and Meaningful Influence:**
    - **Participation:** The extent to which individuals engage in generating solutions. Conversation should not be dominated by one person.
    - **Influence:** The impact a person's perspective has on the final solution.
    - **Common Information Effect:** A pitfall where teams only discuss what everyone already knows, failing to leverage unique individual insights.
- **Blocking Behaviors:** Behaviors that inhibit the team, such as **social loafing** (disengaging), dominating, or fault-finding.
- **Constructive Conflict:** Teams must encourage **task-related (cognitive) conflict** to debate ideas while minimizing **affective (relationship) conflict**.
- **Psychological Safety:** An environment where people feel safe to express ideas and vulnerabilities without fear of penalty.

### 6. Team effectiveness and leadership

A team is effective only if it meets three specific conditions:

1. **Meaningful Results:** Tangible outcomes that meet the standards of those receiving the work.
2. **Member Satisfaction:** Individuals feel their contributions are valued and gain personal growth.
3. **Future Capacity:** Enhancing the ability of members to work together again by building trust and purpose.

**The Role of a Team Leader:** Leaders must gain respect and commitment by being clear, consistent, and creating positive energy. Key behaviors include finding common ground, encouraging and coaching, and sharing information.

**Boundary Management:** A **Boundary Manager** determines how the team works with outsiders (clients, senior management). Teams can be:

- **Informing Teams:** Minimal interaction with outsiders; they rely on internal group knowledge.
- **Parading Teams:** Passive interaction; they promote their activities to external groups.
- **Probing Teams:** Active engagement; they constantly revise information based on outsider needs.

**Leading Virtual Teams:** Requires extra effort, including sharing background information to prevent misinterpretations, finding allies in the organization, and probing for hidden conflicts in digital communication.
