# Chapter 2 - Global Business Environment
## Textbook anchor - global management
- ==Globalization is the ongoing social, economic, political, and technological integration process that changes where firms source, sell, hire, compete, and face regulation.==
- *Mechanism:* global management expands the number of stakeholders and constraints. A manager has to read economic development, culture, institutions, trade rules, currency, labor conditions, environmental expectations, and political risk at the same time.
- The chapter's economy classifications matter because they change the managerial problem:
  - Developed economies usually offer mature infrastructure and purchasing power but intense competition.
  - Emerging markets offer growth and rising purchasing power, but can add institutional and infrastructure uncertainty.
  - Developing economies may offer long-run opportunity while requiring more patience and risk analysis.
  - Transition economies are moving from government-controlled systems toward market systems, so rules and institutions can still be changing.
- International work also changes ethics. Labor practices, bribery, environmental harm, and human rights cannot be dismissed as "local custom" when they create stakeholder harm.
> [!SUMMARY]
> Chapter 2 connects the external environment to managerial behavior: global opportunity is real, but so are coordination costs, cultural misunderstandings, institutional differences, and ethical exposure.


## The global business environment
- Businesses do not operate in isolation. They sit inside a larger global environment shaped by economic, political, technological, social, and legal forces.
- The chapter connects back to VUCA: the business environment is volatile, uncertain, complex, and ambiguous.
- Managers do more than react. They may lobby, identify new customer segments, invest in research and development, and adapt strategy as the environment changes.

## Globalization
- Globalization is the ongoing integration of operations, markets, technology, politics, and society across countries.
- A multinational company operates beyond domestic borders.
- Globalization increases opportunity and pressure at the same time: new customers, new competitors, new suppliers, new standards, and new ethical risks.

## Types of international activity
- Produce domestically and sell domestically and internationally.
- Produce in another country and sell domestically.
- Produce in another country and sell both domestically and internationally.

## Global economy classifications
- **Developed economies:** mature economies with high GDP and high levels of trade and investment.
- **Emerging markets:** rapidly growing markets between developed and developing status.
- **Developing economies:** lower development levels but growth potential.
- **Transition economies:** moving from government-controlled systems toward market-based systems.

## Why emerging markets matter
- Emerging markets contain large populations and rising purchasing power.
- They create growth opportunities for multinational companies.
- They also require attention to infrastructure, law, culture, and ethical responsibility.

## Social dimensions to know
- **Power distance:** shared decision making vs. respect for hierarchy.
- **Individualism vs. collectivism:** individual achievement vs. group goals and relationships.
- **Cooperative vs. competitive orientation:** collaboration vs. assertiveness and achievement.
- **Uncertainty avoidance:** comfort with ambiguity vs. desire for rules and planning.
- **Long-term vs. short-term orientation:** long horizon and persistence vs. shorter-term results.

## Global management challenges
- Politics and laws.
- Environmental factors.
- Macroeconomic conditions such as GDP, unemployment, inflation, inequality, and exchange rates.
- Human rights and labor conditions.
- Cultural differences and language barriers.
- Public scrutiny over bribery, pollution, sweatshop conditions, and climate impact.

## Multinational manager capabilities
- Global mindset.
- Emotional intelligence.
- Long-range perspective.
- Ability to motivate across cultures.
- Negotiation skill.
- Willingness to seek overseas experience.
- Cultural understanding without overconfidence.
