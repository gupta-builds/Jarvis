# Entire Week
## What you must be able to do
- Define ethics, morality, and CSR.
- Explain why ethics is more than legal compliance.
- Apply the four ethical frameworks: utilitarianism, Kantianism, virtue/character, and justice.
- Identify ethical issues in human rights, sustainability, anti-corruption, and labor law.
- Explain triple bottom line: profit, people, planet.
- Explain how CSR can support competitive advantage when aligned with strategy.

## Key ideas (short)
- Ethics is about action, not only statements.
- Legal and ethical are related but not identical.
- Ethical frameworks help managers reason through hard choices, but each has limits.
- CSR only matters when it is connected to real behavior and strategy.

## Lecture
### 1. Ethics begins with action
The lecture opens with quotes that all point to the same idea: ethics is expressed through action. Saying the right thing is not enough if the organization rewards or tolerates the wrong thing.

The Jane Addams line matters because it frames ethics as behavior. For management, this connects directly to culture: what leaders do, reward, and tolerate becomes the real standard.

### 2. Ethical considerations in business
The slides name four business ethics areas:
- Human rights.
- Environmental and sustainability concerns.
- Anti-corruption.
- Labor laws.

These are not side issues. They affect reputation, legal risk, morale, stakeholder trust, and long-term performance.

### 3. Consequences of unethical behavior
The lecture lists several consequences:
- Organizations lose credibility.
- Morale and productivity decline.
- Financial losses from fines or buyers.
- Executives who break the law can lead employees to follow suit.

That final point is important. Ethical failure spreads culturally when employees learn that leaders do not follow the stated rules.

### 4. Ethics and law are not the same
The lecture asks whether ethics are just the law or individualized preferences. The answer is no. Law matters, but frameworks are needed because managers face choices where legal permission does not automatically settle moral responsibility.

### 5. Utilitarianism
- Uses the greatest happiness principle.
- Behaviors are moral if they produce the greatest good.
- Uses cost-benefit analysis.

Problems:
- Ends can be used to justify harmful means.
- Future outcomes are hard to predict.
- Happiness is subjective.
- The majority can dominate the minority.

### 6. Kantianism
- Motives and universal rules matter.
- Decisions are about duty and principle, not just outcomes.

Problems:
- Hard to apply to many situations.
- Humans rarely act from duty alone.
- Leaves little space for emotion.
- Relies heavily on rational decision making.

### 7. Virtue and character
Virtue ethics asks what kind of person the decision maker is becoming and modeling. Character includes:
- Recognizing moral elements of a situation.
- Making moral judgments.
- Acting consistently with judgment.
- Teaching others to show character.

Problems:
- People disagree on what counts as virtue.
- It lacks step-by-step guidance.
- It focuses heavily on the decision maker's character.

### 8. Justice approach
- Society judges moral right and wrong through equity, fairness, and impartiality.
- **Distributive justice:** individual differences are insignificant and people receive similar treatment.
- **Procedural justice:** fair laws and procedures.

Problems:
- Equal treatment may not provide more support to those who need more.
- Some situations are not truly equal.

### 9. Applying frameworks
The lecture uses "taking credit for others' work" as a simple application moment. The frameworks can point in different directions:
- Utilitarianism asks who benefits or gets harmed.
- Kantianism asks whether the behavior could be a universal rule.
- Virtue asks what the action says about character.
- Justice asks whether credit and process are fair.

### 10. CSR and triple bottom line
CSR is a business obligation to pursue policies, decisions, and actions aligned with society's objectives and values.

Triple bottom line:
- Profit.
- People.
- Planet.

CSR can become competitive advantage when it supports key business activities instead of sitting apart from strategy.

## Leadership / entrepreneurship / speaking links
### Leadership link
- Leaders make ethics visible through what they do when performance pressure rises.

### Entrepreneurship link
- Founder ethics become company culture. Early shortcuts are dangerous because they teach the team what success is allowed to cost.

### Speaking skill link
- When defending an ethical decision, name the framework you are using and also name its limitation.
