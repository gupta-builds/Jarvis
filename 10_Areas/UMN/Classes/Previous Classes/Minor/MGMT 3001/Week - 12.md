# Entire Week
## What you must be able to do
- Define globalization and multinational company.
- Distinguish the major types of international business activity.
- Explain why emerging markets matter.
- Compare transnational and multinational corporations.
- Identify challenges in multinational operations.
- Explain social dimensions that affect international management.
- Connect global management to CSR, ethics, negotiation, culture, and entrepreneurship.

## Key ideas (short)
- International management modifies everything else in the course: strategy, culture, leadership, negotiation, and ethics.
- Global expansion creates opportunity and complexity at the same time.
- Social dimensions help managers notice difference, but they should not become stereotypes.
- Multinational managers need global mindset, emotional intelligence, long-range thinking, motivation skill, negotiation skill, and cultural humility.

## Lecture
### 1. Why international management comes late in the course
The lecture says international management belongs near the end because earlier management principles still apply, but they need modification. Strategy, culture, leadership, ethics, and negotiation all become harder when operations cross borders.

### 2. Key definitions
- **Globalization:** ongoing social, economic, political, and technological integration process.
- **Multinational company:** any company that engages in business functions beyond its domestic borders.

### 3. Types of international business
The lecture gives three basic patterns:
- Produce goods domestically and sell domestically and internationally.
- Produce goods in a different country but sell domestically.
- Produce goods in a different country and sell domestically and internationally.

The management issue changes depending on the pattern. Selling globally creates customer and market complexity. Producing globally creates operations, labor, ethics, and supply chain complexity.

### 4. Classifications of global economies
- **Developed economies:** mature economies, high GDP, high trade and investment.
- **Emerging markets:** rapidly growing countries between developed and developing status.
- **Developing economies:** lower development but growth potential.
- **Transition economies:** shifting from government-controlled economic systems toward market systems.

### 5. Importance of emerging markets
Emerging markets matter because:
- They include a large share of the world's population.
- Purchasing power has been rising.
- They account for a growing share of exports.
- They create opportunities for multinational companies.

### 6. Transnational vs multinational corporations
- **Transnational corporation:** offices in multiple countries, with different locations responsible for different facets of the organization.
- **Multinational corporation:** offices in multiple countries, where each office may operate as a fuller local version of the organization.

The Nestle example is used for transnational logic: regional and research responsibilities are distributed. Pepsi is used as a multinational example.

### 7. Challenges
The lecture names several global challenges:
- Politics and laws.
- Environmental factors.
- Macroeconomics: GDP, unemployment, inflation, income inequality, currency rates.
- Human rights.
- Cultural differences and language barriers.

This connects to Week 11 because global operations make CSR more visible and more complicated.

### 8. Global context and environments
The lecture reminds you that global context connects to:
- General environment.
- Task environment.
- Internal environment.

That means global management is not separate from strategy. It changes the conditions around strategy.

### 9. Social dimensions
- **Low vs high power distance:** shared decision making and consensus vs respect for authority.
- **Individualism vs collectivism:** individual goals and achievements vs group goals and relationships.
- **Cooperative vs competitive:** collaboration and participation vs individual achievement and assertiveness.
- **Low vs high uncertainty avoidance:** comfort with ambiguity vs preference for rules, planning, and structure.
- **Long vs short term orientation:** long-term horizon vs shorter-term orientation.

The trap is overconfidence. Use dimensions as prompts for better questions, not as labels that flatten people.

### 10. Foreign direct investment
Starbucks is the lecture example. Licensing to foreign operators gave less control. Starbucks later set up its own businesses or purchased existing companies in some countries.

The management issue: entry mode changes control, risk, cost, and local responsiveness.

### 11. Global products and customers
Companies can sometimes make one or a few versions of a product for the world market. Benefits include economies of scale and consistent quality. But global products work best when customer needs are similar enough.

### 12. Competitors and standards
Global trade creates new competitors in two ways:
- Multinationals using developing countries as low-wage high-tech assembly platforms can transfer technology.
- Aggressive multinationals from emerging countries expand beyond their own borders.

Global standards such as ISO technical and environmental standards reduce friction and create common expectations.

### 13. COVID-19 implications
The slides describe a drop in services trade, a smaller but still real drop in goods trade, shipping cost increases, regional trade imbalance, demand for durable goods, and container shortages.

The point is not memorizing the numbers. The point is that global operations create interdependence and fragility.

### 14. CSR and ethics in multinational operations
Multinational companies face scrutiny around:
- Climate change.
- Environmental degradation and pollution.
- Sweatshop conditions.
- Bribery.

Low labor costs do not erase obligations as an international employer.

### 15. Multinational manager qualities
The lecture lists:
1. Global mindset.
2. Emotional intelligence.
3. Ability to take long-range perspective.
4. Talent for motivating others to achieve excellence.
5. Negotiation skill.
6. Willingness to seek overseas assignments.
7. Understanding of national cultures without overconfidence.

## Leadership / entrepreneurship / speaking links
### Leadership link
- Global leaders need cultural humility. They must coordinate people who may read authority, conflict, uncertainty, and relationships differently.

### Entrepreneurship link
- A startup can be global before it is large: suppliers, contractors, software infrastructure, standards, competitors, and customers may already cross borders.
- Global expansion should not be a vanity move. Ask whether the new country changes customer needs, ethical obligations, cost structure, regulation, and control.

### Speaking skill link
- When explaining a global strategy, separate market opportunity from operating complexity. That keeps the recommendation realistic.
