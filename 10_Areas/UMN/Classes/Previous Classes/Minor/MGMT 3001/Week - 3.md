# Entire Week
## What you must be able to do
- [[Textbook]]
- Explain business-level strategy using the “who / what / how” questions and connect those answers to generic strategies (cost leadership, differentiation, focus).
- Run an external analysis with Porter’s 5 forces, identify the strongest forces, and explain what that implies for profitability and positioning.
- Run an internal analysis (resources, core competence, value chain) and explain how internal strengths can support the strategy.
- Combine internal + external into SWOT and explain what “fit” means (and why it must be revisited).
- Explain why “stuck in the middle” happens, plus the digital-economy nuance (some firms can blend low-cost + differentiation in narrow segments).
## Key ideas (textbook)
- Strategy is choices + trade-offs (including what not to do).
- Sustained competitive advantage comes from resources/capabilities that are valuable, rare, hard to imitate, and non-substitutable.
- Five forces is a snapshot; use it repeatedly as markets change.
- SWOT is the bridge: internal strengths/weaknesses + external opportunities/threats → actions, not just a diagram.

## Lecture
- Strategy framing quotes (Porter; Filek) and why process matters.
- Strategic management process: current state evaluation → external scan → internal scan → formulation → implementation/control.
- Current state evaluation: clarify customers/products/process; examine mission/goals/plans; stakeholder expectations.
- Five forces: practical conditions for each force + limitation (snapshot in time).
- SWOT “fit” is dynamic: align resources/capabilities with industry conditions and adjust as O/T change.
#### What is strategy (framing from lecture)
- “The essence of strategy is choosing what not to do.” (Porter)
- “Strategy without process is little more than a wish list.” (Filek)
#### Strategy goal: sustained competitive advantage (lecture criteria)
The slides define the “holy grail” as sustained competitive advantage, tied to resources/capabilities that are:
1. Valuable
2. Rare
3. Difficult to imitate
4. Unique / non-substitutable
The lecture also listed example sources of advantage (technology, protected markets, access to capital, economies of scale, human capital).
#### Strategic management process (how the pieces fit)
The lecture emphasized this sequence:
- Current state evaluation → external scan → internal scan → redefine/formulate strategy → implement/strategic control
- External scan tools: supply & demand, Porter’s 5 forces, Opportunities/Threats side of SWOT
- Internal scan tools: core competence, value chain analysis, Strengths/Weaknesses side of SWOT
#### Current state evaluation (ties to your “Who/What/How”)
- Clarify products/services, process/approach, and customers
- Examine mission, goals, plans
- Examine stakeholders’ expectations
- Verify reasons for existence
Related lecture definitions:
- Mission statement = reason for existence; effective missions build motivation/commitment, provide direction/inspiration, and assist in strategic trade-offs.
#### Porter’s 5 Forces (lecture details + examples)
The slides listed practical “signals” inside each force (examples below):
- Supplier power factors (e.g., supplier concentration, switching costs, presence of substitute inputs, differentiation of inputs, etc.)
- Entry barriers (economies of scale, customer switching costs, capital requirements, distribution access, government policy)
- Buyer power conditions (buyers concentrated, large volume purchases, undifferentiated products like steel, low switching costs)
- Rivalry gets most intense when competition becomes price-focused; examples used: Burger King vs McDonald’s; “cola wars.”
Limitation called out in lecture:
- Five forces “looks at one point in time,” so it should be used before, during, and after entering a market.
Application example:
- The lecture applied forces to Starbucks (substitutes like tea/soda/other coffee shops; scale makes entry hard at Starbucks’ level; buyer preferences matter).
#### Connecting internal + external scan (SWOT “fit”)
The lecture’s key link was: strategy must “fit” company resources/capabilities with industry/competitive environment, and the fit is dynamic (requires adapting to market opportunities/threats while leveraging resources).
