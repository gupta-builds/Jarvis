# Week 5 - Teams and Team Effectiveness
## What you must be able to do
- [[Chapter - 17|Chapter - 17]]
- Define a team and distinguish it from a work group.
- Explain when teams are useful and when they can become inefficient.
- Identify different types of teams and the situations in which each type fits best.
- Apply the IPO framework to analyze team success or failure.
- Explain how team norms, team identity, trust, psychological safety, cohesion, efficacy, and empowerment shape performance.
- Distinguish constructive task conflict from destructive relationship conflict.
- Explain why social loafing happens and how teams can reduce it.
- Describe the stages of team development and what leaders should do at each stage.
- Evaluate team effectiveness using results, satisfaction, viability, and learning.
- Connect team leadership to entrepreneurship, speaking, and classroom collaboration.
## Key ideas (short)
- A team is not just a group of people in the same place. It requires interdependence, shared goals, and collective responsibility.
- Teams are strongest when the task is complex enough to require multiple perspectives and coordinated action.
- Teams fail not only because of weak talent, but because of weak process.
- The IPO framework explains why some teams succeed: inputs shape team states and processes, which shape outcomes.
- Team norms are one of the biggest hidden drivers of performance.
- Psychological safety is essential — teams cannot learn, give feedback, or handle disagreement well without it.
- Cohesion helps commitment, but too much pressure for agreement creates groupthink.
- The best teams do not avoid conflict. They keep conflict focused on ideas rather than people.
- Team leadership is about shaping conditions, not giving instructions.

## Lecture
### 1. What makes a team a team
The lecture defines a team through several core conditions:
- members work interdependently
- members discuss, decide, and do real work together
- the team has a shared identity or boundary
- the team has collective work goals
- the team creates a common output, service, or result
- the team has at least some authority over how it manages internal work processes

The fastest way to remember this:
> A team = work group + interdependence.

This matters because many people call any assigned group a team, even when members are just dividing work and stitching it together at the end. That is closer to a work group than a real team.

### 2. Why teams can outperform individuals
The lecture emphasizes four main benefits:
- **integrate knowledge and skills**
- **cope with complexity**
- **foster motivation and commitment**
- **create synergy**

This means teams are especially useful when:
- the task is too complex for one person
- the work requires multiple specialties
- the problem needs several viewpoints
- the task requires coordination, not just individual effort

A team is not automatically better. It adds coordination costs. So the real question is:
> Is the task complex and interdependent enough to justify teamwork?

### 3. Why good teams still fail
The lecture gives a strong warning that talented teams often fail because of design and climate problems:
- too many all-stars
- competing agendas
- unresolved conflict
- avoidance of real issues
- lack of trust

This is important because performance is not just about intelligence or skill. A team of highly capable people can still fail if members protect ego, avoid accountability, or push private agendas over shared goals.

### 4. Pros and cons of teams
#### Assets
- broad information and expertise
- diversity of opinion
- legitimacy of process
- stronger solutions

#### Liabilities
- time-consuming
- conformity pressure
- domination by a few
- ambiguous responsibility

This is one of the most practical lecture ideas. Teams offer better decision quality when they use their diversity well. But teams become weak when responsibility gets fuzzy or when one or two voices control discussion.

### 5. Different types of teams
The lecture moves beyond one generic idea of a team and shows several forms:

#### Advisory teams
Used to recommend action to decision-makers.
Examples:
- DEI committee
- green team
- safety committee

#### Task force or project teams
Temporary, cross-functional teams formed to solve a problem or create something.
Examples:
- sustainability task force
- health care transformation task force

#### Skunkworks or special initiative teams
Small, multi-skilled teams with more freedom from normal hierarchy, often separated from the parent organization to encourage speed and experimentation.

#### Virtual teams
Teams that work across time and space using digital tools.
Important because communication must be more intentional.

#### Departmental teams
Teams located within one unit such as HR, finance, or supply chain.
Useful when ongoing coordination inside a function matters.

#### Production/service/leadership teams
Cross-functional teams producing a shared product or service.

#### Self-directed teams
Teams that plan, control, and improve their own work processes.
This matters a lot for class teams and entrepreneurship because it requires members to regulate themselves, not just wait for directions.

### 6. IPO framework: how to analyze team development and success
The lecture uses the IPO model:
- **Inputs**
- **States + Processes**
- **Outputs**

This is the central framework for the week.

#### Inputs
These are the conditions the team starts with:
- **Goal/Purpose** — what the team must accomplish
- **Structure** — roles, authority, status, size, geography
- **People** — expertise, personalities, individual goals
- **Tools** — resources that support teamwork

A weak team often starts with weak inputs:
- vague goal
- wrong team size
- poor role design
- missing skills
- weak tools or systems

#### Team States
These are the shared conditions inside the team:
- norms
- trust / psychological safety
- efficacy
- empowerment
- identity
- cohesion

These are not “soft extras.” They affect whether members speak up, help each other, handle conflict, and stay committed.

#### Team Processes
These are the behaviors through which the team actually works:
- coordination
- peer monitoring / feedback
- conflict
- backup behavior

This is where the team either turns inputs into performance or wastes them.

#### Outputs
The lecture highlights four outcomes:
- meaningful results
- member satisfaction
- viability
- learning and adaptation

A team is not fully effective if it only finishes the task but destroys motivation or makes future collaboration worse.

### 7. Inputs in more detail
The lecture asks four practical questions:
- What needs to be accomplished?
- How many people?
- Who should be on the team?
- What resources will help the team?

These look simple, but they control performance early.

#### Goal / purpose
A clear purpose helps the team know:
- what success looks like
- what priorities matter
- what decisions should be guided by

Without a clear purpose, teams drift into busy work or personal preference battles.

#### Structure
Structure includes:
- roles
- authority
- status
- geographic location

Bad structure creates overlap, silence, confusion, or bottlenecks.

#### People
The lecture reminds you that teams are shaped by:
- expertise
- personality
- individual goals

A team can have technically strong members but still struggle if members have conflicting goals or weak interpersonal ability.

#### Tools
Tools include:
- meeting systems
- communication platforms
- shared files
- planning tools
- resources for execution

Virtual teams especially depend on tools being good enough to support coordination.

### 8. Team norms
The lecture says best practice is to discuss and explain norms in a team charter or contract.

This is one of the biggest practical takeaways of the week.

Norms usually cover:
- meeting expectations
- working standards
- communication rules
- leadership expectations
- respect and consideration

Norms matter because they reduce ambiguity and help teams deal with predictable problems before those problems grow.

A team that never sets norms often ends up arguing later about:
- missed deadlines
- weak participation
- silence in meetings
- uneven work quality
- disrespectful communication

### 9. Team identity
The lecture defines team identity as a shared sense of collective bond and membership.

A strong team identity helps members think in terms of:
- “our outcome”
- “our standard”
- “our success”

The lecture also warns about **faultlines**. Faultlines happen when the team splits into subgroups based on visible or meaningful differences. Once that happens, members may identify more with the subgroup than with the team.

A useful fix is creating a **superordinate identity**, meaning a larger shared identity that everyone can commit to.

### 10. Trust and psychological safety
This is one of the most important lecture sections.

Teams lacking trust and psychological safety:
- hide weaknesses
- avoid asking for help
- avoid giving feedback
- waste energy managing appearances
- hold grudges

Trusting teams:
- are vulnerable with each other
- are candid and honest
- accept interdependence

The key idea:
> Psychological safety does not mean low standards. It means people can speak honestly without fear of embarrassment or punishment.

This is essential in speaking-heavy environments because members need to:
- ask questions
- admit confusion
- disagree respectfully
- raise concerns early

Without safety, silence increases and real problems stay buried.

### 11. Efficacy and empowerment
The lecture distinguishes two related states:

#### Team efficacy
A shared belief that the team can achieve its goals.

#### Team empowerment
A shared sense that members:
- have authority and responsibility
- have a voice in decisions
- are capable of success
- are doing meaningful work

Efficacy answers:
> Can we do this?

Empowerment answers:
> Are we trusted and allowed to do this well?

Both matter because skilled teams can still underperform if they feel powerless or doubtful.

### 12. Cohesion and the risk of groupthink
Cohesion is the degree of attraction people feel toward the team and their motivation to remain part of it.

The lecture says cohesion is influenced by:
- member similarity
- team size
- frequency of interaction
- past success
- external challenge

Cohesion is helpful, but the lecture also raises **groupthink**:
- strong pressure to conform
- high concern for unanimity
- weak critical evaluation
- poor decisions

So the lesson is not “more cohesion is always better.”
It is:
> Teams need commitment and belonging, but they also need room for disagreement.

### 13. Team processes: coordination
The lecture identifies **social loafing** as a major coordination problem.

Social loafing is more likely when:
- the group is large
- contributions are hard to notice
- the task is not interesting
- members feel weak attachment to the team

Social loafing is less likely when:
- contributions are visible
- the task matters
- members value belonging
- goals feel shared

This connects to team design. Large teams often increase hiding, diffusion of responsibility, and weak accountability.

### 14. Peer monitoring and feedback
The lecture lists several factors that shape peer monitoring:
- collocated vs virtual structure
- role clarity
- team history
- trust and psychological safety
- cohesion
- power and status differences
- gender dynamics

This shows why feedback is not just a personal skill issue. It depends on team context.

A team with high trust and clear roles is much more likely to correct problems early.
A team with low trust and status imbalance may notice problems but stay silent.

### 15. Team effectiveness
The lecture defines effective teams as those that:
1. produce meaningful results
2. satisfy team members
3. enhance the ability of members to work well together

That third criterion is easy to miss.
A team may get the job done and still be ineffective if people leave burned out, resentful, or less willing to work together in the future.

### 16. Stages of team development
The lecture uses the five-stage model:
- forming
- storming
- norming
- performing
- adjourning

#### Forming
Members are learning the task, the people, and expectations.

#### Storming
Conflict appears around ideas, roles, pace, standards, or personality.

#### Norming
Standards for working together become clearer.

#### Performing
The team works with stronger coordination and less wasted friction.

#### Adjourning
The team ends, reflects, and separates.

This model matters because conflict during storming is not automatically failure. Often it is a normal transition point. What matters is whether the team builds useful norms afterward.

### 17. Team conflict: constructive vs destructive
The lecture makes a critical distinction:

#### Task conflict
- issue-related
- about ideas or problems
- depersonalized
- can improve creativity and decisions

#### Relationship conflict
- personal
- rooted in anger
- distracts from the work
- harms effort and attention

This is a major exam concept:
> Strong teams do not eliminate disagreement. They protect disagreement from becoming personal.

### 18. Managing conflict
The lecture suggests four ways teams can manage conflict well:
- train emotional management skills
- build early cohesion or psychological safety
- create norms and routines for open debate
- emphasize superordinate identity and shared goals

This means conflict management starts before conflict appears.
It is easier to manage disagreement when:
- norms already exist
- respect is already present
- shared purpose is already strong

## Leadership / entrepreneurship / speaking links
### Leadership link
Leading a team is often less about commanding and more about shaping:
- the purpose
- the structure
- the climate
- the quality of discussion

A strong team leader prevents process problems before they become personal problems.

### Entrepreneurship link
Startups rely heavily on small, fast, self-directed teams.
That means founders need to think carefully about:
- team size
- who is on the team
- how decisions are made
- how conflict gets handled
- how to keep identity and urgency strong

A startup team with no norms may look flexible at first but often becomes chaotic later.

### Speaking skill link
Week 5 is directly tied to speaking because strong teams require:
- concise communication
- active listening
- constructive disagreement
- clear feedback
- honest participation

A strong speaker in a team does not just talk more.
A strong speaker helps the team think better.

Useful discussion structure:
1. State the issue clearly.
2. Separate idea critique from person critique.
3. Ask for input from quieter members.
4. Check for agreement and next steps.
5. Confirm who owns what.
