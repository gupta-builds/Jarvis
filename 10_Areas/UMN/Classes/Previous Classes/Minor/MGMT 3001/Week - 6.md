# Entire Week  
## What you must be able to do  
- [[Textbook]]  
- Explain the 6-step rational decision-making process clearly and in order.  
- Distinguish rational decision making from bounded rationality and satisficing.  
- Explain why managers often cannot be fully rational even when they want to be.  
- Define heuristics and explain how they save time but can distort judgment.  
- Recognize major biases from lecture: availability, representativeness, anchoring/adjustment, confirmation, escalation of commitment, status quo, and framing.  
- Explain how emotions and social situations affect decisions, even when a person thinks they are being objective.  
- Apply the decision-making ideas to leadership, entrepreneurship, and speaking situations.  
- Extend the lecture using innovation-related biases such as conformity bias, authority bias, ambiguity bias, bandwagon bias, and feature-positive effect.  
- Explain how managers can reduce bias rather than pretending bias does not exist.  
  
## Key ideas (short)  
- Decision making is not just choosing; it is choosing under limits.  
- The rational model is useful as a standard, but real people rarely meet all its assumptions.  
- Bounded rationality means people work with incomplete information, limited time, and limited processing ability.  
- Satisficing means choosing an option that is good enough rather than fully optimal.  
- Bias is not random stupidity; it is a patterned shortcut that can help speed and hurt judgment.  
- The strongest practical skill this week is not “never be biased.” It is learning to notice where bias enters the process.  
- In groups, bias becomes even more dangerous because social pressure can make bad thinking feel normal.  
- In innovation settings, bias often pushes people toward the familiar, the safe-looking, and the already-approved path. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

- **Choosing a college** works well for the 6-step rational model because the criteria can be listed, weighted, and compared.

## Lecture  
### 1. The lecture starts with the ideal model  
The lecture opens with the **rational decision-making process**:  
1. define the problem  
2. identify decision criteria  
3. weight the criteria  
4. generate alternatives  
5. evaluate alternatives  
6. compute the optimal decision  
  
This matters because the lecture is not claiming people always decide this way.  
It uses the rational model as a benchmark:  
- what good decision structure looks like  
- what “thorough” decision making would require  
- what gets lost when real-life limits appear  
  
### 2. Rationality depends on strong assumptions  
The lecture then lists the assumptions behind rational decision making:  
- problem clarity  
- clear preferences  
- constant preferences  
- known options  
- no time constraints  
- maximum payoff  
  
This is one of the most important parts of the week because it explains why the model is useful but unrealistic.  
  
The lecture also pairs each assumption with a limit:  
- information may be incomplete, imperfect, or misleading  
- people may lack the background to process information well  
- time may be limited  
- different players may want different things  
  
So the real lesson is:  
> The rational model is strongest as a guide, not as a description of everyday human behavior.  
  
### 3. Bounded rationality explains actual behavior  
The lecture then moves to **Herbert Simon** and bounded rationality.  
  
Bounded rationality means people try to be rational, but their rationality is limited by:  
- the information they have  
- their mental processing limits  
- the time they have to decide  
  
This is a very practical concept because it explains why even smart and careful people still make imperfect choices.  
  
A manager often does not ask:  
- “What is the absolute best option in theory?”  
  
A manager more often asks:  
- “Given the time, evidence, and risk, what is a workable option now?”  
  
That leads to **satisficing**, which means choosing an option that is good enough.  
  
### 4. The lecture broadens decision making beyond logic  
A major strength of the lecture is that it refuses to treat decision making as only a logical spreadsheet process.  
  
It shows that real decisions are shaped by:  
- biases  
- emotions  
- social situations  
  
This is a big shift from “how we should decide” to “how we actually decide.”  
  
It also explains why many weak decisions do not come from lack of intelligence.  
They often come from:  
- fast pattern-matching  
- emotional reactions  
- social pressure  
- overconfidence in familiar reasoning  
  
### 5. The gut-reaction exercise is doing something important  
The lecture asks for quick reactions to:  
- people who drive an electric car  
- people who drive slow  
- people who voted for the opposite candidate  
  
That exercise is not random.  
It is teaching that judgment starts before formal reasoning begins.  
  
The point is:  
- people form impressions very quickly  
- people sort others into categories almost automatically  
- those categories then shape later choices and interpretations  
  
That is why bias is not just a hiring problem or an ethics problem.  
It affects daily leadership, teamwork, persuasion, and customer judgment.  
  
### 6. Heuristics: why shortcuts exist  
The lecture defines **heuristics** as mental shortcuts that help people decide or solve problems quickly with minimal effort.  
  
This is important because the lecture does **not** say shortcuts are always bad.  
Shortcuts exist because:  
- the world is crowded with information  
- people cannot analyze everything from scratch  
- speed is often necessary  
  
But the tradeoff is clear:  
- more speed  
- less full analysis  
- more room for distortion  
  
So heuristics are best understood as efficient but risky.  
  
### 7. Biases emphasized in lecture  
#### Availability bias  
People judge based on what is vivid, recent, emotional, or easy to remember.  
  
Why it matters:  
- recent stories feel more true than base rates  
- personal examples can outweigh broader evidence  
- memorable events can distort risk judgment  
  
Typical result:  
- “I know one example, so I assume it reflects the whole pattern.”  
  
#### Representativeness bias  
People classify based on stereotype fit.  
  
Why it matters:  
- a person or situation gets matched to a mental image  
- people confuse resemblance with probability  
- quick categories can replace actual evidence  
  
Typical result:  
- “This seems like the kind of person/situation that fits X, so it must be X.”  
  
#### Anchoring and adjustment  
People rely too heavily on an initial number, idea, or starting point.  
  
Why it matters:  
- the first figure shapes later judgment  
- adjustments happen, but usually not enough  
- negotiations, prices, estimates, and targets are highly vulnerable  
  
Typical result:  
- “The first number became the reference point.”  
  
#### Confirmation bias  
Once people lean toward a view, they look for support rather than challenge.  
  
Why it matters:  
- people protect earlier judgment  
- disconfirming evidence is neglected  
- search becomes selective rather than objective  
  
Typical result:  
- “I am gathering proof, not testing the idea fairly.”  
  
#### Escalation of commitment  
People continue investing because they already invested a lot.  
  
Why it matters:  
- sunk costs start shaping future choices  
- exit feels like admitting failure  
- people defend the past instead of reassessing the present  
  
Typical result:  
- “We have come too far to stop.”  
  
#### Status quo bias  
People prefer the current state because it feels safer and more familiar.  
  
Why it matters:  
- change is judged more harshly than staying put  
- familiar options feel lower-risk even when they are not  
- comfort can beat better judgment  
  
Typical result:  
- “Let’s stick with what we already know.”  
  
#### Framing  
The same information leads to different choices depending on how it is presented.  
  
Why it matters:  
- wording changes perceived risk  
- gain frames and loss frames do not feel the same  
- presentation can quietly drive decisions  
  
Typical result:  
- “The facts were the same, but the wording changed the response.”  
  
### 8. The lecture’s HBR-style bias slide matters more than it looks  
The lecture describes bias like a fast “danger detector” and compares managing bias to pressing the brakes in a car.  
  
That analogy is helpful:  
- you do not remove the human tendency to react quickly  
- you build interruption points  
- you create systems that slow down bad automatic judgment  
  
This is why the lecture also mentions:  
- priming  
- reorganizing structures and systems  
- new forms of accountability  
  
In other words, bias management is not only personal.  
It is also organizational.  
  
### 9. Innovation bias additions from the outside reading  
The Board of Innovation article adds several bias patterns that fit this week especially well in entrepreneurial and creative work.  
  
It distinguishes **information-processing biases** from **emotional biases**, and argues that both often push teams back toward the familiar, safer-looking option rather than truly new thinking. It also warns that many innovation teams say things like “that’s too disruptive,” “nobody would buy it,” or “we’ve already tested something like that” when biases are shaping the room. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}  
  
Important additions:  
- **Conformity bias**: going along with the group even against your independent judgment. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}  
- **Authority bias**: giving too much weight to the senior person’s view. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}  
- **Ambiguity bias**: preferring the more knowable option over the less certain but possibly better one. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}  
- **Bandwagon bias**: favoring an idea because others are favoring it. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}  
- **Projection bias**: assuming future customers will want what current decision-makers want now. :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}  
- **Pro-innovation bias**: assuming “new” automatically means “good.” :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}  
- **Feature-positive effect**: focusing on appealing benefits while neglecting major downsides or missing information. :contentReference[oaicite:10]{index=10}  
  
These are especially useful because they connect decision-making theory to entrepreneurship and product design.  
  
### 10. Emotions and social situations should not be treated as side notes  
Even though the lecture centers on bias, it also signals that decisions are affected by emotion and social context.  
  
That means:  
- people do not enter decisions as neutral calculators  
- mood can shape risk tolerance  
- identity can shape judgment  
- group setting can shape what feels acceptable to say  
  
This matters in class discussions, team meetings, startup pitches, and leadership situations.  
A person may privately doubt an idea but publicly support it because:  
- a leader already endorsed it  
- the group energy is moving one direction  
- disagreement feels costly  
  
### 11. What the week is really teaching  
The deepest lesson is not:  
- “Use the rational model always”  
  
It is:  
- use the rational model to structure thought  
- expect bounded rationality in real life  
- watch for predictable judgment traps  
- create habits and systems that improve the quality of decisions  
  
## Leadership / entrepreneurship / speaking links  
### Leadership link  
A strong leader does not just make fast calls.  
A strong leader:  
- defines the real problem carefully  
- separates evidence from assumption  
- invites challenge before commitment hardens  
- notices when the group is favoring comfort over truth  
  
### Entrepreneurship link  
Startups are highly exposed to ambiguity, time pressure, and emotional attachment.  
That makes them especially vulnerable to:  
- action bias  
- authority bias  
- ambiguity bias  
- escalation of commitment  
- feature-positive effect  
- projection bias :contentReference[oaicite:11]{index=11}  
  
Founders need to ask:  
- are we learning, or are we protecting our original idea?  
- are we testing customer reality, or only our own preferences?  
- are we moving fast intelligently, or just moving?  
  
### Speaking skill link  
This week strongly supports speaking skill because better speaking can reduce worse decisions.  
  
Useful pattern for team discussion:  
1. State the decision clearly.  
2. Name the criteria.  
3. Separate facts from assumptions.  
4. Ask what evidence would disconfirm the current favorite.  
5. Ask what bias might be shaping the room.  
6. End with a clear next step.  
  
That speaking structure makes meetings sharper and more credible.
