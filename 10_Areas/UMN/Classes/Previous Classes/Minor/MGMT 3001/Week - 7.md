# Week 7 - Organizational Culture
## What you must be able to do
- [[Chapter - 8|Chapter - 8]]
- Define organizational culture clearly and explain why it matters.
- Distinguish artifacts, espoused values, and underlying assumptions.
- Explain how culture affects employee behavior, performance, and coordination.
- Distinguish strong culture from weak culture and explain why strong cultures can help or hurt.
- Explain how founders, leaders, and teams shape culture over time.
- Identify the main tools organizations use to spread and reinforce culture.
- Explain how reward systems, stories, crisis responses, and hiring decisions communicate culture.
- Analyze why culture change is difficult and why many culture-change efforts fail.
- Apply culture ideas to leadership, entrepreneurship, teamwork, and speaking.

## Key ideas (short)
- Organizational culture is the shared way people learn what is acceptable, rewarded, and expected in a workplace.
- Culture often matters more than formal rules because it influences behavior even when no one is watching.
- What an organization says it values is not always the same as what it actually rewards.
- The deepest level of culture is usually invisible; people often stop noticing assumptions once they become normal.
- Strong cultures can improve alignment, clarity, and commitment, but they can also discourage dissent and adaptability.
- Culture spreads through repeated signals: who gets hired, who gets promoted, what gets celebrated, and how leaders act under pressure.
- Leaders do not just “talk about” culture; they teach culture through attention, behavior, systems, and consequences.
- Culture change is hard because it requires changing repeated patterns, not just updating slogans.

## Lecture
### 1. The lecture begins with a strong claim: culture is hard to copy and shapes behavior
The first slides make an important point:
- culture is hard to determine
- culture is difficult to imitate
- culture shapes behavior
- culture may be more valuable than strategy

This is a very strong management claim.
It means that two firms can use similar strategies, tools, and structures, yet get very different results because the “invisible rules” guiding everyday behavior are different.

A strategy tells people where the organization wants to go.
Culture influences how people actually behave while trying to get there.

### 2. What culture actually is
The lecture defines culture through shared:
- assumptions
- values
- beliefs

This matters because culture is not just:
- office decoration
- mission statements
- perks
- fun events

Those things can reflect culture, but they are not the whole thing.

A better way to think about culture:
> Culture is the shared system that tells people what is normal, what is respected, what is risky, and what success looks like.

That is why culture shapes behavior so strongly.
People learn:
- what gets rewarded
- what gets ignored
- what gets punished
- who has influence
- what “good work” looks like

### 3. The company examples are not random; they show different ways culture becomes visible
The lecture uses several companies to make culture concrete.

#### Zappos
Main ideas:
- customer service identity
- hiring for cultural fit
- intensive onboarding
- paying new hires to quit if they are not the right fit

Management meaning:
Zappos treats culture as a filtering device, not just a branding message.
It is trying to make sure that the people who remain actually want the environment.

#### L.L. Bean
Main ideas:
- low turnover
- outdoor activities
- employee development
- celebrations and recognition

Management meaning:
This example shows culture as belonging, appreciation, and lifestyle alignment.
The firm is building a work setting where employees feel connected to the brand and its way of living.

#### Warby Parker
Main ideas:
- team-based onboarding
- social consciousness
- growth opportunities
- honest communication

Management meaning:
This suggests a culture built around inclusion, voice, and purpose.

#### Adobe
Main ideas:
- trust
- support for difficult projects
- goal-based evaluation
- low micromanagement

Management meaning:
This example highlights a culture of autonomy and confidence in employees.

The big lesson from all these examples:
> Culture becomes visible through repeated practices, not only through stated values.

### 4. Elements of a strong company culture
The lecture lists these elements:
- clear mission and values
- encourage participation
- focus on transparency
- celebrate wins
- leaders are present and accessible
- align culture to business

This section is important because it shows that strong culture is not just emotional warmth.
It includes clarity and fit.

A strong culture answers:
- Why are we here?
- What matters most?
- How should people act?
- How do we recognize success?
- Do leaders behave in line with what the organization claims?

The “align culture to business” point is especially important.
A culture is not strong just because it feels positive.
It must also support the actual work and strategic direction of the organization.

### 5. Focus areas for culture
The lecture identifies six focus areas:
- purpose
- opportunity
- success
- appreciation
- wellbeing
- leadership

These are useful because they show the main zones where culture is felt by employees.

#### Purpose
Do people understand why the organization exists and why their work matters?

#### Opportunity
Do people believe they can grow, contribute, and move forward?

#### Success
Does the organization make success visible and meaningful?

#### Appreciation
Do people feel that effort is noticed and valued?

#### Wellbeing
Does the culture treat employees like replaceable labor, or as people whose energy and health matter?

#### Leadership
Do leaders create trust, clarity, and consistency?

Together, these six areas explain why culture affects both performance and commitment.

### 6. Culture and the internal environment
The lecture says successful cultures are:
1. adaptable
2. focused on employee involvement
3. founded on clear vision
4. consistent

This is a very useful four-part framework.

#### Adaptable
The culture can respond to change without freezing or becoming defensive.

#### Employee involvement
People are invited to contribute rather than simply comply.

#### Clear vision
Members know what the organization stands for.

#### Consistent
The culture does not send mixed signals about values and behavior.

This matters because a culture can be positive in one way but weak in another.
For example:
- a company can have a clear vision but weak involvement
- a company can be supportive but inconsistent
- a company can be efficient but not adaptable

### 7. The examples of adaptable, involved, clear-vision, and consistent cultures
The lecture uses several examples to illustrate the four-part framework.

#### Hilton: adaptable culture
The focus is on approachability, service, and openness to new ideas.
This implies a culture that can keep learning while maintaining customer focus.

#### HubSpot: employee involvement
The emphasis is on voice, access, and data-backed initiative.
This shows that involvement does not mean chaos; employees are heard, but still expected to make a reasoned case.

#### Mars: clear vision
The five principles create a moral and operational guide for decisions.
This is important because culture becomes stronger when values are easy to remember and apply.

#### Intuit: consistency
“Do the right thing” works only if the organization actually backs it through support and behavior.
Consistency means the culture does not collapse under pressure.

### 8. The three levels of culture
This is the core conceptual section of the week.

The lecture presents three levels:
- artifacts and symbols
- espoused norms, values, and beliefs
- underlying assumptions

#### A. Artifacts and symbols
These are the visible things a newcomer would notice:
- logos and mascots
- language and slogans
- office layout
- dress
- rituals
- ceremonies
- stories
- legends

Artifacts are easy to observe but hard to interpret correctly.
A company may look innovative, open, or people-centered on the surface, but that surface alone does not reveal the deepest culture.

#### B. Espoused values and beliefs
These are what the organization says it values.
They indicate:
- what is desired
- what is rewarded
- what behavior is expected
- what counts as right and wrong

This level is more meaningful than artifacts, but it still may not tell the whole truth.
Organizations often say one thing and reward another.

#### C. Underlying assumptions
These are the deepest and most powerful layer.
They are:
- hidden
- taken for granted
- often unconscious
- treated as obvious truth inside the organization

Examples:
- “Speed matters more than caution”
- “Leaders should always appear confident”
- “Conflict is dangerous”
- “Customers come before internal convenience”
- “Innovation matters more than stability”

This is the level that really drives behavior because people no longer debate these assumptions; they simply live them.

### 9. Why culture affects performance
Your chapter notes make this very clear.
Culture affects performance because it creates:
- goal alignment
- role clarity
- behavior consistency
- informal coordination
- a moral guide for action

When culture is clear, people do not need constant monitoring.
They already know:
- what matters
- how to act
- what decisions fit the organization

This can reduce friction and increase speed.

But the lecture and chapter both imply an important warning:
> Culture improves performance only when the shared patterns support the right behaviors.

A strong culture can also push performance in the wrong direction if it rewards silence, blind loyalty, or unethical shortcuts.

### 10. Strong culture, weak culture, and the dark side
The chapter adds helpful detail through two dimensions:
- agreement
- intensity

From that, you can think of:
- strong culture = high agreement + high intensity
- weak culture = low agreement + low intensity

This matters because strong culture is not just “lots of passion.”
It also requires shared understanding.

But strong culture has risks:
- discouraging disagreement
- making change harder
- rewarding group loyalty over truth
- creating pressure to conform

That is why strong culture can become rigid.
A company may be very aligned, but aligned around the wrong habits.

### 11. Subcultures matter
The chapter notes explain that subcultures can form by:
- function
- geography
- product line
- team identity

This is important because organizations are rarely culturally uniform.

A finance group may value precision and control.
A sales team may value speed and persuasion.
A design team may value experimentation.

Subcultures are not automatically bad.
They can support local effectiveness.
But they can also create conflict when units must work together and do not share the same priorities or assumptions.

### 12. Who shapes culture
The lecture names three key players:
- founders
- organizational leaders
- teams

#### Founders
Founders shape the earliest assumptions:
- what risks are worth taking
- what the organization exists to do
- how people should behave
- what standards matter

Founders leave a strong imprint because early choices often become “the way we do things here.”

#### Organizational leaders
Leaders reinforce culture by:
- guiding behavior
- signaling priorities
- responding to problems
- deciding what gets measured
- choosing what is tolerated

Leaders are culture teachers whether they intend to be or not.

#### Teams
Teams build routines.
When a team solves a problem repeatedly in a certain way, that routine can become part of the culture.

This matters because culture is not only top-down.
It is also built through repeated group practice.

### 13. Ways organizations spread culture
The lecture lists several mechanisms:
- formal statements
- physical space
- role modeling
- reward systems
- stories and legends
- what is measured and controlled
- leader reactions in crisis
- organization design
- recruiting, selecting, promoting, and managing employees

This is one of the most practical sections of the week because it shows that culture is taught through systems.

#### Formal statements
Mission, purpose, values, and public language.
These matter, but only if other systems support them.

#### Designing physical space
The office or workspace can shape collaboration, creativity, hierarchy, or openness.

#### Role modeling
People watch what leaders do far more closely than what they say.

#### Reward systems
If one behavior gets praised and another gets promoted, employees quickly learn the real rules.

#### Stories and legends
Stories turn values into memorable examples.
They teach what kind of behavior becomes heroic inside the organization.

#### What is measured and controlled
Measurement tells people what leadership truly cares about.

#### Leader reactions in crisis
Crises reveal real culture because priorities become clear under pressure.

#### Organization design
How work is divided and connected influences what behaviors become normal.

#### Hiring and promotion criteria
Who gets in and who advances are among the strongest cultural signals in any organization.

### 14. The examples for each culture mechanism
The lecture gives useful illustrations.

#### Coca-Cola and formal statements
The statement about refreshing mind, body, and spirit connects purpose to inclusion and identity.
This shows how formal values try to frame the organization’s meaning.

#### Google and physical space
Creative office design signals innovation, movement, and interaction.
Space communicates what kind of work is expected.

#### United Airlines and role modeling
Starting board meetings with safety discussion shows that safety is not symbolic; it is central.

#### Publix and reward systems
Employee stock participation teaches shared stake and ownership.

#### Zappos and stories
The long support-call story teaches extraordinary commitment to service.

#### Adobe Kickbox and measurement/control
Giving employees resources to test ideas sends a strong message that innovation is not just a slogan.

#### Chipotle and crisis response
How leaders respond during crisis teaches whether the culture protects image or truly faces responsibility.

### 15. Culture change
The lecture asks whether culture can be changed and notes that success is limited.

This is an essential exam point.
Culture change is difficult because culture is:
- repeated
- social
- emotional
- reinforced by systems
- tied to identity
- partly invisible

People often try to change culture by:
- rewriting values
- launching campaigns
- making speeches

But culture changes only when daily patterns change:
- what leaders reward
- how decisions are made
- how conflict is handled
- how hiring works
- what gets measured
- what happens in crises

The Microsoft example in the lecture is useful because it shows cultural change as:
- breaking silos
- shifting from know-it-all behavior toward learning
- promoting cooperation
- linking compensation to desired values

That is much deeper than “improving morale.”
It is changing what the organization treats as success.

### 16. Why this week matters for leadership
This week is really about leadership through signals.

Leaders shape culture when they:
- choose what matters
- respond to problems
- define acceptable behavior
- decide what excellence looks like
- reinforce values through structure and consequences

A leader who says “we value innovation” but punishes failed experiments creates a culture of caution.
A leader who says “we value honesty” but reacts badly to bad news creates a culture of silence.

### 17. Why this matters for entrepreneurship
Startups often think culture will “just happen.”
But even very early teams already create culture through:
- founder behavior
- who gets hired
- what gets rewarded
- how feedback is handled
- whether urgency becomes panic
- whether flexibility becomes confusion

The earliest routines often become long-lasting assumptions.
That means founders are creating culture from the start, even before they have formal systems.

### 18. Why this matters for speaking skill
Culture and speaking are directly connected.

In a strong speaking culture:
- people know when to speak up
- feedback is expected
- disagreement can happen respectfully
- leaders communicate values clearly
- stories reinforce standards

A speaker who understands culture speaks more effectively because they can:
- match the organization’s values
- read what the group actually respects
- distinguish real norms from official slogans
- persuade in a way that fits the setting

Culture is one of the strongest forms of indirect leadership.
It shapes behavior before a leader enters the room.

Founders create culture through repeated choices long before HR systems exist.
Early hiring, norms, and reactions to pressure matter a lot.

### Speaking skill link
Culture influences which messages sound credible, which behaviors feel acceptable, and whether people feel safe enough to speak honestly.

Useful speaking structure for culture analysis:
1. What visible signals stand out?
2. What values are being claimed?
3. What behaviors are actually rewarded?
4. What hidden assumptions might explain that pattern?
5. What effect does this have on performance and people?

## Flashcards
#cards/MGMT
Organizational culture::A shared pattern of assumptions, values, and beliefs that guides how members think, act, and interact.
Artifacts::Visible signs of culture such as symbols, language, stories, rituals, and physical layout.
Espoused values::What the organization says it values and expects.
Underlying assumptions::Deep, taken-for-granted beliefs that drive behavior and are often invisible.
Strong culture::High agreement and high intensity around shared values.
Weak culture::Low agreement and low intensity around values.
Subculture::A smaller culture within the organization based on function, geography, product, or team.
Adaptable culture::A culture that can respond and adjust to change.
Employee involvement culture::A culture that gives people voice, participation, and ownership.
Clear vision culture::A culture anchored in memorable and guiding principles.
Consistent culture::A culture that sends stable signals about expected behavior.
Culture change::The effort to shift shared assumptions, behaviors, and systems over time.
Role modeling::Leaders teaching culture through their own behavior.
Reward systems::Incentives and recognition that show what the organization truly values.
Stories and legends::Memorable narratives that reinforce culture and ideal behavior.
Leader reaction in crisis::A powerful signal of real priorities and values under pressure.
Cultural fit::The degree to which a person matches and supports the organization’s norms and values.
Organizational commitment::The employee’s attachment to and identification with the organization.
Compliance::Commitment based on exchange, such as pay for work.
Identification::Commitment based on belonging and membership.
Internalization::Commitment based on genuine alignment between personal and organizational values.
