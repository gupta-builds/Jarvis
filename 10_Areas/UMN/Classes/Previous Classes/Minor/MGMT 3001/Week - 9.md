# Entire Week
## What you must be able to do
- Distinguish power, influence, and authority.
- Explain playing high vs playing low and when each is useful.
- Describe the five interpersonal power bases.
- Match influence tactics to power bases.
- Explain how gender norms can shape leader perception.
- Choose an influence tactic based on context, power base, skill, and resistance.

## Key ideas (short)
- Power is potential; influence is power in action; authority is formal right.
- People can have power without authority and authority without much influence.
- Power is necessary for leadership, but misuse of power damages trust.
- Influence works best when the tactic fits the relationship, power base, and request.

## Lecture
### 1. Power, influence, and authority are different
- **Power:** ability to influence another person.
- **Influence:** the process of impacting another person's thoughts, behavior, or feelings.
- **Authority:** the right to influence another person because of a formal role.

This distinction matters because formal authority does not guarantee real influence. A junior person may influence a team through expertise or relationships. A manager may have authority but fail to move people.

### 2. Comfort and discomfort reveal power
The lecture asks students to notice situations where they feel comfortable or uncomfortable. Comfort often appears where a person has power, expertise, or familiarity. Discomfort often appears where the person lacks status, knowledge, or control.

This is not just self-reflection. It helps leaders notice when they are acting from strength, fear, rank, or insecurity.

### 3. Deborah Gruenfeld and acting with power
The lecture uses Deborah Gruenfeld's work to explain that people make choices about how to use power. Sometimes people are in authority roles that do not fit the situation. Sometimes people struggle to take power even when the role requires it.

### 4. Playing high
Playing high signals authority and rank. It can include:
- Expansive body language.
- Relaxation and openness.
- Taking up space.
- Still head.
- Longer eye contact.
- Complete sentences.
- Less monitoring of others' reactions.

Use playing high when rank needs reinforcement or status is up for grabs. The warning from lecture: playing higher than your actual rank can be dangerous.

### 5. Playing low
Playing low builds approachability and rapport. It can include:
- Smaller body posture.
- Leaning forward.
- Less direct eye contact.
- More warmth.
- Incomplete or softer phrasing.
- Making others feel raised up.

Use playing low when relationships are important or when you want to reduce psychological distance.

### 6. Gender norms and perception
The lecture emphasizes that men and women may not behave differently, but they are often perceived differently because of socialization. The Heidi/Howard example shows how the same competence and ambition can be interpreted differently depending on gender expectations.

The practical lesson: power behavior is not judged in a vacuum. Social norms shape how audiences read the same action.

### 7. Interpersonal power bases
- **Legitimate power:** based on formal position.
- **Reward power:** based on the ability to give positive consequences or remove negative ones.
- **Coercive power:** based on the ability to punish.
- **Expert power:** based on knowledge, skill, or information.
- **Referent power:** based on liking, admiration, or identification.

The lecture asks which power works consistently regardless of position. The answer points toward interpersonal power, especially expertise and referent power.

### 8. Social influence
Social influence is power in action. It:
- Operates directionally.
- Is relative.
- Changes behavior or attitudes.
- Affects near-term and long-term career success.

### 9. Influence tactics
- **Rational persuasion:** logical arguments and facts. Strong for upward influence. Usually tied to expert power.
- **Inspirational appeal:** values and ideals. Depends on knowing what matters to the target. Often tied to referent power.
- **Consultation:** ask for input, increasing commitment.
- **Ingratiation:** favors, compliments, or friendliness before asking.
- **Exchange:** quid pro quo.
- **Personal appeal:** loyalty or friendship.
- **Collaboration:** provide help or resources.
- **Apprising:** explain how the request benefits the target personally.
- **Coalition:** use others to persuade or pressure.
- **Pressure:** demands, threats, or persistent reminders.

### 10. Choosing the right tactic
The lecture closes with "it depends." Ask:
- Is the tactic consistent with social norms and role expectations?
- Do I have the appropriate power base?
- How much skill does this tactic require?
- How much resistance already exists?

## Leadership / entrepreneurship / speaking links
### Leadership link
- Leaders need power to create movement, but the power base matters. Expert and referent power often travel better than formal authority.

### Entrepreneurship link
- Founders use power before they have much hierarchy. Credibility, expertise, vision, and trust become early influence tools.
- Coercive pressure may produce short-term speed but can damage the culture early.

### Speaking skill link
- Match body language to purpose. Use high signals when clarity and authority matter; use low signals when openness and rapport matter.

## Takeaways (questions to resolve)
- [ ] What power base do I actually have in this situation?
- [ ] Am I using the tactic that fits the person's interests and the relationship?
- [ ] Would this influence attempt still feel ethical if the target described it back to me?

## Lecture-to-textbook synthesis
- ==Definition anchor: power is the capacity to influence, influence is the process of affecting thoughts/feelings/behavior, and authority is the formal right to influence.==
- *Mechanism:* power works through bases like legitimate, reward, coercive, expert, and referent power. Influence tactics translate those bases into actual attempts to create compliance or commitment.
- Lecture examples/scenarios: playing high signals authority, confidence, and control; playing low signals approachability and reduces psychological distance. Gender norms matter because the same behavior can be interpreted differently depending on expectations.
