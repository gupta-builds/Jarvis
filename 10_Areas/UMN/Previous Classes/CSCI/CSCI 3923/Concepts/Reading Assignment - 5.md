# Chapter - 8
Chapter 8 of _Weapons of Math Destruction_, titled **"Collateral Damage: Landing Credit,"** explores how mathematical models decide who gets to borrow money and how these systems can unfairly punish people for being poor or for living in the "wrong" neighborhood.
1. *The Old Way: The Tall Banker*: Before computers, if you wanted a loan (like for a car or a house), you went to see the **local banker** in your town.
	- **The Problem:** The banker would judge you based on his own feelings. He might look at whether you went to church, who your family was, or even your race. This was often very unfair because "outsiders," women, and minorities were often locked out just because the banker didn't know or trust them.
2. *The "Good" Robot: FICO Scores*: In the 1950s, two men named Bill Fair and Earl Isaac created a math formula called **FICO**.
	- **Why it was better:** It didn't care about your race or what church you went to. It only looked at your **financial behavior**—like if you paid your bills on time and how much debt you had.
	- **A "Healthy" Model:** The author says FICO is a good model because it is **transparent** (you can go to a website to see how to improve your score) and it has a **feedback loop** (if the math is wrong, the companies fix it so they don't lose money).
3. *The "Bad" Robots: E-Scores*: Today, companies use something much scarier called **e-scores**. These are secret credit scores that you aren't allowed to see.
	- **The Data Hunt:** Unlike FICO, which uses your bank records, e-scores use **proxies** (stand-in data). They look at what you search for on Google, what you buy, and even where your computer is located.
	- **Call Center Hell:** A company called **Neustar** uses this to rank callers. If your e-score is high (meaning you look rich), you get to talk to a human right away. If it’s low, you have to wait for a long time or talk to a machine.
4. *"People Like You" (Bucketing)*: The biggest problem with e-scores is that they judge you based on **other people**.
	- **The "Bucket":** If the computer sees you live in a neighborhood where many people have trouble paying loans, it puts you in a "bucket" with them. Even if you are a very responsible person, the computer treats you like a "deadbeat" because of **where you live**.
	- **Zip Codes as a Proxy:** Using a zip code is often a secret way for a computer to use **race** as a factor, which is unfair and often illegal.
5. *The Poverty Trap (Credit Checks for Jobs)*: Many bosses now check your credit report before they hire you.
	- **The Cycle:** If you can't get a job because you have bad credit, you won't have money to pay your bills. This makes your credit even worse, which makes it even harder to get a job.
	- **Wealth vs. Responsibility:** A high credit score is often just a proxy for **wealth**. Rich people can keep their scores high even when things go wrong because they have savings.
6. *When the Robot Makes a Mistake*: Computers often get people mixed up, especially if they have common names.
	- **The Story of Catherine Taylor:** She lost a job and was denied housing because a computer mixed her up with another Catherine Taylor who had committed crimes.
	- **Need for Humans:** In Catherine's case, a nice human worker named Wanda Taylor looked at the file and noticed the criminal had a tattoo on her ankle that the "real" Catherine didn't have. The author warns there aren't enough "Wandas" to fix all the computer's mistakes.
7. *Digital Phrenology and Errors*: The author compares these models to **phrenology**—an old, fake science where people guessed your personality by feeling the bumps on your head.
	- **Google's Mistake:** She mentions a time when Google's photo-tagging app incorrectly labeled African Americans as "gorillas" because the math hadn't been tested properly.
8. *The American Express "Low Blow"*: In 2009, **American Express** lowered the spending limits for some customers simply because they shopped at certain stores.
	- **Guilt by Association:** If you shopped at a place where other people often failed to pay their bills (like a discount store), the computer assumed you were going to fail, too. This made people's credit scores drop even if they hadn't done anything wrong.
9. *ZestFinance: All Data is Credit Data*: A former Google boss started a company called **ZestFinance** that uses 10,000 different data points to decide if you get a loan.
	- **Spelling Matters:** The computer looks at whether you used **capital letters and correct spelling** on your application.
	- **The Injustice:** While "rule-followers" might be better at paying loans, checking for perfect spelling is just another way to punish people who didn't get a good education, which usually hurts poor people and immigrants the most.
10. *Peer-to-Peer Lending (Lending Club)*: Companies like **Lending Club** were supposed to let regular people lend money to each other.
	- **The Reality:** Big banks realized they could use these platforms to use **unregulated e-scores** to avoid government rules. It’s not about helping people; it’s about using secret math to charge people higher interest rates without having to explain why.
**Conclusion of the Chapter:** The author says that while we used to worry about mean or racist bankers, those bankers could at least look you in the eye and listen to your story. Modern WMDs are **black boxes** that judge you based on your "tribe" or your neighbors, and they offer no way for you to argue your case.
# Chapter - 9
Chapter 9 of _Weapons of Math Destruction_, titled **"NO SAFE ZONE: Getting Insurance,"** explains how insurance companies are moving away from sharing risks together as a group and instead using secret math to punish individuals for things they can’t always control.,
1. *The History: A 100-Year-Old "Bad" Model*: The author starts with a story about **Frederick Hoffman**, a math expert in 1896. He wrote a 330-page report using statistics to say that Black Americans were "uninsurable" (meaning they shouldn't be allowed to have life insurance).
	- **The Big Mistake (Causation vs. Correlation):** Hoffman was blinded by racism. He saw that Black people died younger and blamed their race. He ignored the **real reasons** like poverty, lack of clean water, and bad doctors.
	- **The "Same Bucket" Error:** He treated every Black person as if they were exactly the same, whether they were a wealthy teacher in a city or a poor worker in a field.
	- **Redlining:** This led to a cruel practice called **redlining**, where banks and insurance companies drew lines on maps and refused to help anyone living in certain neighborhoods.
2. *How Insurance is Supposed to Work*: The author explains that insurance was originally a way for a **community to help each other**.,
	- **Risk Pooling:** Imagine everyone in a village puts $1 in a jar. If one person’s house burns down, they use the money in the jar to fix it. Because you don’t know _whose_ house will burn, everyone shares the cost.,
	- **The Change:** Today, companies use "Big Data" to find the "riskiest" people and charge them a lot more money in advance.,
3. *Auto Insurance: The "Credit Score" Trap*: A study by _Consumer Reports_ looked at 2 billion price quotes for car insurance and found something shocking.
	- **Money over Safety:** In many states, insurance companies care more about your **credit score** (how you handle money) than your **driving record**.,
	- **The Example:** In Florida, a driver with a perfect record but **bad credit** paid $1,552 **more** than a driver with a drunk driving conviction but **excellent credit**.
	- **Why?** Companies do this because it’s profitable. Poor people are less likely to shop around for better prices, so the computer targets them for higher bills.,
4. *The "Sucker" Score (Price Optimization)*: Some companies, like **Allstate**, use an algorithm to figure out if you are likely to "shop around" for a better deal.
	- **Price Optimization:** If the computer thinks you are too busy or don't know how to switch companies, it charges you **more** just because it can. This has nothing to do with how safe of a driver you are.,
5. *The End of Privacy: "Black Boxes" in Cars*: Many insurance companies now offer you a discount if you put a **tracking device** (a "black box") in your car.,
	- **The "Commute" Penalty:** The computer tracks where you drive. If you have to drive through a "risky" neighborhood to get to work, your rates go up.,
	- **The Privacy Tax:** The author warns that one day, privacy will be a **luxury**. Only rich people will be able to afford the "extra cost" of not being tracked by a computer.
6. *Machine "Tribes"*: A company called **Sense Networks** used cell phone data to track where people went.
	- **Mystery Groups:** The computer sorted people into "tribes" based on their movements. Humans couldn't even explain what the groups were—maybe one group all went to the same hospital at night.,
	- **Black Box Decisions:** This is scary because the computer puts you into a group (like "likely to get sick") and treats you differently, but **never tells you why**.,
7. *Health at Work: "Wellness" Programs*: Because health care is expensive, many bosses now use **"Wellness Programs"** to track your body.,
	- **HealthPoints:** One professor, Aaron Abrams, was told he had to log into a website and earn "points" by proving he went to the doctor or walked enough steps. If he didn't, he had to pay **$50 more** every month.
	- **Corporate Control:** This gives companies power over your body. They are essentially saying, "We will pay you less if you don't let us track your habits.",
8. *The "BMI" Mistake*: Many companies use a number called **BMI** (Body Mass Index) to decide if you are "healthy.",
	- **Mathematical "Snake Oil":** BMI was invented 200 years ago based on an "average man." It doesn't work for individuals.
	- **The LeBron James Problem:** Because muscle weighs more than fat, a super-athlete like LeBron James would be labeled "overweight" by this simple math.
	- **The Injustice:** This math mostly punishes women and people of color. Companies use it to take money out of workers' paychecks through "penalties.",
> [!NOTE] Conclusion: Why it's a WMD
> - The author concludes that these insurance models are **Weapons of Math Destruction** because:
> 	1. **Opacity:** You don't know what "tribe" the computer put you in.
> 	2. **Damage:** It makes life more expensive for poor people while giving the rich "discounts.",
> 	3. **Scale:** These programs are being used on millions of workers and drivers at once.
> 	In the end, she calls these wellness programs a form of **"wage theft"** hidden behind fancy health talk.
# Extra Readings
## Digital equity terms (NDIA-style definitions, condensed)
> [!TIP] How to not mix these up
> - **Divide** = the gap (who is left out)
> - **Equity** = the end state (everyone can fully participate)
> - **Inclusion** = the work (programs + support that gets us there)
### Broadband adoption
- **Meaning (field use):** Not just “subscription,” but *reliable everyday access* that supports common tasks.
- **What it requires:** adequate speed/quality + device + skills + secure/convenient network.
### Digital divide
- **Meaning:** The gap between people who have *affordable access + skills + support* and those who do not.
- **Common pattern:** hits certain groups harder (race/ethnicity, disability, rural, older adults, low-income households).
### Digital equity
- **Meaning:** A condition where everyone has the tech capacity needed for full participation in society/democracy/economy.
- **What it enables:** civic participation, culture, employment, learning, access to services.
### Digital inclusion
- **Meaning:** The activities needed to ensure access + effective use of ICTs.
- **5 elements:** (1) affordable robust internet, (2) appropriate devices, (3) digital skills training, (4) quality tech support, (5) accessible apps/content that support independence and participation.
### Digital inclusion ecosystem
- **Meaning:** Programs + policies in a community that cover *all parts* of the divide (internet, devices, skills, support, navigation) and coordinate across organizations.
- **Signs it’s strong:** multiple service options, multilingual skills supports, tech help, digital navigation, and shared planning with the community.
### Digital literacy (and community language)
- **Meaning (ALA-style):** Ability to find, evaluate, create, and communicate information using digital tools (cognitive + technical).
- **Community practice note:** “digital skills” or “beginner computer training” can land better than “literacy/illiteracy” language.
### Digital navigators
- **Meaning:** Trusted guides who give ongoing, individualized help with connectivity, devices, and skills.
### Digital redlining
- **Meaning:** Discriminatory infrastructure/service decisions by ISPs (deployment, upgrades, maintenance) that disproportionately harm certain areas (often by income/race/ethnicity).
## Issues and Challenges Related to Digital Equity (Overview notes)
> [!NOTE] The big claim
> Digital equity is **more than devices + connectivity**; it includes content, participation, educator capacity, and research support. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}
### Conceptual framework: “levels” of divides → knowledge divide
- **First-level divide:** basic access to infrastructure/devices.
- **Second-level divide:** differences in skills, usage patterns, and who produces vs consumes content.
- **Third-level divide:** differences in *returns* from internet use (two people may “use the internet” similarly, but one gets far more benefits).
- Result: the gap can become a **knowledge divide** as information and learning benefits concentrate.
### Two focus areas
- **Digital equity for social inclusion:** digital exclusion compounds broader exclusion; literacy, disability supports, and structural barriers matter.
- **Digital equity in education:** tech can support learning, but impact depends on systems (content, teacher support, and context).
### Five dimensions of digital equity (use these as your checklist)
1. **Hardware/software/connectivity**  
2. **Meaningful, high-quality, culturally relevant content in local languages**  
3. **Creating, sharing, exchanging digital content**  
4. **Educators who can use digital tools well**  
5. **High-quality research on how digital tech supports learning**
### Access to hardware, software, connectivity
- **Issues:** geography shapes access; infrastructure may exist unevenly; connectivity and reliability vary.
- **Challenges:** affordability + capacity limits as audio/video use grows; bandwidth bottlenecks show up even in well-resourced schools. 
- **Strategy idea:** planning + leadership matter; “tech leadership” is a persistent challenge even after early adoption.
### Access to meaningful, high-quality, culturally relevant content (local languages)
- **Issue:** availability of quality content (especially local-language content) is uneven.
- **Informed strategy:** use OER + open licensing + open source tools to support community empowerment and local adaptation.
- **Concrete example from the reading:** indigenous communities using tech for “digital repatriation” and culturally responsive learning materials.
### Access to creating, sharing, exchanging digital content
- **Issue:** participation is not just consuming—equity includes opportunities to create and share knowledge.
- **Challenge:** content creation requires trust in learners’ capability and supportive learning cultures.
- **Informed strategy:** treat knowledge creation as a right of teachers and students; use tools to preserve local languages and elevate local knowledge.
### Access to educators who can use digital tools effectively
- **Issue:** “digital use divide” shows up when tech is used to replicate old instruction rather than transform learning.
- **Challenge:** scaling beyond a small set of early adopters requires support and leadership.
- **Informed strategy:** sustained professional development, communities of practice, and teacher networks (online + onsite).
### Access to high-quality research on digital learning
- **Issue:** decision-makers want evidence, but “gold standard” approaches can still mislead if implementation factors (training/support) are ignored.
- **Challenge:** we need research methods that fit complex learning environments; design research supports iterative improvement with teachers and researchers together.
