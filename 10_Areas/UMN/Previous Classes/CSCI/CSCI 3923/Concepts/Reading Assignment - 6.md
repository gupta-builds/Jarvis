# Chapter - 10
Chapter 10 of _Weapons of Math Destruction_, titled **"THE TARGETED CITIZEN: Civic Life,"** explores how big tech companies and politicians use secret math to change how we think, how we feel, and how we vote.

Here is a detailed breakdown of everything in the chapter, explained for a beginner:

1. The Facebook "News Feed" is a Gatekeeper

When you post something on Facebook, you might think all your friends see it. But the author explains that a computer program (an **algorithm**) decides who sees what based on what it thinks will get the most "clicks".

- **The Power:** Because so many people get their news from Facebook, the company has the power to "game" or trick the political system by choosing which news stories to show people.
- **The Experiment:** Facebook once showed more "hard news" to 2 million people and found it actually made them go out and vote more.

2. The "Voter Megaphone" (Peer Pressure)

Facebook used a tool called a "voter megaphone" to encourage people to vote.

- **The Trick:** They showed users a button that said "I Voted," along with photos of their friends who had already clicked it.
- **The Result:** Seeing your friends vote makes you want to do it, too. This "peer pressure" from a computer increased the number of voters by 340,000 people.

3. Playing with Your Emotions

In 2012, Facebook did a "mood" experiment on 680,000 users without telling them.

- **Positive vs. Negative:** They showed some people happy posts and others sad posts.
- **The "Contagion":** They found that emotions are "contagious." People who saw sad posts started writing sad things themselves. This is scary because it means a computer can change your mood without you knowing it.

4. The "Wizard of Oz" Problem (Opacity)

The author compares Facebook to the **Wizard of Oz** because users can't see the "man behind the curtain".

- **Invisible Rules:** Most people (62%) don't even know that a computer is picking what they see; they think it’s all just "natural".
- **Search Engines:** We trust Google to be fair, but if Google changed its search results to favor one politician, it could change the minds of 20% of undecided voters.

5. Microtargeting: The "Many Faces" of a Politician

**Microtargeting** is when a politician sends a very specific message to a tiny group of people so that no one else sees it.

- **Multiplicity:** This allows a politician to be "many things to many people." They can tell one group they love the environment and tell another group they love big factories, and the two groups might never find out they are being told different things.
- **The "47%" Gaffe:** Mitt Romney once got in trouble because he was caught on a secret camera saying something to rich donors that he would never say to the general public. This showed why politicians love secret "safe zones" where they can target specific people.

6. The Supermarket Model

Rayid Ghani, a data scientist for Barack Obama, used his experience modeling **supermarket shoppers** to find voters.

- **Swing Voters = Brand Switchers:** In a store, some people always buy the same ketchup, but others switch to whatever is on sale (the "persuadables").
- **Bucketing:** Ghani put voters into "buckets" (groups) based on their habits. He then sent coupons—or political ads—specifically to the people most likely to change their minds.

7. Confirmation Bias

This is a "glitch" in the human brain where we only believe information that fits what we already think is true. Politicians use microtargeting to feed people's **confirmation bias**, making them more certain of their own side and more angry at the other side.

8. Psychographic Profiles (Cambridge Analytica)

The chapter discusses how some groups paid to get data from Facebook "likes" to build **psychographic profiles**.

- **The Big Five:** They ranked 40 million voters on personality traits like how "open" or "anxious" they were.
- **Personalized Fear:** They used these profiles to send "ideological bombs" or scary ads about immigration to people they knew were easily frightened, while hiding those ads from people who would be disgusted by them.

9. Asymmetry of Information

This is a big word that means one side knows everything and the other side knows nothing.

- **The Imbalance:** Politicians have "deep dossiers" (huge files) of data on us, including our social security numbers and what we buy. But we are "kept in the dark" about what our neighbors are being told. This makes it hard for people to join forces and talk to each other.

10. The Death of Democracy in "Safe" States

Because campaigns want the most "bang for their buck," they ignore almost everyone except a few people in "swing states" like Florida or Ohio.

- **Disenfranchisement:** If you live in a state that always votes for one party, the computer predicts you won't change your mind, so the politicians ignore you. This creates a "feedback loop" where ignored voters get bored and stop caring about politics.

11. Conclusion: Disarming the WMD

The author says the problem is the **objective** (the goal) of the math. Currently, the goal is to "leech" off people and manipulate them.

- **The Solution:** We could change the goal to **helping people**. Instead of using math to scare people about high rent, we could use it to find people who need affordable housing and help them find a place to live. If we change the goal, the "Weapon" becomes a tool for good.

### A) IEA — _Energy Technology Perspectives 2024_ (ETP 2024)

**What the report is doing**

- Frames the clean-tech transition as a combined **energy + industry + trade** challenge: scaling deployment while managing supply chain concentration and trade dependencies.
    

**Key quantitative claims (high-value evidence)**

- Market for six mass-manufactured clean technologies (solar PV, wind, EVs, batteries, electrolysers, heat pumps) has grown sharply; IEA highlights **~$700B in 2023** and **> $2T by 2035** under current policies.
    
- Clean technology trade is substantial and growing; IEA projects overall clean tech trade to reach **$575B by 2035** under today’s policies.
    

**How this connects to your midterm theme**

- ETP gives you the “systems” spine: scaling tech changes material demand (minerals), grid demand (electricity), and later waste streams (e-waste). It helps justify why ethics is not just about the device or the app, but the full lifecycle.

### E) PBS (2016) — _Where does America’s e-waste end up? GPS tracker tells all_

The PBS page is accessible and you pasted the full text.

**Method**

- BAN + MIT Senseable City Lab placed **200 GPS trackers** inside devices dropped at recyclers/donation/takeback programs marketed as responsible.
    

**Findings**

- About **one-third** of tracked items went overseas, reaching countries including Mexico, Taiwan, China, Pakistan, Thailand, Dominican Republic, Canada, Kenya; often to **Hong Kong’s New Territories** (your pasted PBS text; PBS snippet confirms “about a third”).
    
- On-the-ground harms: dismantling LCDs with broken fluorescent tubes releasing mercury vapor; workers without PPE; toner dust exposure; junkyard fires producing toxic smoke; mentions dioxins as known products of burning e-waste.
    

**Governance/market dynamics**

- US is described as not having ratified Basel Convention (in the PBS piece).
    
- Patchwork state laws; lack of federal export ban; certification/audits not sufficient.
    
- A key twist: even “reputable” or certified channels exported; Total Reclaim (e-Stewards certified) suspended; Dell Reconnect pathway saw overseas travel in BAN’s tracker sample (all from your PBS text).
    

**Why this matters**

- It supports a strong claim: “consumer intent + green marketing” is not reliable governance; verification and enforcement matter.
