# Entire Week
## What you must be able to do
- Explain the full lifecycle of a device: **extract → manufacture → use → dispose**, and why the biggest harms often occur *before* you ever turn it on.
- Convert and interpret energy scale terms used in lecture (**W, kW, MW, GW, TW**) and connect them to tech infrastructure (homes, wind turbines, nuclear plants, global use).
- Describe why **data centers** are a major and growing electricity load, and why the growth is being pushed by AI.
- Explain why **end-user devices** (phones, laptops, wearables, IoT) can create more emissions than data centers, and why “upgrading faster” matters ethically.
- Identify the two major environmental “inputs” for AI + cloud systems:
	- **carbon footprint** (electricity generation)
	- **water footprint** (cooling, especially in arid regions)
- Define e-waste as a global justice issue: who generates the most per person vs who bears the processing burden.
- List the key facts about e-waste: scale, recycling rate, where it goes, and the major health/environment risks.
- Explain what makes e-waste dangerous (heavy metals, toxic chemicals, battery fires, refrigerants).
- Identify what’s inside a smartphone (major materials) and the ethical issues tied to extraction (labor conditions, pollution, water depletion, indigenous rights, conflict minerals).
- Define **planned obsolescence**, recognize common patterns (software end-of-life, parts pairing, glued components), and explain why it increases waste.
- Explain the **Right to Repair** argument and the core policy levers used to support it (repairability labeling, parts availability, manuals, removable batteries, bans on forced obsolescence).
- Compare and evaluate the main global approaches to e-waste governance (Basel Convention, E-Stewards certification, EU WEEE directive) including their limits.
- Use a “circular economy” lens to propose changes at 3 levels: **individual**, **corporate**, **policy**.

## Key ideas (readings)
- **Chapter 8 (Landing Credit):**
	- Why older lending could be unfair: face-to-face judgment baked in bias.
	- Why FICO can behave like a “healthier” model: clearer inputs + feedback + a path to improve.
	- What makes modern “e-scores” dangerous: secret scoring using proxies (location, browsing, purchasing behavior).
	- “Bucketing” problem: you get judged by “people like you,” so neighborhood becomes destiny.
	- Poverty trap: credit checks can block jobs → less income → worse credit → harder to recover.
	- Error problem: identity mix-ups can wreck opportunities, and there aren’t enough “humans in the loop” to fix everything.
	- “Digital phrenology”: using weak behavioral signals as if they reveal character.
- **Chapter 9 (Getting Insurance):**
	- Insurance originally worked by **risk pooling** (shared risk); big data shifts it toward individualized pricing.
	- Historical warning: “uninsurable” claims were built on racism + correlation mistaken as causation.
	- Credit score trap: financial proxies can outweigh safety behavior (driving record).
	- Price optimization: the model predicts who will shop around, then charges more if it thinks you won’t.
	- Tracking/black boxes: privacy becomes something only some people can afford.
	- “Tribes” and black-box grouping: you can be sorted into categories no one can explain.
	- Wellness programs: employer control can become coercive, using metrics like BMI that don’t fit real bodies.
	- Why it’s WMD-like: opacity + scale + harm concentrated on people with less power.
- **Extra readings (digital equity + inclusion terms):**
	- Keep the definitions distinct:
		- **Digital divide** = the gap in access/skills/support.
		- **Digital equity** = the end condition (full participation possible).
		- **Digital inclusion** = the work (broadband, devices, skills, support, accessible design).
	- “Issues and Challenges” overview:
		- Digital equity includes more than connectivity: content, participation, educator capacity, and research.
		- Three levels of divides: access → skills/usage + producer/consumer gap → unequal “returns” from similar use.
		- Two focus areas: social inclusion and education.
		- Five dimensions checklist: hardware/connectivity; local-language meaningful content; creating/sharing content; educator capability; strong research evidence.

- **Section 1: Understanding energy + data centers**
	- Energy scale: W → kW → MW → GW → TW (used to reason about “small per action, huge at scale”).
	- Global electricity consumption distribution: large regional differences.
	- Data centers as a growing load, pushed by AI demand.
	- AI footprint framing:
		- Carbon emissions scale is large when usage scales.
		- Water usage matters because cooling is physical and location-specific.
	- Key tension emphasized: net-zero pledges vs rising infrastructure demand.
- **End-uses devices are the “hidden majority”**
	- Main idea: devices people buy/replace can create more emissions than data centers.
	- Manufacturing dominates a phone’s lifetime footprint more than daily charging does.
	- Ethics angle: “upgrade cycles” are environmental policy, even if no one calls them that.
- **Data centers as a geographic problem**
	- Concentration effects: some regions carry disproportionate electricity load.
	- Cooling strategies: location choices shift emissions and water burden.
	- Local impacts: electricity prices and resource competition can shift onto residents.
- **Section 2: E-waste**
> [!NOTE] The World's Fastest-Growing Waste Stream
- Core numbers: e-waste is rising quickly; formal recycling is a small fraction.
	- Device mix: “small stuff” (phones, earbuds, vapes, trackers) is a huge share and often not collected.
	- Where it goes: much is untracked; informal processing concentrates harms.
	- Health harms: heavy metals + toxic chemicals → long-term respiratory/neurological impacts; children are especially vulnerable.
	- Environmental harms: soil/water contamination; battery fires; refrigerants contribute to climate impact.
	- Inequality framing: higher per-capita generation in wealthy countries vs processing burden elsewhere.
- **Section 3: Raw materials inside devices**
	- Smartphones contain many elements; extraction ties devices to global supply chains.
	- Key materials and issues:
		- Cobalt: hazardous mining conditions; traceability challenges.
		- Copper: pollution risks + displacement concerns; global demand pressure.
		- Lithium: water depletion concerns in arid regions.
		- Rare earths: toxic waste from processing + geopolitical control.
		- Tantalum: conflict mineral risk.
	- Ethics point: the true human/environmental costs are not visible at purchase time.
- **Section 4: Planned obsolescence + right to repair**
	- Planned obsolescence definition: designing products to become obsolete on a schedule.
	- Timeline highlights:
		- annual model changes → modern software throttling/compatibility cutoffs → policy responses.
	- Case patterns:
		- software updates that slow older devices,
		- sealed/glued components,
		- proprietary screws and restricted parts,
		- support expiration dates (especially in schools).
	- IoT disposability problem:
		- short lifespans + sealed batteries + cloud dependency = replacement becomes the default.
> [!IMPORTANT] “We shouldn't tolerate disposable electronics or unnecessary 'smart' products.”
- **Global + policy responses**
	- Right to Repair movement:
		- laws and labeling; parts/manual access; bans on some anti-repair tactics.
	- International approaches to e-waste:
		- Basel Convention (limits dumping; enforcement challenges)
		- E-Stewards certification (strong standards; voluntary)
		- EU WEEE directive (manufacturer responsibility; collection targets still hard to meet)
- **Circular economy**
	- Linear model: Extract → Manufacture → Use → Dispose
	- Circular model: Design for longevity → repair/refurbish → reuse/resell → recycle materials
	- “What can we do?” actions grouped by:
		- individual (extend lifespan, refurbish, reduce IoT clutter, recycle properly)
		- corporate (repairability, longer support, take-back, supply chain transparency)
		- policy (repair rights, obsolescence bans, recycling targets, enforcement)

7. How does a circular economy change the device lifecycle?::It shifts from “use then discard” to longevity, repair/refurbish, reuse/resell, and materials recycling.
