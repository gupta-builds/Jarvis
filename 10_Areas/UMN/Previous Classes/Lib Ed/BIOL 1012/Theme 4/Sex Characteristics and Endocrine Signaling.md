# Sex Characteristics and Endocrine Signaling

## Exam Objectives Covered
- SG1-SG2: identify reproductive structures and basic functions.
- SG3-SG4: trace sperm and ova paths.
- SG5: match structures from common embryonic precursors.
- SG6: separate gender from sex characteristics.
- SG7: explain chromosomes, genes, and hormones in development.
- SG8: explain hormone roles in intersex development and possible phenotypes.
- EN1-EN3: define hormone/endocrine system and identify stimulus, source, target, receptor, response.

## 30-Second Version
Sex characteristics are biological traits like chromosomes, gonads, hormones, internal anatomy, and external anatomy. Gender is a person's identity/social category, not the same thing as anatomy or chromosomes.

Early embryos start with **bipotential/indifferent structures**. After about 6 weeks, genes and hormones push development toward different reproductive structures. The key exam mechanism is not "XX = always female anatomy" or "XY = always male anatomy." The actual pathway depends on genes, hormone production, and whether target cells have working receptors.

## Sex Characteristics Gender and Orientation
- **Sex characteristics**: genes, chromosomes, hormones, gonads, internal reproductive anatomy, external reproductive anatomy, and traits that appear at puberty.
- **Sex assigned at birth**: usually based on external anatomy and recorded as male/female.
- **Gender identity**: a person's deeply held sense of being a man, woman, both, neither, or another identity in their culture.
- **Cisgender**: gender identity matches cultural expectations of sex assigned at birth.
- **Transgender**: gender identity and/or expression differs from cultural expectations of sex assigned at birth.
- **Sexual orientation**: who someone is attracted to. It is separate from sex characteristics and gender identity.

Exam trap: do not use chromosomes, anatomy, gender identity, and sexual orientation as if they are the same variable.

## Chromosomes Genes and Hormones
### Karyotype basics
- **Karyotype**: arrangement of the chromosomes in a cell so homologous chromosomes are next to each other.
- Humans usually have **23 pairs** of chromosomes.
- **Homologous chromosomes**: pairs with the same genes in the same locations; one chromosome comes from each parent.
- Most body cells are **diploid**: two copies of each chromosome.
- Ova contribute an X chromosome.
- Sperm contribute either an X or a Y chromosome.

### Chromosomal sex is not the whole story
- Typical XX: chromosomally female.
- Typical XY: chromosomally male.
- Other combinations exist: X, XXY, XXX, mosaicism, or chromosome rearrangements.
- A Y chromosome usually matters because it can carry **SRY**, a gene involved in initiating testes development.

### Developmental logic
1. Embryo starts with bipotential gonads and bipotential genital structures.
2. If SRY-driven signaling leads to testes development, testes can release hormones such as testosterone and **AMH (Anti-Müllerian Hormone)**.
3. **AMH** causes the Müllerian ducts (which would become fallopian tubes, uterus, upper vagina) to regress/degenerate.
4. **Testosterone** promotes development of the Wolffian ducts into epididymis, vas deferens, and seminal vesicles.
5. **DHT (dihydrotestosterone)**, converted from testosterone, drives external male genital development.
6. Hormones bind receptors in target tissues.
7. Target tissues change development.
8. If hormone is missing, receptor is missing, or receptor does not respond, development can differ from the typical XY pattern.
9. Without AMH and testosterone signaling (typical XX pathway): Müllerian ducts develop into fallopian tubes/uterus/upper vagina; Wolffian ducts degenerate; external genitals develop as clitoris/labia.

## Reproductive Anatomy
### Structures involved in sperm production and transport
| Structure | Basic function |
|---|---|
| Testes | Gonads that produce sperm and testosterone. |
| Seminiferous tubules | Site where spermatogenesis occurs. |
| Spermatogonia | Diploid germ cells that can produce sperm through meiosis. |
| Sertoli cells | Support sperm development; respond to FSH. |
| Leydig cells | Produce testosterone; respond to LH. |
| Epididymis | Sperm mature and are stored. |
| Vas deferens | Transports sperm toward ejaculatory duct. |
| Ejaculatory duct | Connects vas deferens path into urethra. |
| Urethra | Passageway through penis for semen and urine, not at the same time. |
| Penis | External structure that delivers semen. |
| Scrotum | Holds testes outside body cavity; helps maintain temperature for sperm production. |
| Prostate gland | Adds fluid to semen. |

### Structures involved in ova production, transport, and pregnancy
| Structure | Basic function |
|---|---|
| Ovary | Gonad that produces ova and ovarian hormones. |
| Follicle | Structure around developing oocyte; secretes estrogen during follicular phase. |
| Corpus luteum | Forms from follicle after ovulation; secretes progesterone and estrogen. |
| Fimbriae | Fingerlike ends that help sweep ovulated oocyte into fallopian tube. |
| Fallopian tube / oviduct | Oocyte transport; usual site of fertilization. |
| Uterus | Organ where embryo/fetus develops during pregnancy. |
| Endometrium | Inner uterine lining; thickens and is shed during menses if pregnancy does not occur. |
| Myometrium | Smooth muscle layer of uterus; contracts during childbirth. |
| Cervix | Narrow opening between uterus and vagina. |
| Cervical orifice | Opening of cervix. |
| Vagina | Canal receiving sperm and route for menstrual fluid/birth. |
| Labia majora | External folds protecting genital opening. |
| Labia minora | Inner external folds. |
| Clitoris | External erectile structure. |

## Sperm and Ova Paths
### Sperm path: SEVEN-UP
Use the lecture/practice memory anchor:

1. **S**eminiferous tubules
2. **E**pididymis
3. **V**as deferens
4. **E**jaculatory duct
5. **N**othing / no structure here; memory anchor spacer
6. **U**rethra
7. **P**enis

Exam answer version: **seminiferous tubules -> epididymis -> vas deferens -> ejaculatory duct -> urethra -> penis**.

### Ovulated ova path if not fertilized
**Ovary -> fallopian tube -> uterus -> cervix -> vagina -> vaginal opening**.

Important: an ovulated oocyte is released from the ovary, enters the fallopian tube, and if not fertilized eventually leaves with menstrual fluid after the uterine lining is shed.

## Embryonic Homologues
Homologues are structures that develop from the same embryonic precursor.

| Embryonic precursor | Typical pathway with testes/androgen signaling | Typical pathway without that signaling |
|---|---|---|
| Indifferent gonad | Testes | Ovaries |
| Wolffian ducts (mesonephric) | Epididymis, vas deferens, seminal vesicles (maintained by testosterone) | Degenerate (no testosterone to maintain them) |
| Müllerian ducts (paramesonephric) | Degenerate (AMH from Sertoli cells causes regression) | Fallopian tubes, uterus, cervix, upper vagina |
| Genital tubercle | Penis (requires DHT) | Clitoris |
| Labioscrotal swellings | Scrotum (fuses in midline under androgen influence) | Labia majora |
| Urogenital folds | Ventral penis/urethra | Labia minora |

**Key mechanism:** Both duct systems exist in early embryos. Which one persists depends on hormonal signals:
- AMH present → Müllerian ducts degenerate
- Testosterone present → Wolffian ducts are maintained and develop
- Neither AMH nor testosterone → Müllerian ducts develop, Wolffian ducts degenerate

Exam trap: "same precursor" does not mean "same adult function." It means the embryo had a structure that could develop down more than one path.

## Primary vs Secondary Sex Characteristics
- **Primary sex characteristics**: reproductive structures present at birth or directly involved in reproduction.
  - Examples: testes, ovaries, penis, uterus, fallopian tubes, vagina.
- **Secondary sex characteristics**: traits that develop at puberty and are not required for reproduction itself.
  - Examples often emphasized in lecture: breast development, enlarged hips, facial/body hair, voice changes, increased muscle growth.

Hormone point: all adults have some estrogen, progesterone, and testosterone. The difference is usually relative level and tissue response, not total absence.

## Intersex Development
**Intersex** describes differences in sex traits or reproductive anatomy that do not fit the expected typical male/female pattern for chromosomes, gonads, hormones, internal structures, or external genitals.

### Mechanism to know: XY embryo with female-like external genitals
Typical XY development requires:
- testosterone or androgen production
- functional androgen/testosterone receptors
- target tissues that can respond

If testosterone is not produced, or if the androgen receptor does not respond, external genital development may be female-like even with XY chromosomes.

### Mechanism to know: XX embryo with intersex external genitals
Typical XX development usually involves very low testosterone.

If testosterone/androgen levels are intermediate or high during development, external genitals may develop in an intersex pattern. Lecture example: **congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH)** can reduce cortisol/aldosterone production and shift hormone precursors toward androgen/testosterone production.

Exam trap: testosterone level alone is not enough. Hormone signaling requires both **hormone** and **receptor**.

## Endocrine Signaling
### Definitions
- **Hormone**: chemical messenger secreted by an endocrine cell/gland and carried through blood.
- **Endocrine system**: glands/cells that produce hormones to regulate body processes.
- **Target cell/organ**: cell or organ with the receptor needed to respond to a hormone.
- **Receptor**: protein on or in a cell that binds a specific hormone.

### Pathway components
| Component | What to ask |
|---|---|
| Stimulus | What signal causes hormone release? |
| Source | What endocrine gland/cell secretes the hormone? |
| Hormone | What chemical messenger is released? |
| Target | What cell/organ has the receptor? |
| Receptor | What binds the hormone? |
| Response/action | What changes after hormone binding? |

### Why hormones are specific
Hormones travel widely in blood, but they only affect cells with the right receptor. A cell without the receptor is not a target for that hormone.

### Steroid vs non-steroid hormone logic
- **Non-steroid hormones** often bind receptors on the cell surface and trigger internal signaling cascades.
  - Lecture example: adrenaline increases heart rate through cell-surface signaling.
- **Steroid hormones** can diffuse through the cell membrane, bind internal receptors, and change gene activity.
  - Lecture examples: cortisol, testosterone, estrogen, progesterone.

## Exam-Style Applications
- If a passage gives "blood glucose is low; alpha cells release glucagon; liver breaks down glycogen," then:
  - stimulus = low blood glucose
  - source = pancreatic alpha cells
  - hormone = glucagon
  - target = liver cells
  - response = glycogen breakdown and glucose release
- If an XY fetus makes testosterone but target tissues lack androgen receptors, male external genital development may not occur.
- If an XX fetus has unusually high androgen exposure, external genitals may develop in an intersex pattern.
