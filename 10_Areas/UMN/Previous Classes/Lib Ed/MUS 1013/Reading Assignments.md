Instead of just notes, he layered growls, shrieks, split tones. Overblowing let him bend reality. Multiphonics gave depth, like hearing two thoughts at once. Expression came first, always. Technique served feeling, never the reverse. America shaped his roots, though music carried him far beyond borders. From the beginning, music took him through local R&B gigs on saxophone. Oakland came next in 1959, pulling him west. By 1962, New York had drawn him in - jazz now his main aim. A shift came in the mid-1960s when he began playing alongside Sun Ra - Sun Ra is the one who named him “Pharoah.” After that chapter, his path led straight into John Coltrane’s ensemble, where he showed up on key albums from Coltrane’s later years. Currie points out Sanders' open-ended way of playing - spontaneous runs, piercing highs, sliding tones, plus a deep sense of purpose - that many reviewers tied to an intense, physical take on avant-garde jazz. That matches the descriptions found in well-known biographies and memorials. Albums like Tauhid and Karma - released on Impulse! around the late Sixties into the early Seventies - stand out as his most recognized work fronting bands. Few knew where it began - Little Rock gave him life in 1940. Death arrived much later, far west in Los Angeles, nearly eight decades after the start. The city lights faded just around 2022.
