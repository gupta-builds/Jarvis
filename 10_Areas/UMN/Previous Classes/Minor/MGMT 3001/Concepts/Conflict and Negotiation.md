# Conflict and Negotiation

## ==Definition==
- Conflict is an emotional or cognitive response when one party perceives that another party's interests, perspectives, or behaviors oppose or negatively affect them.
- Negotiation is a process where two parties attempt to reach agreement by offering and reviewing positions or courses of action.

## Why it matters
- Conflict is inevitable in organizations because people have different goals, roles, incentives, identities, and resources.
- Conflict is not always bad. Task conflict can improve decisions when it stays focused on the work.
- Negotiation turns conflict into a process.

## Position vs. interest
- **Position:** what someone wants.
- **Interest:** why they want it.
- Effective conflict management asks for the interest behind the position.
- Example: "I want $75,000" is a position. Paying loans, affording rent, and feeling valued are possible interests.

## Types of conflict
- **Cognitive or task conflict:** issue-based, depersonalized disagreement about ideas, plans, or work. It can stimulate creativity and improve decision quality.
- **Affective or relational conflict:** person-based conflict rooted in anger, friction, personality tension, or threat. It interferes with attention and effort.
- Task conflict becomes dangerous when tone, timing, or trust lets it spill into relationship conflict.

## Conflict management styles
- The styles are based on assertiveness and cooperativeness.
- **Competing/forcing:** high assertiveness, low cooperativeness.
- **Collaborating/problem solving:** high assertiveness, high cooperativeness.
- **Compromising:** middle ground.
- **Avoiding:** low assertiveness, low cooperativeness.
- **Accommodating:** low assertiveness, high cooperativeness.

## Choosing a style
- Consider issue importance.
- Consider relationship importance.
- Consider relative power.
- Consider time constraints.
- No time for full problem solving can make forcing, accommodating, or avoiding more likely, but that does not make them ideal long-term.

## Managing conflict on teams
- Train emotional management skills.
- Build early cohesion or psychological safety.
- Build norms for constructive debate and open communication.
- Emphasize superordinate identity and goals.

## Distributive negotiation
- Fixed-pie logic.
- Resources are distributed.
- There is a focus on winning.
- There are limits.
- There is a bargaining zone.
- **BATNA:** best alternative to a negotiated agreement.
- **Reservation value:** walk-away point.
- **Positive bargaining zone:** acceptable positions overlap.
- **Negative bargaining zone:** no overlap, so no settlement is possible.

## Bargaining effectively
- Assess your BATNA.
- Calculate your reservation value.
- Assess the other party's BATNA.
- Estimate the other party's reservation value.
- Evaluate the zone of possible agreement.
- Identify multiple interests.
- Create a scoring system.
- Calculate a package reservation value.
- Identify the other party's multiple interests.

## Costly mistakes
- Irrational escalation of commitment.
- Anchoring and adjustment.
- Framing.
- Availability of information problems.
- Negotiator overconfidence.
- Cross-cultural misreading.

## Cross-cultural negotiation
- Culture affects positions and interests.
- Egalitarian vs. hierarchy expectations change how people treat authority.
- Low-context cultures rely more on explicit information.
- High-context cultures rely more on relationship, context, and indirect meaning.

## Course examples
- The lecture's *position vs. interest* distinction explains why "I want X" is only the surface of a conflict; the useful managerial work is finding the need, goal, value, or belief underneath.
- The conflict styles grid shows why collaborating is powerful but expensive: it requires both high assertiveness and high cooperativeness.

## Common traps
- Arguing over positions while ignoring interests.
- Treating all conflict as destructive.
- Avoiding task conflict until it becomes relationship conflict.
- Entering negotiation without BATNA or reservation value.
- Assuming your framing is neutral.

## How to apply
> [!TIP]
> Separate positions from interests before choosing a conflict style. If the issue and relationship both matter, collaborating is worth the time. If the issue is minor or timing is bad, avoiding or accommodating may be rational.
> [!WARNING]
> Common trap: negotiating without a BATNA or reservation value. Without a walk-away point, pressure and anchoring can quietly set the terms for you.
> [!SUMMARY]
> Conflict is useful when it improves the work. It becomes costly when the disagreement shifts from the task to identity, status, or personal attack.

## Entrepreneurship use
- Founders negotiate constantly: salary, equity, vendor terms, cofounder roles, customer contracts, investor rights.
- A founder should separate relationship preservation from issue clarity. You can be direct about the issue without making the person the problem.
- BATNA thinking keeps desperation from setting the terms.
