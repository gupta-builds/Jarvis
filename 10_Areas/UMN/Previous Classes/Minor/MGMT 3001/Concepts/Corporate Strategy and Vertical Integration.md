# Corporate Strategy and Vertical Integration

## ==Definition==
- **Corporate-level strategy** asks what businesses or industries the firm should be in and how those businesses should fit together.
- **Vertical integration** is ownership or control over stages of the value chain, either backward into inputs or forward toward customers.

## Why it matters
- Business-level strategy is about how to compete in one arena.
- Corporate-level strategy is about scope: where the firm plays, what it owns, what it partners for, and how units reinforce each other.
- The key test is whether the collection of businesses is worth more together than apart.

## Corporate-level options
- **Single-business strategy:** concentrate on one main business.
- **Related diversification:** enter businesses that share resources, capabilities, customers, technologies, or activities.
- **Unrelated diversification:** enter businesses with little operational connection, usually for financial economies or portfolio logic.
- **Vertical integration:** own upstream or downstream stages.
- **Partnerships and alliances:** coordinate without full ownership.
- **Internationalization:** expand across countries to reach markets, scale, resources, or location advantages.

## Diversification logic
- Diversification can create value through economies of scope, shared activities, shared brand, shared knowledge, risk spreading, or market power.
- Related diversification usually has more value potential because capabilities can transfer.
- Unrelated diversification is harder because the businesses may not reinforce each other.

## The diversification test
- **Attractiveness test:** is the target industry structurally attractive?
- **Cost-of-entry test:** does entry cost consume the expected value?
- **Better-off test:** are the businesses better together than separate?

## Vertical integration
- **Backward integration:** move toward suppliers or inputs. Example logic: secure supply, protect quality, reduce supplier dependence.
- **Forward integration:** move toward distribution or customers. Example logic: control customer experience, capture margins, improve market access.
- Integration helps when transaction costs in the market are higher than internal administrative costs.
- It hurts when ownership adds rigidity, complexity, or coordination burden.

## Alternatives to ownership
- **Outsourcing:** use an outside firm for an activity.
- **Spot contracts:** simple market transactions.
- **Strategic alliances:** ongoing collaboration without full ownership.
- **Joint ventures:** shared ownership of a new entity.
- **Mergers and acquisitions:** full or partial ownership to gain assets, capabilities, or market access.
## Intended, emergent, and realized strategy
- **Intended strategy:** what the firm planned.
- **Deliberate strategy:** the intended part that is actually executed.
- **Emergent strategy:** pattern that develops through learning and opportunity.
- **Unrealized strategy:** planned action abandoned or never executed.
- **Realized strategy:** what the firm actually does after deliberate and emergent elements combine.
## Common traps
- Assuming growth is automatically good.
- Calling unrelated diversification "synergy" without a real shared capability.
- Ignoring the cost of entry.
- Treating vertical integration as pure control without counting administrative cost.
- Dismissing emergent strategy as failure. Sometimes learning changes the right plan.
## Course examples
- McDonald's helps show single-business focus, related expansion, supplier coordination, partnerships, and international adaptation.
- Amazon shows broad diversification. The test is whether logistics, data, capital allocation, cloud infrastructure, and customer relationships create real connections or just complexity.
- ESPN illustrates emergent strategy: a narrower initial idea became a larger sports media pattern through opportunity and learning.
## How to apply
> [!TIP]
> For diversification, run the attractiveness, cost-of-entry, and better-off tests before getting excited about scope. For vertical integration, compare transaction costs with administrative costs before deciding to own the activity.

> [!WARNING]
> Common trap: assuming bigger means stronger. The textbook treats scope as a value-creation question, not an empire-building question.

> [!SUMMARY]
> Corporate strategy is about boundaries: what to own, what to partner for, what to outsource, and where ownership creates real advantage.
## Entrepreneurship use
- Founders should delay diversification until the first business has a clear advantage.
- Before building in-house, ask whether owning the activity really improves quality, speed, data, customer experience, or margins enough to justify complexity.
- Partnerships can be the founder's friend: they give speed and access while preserving focus.
