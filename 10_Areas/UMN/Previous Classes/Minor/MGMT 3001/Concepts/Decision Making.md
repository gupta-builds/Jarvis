# Decision Making

## ==Definition==
- Decision making is the process of identifying a problem or opportunity, evaluating criteria and alternatives, and choosing a course of action.
- The course contrasts the rational model with how people actually decide under limited information, limited time, emotions, social pressure, and bias.

## Rational decision-making process
1. Define the problem.
2. Identify decision criteria.
3. Weight the criteria.
4. Generate alternative courses of action.
5. Evaluate each alternative.
6. Compute the optimal decision.

## Assumptions of rational decision making
- Problem clarity.
- Clear and constant preferences.
- Known options.
- No time constraints.
- Maximum payoff.

## Limits of rational decision making
- Information can be incomplete, imperfect, or misleading.
- People have limited background or ability to process all information.
- Time is limited.
- Stakeholders may have conflicting preferences, incentives, or goals.

## Bounded rationality and satisficing
- **Bounded rationality:** rationality is limited by information, cognition, and time.
- **Satisficing:** choosing a solution that is good enough rather than perfectly optimal.
- This does not mean managers are lazy. It means the real world rarely gives perfect information and unlimited time.

## Heuristics and biases
- **Heuristic:** mental shortcut used to decide quickly.
- **Availability bias:** recent, vivid, or emotional examples feel more likely or important.
- **Representativeness bias:** judging by fit with a stereotype or category.
- **Anchoring and adjustment:** starting point pulls later judgment toward it.
- **Confirmation bias:** seeking or valuing evidence that supports what you already think.
- **Escalation of commitment:** continuing a failing course because of prior investment.
- **Status quo bias:** preferring the current state because change feels risky.
- **Framing:** the way a choice is presented changes how people evaluate it.

## Emotional and social side
- The lecture's gut-reaction exercises are not random. They show how quickly judgment happens before evidence is reviewed.
- Bias is not only individual. Groups can amplify bias through hierarchy, conformity, and the desire to avoid conflict.
- A good decision process slows down the moments where intuition is likely to outrun evidence.

## How to improve decisions
- Write the problem before arguing over solutions.
- Separate criteria from alternatives.
- Weight criteria before seeing which option "wins."
- Ask what evidence would change your mind.
- Make sunk costs visible.
- Seek disconfirming information.
- Use independent first judgments before group discussion.
- Reframe the same choice as gains, losses, risks, and opportunity costs.

## Common traps
- Treating the rational model as a description of how people naturally decide.
- Treating bias as something only other people have.
- Ignoring criteria weights.
- Picking the first acceptable solution without noticing what was sacrificed.
- Letting the loudest person set the anchor.

## Course examples
- Choosing where to go to college is used to walk through problem definition, criteria, weights, alternatives, evaluation, and optimal choice.
- Fear of flying after a vivid crash example demonstrates availability bias.
- Salary negotiation demonstrates anchoring.
- Hiring a person who "looks the part" demonstrates representativeness bias.

## How to apply
> [!TIP]
> Name the decision condition first: certainty, risk, uncertainty, or ambiguity. Then choose a process: rational analysis for clear problems, experiments for uncertainty, outside view for bias, and coalition-building when stakeholders have competing goals.
> [!WARNING]
> Common trap: using the rational model as if managers have perfect time, information, and agreement. Chapter 15 says bounded rationality is the normal condition.
> [!SUMMARY]
> The rational process gives discipline; bounded rationality explains reality. Better decisions come from using structure while actively checking bias, emotion, and politics.

## Entrepreneurship use
- A founder makes decisions under extreme uncertainty, so bounded rationality is normal.
- Use fast experiments to improve information rather than pretending you can compute the perfect answer.
- Watch escalation of commitment: a bad product, bad hire, or bad market choice becomes harder to quit when identity is attached to it.
