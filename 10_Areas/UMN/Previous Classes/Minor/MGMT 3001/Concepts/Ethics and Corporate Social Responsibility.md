# Ethics and Corporate Social Responsibility

## ==Definition==
- Ethics is the study of moral standards used to judge right and wrong.
- Morality is the set of standards people use to judge right/wrong or good/evil.
- Corporate social responsibility (CSR) is a business's obligation to pursue policies, decisions, and actions that align with the objectives and values of society.

## Why it matters
- The lecture treats ethics as foundational to both reputation and business success.
- Law is not enough. Some legal actions can be unethical, and some ethical responsibilities appear before law catches up.
- Ethical failures can destroy credibility, morale, productivity, finances, and leader legitimacy.

## Ethical considerations in business
- Human rights.
- Environmental and sustainability concerns.
- Anti-corruption.
- Labor laws.

## Ethical frameworks
### Utilitarianism
- Moral behavior produces the greatest good for the greatest number.
- Uses cost-benefit thinking.
- Problems: can justify harmful means, assumes future outcomes can be predicted, treats happiness as measurable, and can let the majority override minority rights.

### Kantianism
- Motives and universal rules matter.
- Asks whether the action respects duty and could be applied as a general rule.
- Problems: hard to apply in messy cases, leaves little room for emotion, and depends heavily on rational decision making.

### Virtue and character
- Morality develops virtuous character.
- Character includes recognizing moral features, making moral judgments, acting consistently, and teaching character to others.
- Problems: cultures differ on virtues, it lacks a clear step-by-step conflict process, and it focuses heavily on the decision maker.

### Justice approach
- Moral behavior is based on equity, fairness, and impartiality.
- **Distributive justice:** treatment and outcomes are distributed fairly.
- **Procedural justice:** fair processes and procedures.
- Problems: equal treatment may not meet unequal needs, and not every situation is equal in reality.

## Triple bottom line
- Profit.
- People.
- Planet.
- The point is that business decisions should be judged beyond short-term money when social and environmental consequences are part of the decision.

## CSR and advantage
- CSR can support brand, trust, employee commitment, customer loyalty, and access to stakeholders.
- CSR must align with strategy. Unfocused CSR can become symbolic, costly, or disconnected from real business behavior.

## Common traps
- Treating ethics as only legal compliance.
- Treating CSR as public relations.
- Using utilitarian math to erase rights or justice concerns.
- Assuming everyone shares the same virtue definition.
- Ignoring global/cultural differences in ethical expectations.

## Course examples
- Shell and the Ogoni people illustrate human-rights concerns.
- Volkswagen emissions scandal illustrates environmental and legal/ethical failure.
- ImClone illustrates anti-corruption and insider trading concerns.
- Labor law examples show that employment practices are ethical as well as legal issues.
- Lego and Starbucks are used as CSR/triple-bottom-line examples.

## How to apply
> [!TIP]
> Run important decisions through all four lenses: consequences, duties, character, and justice. Then check whether the decision also fits the triple bottom line of profit, people, and planet.
> [!WARNING]
> Common trap: using CSR as image management. Chapter 3 treats CSR as credible only when it changes policies, decisions, operations, and stakeholder outcomes.
> [!SUMMARY]
> Ethics is decision infrastructure. It protects trust, legitimacy, culture, and long-term performance when short-term incentives push the other way.

## Entrepreneurship use
- Founder ethics scale. Early shortcuts become norms.
- CSR is strongest when built into the business model, not bolted on for appearances.
- If a company says one thing and rewards another, the reward system wins.
