# Concept - IPO framework and team effectiveness
## Core idea
The IPO framework explains team performance by asking a simple question:

> What conditions went into the team, what happened inside the team, and what came out?

IPO stands for:
- **Inputs**
- **Processes / States**
- **Outputs**

This framework matters because teams do not fail only at the end. Most failure starts earlier, in poor design, weak norms, unclear roles, or a climate where members cannot speak openly.

## 1. Inputs: what the team starts with
Inputs are the starting conditions of the team.

### A. Goal / purpose
This is the answer to:
- What are we trying to accomplish?
- What does success look like?
- What is the priority?

A vague goal creates confusion and scattered effort.
A strong goal creates focus and helps members decide what matters most.

### B. Structure
Structure includes:
- team size
- roles
- authority
- status relationships
- whether the team is collocated or virtual

This matters because poor structure leads to overlapping work, weak accountability, and communication breakdowns.

A practical insight from lecture:
- larger teams often struggle more with coordination and social loafing
- smaller teams can move faster and make accountability clearer

This is why the “two-pizza rule” is useful: if a team gets too large, coordination starts to cost more than the extra talent helps.

### C. People
This includes:
- expertise
- personalities
- motivation
- individual goals

A team is not just a set of skills. Members bring preferences, ambitions, and interpersonal habits into the team.

A technically strong team can still underperform if:
- members compete for recognition
- members do not trust each other
- members want different outcomes

### D. Tools
Tools include:
- shared documents
- communication platforms
- planning systems
- decision tools
- information access

Tools shape how well a team can coordinate.
In virtual teams, weak tools create delay, confusion, and missed accountability.

## 2. Team states: what the team becomes like internally
States are shared conditions that develop inside the team over time.

### A. Norms
Norms are the team’s informal or explicit rules for behavior.

Examples:
- when meetings happen
- who communicates updates
- how conflict is handled
- how deadlines are enforced
- what quality level is expected

Norms are one of the strongest hidden drivers of performance.
Good norms reduce ambiguity before tension grows.

### B. Identity
Team identity is a shared sense of belonging.
It helps members think beyond “my part” and toward “our outcome.”

Strong identity:
- increases commitment
- makes coordination smoother
- supports accountability

But identity can weaken if the team develops faultlines, where members split into subgroups.

### C. Trust and psychological safety
These are central to strong teams.

Trust means members believe others will act honestly and reliably.
Psychological safety means members can:
- ask questions
- admit confusion
- give feedback
- raise disagreement
- ask for help

Without safety, teams often appear calm but actually become silent, guarded, and weak at learning.

### D. Efficacy
Team efficacy is a shared belief that the team can succeed.

This affects effort and resilience.
A team with high efficacy is more likely to keep working through setbacks.

### E. Empowerment
Team empowerment means members feel:
- they have real responsibility
- their voice matters
- they have some control over the work
- their effort is meaningful

Empowerment is especially important in self-directed teams, where members must regulate the work themselves.

### F. Cohesion
Cohesion is the level of attraction members feel toward the team and the desire to stay involved.

Cohesion can increase persistence and morale, but it becomes dangerous if it creates pressure for artificial agreement.

## 3. Team processes: what the team actually does
Processes are the behaviors through which the team works.

### A. Coordination
Coordination means organizing effort across people so that work fits together.

Poor coordination often appears as:
- duplicated work
- missed handoffs
- unclear responsibility
- uneven pace
- low accountability

A big risk here is **social loafing**.
Social loafing becomes more likely when:
- groups are large
- effort is hard to track
- tasks feel boring
- belonging is weak

### B. Peer monitoring and feedback
Effective teams do not depend only on the formal leader.
Members also help regulate team quality.

Peer monitoring includes:
- checking progress
- noticing quality issues
- reminding others of standards
- raising concerns early

This works best when trust, role clarity, and safety are strong.

### C. Conflict
Teams need conflict, but not all conflict helps.

- **Task conflict** improves ideas when members disagree about the issue.
- **Relationship conflict** hurts performance when disagreement becomes personal.

An effective team protects task conflict and reduces relationship conflict.

### D. Backup behavior
Backup behavior means members help each other when needed.
Examples:
- stepping in when someone falls behind
- sharing knowledge
- covering gaps
- redistributing effort under pressure

This is one sign that a team has moved from individual effort to real interdependence.

## 4. Outputs: how team effectiveness is judged
The lecture gives four major outputs.

### A. Meaningful results
Did the team produce a strong product, service, solution, or recommendation?

### B. Member satisfaction
Did the experience feel worthwhile and respectful?
Did members feel heard and valued?

### C. Viability
Can the team continue working well together in the future?

A team may finish the task but still damage viability if trust collapses or resentment builds.

### D. Learning and adaptation
Did the team improve its methods?
Did it learn anything useful for future work?

Strong teams do not only perform.
They also get better at performing.

## 5. Why the IPO framework matters for leadership
The IPO framework shows that leaders do not only manage people during execution.
They shape:
- the design of the team
- the clarity of goals
- the climate of safety
- the standards for interaction
- the review of outcomes

A team leader should ask:
1. Are the inputs strong enough?
2. Are the team states healthy enough?
3. Are the processes productive enough?
4. Are the outputs strong in more than one dimension?

## 6. Entrepreneurship angle
For startups and project teams, IPO thinking is practical:
- choose the right people, not just the most impressive people
- define the goal before dividing the work
- agree on norms early
- keep teams small when possible
- create visible accountability
- review not only the result, but the process used to get it

## 7. Speaking tip
When explaining a team problem in class, use this order:
1. Input problem
2. State problem
3. Process problem
4. Output consequence

Example:
> The team’s goal was vague, which weakened role clarity. That reduced trust and made peer feedback harder. As a result, coordination suffered and the final output lacked quality.

## Quick memory line
> Inputs shape the climate. The climate shapes the process. The process shapes the result.

# Concept - Team conflict, trust, and psychological safety
## Core idea
Most weak teams do not fail because members never disagreed.
They fail because disagreement became unsafe, personal, or invisible.

This concept connects three major ideas:
- trust
- psychological safety
- constructive conflict

Together, these explain why some teams improve through disagreement while others break down because of it.

## 1. Trust: the base condition
Trust means members believe others are dependable, honest, and not acting in bad faith.

In a trusting team, members are more willing to:
- admit mistakes
- ask for help
- accept feedback
- challenge weak ideas
- rely on each other

In a low-trust team, members often:
- hide weaknesses
- avoid honesty
- protect image
- hold grudges
- assume bad intent

Trust matters because teamwork requires vulnerability.
You cannot truly collaborate if every member is performing confidence instead of sharing reality.

## 2. Psychological safety: the communication climate
Psychological safety means people feel able to speak openly without fear of embarrassment, punishment, or retaliation.

It does **not** mean:
- everyone agrees
- standards are relaxed
- no one gets challenged

It **does** mean:
- people can disagree respectfully
- people can ask “basic” questions
- people can say they are confused
- people can raise concerns early
- people can admit they need help

A team can have smart people and still underperform if members do not feel safe enough to be honest.

## 3. Why safety matters for performance
Safety improves team performance because it supports:
- feedback
- learning
- problem detection
- coordination
- adaptation

When safety is low, teams often look fine on the surface but experience:
- silence instead of discussion
- fake agreement instead of commitment
- hidden errors instead of correction
- private frustration instead of open problem-solving

So safety is not just about comfort.
It is about access to real information.

## 4. Constructive conflict vs destructive conflict
The lecture makes a sharp distinction between two kinds of conflict.

### Constructive conflict = task conflict
This is disagreement about:
- ideas
- methods
- evidence
- priorities
- solutions

It is helpful when it stays focused on the issue.
Benefits include:
- better thinking
- more creativity
- stronger decisions
- fewer blind spots

### Destructive conflict = relationship conflict
This is disagreement that becomes:
- personal
- emotional
- hostile
- identity-based

It harms performance because it shifts attention from the work to the person.

A useful rule:
> Critique the idea, not the worth of the person presenting it.

## 5. Why teams often slide from task conflict into relationship conflict
This shift usually happens when:
- norms are unclear
- trust is low
- stress is high
- members interrupt or dismiss others
- status differences prevent equal voice
- criticism becomes sarcastic or personal

Teams rarely plan to become personal.
They drift there when process is weak.

## 6. Cohesion, conformity, and groupthink
Cohesion is often treated as automatically good, but the lecture shows a risk:
- teams with strong pressure for unity may avoid honest disagreement
- members may protect harmony rather than decision quality
- the result can be groupthink

Groupthink happens when:
- conformity norms become too strong
- unanimity is valued more than critical thinking
- alternatives are not evaluated seriously

That means a team can be highly connected and still make weak decisions.

The goal is not:
- no cohesion
and not:
- total harmony

The goal is:
> enough cohesion for commitment, enough openness for debate.

## 7. Norms that support healthy conflict
Teams handle conflict much better when norms are made early.

Useful norms include:
- disagreement should target the issue
- everyone speaks before major decisions
- quieter members are invited in
- evidence matters more than confidence
- interruptions are limited
- missed commitments are addressed directly
- concerns are raised early, not stored privately

This is why team charters matter.
They turn expectations into something visible.

## 8. Emotional management
The lecture emphasizes emotional management because conflict is not only a logic problem.
It is also a self-regulation problem.

Strong emotional management means members can:
- notice defensiveness
- pause before reacting
- ask clarifying questions
- keep tone respectful
- separate frustration from attack

Teams that lack emotional regulation often convert small disagreements into long-term resentment.

## 9. Superordinate identity
One way to reduce personal conflict is to build a larger shared identity.

Instead of:
- marketing vs finance
- old members vs new members
- quiet members vs outspoken members

the team needs:
- one larger identity
- one shared purpose
- one common standard

This is called a superordinate identity.
It helps members think:
> We are solving the same problem together.

## 10. What leaders should do
A good team leader does not suppress disagreement.
A good team leader creates conditions where disagreement improves thinking.

Leader actions that help:
- build trust early
- model vulnerability
- ask genuine questions
- frame the work as learning
- create and reinforce norms
- interrupt personal attacks
- refocus the team on shared goals
- appreciate honesty, not just certainty

A leader should watch for signs that conflict is turning destructive:
- repeated interruptions
- sarcasm
- silence after criticism
- alliance-building outside meetings
- members attacking motives, not arguments

## 11. Entrepreneurship angle
Startup teams often face pressure, ambiguity, and strong opinions.
That makes conflict management especially important.

A founder should create a team climate where members can say:
- “I think this plan is weak”
- “I’m not convinced by the evidence”
- “I need help”
- “We are avoiding the real issue”

Without that, teams move fast but make fragile decisions.

## 12. Speaking tip
When disagreeing in a team, use this format:
1. Name the issue
2. Give your reasoning
3. Ask for response
4. Keep the focus on the decision

Example:
> I see the timeline differently. My concern is that we are underestimating testing time. Can we compare both versions before choosing?

This keeps your speaking direct without making it personal.

## Quick memory line
> Trust makes honesty possible. Safety makes honesty usable. Task conflict makes honesty productive.
