# Organizational Culture

## ==Definition==
- Organizational culture is the shared assumptions, values, and beliefs that shape what people see as appropriate or inappropriate behavior.
- The textbook frames culture as learned assumptions taught to new members as the correct way to perceive, think, and feel about organizational problems.

## Source map
- Lecture: [[50_Archive/Previous Classes/Minor/MGMT 3001/Week - 7|Week - 7]], `Week 7.pdf`, and `en-Org Culture Lecture.txt`.
- Textbook: [[10_Areas/UMN/Previous Classes/Minor/MGMT 3001/Textbook/Chapter - 8|Chapter - 8]].

## Why it matters
- Culture is hard to determine from the outside.
- Culture is difficult to imitate.
- Culture shapes behavior and decisions.
- The lecture emphasizes that culture can be more valuable than strategy because it affects retention, investment, and everyday behavior.

## Strong culture elements from lecture
- Clear mission and values.
- Participation.
- Transparency.
- Structure for celebrating wins.
- Present and accessible leaders.
- Alignment between culture and business.

## Focus areas for culture
- Purpose.
- Opportunity.
- Success.
- Appreciation.
- Wellbeing.
- Leadership.

## Culture and internal environment
- Cultures that foster success are adaptable, focused on employee involvement, founded on clear vision, and consistent.
- Adaptability helps the organization respond to external shifts.
- Employee involvement gives people voice and ownership.
- Clear vision gives direction.
- Consistency makes behavior predictable.

## Three levels of culture
- **Artifacts and symbols:** visible signs a newcomer can observe, such as dress, language, layout, rituals, stories, and tools.
- **Espoused values and beliefs:** what the organization says it values.
- **Underlying assumptions:** deep taken-for-granted beliefs people may not even realize they hold.
- Trap: artifacts are easiest to see but easiest to misread.

## Culture strength and dark side
- Strong culture helps coordination because people know what matters without asking every time.
- Strong culture can improve commitment and retention.
- The dark side is conformity, resistance to change, exclusion, or silence when the shared assumptions are wrong.
- Subcultures matter because teams, functions, or locations may interpret the broader culture differently.

## Who shapes culture
- **Founders:** imprint early values, assumptions, and stories.
- **Organizational leaders:** reinforce what matters through attention, decisions, rewards, and crisis behavior.
- **Teams:** translate culture into daily norms.

## How culture spreads
- Formal statements.
- Physical space.
- Role modeling.
- Reward systems.
- Stories and legends.
- Measurement and control.
- Leader reactions in crisis.
- Organization design.
- Hiring and promotion criteria.

## Culture change
- Culture change is hard because assumptions feel normal to insiders.
- Change requires leaders to signal new priorities through behavior, rewards, hiring, promotion, measurement, and crisis response.
- Culture change is not a memo. People watch what leaders tolerate, measure, and reward.

## Common traps
- Calling perks culture.
- Believing culture can be copied from another company.
- Confusing espoused values with underlying assumptions.
- Ignoring subcultures.
- Trying to change culture without changing systems.

## Course examples
- Zappos hires for culture, uses culture camp, and offers new hires money to quit.
- L.L.Bean uses outdoor practices, low turnover, and recognition to reinforce culture.
- Warby Parker uses onboarding, social consciousness, career opportunities, and open communication.
- Adobe uses trust, challenging projects, goal-based performance evaluation, and avoidance of micromanagement.
- Hilton, HubSpot, Mars, and Intuit illustrate adaptable, involved, clear-vision, and consistent cultures.

## How to apply
> [!TIP]
> Read culture by comparing artifacts, stated values, and underlying assumptions. Then look at what leaders measure, reward, punish, and do during pressure.
> [!WARNING]
> Common trap: treating culture as slogans. The textbook's deeper claim is that culture becomes real through repeated assumptions, routines, rewards, and socialization.
> [!SUMMARY]
> Culture can coordinate behavior without constant supervision, but strong culture can also block dissent, ethics, and adaptation when the assumptions are wrong.

## Entrepreneurship use
- In a startup, culture forms before anyone names it.
- Hiring, first customers, first crisis, first firing, and first reward system all teach people what is real.
- A founder should design culture through repeated behavior, not slogans.
