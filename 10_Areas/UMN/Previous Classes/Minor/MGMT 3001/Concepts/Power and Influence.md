# Power and Influence

## ==Definition==
- **Power** is the potential ability to influence another person's behavior, thinking, or attitudes.
- **Influence** is the means or process by which power is exercised.
- **Authority** is the formal right to influence another person.
- The lecture is explicit: power, influence, and authority are not synonyms.

## Why it matters
- The textbook states that leadership requires power and the effective use of influence.
- Power is not automatically manipulation. It becomes dangerous when used without awareness, ethics, or fit.
- Power matters more when resources are scarce, decisions are uncertain, conflict is high, or stakeholders disagree.

## Playing high and playing low
- **Playing high:** reinforces rank. It uses directness, stillness, space, eye contact, complete sentences, and authority signals.
- **Playing low:** builds approachability. It reduces psychological distance, lifts others up, invites connection, and can build rapport.
- Use high when rank must be clarified or status is contested.
- Use low when relationships, trust, or openness matter.
- Trap: playing higher than your actual rank can backfire.

## Gender norms
- The lecture emphasizes that men and women are often perceived differently even when behavior is similar.
- The Heidi/Howard example shows the same competence can be judged differently depending on gender expectations.
- Women are often expected to play low, which creates a leadership double bind.

## Interpersonal power bases
- **Legitimate power:** based on formal position.
- **Reward power:** ability to provide positive outcomes or remove negative outcomes.
- **Coercive power:** ability to punish. It can create quick compliance but damage morale.
- **Expert power:** based on knowledge, skill, or information.
- **Referent power:** based on liking, admiration, or desire to emulate.
- One person can use several bases at once.

## Influence tactics
- **Rational persuasion:** logic and facts; consistently useful for upward influence; tied strongly to expert power.
- **Inspirational appeal:** appeal to values and ideals; depends on knowing what matters to the target.
- **Consultation:** ask for input so commitment increases.
- **Ingratiation:** use friendliness, favors, or compliments before asking.
- **Exchange:** offer something for compliance.
- **Personal appeal:** appeal to loyalty or friendship.
- **Collaboration:** provide help or resources so the target can complete the request.
- **Apprising:** explain how compliance benefits the target personally.
- **Coalition:** use others to persuade or pressure the target.
- **Pressure:** demands, threats, or persistent reminders.

## Choosing a tactic
- Is the tactic consistent with social norms and role expectations?
- Do you have the necessary power base?
- How much skill does the tactic require?
- How much resistance already exists?

## Common traps
- Confusing authority with actual influence.
- Using coercive power because it is fast, then paying for it with trust.
- Using rational persuasion when the person cares more about values, identity, or relationship.
- Treating referent power as charm rather than earned credibility.
- Ignoring gendered interpretation of power behavior.

## Course examples
- Social movements show power without formal authority.
- Convincing parents to buy a dog shows influence tactics.
- Instructor grading shows authority.
- Oprah's endorsement of Obama illustrates broad referent power and social influence.
- Xi Jinping and Jeff Bezos are discussed as people with multiple bases of power.

## How to apply
> [!TIP]
> Pick the influence tactic by the commitment you need. Use rational persuasion for evidence, inspirational appeal for values, consultation for ownership, collaboration for support, and pressure only when urgency and authority justify the cost.
> [!WARNING]
> Common trap: confusing authority with influence. Authority gives the right to direct, but commitment depends on credibility, incentives, relationships, expertise, and perceived fairness.
> [!SUMMARY]
> Power is not automatically bad. The managerial question is whether power is used to create coordinated action without destroying trust.

## Entrepreneurship use
- Founders have authority only after people accept it. Early influence comes from expertise, credibility, vision, and relationships.
- Use power to build commitment, not just compliance.
- The founder's influence style becomes part of culture.
