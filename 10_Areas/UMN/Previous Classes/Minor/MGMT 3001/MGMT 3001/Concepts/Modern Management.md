# Modern Management Theories

## Core idea
Management theory did not arrive fully formed. It evolved because different problems kept exposing the limits of the previous approach. Understanding the progression lets you recognize **which tool fits which situation** — the core insight of the contingency view.

---

## Timeline overview
Each perspective emerged roughly in sequence, but all remain in use today:

1. Classical Perspective (Scientific Management + Bureaucracy) → ~1870s–1950s
2. Humanistic Perspective → ~1920s onward
3. Quantitative / Management Science → ~1940s onward
4. Organization-Environment Perspective (Systems + Contingency) → ~1960s onward

---

## 1. Scientific Management
**Key figures:** Frederick Taylor, Henry Gantt, the Gilbreths
**Problem it solves:** How do we make work faster and more efficient?

**Core idea:** Systematically study how work is performed, find the best method, and standardize it.

**How to increase efficiency (Taylor's steps):**
1. Study how the job is currently done; determine better methods and codify them into rules
2. Select workers whose skills match the job requirements
3. Train workers in the standard method
4. Plan work and eliminate interruptions so workers can focus
5. Establish a fair performance-incentive standard (link pay to output)

**Key tools:**
- **Time and motion studies** — measure every part of a task to eliminate waste
- **Gantt charts** — visual project scheduling tool still used today
- **Task-based bonus system** — reward workers for meeting or exceeding standards

**Where it still shows up:**
- Fast-food operations (McDonald's assembly-line kitchen)
- Warehousing and logistics
- Call center workflow design

**Limitations:**
- Specialized, repetitive jobs become boring and demoralizing
- Managers may not share the productivity gains with workers
- Workers can deliberately underperform to protect their output standard ("ratcheting")
- Treats people as interchangeable machine parts

---

## 2. Bureaucracy
**Key figure:** Max Weber
**Problem it solves:** How do large organizations stay consistent, fair, and coordinated without relying on personal favors or arbitrary power?

**Core definition:** Bureaucracy is a **formal system of organization and administration** designed to ensure effectiveness and efficiency through rules, hierarchy, and impersonal processes.

**Seven elements of a bureaucratic organization:**
1. **Qualification-based hiring** — jobs given based on skills, not connections
2. **Merit-based promotion** — advancement based on performance
3. **Chain of command** — clear lines of authority from top to bottom
4. **Division of labor** — specialized roles so each person masters a specific task
5. **Impartial application of rules and procedures** — same rules for everyone
6. **Recorded in writing** — decisions and procedures documented for accountability
7. **Managers separate from owners** — professional management, not family/personal control

**Contrast with what came before:**
Weber was reacting against monarchy and personal empires where authority came from birth or loyalty. Bureaucracy grounds authority in **knowledge and rules** instead.

**Strengths:**
- Creates consistency and fairness
- Scales to large organizations
- Reduces favoritism and arbitrary decisions

**Limitations:**
- Can become rigid and slow to adapt
- Rule-following can replace good judgment
- Employees may hide behind procedures

## 3. Human Relations / Humanistic Perspective
**Key figures:** Elton Mayo (Hawthorne Studies), Mary Parker Follett, Douglas McGregor
**Problem it solves:** Why don't workers always respond to better pay and conditions the way classical theory predicts?

**Hawthorne Studies (1924–1932):**
- Western Electric factory study — researchers changed lighting conditions to measure productivity
- Result: **productivity rose regardless of whether light went up or down**
- Conclusion: Workers were responding to the **attention** they received as part of the study, not the physical conditions
- This revealed that **social factors and recognition** powerfully shape performance

**Mary Parker Follett's key ideas:**
- Workers who have knowledge of a task should have control over it (early idea of empowerment)
- Managers and workers should co-analyze jobs to find improvements
- Informal groups inside organizations matter and deserve respect

**Theory X vs. Theory Y (Douglas McGregor):**
These are two opposing sets of assumptions managers can hold about workers.

| Theory X | Theory Y |
|----------|----------|
| Workers are lazy and dislike work | Workers are not inherently lazy |
| Workers must be closely supervised | Workers will do what is good for the org if given the chance |
| Managers need strict rules and punishments | Managers should decentralize authority and provide resources |
| Workers avoid responsibility | Workers can exercise self-direction and initiative |

**Practical implication:** A manager's assumption changes everything — structure, motivation system, communication style, and whether people feel safe sharing ideas.

**Theory X example:** "Everyone must complete the survey by Wednesday — I will monitor compliance."
**Theory Y example:** "I completed the survey and encourage you all to do the same — your input shapes our future."

## 4. Quantitative / Management Science Perspective
**Problem it solves:** How do we make better decisions about complex resource and operations problems?

**Core idea:** Apply rigorous mathematical and analytical techniques to management decisions.

**Main tools:**
- **Operations research** — use quantitative techniques (linear programming, queuing, simulation) to analyze production and logistics
- **Management Information Systems (MIS)** — structured information flows so decision-makers always have what they need
- **Forecasting and modeling** — use data to predict demand, cost, and outcomes

**Where it shows up today:**
- Airline seat pricing and scheduling
- Supply chain optimization
- Financial modeling and budgeting

---

## 5. Systems Thinking
**Problem it solves:** How do we see the whole organization, not just the individual parts?

**Core idea:** Organizations are **interconnected systems**. A change in one part — marketing, finance, HR, operations — affects all other parts.

**Key insight:** Managers who only optimize their own function may actually harm the whole system. Good decisions require understanding linkages.

**Example:** If operations cuts costs by reducing quality, marketing suffers, customer service is overwhelmed, and brand reputation drops. The "savings" create bigger costs elsewhere.

---

## 6. Contingency View
**Problem it solves:** Why do some management approaches work in one setting but fail in another?

**Core idea:** There is **no single best way** to manage every organization. Effective management depends on **fit** between the organization and its specific environment, task, people, and strategy.

**Three positions in contingency thinking:**

| View | Claim |
|------|-------|
| Universalist | There is one best way — apply it everywhere |
| Case | Every situation is completely unique — no generalizations possible |
| **Contingency** | Organizational situations follow patterns; managers can identify the right approach for each type of situation |

**Why this is the most important takeaway:**
When an exam asks "what should a manager do?" — the contingency answer is: **it depends on the fit between the approach and the context.**

---

## How to use these on exams

A strong exam answer names the theory, states what problem it was designed to solve, and explains its key characteristics. Avoid just listing names — connect each theory to what it actually changed.

**Quick test:**
- Scientific Management → efficiency → standard methods + incentives
- Bureaucracy → fairness and scale → rules + hierarchy + merit
- Human Relations → people and motivation → social factors + participation + Theory X/Y
- Quantitative → data-driven decisions → models + MIS
- Systems → interconnection → whole-org thinking
- Contingency → fit → no one best way

## Flashcards
#cards/MGMT
Scientific management::A theory focused on improving efficiency by systematically studying and standardizing work methods. Associated with Taylor, Gantt, and the Gilbreths.
Bureaucracy::A formal management system using qualification-based hiring, merit promotion, chain of command, division of labor, impartial rules, and written records. Associated with Max Weber.
Hawthorne effect::The finding that workers become more productive when they receive attention and feel observed, regardless of physical changes to their work environment.
Theory X::Assumes workers are lazy, dislike work, avoid responsibility, and must be closely supervised and controlled.
Theory Y::Assumes workers are not inherently lazy, can be self-directed, and will act in the organization's interest when given autonomy and the right environment.
Human relations view::A management perspective emphasizing social interaction, motivation, informal groups, and the importance of attention and participation in driving performance.
Quantitative management science::A perspective that uses mathematical models, operations research, and information systems to support management decisions.
Systems thinking::A perspective that views the organization as an interconnected whole, where changes in one part affect all other parts.
Contingency view::The idea that effective management depends on fit between the approach and the specific situation — there is no one best way to manage every organization.
