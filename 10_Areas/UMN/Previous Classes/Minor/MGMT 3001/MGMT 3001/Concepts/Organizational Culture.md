# Organizational Culture

## Core idea
Organizational culture is a **pattern of shared basic assumptions** that a group learns as it solves its problems. These assumptions work well enough to be considered valid, and are taught to new members as the **correct way to perceive, think, and feel** about challenges.

Think of culture as a **roadmap and a set of invisible rules** — it guides behavior even when no policy manual is being read.

---

## Why culture matters
Culture is a powerful driver of performance because:
- It operates constantly, not just when managers are watching
- It shapes behavior even in novel situations where no formal rule exists
- It is **hard to imitate** — giving it potential as a source of sustainable competitive advantage
- It determines what behaviors get rewarded, which shapes who stays and who leaves
- It is often the dominant factor in defining ethical and unethical behavior — more influential than formal compliance programs

---

## The Three Levels of Culture
*(Critical exam topic — know all three and be able to distinguish them)*

### Level 1: Artifacts (Visible)
The **observable surface** of culture — what a newcomer can see, hear, and feel.

Examples:
- Physical layout of the office (open vs. closed, hierarchical vs. flat)
- Dress code and style of communication
- Company logos, slogans, and language
- Ceremonies, rituals, and celebrations
- Stories and myths that circulate about the organization
- How meetings are run, who speaks first, how decisions get announced

**Key point:** Artifacts are the most visible level, but they are also the most **ambiguous** — you can see them without understanding what they mean. Two companies can both have open-plan offices but with very different cultures underneath.

### Level 2: Espoused Values and Beliefs (Known)
The **meanings and principles** that members attach to artifacts.

Examples:
- Stated company values and mission statements (e.g., "We value transparency")
- What leaders say they believe about customers, employees, and success
- Formal codes of conduct

**Key point:** Espoused values often originate from the company's founder. They represent what the organization *says* it cares about — but they may or may not match actual behavior. The gap between espoused and enacted values is where culture problems often hide.

### Level 3: Underlying Assumptions (Deeply Embedded)
The **deepest and least visible** level. These are beliefs that once successfully solved a problem and gradually became unconscious — so deeply embedded that they are no longer questioned.

Examples:
- "Around here, if you speak up and challenge leadership, your career suffers" (even if the official value is "speak your truth")
- "Success means working 60+ hours a week" (even if the official policy is flexibility)
- "Sales always trumps compliance" (even if the company has an ethics committee)

**Key point:** Underlying assumptions are the most powerful drivers of behavior and the hardest to change. They are often invisible to insiders because they feel like reality rather than choice.

## Reading culture — five diagnostic lenses

To assess an organization's actual culture, look at:

| Lens | What to observe |
|------|----------------|
| **Values and philosophy** | What does the company say it stands for? What purpose does it claim? |
| **Group boundaries** | What makes someone an insider? What is expected of members? |
| **Power structure** | Who advances? How is status earned, kept, and lost? |
| **Work norms** | What are the implicit standards for a "good day's work"? What gets sanctioned? |
| **Reward and punishment system** | What behaviors get celebrated? What gets ignored or punished? |

The gap between the stated answer and the observable reality on each lens reveals the true culture.

## Cultural strength — four combinations

Cultural strength is measured on two dimensions:
- **Agreement** — how widely values are shared across the organization
- **Intensity** — how passionately members hold those values

| Combination | Agreement | Intensity | Result |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Strong culture** | High | High | Clear, unified behavior across the organization |
| **Passive culture** | High | Low | Everyone knows what is valued but few act on it |
| **Competing forces** | Low | High | Different groups fight fiercely for different values |
| **Weak culture** | Low | Low | No clear direction; high individual variation |

**Strong culture benefits:**
- Less monitoring needed — people act consistently without being watched
- Faster decisions — shared assumptions reduce coordination costs
- Stronger commitment — members feel part of something meaningful

**Strong culture risks:**
- Suppresses dissent — disagreement is seen as disloyalty
- Resists change — deeply embedded assumptions make adaptation difficult
- Can reward unethical behavior — if winning "at all costs" becomes a core assumption

## Subcultures

Large organizations often contain **subcultures** that form around:
- Geographic locations (different offices develop different norms)
- Product lines (different business units)
- Functional units (engineering culture vs. sales culture vs. legal culture)

**Implications:**
- Subcultures can empower and energize specific groups
- They can also create conflict and coordination problems when different units must collaborate
- A company's "culture" is often really an overlapping set of subcultures, not a monolith

## Who builds culture — roles and responsibilities

### Founders
Founders "imprint" their assumptions and beliefs onto the organization from its earliest days. Their choices about what to reward, how to handle conflict, what to prioritize, and what to avoid become the first cultural templates.

### Organizational leaders
Leaders are the most powerful ongoing shapers of culture through:
- **What they pay attention to and measure** — signals what matters
- **How they react to crises** — reveals the true priorities when values are tested
- **How they allocate resources** — money follows actual priorities
- **Role modeling** — leaders teach culture by their own behavior more than by any written policy
- **Who they hire, promote, and let go** — selection is a powerful culture transmission mechanism

### Teams
When teams repeatedly solve problems the same way, those methods become **organizational routines**. Once applied without question, those routines become embedded cultural assumptions at the team level.

---

## How culture spreads — socialization tools

**Socialization** is how new members learn what behavior is rewarded and expected.

Key tools:
- **Formal statements** — credos, values documents, onboarding scripts
- **Physical space** — the office layout, decor, and ambiance communicate values non-verbally
- **Reward systems** — recognizing behaviors that align with stated values
- **Stories and myths** — parables about organizational heroes who took risks, served customers exceptionally, or embodied values under pressure
- **Recruiting and selection** — hiring for cultural fit, assessed through structured behavioral interviews
- **Onboarding** — deliberate early experiences that transmit assumptions

---

## Organizational commitment — stages

The desired outcome of socialization is deep organizational commitment. It builds through three stages:

| Stage | Basis of commitment |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Compliance** | "I act in line with the rules in exchange for pay" — transactional |
| **Identification** | "I identify with this group and want to belong" — relational |
| **Internalization** | "My values and the firm's values are aligned — this place represents what I believe" — intrinsic |

Employees who reach internalization often act like owners — they go beyond their job description because they genuinely believe in the mission.

## Culture during critical moments

### Joining a new firm
New members must quickly decode which artifacts reflect real values and which are aspirational fiction. The fastest way to learn: watch what happens when someone deviates from expected behavior.

### Mergers and acquisitions (M&A)
Research shows **90% of mergers fail to meet expectations**, and cultural clash is a primary reason. Before acquiring, leaders must evaluate both cultures and decide:
- Whose culture dominates?
- Which elements from each culture should be preserved?
- Is it possible to genuinely merge into a new culture — or will one simply absorb the other?

The most ambitious goal — creating a genuinely new merged culture — is also the hardest and rarest to achieve.

---

## Culture change

**Changing culture is extremely difficult** because underlying assumptions are, by definition, no longer being consciously questioned. Culture change requires:
- Sustained leadership attention over years, not months
- Changing *systems* (rewards, promotions, measurement) — not just language
- Replacing deeply held assumptions with new ones through repeated new experiences
- Consistent modeling from the top — leaders who preach new values but embody old ones undermine every initiative

**Most culture "change" programs fail** because they address artifacts (new values posters, new slogans) without touching underlying assumptions or structural reinforcers.
