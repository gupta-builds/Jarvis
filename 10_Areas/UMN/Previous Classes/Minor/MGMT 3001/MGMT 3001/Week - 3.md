# Week 3 — Business-Level Strategy & Sustainable Competitive Advantage

## What you must be able to do
- Explain what sustainable competitive advantage means and identify the four VRIN conditions.
- Use the full strategic management process from current-state evaluation through implementation.
- Distinguish mission, vision, goals, and plans — and explain how they relate to each other.
- Use Porter's five forces to evaluate an industry's profit potential.
- Explain the resource-based view, value chain, and how they connect to internal analysis.
- Use SWOT as a bridge between external and internal analysis.
- Distinguish cost leadership, differentiation, and focus strategies.
- Explain what it means for a firm to be "stuck in the middle" and why it leads to poor performance.

## Key ideas
- Strategy is about making deliberate trade-offs — not doing everything.
- Advantage depends on BOTH the industry (external) AND the firm's internal capabilities.
- External and internal analysis must connect — SWOT is the bridge.
- SWOT is only useful when it leads to action and decisions.
- Firms need a clear, coherent position — not a bit of everything.

## Lecture

### 1. Why strategy = making choices

The lecture opens with a crucial insight: **strategy means choosing what NOT to do as much as what to do.**

Firms gain position by making focused, coherent choices — not by copying every rival. The temptation is always to add features, serve more segments, and try more approaches. Strong strategy resists that temptation and concentrates resources where they create the most distinctive value.

**Real-world strategy shifts from lecture:**
- **Pepsi:** Lockdown caused a 3.1% drop in sales. Response: launched Snacks.com and PantryShop.com — a direct-to-consumer pivot. This is strategic adaptation driven by an external shift in how customers buy
- **Kodak:** Ceded ground in the camera industry to competitors and smartphones. In July 2020, Kodak entered a completely new industry — generic pharmaceutical ingredients. A dramatic strategy pivot when the core business became uncompetitive

**Why these examples matter:** Companies can't stay static when markets shift. Strategy requires not just having a plan — but being willing to abandon it when reality changes.

**Three strategic questions that anchor everything:**
- **Who** do we serve? (Broad market or narrow segment?)
- **What** do we provide? (Range and type of product or service?)
- **How** do we provide it uniquely? (Processes, capabilities, and delivery methods?)

### 2. The strategic management process — step by step

This is a testable sequence. Learn it in order.

| Step | What happens |
|------|-------------|
| **1. Current-state evaluation** | Where are we now? Clarify mission, vision, goals |
| **2. External environment analysis** | What forces shape our industry? (Porter's Five Forces) |
| **3. Internal environment analysis** | What can we do especially well? (RBV, Value Chain) |
| **4. SWOT analysis** | Bridge external and internal — find fit between capability and opportunity |
| **5. Strategy formulation** | Choose generic strategy (cost, differentiation, or focus) |
| **6. Implementation and strategic control** | Execute, measure, adapt |

---

### 3. Mission, Vision, Goals, Plans — the planning hierarchy

These are frequently tested and frequently confused:

| Term | Definition | Scope |
|------|-----------|-------|
| **Mission** | Why the organization exists — its fundamental purpose | Timeless; rarely changes |
| **Vision** | What the desired future looks like — where the org is heading | Long-term aspiration |
| **Goals** | Specific desired outcomes, often measurable | Medium-term targets |
| **Plans** | Concrete actions and resource allocations to achieve goals | Short-term operational detail |

**How they cascade:**
Mission (purpose) → Vision (future picture) → Goals (measurable targets) → Plans (specific actions)

**Example:**
- Mission: "We exist to make transportation accessible to everyone"
- Vision: "A world where no one needs a personal car"
- Goal: "Achieve 30% market share in 10 major metros by 2027"
- Plan: "Hire 500 drivers, launch in 3 new cities, invest $50M in the app"

### 4. Sustainable competitive advantage — the VRIN framework

A firm has sustainable competitive advantage when it creates more value than rivals in a way that is **hard to match or replace over time**.

The four VRIN conditions — all must be met:

| Condition | Meaning | Example |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Valuable** | Resource creates value for customers | Superior customer service system |
| **Rare** | Few or no competitors possess it | Proprietary technology or formula |
| **Inimitable** | Hard to copy, replicate, or acquire | Culture built over decades |
| **Non-substitutable** | No equivalent alternative rivals can use | Network effect with millions of users |

**If any condition is missing:**
- Not valuable → advantage fails to create customer preference
- Not rare → rivals have it too; no differentiation
- Imitable → rivals copy it and erode the advantage quickly
- Substitutable → rivals find another route to the same outcome

**Real-world sources of competitive advantage (from lecture):**
1. **Product or process technology** → Pfizer's lead on COVID vaccinations — proprietary mRNA technology competitors couldn't replicate quickly
2. **Protected or regulated markets** → Gas/Oil — government licensing and regulatory access keep most players out
3. **Access to capital** → SpaceX (Elon Musk) — billions in private funding allowed R&D investment no startup could match
4. **Economies of scale** → Walmart — 10,500+ stores across 24 countries means per-unit costs are dramatically lower than any regional competitor
5. **Human capital** → Southwest Airlines — their workforce culture and behaviors are their service advantage and extremely hard to replicate

### 5. External analysis: Porter's Five Forces in depth

Use to assess **industry attractiveness** and identify where profit pressure comes from.

#### Force 1: Threat of New Entrants
New competitors erode existing firms' profits. Barriers that protect incumbents:
- Economies of scale (high volume → low unit cost)
- Network effects (more users → more valuable product)
- Customer switching costs — e.g., moving from PC to Apple
- Capital requirements (expensive to enter)
- Incumbency advantages (brand, access, regulatory knowledge)
- Unequal access to distribution channels — e.g., products bottled by Coke or Pepsi lock out newcomers
- Government policy (licenses, patents)

**Exam insight:** Threat of entry depends not just on barriers — also on how aggressively *existing* competitors can be expected to retaliate.

#### Force 2: Supplier Power
Strong when suppliers can dictate terms and reduce the profitability of the industry.
- Suppliers are more concentrated than buyers
- No substitute inputs exist
- Switching suppliers is costly
- Supplier's product is critical to buyer quality

**Classic example (lecture):** Microsoft's supplier power over Windows — computer manufacturers will almost pay any price to have it on their devices.

#### Force 3: Buyer Power
Strong when buyers or suppliers exert pressure on the industry.
- Groups are concentrated and purchase in large volumes
- Seller's products are largely undifferentiated (e.g. steel)
- Few switching costs for buyers

#### Force 4: Threat of Substitutes
A substitute is a *different product* meeting the *same need*.
**Lecture example:** Uber — a technology platform for matching people with transportation needs — is a substitute for traditional taxi services, not a competitor in the same category.

Substitutes cap prices an industry can charge.

#### Force 5: Rivalry Among Existing Competitors
Most intense when competition is focused on price.
- **Lecture example:** Burger King vs. McDonald's — direct price competition
- Intense rivalry can cause firms to do unintelligent things — e.g., **Cola Wars (Coke vs. Pepsi)** — years of costly promotional battles that damaged both firms

Most intense when:
- Products are undifferentiated (price war incentives)
- Fixed costs are high (must compete to fill capacity)
- Product is perishable (pressure to sell immediately)
- Exit barriers are high (firms that can't leave keep fighting)

**Important limitation:** Five forces is a snapshot of current conditions — it must be revisited as markets evolve. It should be used *before, during, and after* entering a market.

### Applying Five Forces to Starbucks (lecture case study)

The lecture walked through Starbucks as a real-world Five Forces application. Here's the full analysis:

| Force | Starbucks Analysis |
|-------|--------------------|
| **Supplier Power** | Moderate-low for Starbucks — if a supplier loses a Starbucks contract, they lose enormous volume. Starbucks has significant leverage over coffee suppliers |
| **Threat of New Entry** | Not hard to enter the coffee shop business, but *extremely* hard to enter at Starbucks's scale — brand, real estate, loyalty program, and supply chain create massive barriers to matching them |
| **Threat of Substitutes** | High — lots of other options (tea, soda, other coffee shops, home brewing). But Starbucks brand loyalty and the "third place" experience reduce actual switching |
| **Buyer Power** | Buyer preferences do matter (e.g., consumer trends toward healthier or cheaper options), but individual customers have little individual bargaining power |
| **Competitive Rivalry** | Strong rivalry with Dunkin', local cafes, McDonald's McCafé — but Starbucks's brand differentiation partially insulates it from pure price competition |

### 6. Internal analysis: Resource-Based View

The RBV asks: **what internal resources and capabilities give us an edge?**

| Resource type | Examples |
|--------------|---------|
| **Tangible** | Real estate, machinery, inventory, cash |
| **Intangible** | Brand, patents, proprietary processes, culture |
| **Human** | Employee knowledge, leadership, institutional memory |

**Core competencies** emerge when multiple resources combine in ways rivals cannot easily replicate.

---

### 7. Value chain — finding where value is created

**Primary activities** (create and deliver the product):
1. Inbound logistics — receiving inputs
2. Operations — converting inputs into product
3. Outbound logistics — delivering product
4. Marketing and sales — communicating value
5. Service — maintaining value after sale

**Support activities** (enable primary activities):
- Procurement, technology development, HR management, firm infrastructure

**How to use it:** Identify which activities are sources of differentiation or cost advantage. Then compare to competitors and suppliers (the broader "value system").

---

### 8. SWOT — the bridge between external and internal

| Category | Source | Strategic question |
|----------|--------|-------------------|
| **Strengths** | Internal | What do we do better than rivals? |
| **Weaknesses** | Internal | Where are we vulnerable? |
| **Opportunities** | External | What market conditions favor us? |
| **Threats** | External | What forces could undermine our position? |

**The bridge logic:**
- Match **Strengths → Opportunities** (deploy advantages where market rewards them)
- Defend against **Threats → using Strengths** (protect position with existing advantages)
- Improve **Weaknesses** that block key **Opportunities**
- Avoid **Weaknesses + Threats** combinations (most dangerous)

**Critical exam point:** A SWOT that doesn't lead to action is just a list. The value comes from the strategic choices it informs.

### 9. Generic competitive strategies

#### Cost Leadership
- Lowest cost producer across a broad market
- Achieves this through: economies of scale, efficient operations, tight cost controls, standardization
- **Lecture example: Spirit Airlines** — bare-bones flying, unbundled pricing, ultra-low fares. Every decision (no seat-back screens, fees for carry-ons) serves the cost-leadership position
- Risk: rivals match costs; technology disrupts production

#### Differentiation
- Unique value that customers will pay a premium for
- Differentiate through: product features, service, design, brand, customer experience
- **Lecture example: Apple** — premium pricing justified by ecosystem integration, design, brand identity, and user experience. Customers pay more because no other product feels quite the same
- Risk: rivals copy the differentiation; customers stop valuing the difference enough to pay more

#### Focus
- Serve a narrow segment: geographic area, customer group, or product type
- Types: Cost focus OR Differentiation focus
- Example: Genzyme focused on rare "orphan" diseases where large competitors were absent
- Risk: the niche shrinks; a broader competitor enters with more resources

#### Stuck in the Middle
Occurs when a firm tries to pursue multiple generic strategies without coherent investment in any:
- Higher cost than cost leaders
- Less distinct than differentiators
- No clear customer segment
- Leads to weak financial performance

**Trade-off insight:** Committing to one strategy means intentionally NOT pursuing others. This is uncomfortable but necessary. Firms that try to please everyone typically excel at nothing. As Michael Porter said: *"The essence of strategy is choosing what not to do."*

## Mission statement examples (from lecture)
These are real mission statements shown in class — useful for understanding what an effective mission looks like:

- **LinkedIn:** "To connect the world's professionals to make them more productive and successful." → Defines purpose AND customer outcome
- **Tesla:** "To accelerate the advent of sustainable transport by bringing compelling mass market electric cars to market as soon as possible." → Action-oriented with urgency
- **Nike:** "Bring inspiration and innovation to every athlete in the world. If you have a body, you are an athlete." → Expansive scope that broadens the market definition

**What makes these effective:** They define purpose, build motivation, provide direction and inspiration, serve as a focal point, and assist in making strategic trade-offs.

## Flashcards
#cards/MGMT
Strategic management process::Current-state evaluation → External analysis → Internal analysis → SWOT → Strategy formulation → Implementation and control.
Mission::The organization's fundamental reason for existing — its timeless purpose.
Vision::A picture of the desired future state the organization aspires to reach.
Goals::Specific, measurable desired outcomes derived from the vision.
Plans::Concrete actions and resource allocations designed to accomplish goals.
Sustainable competitive advantage::Value creation that is Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, and Non-substitutable (VRIN).
Porter's Five Forces::Threat of new entrants, supplier power, buyer power, threat of substitutes, and rivalry among existing competitors.
Resource-Based View::Competitive advantage explained through unique internal resources — tangible, intangible, and human.
SWOT analysis::Linking internal strengths and weaknesses to external opportunities and threats to guide strategic action.
Cost leadership::Competing through lowest cost across a broad market through scale, efficiency, and standardization.
Differentiation::Competing through uniqueness that customers value enough to pay a premium for.
Focus strategy::Competing in a narrow segment using either cost or differentiation emphasis.
Stuck in the middle::The weak competitive position that results from failing to commit to a clear generic strategy.
