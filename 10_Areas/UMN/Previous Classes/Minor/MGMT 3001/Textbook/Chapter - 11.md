# Chapter 11 - Organizational Change
## Textbook anchor - change mechanics
- ==Organizational change succeeds when dissatisfaction, a credible new model, and an implementation process become stronger than resistance and the costs of change.==
- *Mechanism:* the equation is multiplicative on the left side. Weak dissatisfaction, a vague destination, or a missing process can collapse the whole effort.
- Resistance is not automatically irrational. People can resist because change threatens routines, identity, status, workload, relationships, competence, or resources.
- Change leaders matter because they create movement across systems: they communicate the gap, model the new behavior, challenge old power bases, build new capabilities, and keep learning after launch.
> [!WARNING]
> A kickoff meeting is not change. The test is whether roles, routines, metrics, rewards, and everyday behavior shift after the announcement.


## Organizational change
- Organizational change is the work of adapting, innovating, and transforming so the organization remains aligned with its environment and strategy.
- The chapter connects change to globalization, regulation, technological innovation, competition, and the need for agile management.
- Organizations change to compete, improve performance, or survive.

## Why change is hard
- Organizations develop routines, structures, power relationships, and assumptions that resist movement.
- Change can create social, psychological, emotional, political, and financial costs.
- People may resist overtly or through passive-aggressive behavior.

## Change types
- **Proactive change:** started in anticipation of a future event.
- **Reactive change:** started because of a present threat or opportunity.
- **Planned change:** predetermined and usually top-down.
- **Organic change:** emerges from lower levels as people solve problems.
- **Incremental change:** small improvement during stable times.
- **Transformative change:** radical shift, often tied to major threat or opportunity.

## Change process equation
- Change needs dissatisfaction with the status quo, a new model, and a process for change.
- Those forces must be stronger than resistance and cost.
- If there is no dissatisfaction, people stay comfortable.
- If there is no model, people do not know where to go.
- If there is no process, change does not stick.

## Change leaders
- Commit to a better way.
- Challenge existing power bases and norms.
- Move beyond defined boundaries.
- Embrace tensions, hold people accountable, build new capabilities, and support continuous learning.
