# Chapter 15 - Decision Making
## Textbook anchor - decision quality
- ==Decision making is the process of choosing among alternatives under constraints of information, time, goals, risk, uncertainty, and human judgment.==
- *Mechanism:* the rational model gives structure, but bounded rationality explains why real managers often satisfice, use heuristics, rely on intuition, and negotiate with other stakeholders.
- Conditions matter:
  - *Certainty* means alternatives and outcomes are clear.
  - *Risk* means outcomes have probabilities or known uncertainty.
  - *Uncertainty* means information about outcomes is incomplete.
  - *Ambiguity* means the problem itself may be unclear.
- Biases are predictable shortcuts: availability, representativeness, anchoring/adjustment, confirmation, escalation of commitment, status quo bias, and framing.
- Organizational decisions add politics. Classical models assume optimization, administrative models accept bounded rationality, political models emphasize coalitions, and garbage-can models show how problems, solutions, and participants can collide unpredictably.
> [!TIP]
> For a decision scenario, name the decision condition first, then choose the process: rational analysis for clarity, experiments for uncertainty, outside view for bias, coalition-building when stakeholders have different goals.

## Textbook
Chapter 15 covers the theory of how decisions should be made versus the reality of how they are made, the biases that cloud judgment, and the models used within organizations.
### 1. Rational decision making
Historically, research focused on the theory of rational choice, which suggests individuals make decisions based on a rational process that optimizes self-interest. This is often measured through the expected utility model, where individuals assign numerical values to every alternative and select the one with the highest utility or payoff.

The rational decision-making process follows six distinct steps:
1. **Define the problem or opportunity:** clearly state the organizational goal or issue.
2. **Identify objectives and goals:** determine what you want to achieve.
3. **Weight objectives:** rank objectives by importance.
4. **Identify possible courses of action:** brainstorm alternatives to solve the problem.
5. **Rate each alternative:** evaluate how well each specific action achieves each specific objective.
6. **Choose the optimal decision:** calculate the weighted ratings to find the solution that mathematically maximizes value.

### 2. How managers actually make decisions

While the rational model is a structured goal, it is often impractical because managers are busy and frequently lack the time or perfect information required. This leads to the concept of **bounded rationality**: a set of constraints (like limited time, incomplete information, or limited processing ability) that complicates the rational process.

Because of these boundaries, managers often engage in **satisficing**, which is the act of choosing a solution that is "good enough" to meet minimum criteria rather than searching indefinitely for the perfect one.

#### Conditions of ambiguity

Decision making rarely happens under **conditions of certainty**, where all information is available. Instead, managers face **ambiguity**—situations where uncertainty and risk are high and the "correct" choice is not obvious.

- **Conditions of Risk:** Goals and alternatives are known, but the outcomes of each action are not fully predictable.
- **Conditions of Uncertainty:** Objectives are clear, but information about alternatives and their potential outcomes is incomplete.

#### Intuition and thin-slicing

When speed is vital, managers often use **intuitive decision making**—insights or "hunches" that are not always fully understood subconsciously. This involves **pattern recognition**, where the brain synthesizes past experiences to understand the present. Malcolm Gladwell calls this **"thin-slicing"**: the ability to find patterns based on narrow slices of experience to make rapid, often accurate, snap judgments.

### 3. How biases impact decision making

Individuals use **heuristics**, or mental "rules of thumb," to save time when making complex decisions. While efficient, these often cause systematic deviations from rationality.

#### Primary heuristics

- **Availability Heuristic:** People assess the probability of an event based on how easily examples come to mind, often favoring vivid, emotional, or recent memories.
- **Representativeness Heuristic:** The tendency to look for traits in a person or situation that match previously formed stereotypes.
- **Adjustment Heuristic:** The tendency to make estimates or choices based on an initial starting point, or "anchor," and failing to adjust away from it even when given new data.

#### Other common biases

- **Confirmation Bias:** Seeking out information that confirms a decision we have already made while ignoring disconfirming evidence.
- **Escalation of Commitment:** Continuing to invest time or money in a failing course of action to justify previous investments (sunk costs).
- **Status Quo Bias:** The tendency to favor the "here and now" and reject potential changes, even if they are beneficial, to avoid risk or embarrassment.
- **Framing:** The way information is presented significantly alters the decision. People are generally **risk-averse** when choices are framed as **gains** (saving lives) but **risk-seeking** when choices are framed as **losses** (people dying).

### 4. Emotions and social situations

- **Emotions:** Moods drastically impact judgment; people in good moods are often more optimistic, while fear or anxiety can cause risk-aversion.
- **Social Situations:** Cues from others influence decisions. The **Asch experiments** showed that people often conform to the "majority rule" even when they know the majority is wrong.
- **Appropriateness Framework:** A process of making decisions based on societal norms and expectations ("What does a person like me do in this situation?") rather than a simple calculation of utility.

### 5. Decision making in organizations

Organizational decisions are categorized by their frequency and nature:

- **Programmed Decisions:** Made in response to recurring problems using established rules and standard operating procedures (e.g., reordering office supplies).
- **Nonprogrammed Decisions:** Made in response to novel, unstructured, or poorly defined situations that require unique judgment (e.g., integrating a newly acquired firm).

#### Four models of organizational decision making

1. **Classical Model:** Aimed at maximizing economic outcomes through a purely rational, logical process.
2. **Administrative Model:** Acknowledges that managers may be unable to be perfectly rational due to bounded rationality and information constraints; it relies heavily on intuition and satisficing.
3. **Political Model:** Recognizes that most decisions involve many managers with different goals who must bargain and build **coalitions** to reach an agreement.
4. **Garbage Can Model:** Describes organizational decision making as a random flow of problems, solutions, and participants. A solution might be proposed even when no problem is specified, making the process unpredictable.

#### Improving decision-making skills

Managers can improve their results by:

- **Managing Biases:** "Unfreezing" ingrained thinking, acquiring more expertise, using statistical models, and taking an **outsider’s view** to challenge existing perspectives.
- **Preparing for Tough Calls (SCRIPTS):** A seven-step process: (1) Search for signals, (2) Find causes, (3) Evaluate risks, (4) Apply intuition/emotion, (5) Consider different perspectives, (6) Consider the time frame, and (7) Solve the problem.
- **High-Reliability Organizations (HROs):** Systems (like airlines or hospitals) that avoid catastrophic error by analyzing failures, relying on frontline experts, and embracing complexity.
- **Playfulness:** The deliberate, temporary relaxation of rules to explore new, unconventional alternatives.
