# Chapter 3 - Ethics and CSR
## Textbook anchor - ethics and CSR
- ==Ethics is about what managers and organizations should do, not merely what they are legally allowed to do.==
- *Mechanism:* ethical frameworks slow managers down by forcing them to inspect consequences, duties, character, and fairness before choosing.
- Use the four frameworks as lenses:
  - *Utilitarianism:* choose the action that produces the greatest net good, while watching for hidden harm to smaller groups.
  - *Kantianism:* ask whether the action respects duties and could be universalized without contradiction.
  - *Virtue ethics:* ask what the action says about the decision maker's character and habits.
  - *Justice:* ask whether outcomes and procedures are fair.
- CSR becomes strategic only when it affects how the firm actually operates: product choices, supply chain standards, labor practices, environmental decisions, and stakeholder relationships.
> [!WARNING]
> A legal decision can still be unethical, and a CSR message can still be empty if the operating system rewards the opposite behavior.


## Ethics and CSR
- Ethics is the study of moral standards.
- Morality is the set of standards people use to judge right and wrong.
- Managers face ethical pressure because economic, legal, and ethical obligations do not always point in the same direction.

## Why law is not enough
- Legal action is not always ethical.
- Ethical obligations can appear before a law changes.
- Business decisions should consider customers, employees, communities, investors, suppliers, and broader society.

## Ethical frameworks
### Utilitarianism
- Judges behavior by consequences.
- The moral choice produces the greatest good for the greatest number.
- Weakness: can justify harm to a minority if the majority benefits.

### Kantianism
- Judges behavior by duty, motives, and universal rules.
- The moral choice respects principles that could be applied broadly.
- Weakness: hard to apply when duties conflict or outcomes matter deeply.

### Virtue and character
- Focuses on the character of the decision maker.
- A moral person recognizes ethical elements, judges well, acts consistently, and models character for others.
- Weakness: different societies and people may disagree about which virtues matter most.

### Justice approach
- Focuses on fairness, impartiality, and equity.
- Distributive justice asks whether outcomes are fairly distributed.
- Procedural justice asks whether the process is fair.
- Weakness: equal treatment is not always enough when people have different needs or starting conditions.

## Corporate social responsibility
- CSR is the obligation to pursue policies, decisions, and actions aligned with society's objectives and values.
- CSR can improve brand, trust, employee commitment, customer loyalty, and stakeholder relationships.
- CSR must fit strategy. Symbolic CSR without operational alignment can become hollow.

## Triple bottom line
- Profit.
- People.
- Planet.
- A manager using this frame asks whether a decision creates economic, social, and environmental value.
