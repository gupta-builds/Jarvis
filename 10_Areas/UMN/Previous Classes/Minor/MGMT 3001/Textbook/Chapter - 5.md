# Chapter 5 - Business-Level Strategy
## Textbook anchor - strategy mechanics
- ==Business-level strategy answers who the firm serves, what it offers, and how it delivers value differently from rivals.==
- *Mechanism:* Chapter 5 combines external fit and internal capability. Five forces explains industry pressure; RBV and value chain explain what the firm can do about it.
- Five forces is not just a list. Read it as pressure on profit:
  - entry pressure lowers returns when barriers are weak,
  - supplier power raises input costs,
  - buyer power pushes prices and terms against the firm,
  - substitutes cap willingness to pay,
  - rivalry burns profit through price cuts, advertising, and imitation.
- RBV asks whether the firm's resources and capabilities can support a position that competitors cannot easily copy.
- Value chain analysis asks where value is created, lost, or made hard to imitate across primary and support activities.
> [!TIP]
> In a strategy scenario, do this order: identify the customer and offer, scan the five forces, identify the firm's resource/capability base, then choose cost leadership, differentiation, or focus.
> [!WARNING]
> "Stuck in the middle" does not mean a firm cannot be efficient and distinctive. It means the firm's activities do not reinforce a clear competitive position.

## Textbook
Chapter 5 focuses on how a company competes within its specific industry to build a sustainable competitive advantage. This involves evaluating industry attractiveness and determining a unique position relative to competitors.
### 1. Core of business-level strategy
To develop an effective strategy, a manager must answer three fundamental questions:
- Who do we serve? (Choosing a broad or narrow market segment).
- What do we provide? (Determining the range of products or services).
- How do we provide it? (Defining unique production or delivery processes).

The answers determine whether a firm follows a low-cost, differentiation, or focused strategy. Success often depends on a firm's ability to adapt to changes in the marketplace, as seen in the contrast between Starbucks, which evolved its strategy for a leaner economy, and Krispy Kreme, which initially failed to adjust to changing consumer dieting fads.
### 2. Evaluating industry attractiveness: Porter's five forces
Michael Porter's five forces model assesses an industry's long-term profit potential by exploring five basic competitive forces.
- **Threat of new entrants:** new competitors can decrease profits for everyone by increasing supply. Barriers to entry include supply-side economies of scale, demand-side benefits of scale, customer switching costs, capital requirements, incumbency advantages, unequal access to distribution channels, and restrictive government policy.
- **Bargaining power of suppliers:** suppliers are powerful if they are more concentrated than the industry they sell to, if there are no substitutes for their products, or if switching costs are high.
- **Threat of substitutes:** a substitute is a different product that meets the same need (e.g., Uber substituting for taxis). Substitutes place a ceiling on the prices an industry can charge.
- **Bargaining power of customers:** customers have power when they purchase in large volumes or when products are undifferentiated, allowing them to force prices down.
- **Rivalry among existing competitors:** competition is most intense when products lack differentiation, fixed costs are high, the product is perishable, or exit barriers are high.

The five forces tool is a starting point to identify the strengths and weaknesses of an industry, and it can also highlight which forces a firm might try to modify. Threat of entry depends not only on barriers, but also on the reaction existing competitors can be expected to make (retaliation).
### 3. Internal environment: the resource-based view (RBV)
The RBV argues that a firm's internal resources are strengths that firms can use to conceive of and implement their strategies. Resources are classified into three categories:
- **Tangible resources:** recognizable assets like real estate, inventory, and manufacturing plants.
- **Intangible resources:** less visible assets like internal processes, brand names, technology, and culture.
- **Human resources:** the collective skills, knowledge, and expertise of all employees.

Sustainable competitive advantage occurs when these resources fit together into core competencies that are difficult for rivals to replicate.
### 4. The firm as a value chain
Value chain analysis is a systematic way to examine all activities a firm performs to understand costs and potential sources of differentiation.
- **Primary activities:** directly involved in creating and selling the product — inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing and sales, and service.
- **Support activities:** necessary for primary activities to occur — procurement, technological development, human resource management, and firm infrastructure.

After mapping the value chain, managers should (1) compare the firm's value chain to competitors' chains, and (2) place it in the broader value system (suppliers + distribution channels) to understand linkages that can strengthen advantage.
### 5. SWOT analysis
A SWOT analysis provides a snapshot of a firm's strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats. It helps managers identify their unique selling proposition and develop strategic flexibility to react to environmental changes.

SWOT works best when it becomes the basis of strategic action and is revisited regularly. The chapter's SWOT bridge: connect internal strengths/weaknesses to external opportunities/threats, including your position relative to the five forces.
### 6. Generic competitive strategies
Competitive advantage comes from creating value — providing a product for a lower price than rivals or offering unique benefits that justify a higher cost. Three generic strategies:
- **Cost leadership:** providing products at the lowest possible price to a broad audience by exploiting economies of scale and efficient distribution.
- **Differentiation:** seeking to be unique in ways valued by consumers to command a premium price. This can be based on product features, service, or reputation.
- **Focus:** aiming sales efforts at a specific geographical region, group of purchasers, or product type. This can be a low-cost focus or a differentiation focus.

Firms that fail to choose and master one of these strategies are considered "stuck in the middle." The chapter notes a nuance: some firms in the digital economy can integrate low-cost + differentiation for narrowly targeted groups because web tech reduces targeting costs. Generic strategies can reduce (not eliminate) the impact of the five forces.
### 7. The role of employees
A firm's chosen strategy dictates its internal culture. A cost leadership strategy requires tight controls and cost minimization, favoring employees who thrive in disciplined environments. A differentiation strategy requires a culture that encourages innovation and risk-taking, appealing to creative and autonomous individuals.
