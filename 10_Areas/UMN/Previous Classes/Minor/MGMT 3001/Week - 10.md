# Entire Week
## What you must be able to do
- Define conflict and negotiation.
- Distinguish positions from interests.
- Distinguish task/cognitive conflict from relationship/affective conflict.
- Choose a conflict management style based on assertiveness, cooperativeness, time, relationship, issue importance, and power.
- Explain distributive negotiation and the bargaining zone.
- Use BATNA, reservation value, and multiple interests in negotiation.
- Identify negotiation mistakes such as anchoring, framing, escalation, and overconfidence.

## Key ideas (short)
- Conflict is not automatically bad. Task conflict can improve work when it stays depersonalized.
- Relationship conflict is dangerous because it steals attention from the task.
- Negotiation gets better when you understand interests, not just positions.
- BATNA and reservation value protect you from bad agreements.

## Lecture
### 1. Conflict starts with perceived opposition
The slides define conflict as a process where one party perceives that its interests are opposed or negatively affected by another party. The transcript adds a practical point: conflict is easier to work through when you understand the why underneath each side.

### 2. Positions and interests
- **Position:** what a person wants.
- **Interest:** why the person wants it.

The salary example makes this concrete. "I need $75,000" is not the whole conflict. The interests might be paying loans, affording rent, feeling fairly valued, or matching market rates.

### 3. Task conflict vs relationship conflict
- **Task/cognitive conflict:** issue-based disagreement about ideas, plans, or projects. It is depersonalized and can stimulate creativity, constructive criticism, and better decision quality.
- **Relationship/affective conflict:** personal tension rooted in anger, friction, personality clashes, or threat. It interferes with effort and attention.

The main management task is not to eliminate conflict. It is to keep useful conflict focused on the work.

### 4. Conflict management styles
The styles sit on two axes: assertiveness and cooperativeness.

- **Collaborating/problem solving:** high assertiveness, high cooperativeness.
- **Competing/forcing:** high assertiveness, low cooperativeness.
- **Accommodating:** low assertiveness, high cooperativeness.
- **Avoiding:** low assertiveness, low cooperativeness.
- **Compromising:** middle position.

### 5. Choosing a conflict approach
The lecture tells you to consider:
- Issue importance.
- Relationship importance.
- Relative power.
- Time constraints.

Assertive approaches include forcing and problem solving. Cooperative approaches include accommodating and problem solving. High-power approaches may include forcing or accommodating. When there is no time for problem solving, people often turn to forcing, accommodating, or avoiding.

### 6. Managing conflict on teams
Teams can reduce destructive conflict by:
- Training emotional management skills.
- Building early cohesion or psychological safety.
- Creating norms and routines for constructive debate.
- Emphasizing superordinate identity and goals.

This connects back to Week 5. Team conflict is easier to manage when trust and norms already exist.

### 7. Negotiation
Negotiation is a process where two parties try to reach agreement by offering and reviewing positions or courses of action.

Key definitions:
- **Issue:** subject of discussion.
- **Position:** perspective taken on the issue.
- **Interests:** underlying reasons or needs.

### 8. Distributive negotiation
Distributive negotiation is the fixed-pie model:
1. Resources are distributed.
2. There is focus on winning.
3. There are limits.
4. There is a bargaining zone framework.

This often appears around one visible issue: money, space, time, or location.

### 9. Bargaining zone
- Each side has a walk-away point.
- Positive bargaining zone: acceptable positions overlap.
- Negative bargaining zone: no overlap, so settlement is not possible.

### 10. Bargaining effectively
The lecture gives a full process:
1. Assess your BATNA.
2. Calculate your reservation value.
3. Assess the other party's BATNA.
4. Estimate the other party's reservation value.
5. Evaluate the zone of possible agreement.
6. Identify multiple interests.
7. Create a scoring system.
8. Calculate a package reservation value.
9. Identify the other party's multiple interests.

### 11. Costly mistakes
- Irrational escalation of commitment.
- Anchoring and adjustment.
- Framing.
- Availability of information problems.
- Negotiator overconfidence.

### 12. Negotiating across cultures
The lecture closes by warning that culture affects position and process. Watch for:
- Egalitarian vs hierarchical expectations.
- Low-context vs high-context norms.

## Leadership / entrepreneurship / speaking links
### Leadership link
- Leaders set the conflict climate. If leaders punish dissent, task conflict disappears until mistakes become expensive.

### Entrepreneurship link
- Cofounder conflict, hiring negotiations, vendor terms, customer pricing, and investor conversations all require BATNA thinking.
- Startups need fast conflict repair because unresolved relationship conflict spreads into execution.

### Speaking skill link
- Name the interest behind your position. This makes you sound clearer and makes the negotiation less personal.
