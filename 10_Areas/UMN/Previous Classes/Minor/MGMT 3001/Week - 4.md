# Week 4 - Corporate Strategy and Vertical Integration
## What you must be able to do
- [[50_Archive/Previous Classes/Minor/MGMT 3001/Textbook/Chapter - 6|Chapter - 6]]  
- Distinguish business-level strategy from corporate-level strategy.  
- Explain why firms pursue diversification and when diversification creates value.  
- Compare single-business, related diversification, and unrelated diversification.  
- Apply the diversification test: attractiveness, cost of entry, and better-off test.  
- Explain vertical integration, including backward vs forward integration.  
- Weigh the benefits and risks of vertical integration using administrative costs vs transaction costs.  
- Recognize alternatives to ownership such as outsourcing, alliances, and joint ventures.  
- Explain why firms internationalize at the corporate level.  
- Distinguish intended, deliberate, emergent, unrealized, and realized strategy.  
- Connect strategy formulation to implementation through structure, human resources, and information/control systems.  
## Key ideas (short)  
- Business-level strategy asks **how to compete**; corporate-level strategy asks **what businesses or industries to be in** and how to coordinate them.  
- Corporate-level strategy is about scope, resource coordination, and ownership choices across multiple businesses.  
- Diversification only makes sense when the combined businesses are worth more together than apart.  
- Related diversification usually creates more value than unrelated diversification because firms can share resources and capabilities.  
- Vertical integration is useful when owning inputs or distribution lowers transaction problems enough to justify the extra coordination burden.  
- Partnerships can give access, speed, and flexibility without full ownership.  
- Internationalization is often used to find new markets, scale operations, and benefit from local advantages.  
- Strategy is not finished once chosen; implementation and control determine whether intended strategy becomes realized strategy.

## Examples worth keeping  
- **McDonald’s:** single core business in fast food, related diversification into real estate, elements of vertical integration through supplier coordination, partnerships for growth in China, and internationalization through localized products.  
- **Amazon:** example of broad diversification across retail, hardware, grocery, media, and digital services.  
- **ESPN:** useful for understanding emergent strategy; it began with a narrower idea and evolved into a larger sports media business as new opportunities appeared.  
## Lecture  
- Strategic management process links current state evaluation, internal/external analysis, strategy formulation, and implementation/control.  
- Corporate-level strategy options from lecture: single business, diversification, vertical integration, partnership, and internationalization.  
- Related diversification aims for economies of scope; unrelated diversification aims for financial economies.  
- Diversification should be judged by industry attractiveness, entry cost, and whether the firm will truly be better off.  
- Vertical integration provides more control and can reduce transaction costs, but may increase administrative burden and reduce flexibility.  
- Partnerships sit on a spectrum from low-collaboration supplier arrangements to high-collaboration mergers and acquisitions.  
- Internationalization is often pursued for new markets, economies of scale, and location/resource advantages.  
- After strategy formulation, leaders still need structure, human resource systems, and information/control systems to make the strategy work.  
- Real outcomes often reflect a mix of deliberate plans and emergent learning.  
## Textbook additions that are easy to miss  
### Why firms diversify  
- Firms may diversify to create **synergy**, where shared activities or shared resources reduce cost or improve performance.  
- Diversification can also support growth, spread risk, and sometimes increase market power.  
- Not every diversification decision is value-creating; some are driven by managerial self-interest rather than firm advantage.  
### Portfolio thinking  
- The chapter introduces the **BCG Growth-Share Matrix** as a way to think about a portfolio of businesses:  
- **Stars:** high growth, high share  
- **Cash Cows:** low growth, high share  
- **Question Marks:** high growth, low share  
- **Dogs:** low growth, low share  
- This helps leaders think about where cash is generated, where investment is needed, and what businesses may need to be sold.  
### Diversification results  
- The relationship between diversification and performance is often described as an **inverted U-shape**:  
- moving from a single-business strategy to related diversification often improves performance  
- moving too far into unrelated diversification often reduces performance  
- Main reason: related businesses can share capabilities better, while unrelated businesses are harder to coordinate meaningfully.  
### Ownership is a choice  
- A major corporate-level question is not just **what market to enter**, but **whether to own, partner, or contract**.  
- The ownership test matters because firms sometimes gain more from alliances or outsourcing than from acquisition.  
### Alternatives to vertical integration  
- **Outsourcing:** buy the activity from outside instead of doing it internally.  
- **Spot contracts:** simple market transactions for specific inputs or services.  
- These options preserve flexibility when full ownership would be too costly or rigid.  
## Leadership / entrepreneurship / speaking links  
### Leadership link  
- Corporate-level strategy needs strong **conceptual skill** because leaders are choosing scope, fit, tradeoffs, and resource allocation across multiple units.  
- It also needs communication skill because leaders must explain **why** the firm is entering, exiting, partnering, or integrating.  
### Entrepreneurship link  
- Founders often start with a single-product strategy.  
- As they scale, they face corporate-level questions: expand product lines, acquire, partner, outsource, or integrate.  
- A good founder does not expand just because growth sounds attractive; they ask whether the expansion creates real advantage.  
### Speaking skill link  
- When presenting a strategy recommendation, use this order:  
1. What is the current business position?  
2. What new option is being considered?  
3. What value is expected: scope, scale, control, or market access?  
4. What are the risks and costs?  
5. Why is ownership, partnership, or outsourcing the best fit?  
- This structure makes strategy explanations clearer and more persuasive.

## Lecture-to-textbook synthesis
- ==Definition anchor: corporate-level strategy decides the firm's scope - what businesses to be in, what to own, and how to coordinate across units.==
- *Mechanism:* the lecture and textbook move from business-level positioning to corporate boundaries. Diversification asks whether multiple businesses create more value together; vertical integration asks whether the firm should own upstream inputs or downstream distribution.
- Lecture example/scenario: a startup deciding whether to build an internal sales team or use channel partners is already making a corporate-scope decision. The question is not "can we own it?" but "does ownership create enough value to justify complexity?"

## Flashcards
#cards/MGMT 
1. Business-level vs corporate-level strategy::Business-level strategy is how a firm competes in one business; corporate-level strategy is what businesses it should be in and how those businesses should be coordinated.  
2. Corporate advantage::Value created when a firm’s collection of businesses performs better together than they would separately.  
3. Single-business strategy::A firm focuses on one main product/market, building depth but facing more cyclicality risk.  
4. Related diversification::Owning businesses that share similar resources/capabilities to create economies of scope.  
5. Unrelated diversification::Owning businesses with little connection, usually to create financial economies.  
6. Economies of scope::Cost or value benefits created by sharing activities or resources across businesses.  
7. Diversification test::Attractiveness, cost of entry, and better-off test.  
8. Backward integration::Owning or controlling inputs needed for production.  
9. Forward integration::Owning or controlling distribution channels or customer-facing stages.  
10. Administrative costs::Internal coordination costs created by managing multiple linked units.  
11. Transaction costs::Costs of buying from outside suppliers or contractors.  
12. Outsourcing::Using an external firm to perform an activity instead of doing it internally.  
13. Emergent strategy::A pattern of action that develops over time rather than being fully planned in advance.  
14. Realized strategy::The strategy the firm actually ends up following after deliberate and emergent elements combine.
