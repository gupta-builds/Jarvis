# Entire Week
## What you must be able to do
- Explain why organizations change: compete, improve performance, or survive.
- Distinguish proactive vs reactive change, planned vs organic change, and incremental vs transformative change.
- Apply the change process equation to a real organization.
- Explain why resistance happens and why change has social, psychological, emotional, political, and financial costs.
- Describe what effective change leaders do.
- Connect change management to culture, strategy, power, and decision making.

## Key ideas (short)
- Change is necessary because the environment moves: globalization, technology, regulation, competition, and internal practices shift.
- Change is not an event. It is a process that needs dissatisfaction, a new model, and implementation.
- Resistance is not just stubbornness. People often resist because change imposes real costs.
- Strong change leaders do not only announce change; they build the conditions for it to stick.

## Lecture
### 1. The lecture starts with why change is unavoidable
The lecture frames change management as a response to internal and external movement. External forces include globalization and technology. Internal forces include organizational practices and the need for innovation. This connects directly to earlier weeks: strategy must fit the environment, culture shapes behavior, and leaders must move people through uncertainty.

Organizations begin change initiatives for three big reasons:
- To compete more effectively.
- To improve performance.
- To survive.

Netflix is the cleanest lecture example. If Netflix had stayed with DVDs by mail, it would not fit the current environment. The point is not "streaming is good"; the point is that organizations must notice when the old model no longer fits.

### 2. Change triggers: proactive vs reactive
- **Proactive change** is initiated because leaders anticipate an event or opportunity.
- **Reactive change** is initiated in response to a known threat or opportunity.

Proactive change is usually stronger because the organization has more time to build capacity. Reactive change may be necessary, but it often starts with pressure already high.

### 3. Origination: planned vs organic
- **Planned change** is predetermined and usually rolled out top-down.
- **Organic change** emerges from lower in the organization as people innovate, solve problems, or seek better ways of working.

The Domino's example matters because it shows that useful change does not always begin with formal executives. Sometimes the people closest to customers or operations see the problem first.

### 4. Magnitude: incremental, disruptive, transformative
- **Incremental change** is small improvement, usually during stable times.
- **Disruptive change** forces significant organizational adjustment.
- **Transformative change** is radical or disruptive change, often in response to a major competitive threat or major opportunity.

The practical question is not just "are we changing?" It is "how much system movement does this change require?"

### 5. The change process equation
The slides describe change as:

Dissatisfaction with the status quo x New model for the organization x Process for change > Resistance to change + Cost of change

This matters because the first three terms multiply. If dissatisfaction is weak, the model is vague, or the process is missing, the change effort loses force.

### 6. Dissatisfaction
Dissatisfaction provides energy for breaking organizational inertia. It is higher in crisis situations, but crisis is not the only path. Leaders can build dissatisfaction by showing the gap between current performance and needed performance.

For dissatisfaction to gain traction, it must extend beyond a few scattered individuals. One frustrated person is a complaint. Shared recognition of a real gap can become momentum.

### 7. New model for change
The model explains what is changing and why. It should show where the organization wants to go and what it needs to do to get there.

A weak model sounds like: "we need to be more innovative." A stronger model explains which practices, roles, metrics, customer promises, or capabilities will change.

### 8. Process for change
The process includes:
- Detailed implementation plan.
- Communication process.
- Mechanism for measuring progress.
- Embedding behaviors so the organization does not fall back into old patterns.

This is where the lecture is blunt: change is not an event. A launch meeting is not the change. The change is the repeated behavior that survives after the meeting.

### 9. Resistance and cost
Resistance originates at the individual level and grows when aggregated across groups. People may resist by refusing openly or by passive-aggressive behavior.

Costs include:
- Social cost.
- Psychological cost.
- Emotional cost.
- Political cost.
- Financial cost.

This is why leaders should not treat resistance as merely irrational. A change can threaten status, identity, routines, relationships, and resources.

### 10. Change leaders
The lecture gives three core traits:
1. Commitment to a better way.
2. Courage to challenge existing power bases and norms.
3. Personal initiative to move beyond defined boundaries.

The HBR-style slide adds that strong change leaders:
- Embrace tensions.
- Hold everyone accountable.
- Invest in new organizational capabilities.
- Emphasize continuous learning.

### 11. Change tensions
The lecture lists tensions that make change hard:
- Revitalization vs normalization.
- Globalization vs simplification.
- Innovation vs regulation.
- Optimization vs rationalization.
- Digitization vs humanization.

Good change leaders do not pretend these tensions disappear. They name them and manage through them.

### 12. Skills of change managers
The lecture closes with practical skills:
- Clear communication and active listening.
- Emotional intelligence.
- Organization.
- Eye for detail.
- Problem solving and decision making.
- Delegation without micromanagement.

## Leadership / entrepreneurship / speaking links
### Leadership link
- Change requires trust, communication, and power. Leaders must explain the gap, model the new direction, and change systems so the behavior sticks.

### Entrepreneurship link
- A startup pivot is a change-management problem. You still need dissatisfaction, a new model, and process.
- Founder trap: calling every new idea a pivot without changing roles, metrics, customer focus, or behavior.

### Speaking skill link
- When explaining a change, use this order: why the current state no longer fits, what the new model is, what the process is, what resistance/costs are real, and how progress will be measured.
