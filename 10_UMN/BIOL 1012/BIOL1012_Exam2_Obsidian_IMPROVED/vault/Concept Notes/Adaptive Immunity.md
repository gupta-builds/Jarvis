# Adaptive Immunity

## Immune 6 — Antigen Definition and Examples

### What is an Antigen?
- A **small, specific molecular feature** recognized by adaptive immune cells.
- Usually a fragment of a molecule found on pathogens or foreign cells.
- "Anti-gen" = "produces against" (it produces an immune response against itself).

### Examples of Antigens
- A short sequence (~8 amino acids) from a protein found only on a specific bacterial species.
- A small segment of carbohydrate from the cell wall of a specific bacterium.
- A viral surface protein fragment found on a specific virus but never on human cells.
- A cell-surface marker from another person's tissue (relevant to transplants).
- Markers on cancerous cells that are abnormal or absent on normal cells.

### Broad foreign molecules (innate) vs. specific antigens (adaptive)
- Innate: recognizes **broad categories** (bacterial cell walls, viral proteins never in human cells, cancer cell proteins).
- Adaptive: recognizes **specific antigens** — just a few amino acids from one particular pathogen protein.

## Immune 7 — BCRs, TCRs, and Antibodies

### B Cell Receptors (BCRs)
- Receptor proteins on the **surface membrane** of B cells.
- Each B cell has receptors with **one specificity** (binds one antigen).
- Structure includes:
  - **Variable region** — unique to each B cell; determines which antigen is recognized.
  - **Constant region** — same in all B cells.
  - **Transmembrane region** — anchors receptor in membrane; transmits signal inside the cell when antigen binds.

### T Cell Receptors (TCRs)
- Receptor proteins on the **surface membrane** of T cells (both helper T and cytotoxic T).
- Also antigen-specific; also have variable, constant, and transmembrane regions.
- **Stay on the cell surface — never secreted.**
- T cells encounter antigens when phagocytes display antigen fragments to them.

### Antibodies (Immunoglobulins)
- Proteins **secreted by activated B cells** (effector B cells = plasma cells).
- Same antigen specificity as the BCR of the B cell that made them.
- **Travel freely in body fluids** (blood, lymph) — can reach antigens far from the B cell.
- Functions:
  - **Neutralize pathogens** (block them from entering cells).
  - **Promote phagocytosis** (coat antigens so phagocytes engulf them more easily = opsonization).
  - **Activate complement** → triggers complement cascade → opsonization, inflammation, cytolysis.
  - **Clump pathogens together** (agglutination) to keep them from spreading.

### Key Distinction
| Feature | BCR | TCR | Antibody |
|---------|-----|-----|----------|
| Location | B cell membrane | T cell membrane | Secreted into body fluids |
| Moves freely? | No | No | **Yes** |
| Antigen specificity | Yes | Yes | Yes (same as B cell that made it) |

## Immune 8 — B Cells, Helper T Cells, and Cytotoxic T Cells

### B Cells
- Best for pathogens **outside cells, in body fluids** (blood, lymph).
- Attack mechanism: **secrete antibodies**.
- Antibodies: neutralize, promote phagocytosis, activate complement.
- B cells can also **phagocytose** microbes directly.
- **Effective especially against bacteria** (which typically live outside human cells).

### Helper T Cells (TH cells)
- **Do NOT directly kill** pathogens or infected cells.
- Attack mechanism: **secrete cytokines** that stimulate other immune cells.
- Cytokines from helper T cells activate:
  - **B cells** → make more antibodies faster.
  - **Cytotoxic T cells** → enhanced killing of infected cells.
  - **Innate immune phagocytes** → more phagocytic activity.
- Helper T cells are the **coordinators** of the immune response.

> **HIV/AIDS Connection:** HIV preferentially infects **Helper T cells**. When helper T cells are depleted, B cells and cytotoxic T cells cannot be properly activated. The immune system loses its ability to fight even mild infections → patients die from **opportunistic infections** that a healthy immune system would control.

### Cytotoxic T Cells (Killer T cells, TC cells)
- Directly **kill infected human cells** and cancerous/abnormal cells.
- Mechanism: **inject toxic granules** into target cells → triggers cell death (apoptosis).
- Best for pathogens **inside human cells**, especially **viruses** (viruses must enter cells to replicate).
- Recognize infected cells by detecting antigens displayed on the cell surface.

### Who does what?

| Function | B Cells | Helper T Cells | Cytotoxic T Cells |
|---------|---------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Directly kill cells?** | No | No | **Yes** |
| **Secret important molecules?** | **Antibodies** | **Cytokines** | Toxic granules |
| **Best against...** | Extracellular pathogens (bacteria) | Coordination | Intracellular pathogens (viruses) |
| **Make memory?** | Yes | Yes | Yes |

## Immune 9 — Four Steps in Adaptive Immune Response

### The 4 Steps (for ALL lymphocytes)

### Step 1: Encounter
- A lymphocyte's receptor binds its **matching antigen** — lock-and-key specificity.
- Only the lymphocyte(s) with the matching receptor will respond.
- T cells encounter antigens that have been **displayed by phagocytes** (innate-adaptive cross-talk).
- B cells encounter antigens directly.

### Step 2: Activate
- Binding of antigen to receptor triggers **activation**.
- **Clonal expansion:** the lymphocyte undergoes multiple rounds of cell division → thousands of identical copies = a **clone**.
- All clone cells have the **same receptor** and can bind the same antigen.
- Some become **effector cells** (fight immediately); others become **memory cells** (for future responses).
- This is why high WBC counts appear during infection — clonal expansion has occurred.

### Step 3: Attack

| Cell | Attack Mechanism |
|------|-----------------|
| **Effector B cells (Plasma cells)** | Secrete antibodies; can also phagocytose |
| **Effector Helper T cells** | Secrete cytokines that stimulate B cells, cytotoxic T cells, and innate cells |
| **Effector Cytotoxic T cells** | Inject toxic granules → kill infected/cancerous cells |

- Antibodies can activate complement → triggering **phagocytosis, inflammation, and cytolysis** simultaneously (antigen-antibody complex activates all three).

### Step 4: Memory
- Memory B and T cells survive long-term (weeks to years).
- They **lay in wait** until the same antigen appears again.
- See Immune 11 for the secondary response explanation.

## Immune 10 — Missing Immune Cells: Predicted Signs and Symptoms

| Missing Cell/Function | Predicted Signs/Symptoms |
|----------------------|-------------------------|
| **B cells / antibodies** | Trouble controlling extracellular bacteria, repeated infections, weak long-term protection |
| **Helper T cells** | Very weak overall immune response (both B and cytotoxic T cells under-activated); fatal in HIV/AIDS from opportunistic infections |
| **Cytotoxic T cells** | Difficulty clearing virus-infected cells; cancer cells may evade elimination |
| **NK cells** | Virus-infected or cancerous cells survive longer; poor early viral control |
| **Phagocytes** | Poor initial clearance of microbes; debris accumulation |
| **Memory cells** | Every re-exposure behaves like a first exposure — slow, weak response each time |
| **Complement** | Poor opsonization; harder for phagocytes; reduced innate attack on microbes |

## Immune 11 — Primary vs. Secondary Immune Response

### Primary Response
- Occurs on **first exposure** to an antigen.
- Takes time: rare matching lymphocytes must be found, clonally expanded, and differentiated.
- Slower, lower antibody peak, shorter duration.

### Secondary Response
- Occurs on **re-exposure** to the same antigen.
- Memory cells already exist → they activate quickly → massive clonal expansion begins almost immediately.
- **Faster, higher antibody peak, longer-lasting protection.**

### Graph Pattern to Know (Figure 6)
- Primary response: small, delayed rise in antibodies.
- Secondary response: faster rise, much higher peak, sustained elevation.

### 3 Differences Between Primary and Secondary Response (Lecture Emphasis)
1. **Speed:** Secondary is faster.
2. **Magnitude:** Secondary produces higher antibody concentrations (more antibodies per B cell, more B cells making antibodies).
3. **Duration:** Secondary response lasts longer.

### What measurements show the difference?
- Antibody concentration over time (Figure 6).
- Speed of rise after exposure.
- Peak antibody level.
- How long antibody levels remain elevated.

### Why is the secondary response stronger?
- Clonal expansion during the **primary response** generated **many memory cells**.
- All these memory cells carry the same receptor → when they see the antigen again, **all of them activate at once** → massive rapid response.

## Key Cross-Talk: Innate ↔ Adaptive

- Phagocytes (innate) engulf pathogens → display antigens → activate T cells (adaptive).
- Helper T cells (adaptive) secrete cytokines → stimulate phagocytes, B cells, cytotoxic T cells.
- B cell antibodies activate **complement** (innate) → promote phagocytosis, inflammation, cytolysis.
- This integration means neither system works optimally without the other.
