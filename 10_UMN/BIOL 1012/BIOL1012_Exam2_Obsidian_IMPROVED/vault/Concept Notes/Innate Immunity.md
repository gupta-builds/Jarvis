# Innate Immunity

## Immune 1 — Three Lines of Defense

### Line 1: Physical and Chemical Barriers (Innate)
- Keep pathogens from **entering** the body.
- Examples: skin, mucus, saliva, tears, digestive enzymes, stomach acid, beneficial microbes (gut flora), cilia in respiratory tract.
- Note: the inside of the digestive tract is technically *outside* the body (open on both ends).

### Line 2: Internal Defenses of Innate Immunity (Innate)
- Act after pathogens **get inside**.
- **Non-specific** — respond to broad categories of foreign molecules, not individual pathogens.
- Includes cells, proteins, and responses listed below.

### Line 3: Adaptive Immune System (Specific)
- Recognizes **specific antigens** on individual pathogens.
- Has **memory** — second response is faster and stronger.
- See [[Adaptive Immunity]].

---

## Immune 2 — Innate vs. Adaptive: Full Comparison

| Feature | Innate Immune System | Adaptive Immune System |
|---------|---------------------|----------------------|
| **Speed (1st exposure)** | Fast (immediate to hours) | Slow (days) |
| **Specificity** | Non-specific — broad patterns | Specific — individual antigens |
| **What it recognizes** | Broad categories: cell wall carbohydrates, proteins only on cancer cells, viral proteins not found in human cells | Specific antigens: e.g., 8 amino acids from one bacterial protein, or a specific viral surface protein |
| **Memory** | No true memory — same response speed/strength if exposed again | Yes — second response is faster and stronger |
| **Cells involved** | Mast cells, phagocytes (neutrophils, macrophages), NK cells | B cells, Helper T cells, Cytotoxic T cells (lymphocytes) |

## Immune 3 — Phagocytes and Natural Killer Cells

### Phagocytes
- Include **neutrophils** and **macrophages** (macrophages mature from monocytes).
- Lecture memory cue: **"Number Munchers"** — they engulf and destroy.
- Function:
  - **Phagocytose** (engulf and destroy) microbes and cellular debris.
  - Present pathogen information to adaptive immune cells (helps activate adaptive response).
  - Respond to cytokines from helper T cells to become more active.

### Natural Killer (NK) Cells
- Kill **infected human cells** and **cancerous cells**.
- Recognize **broad defects** — not one specific antigen.
- Key mechanism: they check for **HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen) "name tags"** on cells.
  - Normal healthy cells display HLA → NK cell recognizes it → **does NOT kill**.
  - Virus-infected or cancerous cells often lose or alter HLA → NK cell **does not detect normal HLA tag → KILLS the cell**.
- This is distinct from cytotoxic T cells, which recognize specific antigens on cells.

## Immune 4 — Complement Proteins, Interferons, and Cytokines

### Complement Proteins
A group of blood proteins that defend against microbes through **three main mechanisms:**

1. **Opsonization** — coat the surface of a microbe, making it easier for phagocytes to engulf it.
2. **Trigger inflammation** — complement binding releases signaling molecules that recruit leukocytes to the site.
3. **Cytolysis** — assemble a pore in the microbial membrane, causing the cell to burst.

> *Can also target viruses.*  
> Antibodies (from B cells) can also activate complement — linking innate and adaptive defenses.

### Interferons
- A special type of **cytokine** released by virus-infected cells.
- Function: **inhibit viral replication** in neighboring cells.
- Warn nearby cells that a virus is present, prompting those cells to activate antiviral defenses.
- Part of the innate **antiviral response**.

### Cytokines
- Signaling proteins released by many immune cells (macrophages, mast cells, T cells, and others).
- Functions:
  - **Communicate** between immune cells.
  - **Amplify** immune responses — increase activity of B cells, cytotoxic T cells, phagocytes.
  - **Recruit** more immune cells to the site.
  - **Link innate and adaptive responses** — cytokines from phagocytes activate T cells.

## Immune 5 — Fever and the Inflammatory Response

### Inflammatory Response
- Triggered by **trauma, irritation, or pathogen entry**.
- Steps:
  1. Pathogens/damage cause **mast cells** to release chemicals (including histamine).
  2. **Cytokines** released by immune cells trigger more cytokine release and enhanced immune activity.
  3. **Capillaries become more permeable** (leaky).
  4. Serum leaks out of capillaries into tissues → **swelling, pain**.
  5. Increased blood flow → **heat, redness**.
  6. **Leukocytes exit** capillaries through the permeable walls and enter infected/damaged tissue.
  7. Phagocytes perform **phagocytosis** of microbes and debris.

- Cardinal signs of inflammation: **heat, redness, swelling, pain**.
- Purpose: contain infection, recruit more defenders, begin repair.

### Fever
- Cytokines signal the **hypothalamus** (the body's thermostat) to raise body temperature.
- Benefits:
  - Makes environment less favorable for many pathogens.
  - Speeds up immune cell activity and tissue repair.
- Risk: **excessive or prolonged fever is harmful** to the body.

### Cytokine Storm
- An **overactive, systemic inflammatory response** — seen in severe COVID-19 cases.
- Circulating cytokines and hyperactive immune cells cause system-wide damage.
- Consequences: **rapid loss of blood pressure, ARDS (Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome), organ failure, death**.
- This is an example of the immune system *causing* serious harm when not properly regulated.

## How Innate Immunity Activates Adaptive Immunity

- Phagocytes engulf pathogens → **display antigen fragments** on their surface.
- These antigen-displaying phagocytes interact with T cells → **activates adaptive immune response**.
- Cytokines from innate cells also stimulate T and B cell responses.
- This is called **"cross-talk"** between innate and adaptive immunity.

## Summary: Innate Immune Cells and Molecules

| Component | Main Function |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Barriers** | Block entry of pathogens |
| **Mast cells** | Release histamine; trigger inflammation |
| **Phagocytes** (neutrophils, macrophages) | Engulf and destroy microbes; present antigens |
| **NK cells** | Kill infected or abnormal cells (check HLA tags) |
| **Complement proteins** | Opsonize, trigger inflammation, lyse microbes |
| **Interferons** | Slow viral replication in nearby cells |
| **Cytokines** | Signal and coordinate immune responses |
| **Inflammatory response** | Recruit defenders, isolate damage, start repair |
| **Fever** | Create hostile environment for pathogens, speed repair |

---

## Signs of Missing Innate Immune Functions

- Missing phagocytes → poor early pathogen clearance, debris accumulation.
- Missing NK cells → more virus-infected or cancerous cells surviving.
- Missing complement → poor opsonization, harder for phagocytes to engulf microbes.
- Missing interferons → faster viral spread between cells.
- Missing mast cell signaling → blunted early inflammatory response.
