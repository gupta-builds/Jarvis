## Key ideas
### DNA structure + “genetic code” (Obj: DNA structure, nucleotide, code, mutation, gene/allele/locus)
- DNA is built from **nucleotides**.
	- Nucleotide = **sugar + phosphate + base**
	- Bases = **A, T, C, G**
	- The **sequence/order of bases** is the “genetic code.”
- DNA is stored as chromosomes (humans: **23 pairs**).
- **Mutation** = a change in DNA sequence (often replication error). If it happens in sperm/egg, it can be inherited.
- Terminology you must say precisely:
	- **Gene** = DNA segment that helps produce a trait (often via a protein).
	- **Allele** = version of a gene.
	- **Locus** = the gene’s physical location on a chromosome.
### How cells “know” what proteins to make (Obj: protein choice, differentiation)
- All (most) body cells contain the same genome, but different cell types look/act different because they:
	- **use different subsets of genes**
	- make different sets/amounts of proteins
- High-level flow you should be able to explain in plain language:
	- DNA information is “read” to produce molecules that cells use to build proteins (proteins drive cell function).
	- Different cells activate different genes → different proteins → different cell structure and function.
### Levels of organization (Obj: order smallest → largest)
- Core ladder (small → big):
	- subatomic → atom → molecule → organelle → cell → tissue → organ → organ system → organism
- Exam trap: organelles are *inside* cells (so they come before “cell”).
### Evolution + natural selection (Obj: define evolution; steps; apply to examples)
- Evolution (exam definition): **change in allele/trait frequency in a population over time** (not “individuals change because they need to”).
- Natural selection requires 3 conditions (write these fast from memory)
	1. **Variation** in a trait in the population
	2. Trait is **heritable**
	3. Trait differences cause **differences in reproductive success (fitness)**
- Vocabulary you must use correctly:
	- **Adaptation**: trait that increases reproductive success in a specific environment.
	- **Fitness**: reproductive success (contribution to next generation).
	- **Selective agent**: environmental factor that drives selection (causes unequal survival/reproduction).
### Other mechanisms (Obj: selection vs drift vs gene flow; random vs not; variation up/down)
- **Natural selection**: non-random w.r.t. fitness; can decrease variation (often) but not always.
- **Genetic drift**: random sampling; strongest in small populations; tends to reduce variation over time.
- **Gene flow**: movement of alleles between populations; often increases variation within a population.
### Phylogeny basics you’ll need next (Obj: relatedness + shared traits from a tree)
- Relatedness depends on **most recent common ancestor**:
	- more recent common ancestor = more closely related
- Trees show **branching patterns**, not “progress” or “who came from who.”
## Textbook Definitions
### [[10_UMN/BIOL 1012/Textbook/Chapter - 1#1.1 Structural Organization of the Human Body|Chapter - 1|1.1 Structural Organization of the Human Body]]
**Big picture:** Molecules → cells → tissues → organs → organ systems → organism.
**Key definitions (tight + exam-friendly)**
- **Cell:** smallest independently functioning unit of life.
- **Tissue:** group of similar/closely related cells working together for a function.
- **Organ:** structure made of ≥2 tissue types; performs one or more functions.
- **Organ system:** group of organs working together to meet major body needs.
- **Organism:** living being that can carry out needed life functions independently.
**Order practice (smallest → largest)**: subatomic particles → atoms → molecules → organelles → cells → tissues → organs → organ systems → organism → biosphere
### [[10_UMN/BIOL 1012/Textbook/Chapter - 1#1.2 DNA Overview|1.2 DNA Overview]]
**What DNA is used for:** DNA contains a code (“blueprint”) that cells use to build proteins needed for life. DNA is in the *nucleus*; every cell has a complete copy (*genome*), but different cell types use different subsets of instructions.
**Nucleotide basics (must know)**
- **Nucleotide components:** sugar + phosphate group + base.
- **Bases:** A, T, C, G; the order of bases is the DNA “code.”
- **Chromosomes:** human DNA is organized into *23 pairs*.
- **Mutation:** an error/change in DNA sequence (often from replication errors) that can change proteins; if it occurs in sex cells (sperm/eggs), it can be inherited.
### [[10_UMN/BIOL 1012/Textbook/Chapter - 1#1.3 Human DNA and Genetic Diversity|1.3 Human DNA and Genetic Diversity]]
**Key terms**
- **Gene:** segment of DNA that codes for a trait/protein.
- **Allele:** version of a gene (different variants at the same location).
- **Locus:** location of a gene on a chromosome. (This exact language is tested in practice questions.)
**Human Genome Project (overview)**
- Human genome is ~3 billion base pairs; ~22,300 protein-coding genes.
**Where most variation comes from**
- Most DNA variation between individuals shows up as **single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs)**: a one-base difference (e.g., A instead of C).
### [[10_UMN/BIOL 1012/Textbook/Chapter - 1#1.4 The Genetic Basis of Evolution|1.4 The Genetic Basis of Evolution]]
**Exam definition framing (population genetics style):**
- Evolution = change in allele/trait frequencies in a population over time (not change within one individual).
**Natural selection (core conditions)**
To get evolution by natural selection for a trait:
- variation in the trait exists
- trait is heritable (linked to DNA, at least partly)
- trait affects reproduction/survival to reproduction (fitness)  
    (These ideas show up directly in the practice key.)
### [[10_UMN/BIOL 1012/Textbook/Chapter - 1#1.5 Mechanisms of Evolution|1.5 Mechanisms of Evolution (the “four forces” set)]]
- **Natural selection:** non-random increase in alleles that improve reproductive success in that environment.
- **Mutation:** source of new alleles; small per generation, but ultimate source of new variation.
- **Genetic drift:** random change in allele frequencies; strongest in small populations; can fix or lose alleles by chance.
- **Gene flow:** movement of alleles between populations via migration (individuals or gametes).
**Compare them (quick)**
- Random? drift ✅, mutation ✅ (random in origin), gene flow ✅/contextual, selection ❌ (non-random wrt fitness).
- Increases variation within a population? gene flow often ✅; mutation creates new alleles ✅; drift often reduces variation over time; selection can reduce or maintain depending on context.
