# Corporate-level strategy and diversification
## Core idea
Corporate-level strategy is the set of top-level choices about **what businesses a firm should be in** and **how those businesses should fit together**. It matters once a company is operating across more than one product line, market, or industry.
A simple contrast:
- **Business-level strategy:** how do we win in one industry?
- **Corporate-level strategy:** what industries should we be in, and how should we coordinate them?
## What leaders are deciding
Corporate-level strategy usually includes three big choices:
1. **Scope** — what industries, markets, or businesses the firm should enter or stay in.
2. **Coordination** — how resources, capabilities, and activities should be shared across units.
3. **Ownership** — whether to own the business fully, partner with others, or contract externally.
The goal is **corporate advantage**: the businesses should be stronger together than they would be as separate firms.
## Main approaches
### 1. Single-business strategy
The firm stays focused on one product or one market.
- **Strength:** stronger core competence and clearer focus
- **Risk:** higher dependence on one industry, so more exposure to market swings
### 2. Related diversification
The firm expands into businesses that share similar resources, capabilities, customers, channels, technology, or brand logic.
- Goal: create **economies of scope**
- Example logic: shared distribution, shared brand, shared know-how, shared operations
- Usually the strongest path when diversification is done well
### 3. Unrelated diversification
The firm owns businesses that do not have strong operational links.
- Goal: create **financial economies**, spread capital across units, and manage a broad portfolio
- Harder to create operational synergy
- More likely to add complexity without enough shared value
## Why firms diversify
Firms diversify for several reasons:
- To grow faster than one market allows
- To reduce dependence on a single industry
- To create synergy across business units
- To gain market power or reach new customer groups
- To expand internationally
But growth alone is not a good enough reason. The important question is: **Does this move create value that would not exist otherwise?**
## The diversification test
Before a firm diversifies, it should ask:
### 1. Attractiveness test
Is the new industry profitable or capable of becoming profitable?
### 2. Cost-of-entry test
Will the cost of entering the new industry leave enough upside?
### 3. Better-off test
Will the new business and the existing firm be stronger together?
If the answer to the better-off test is weak, diversification is probably just expansion without advantage.
## A result that matters for exams
Diversification often follows an **inverted U-shape**:
- moving from single-business to related diversification often improves performance
- moving too far into unrelated diversification often hurts performance
Reason: related businesses can share useful resources, but unrelated ones often create coordination burden without enough payoff.
# Vertical integration, partnerships, and realized strategy
## Core idea
Once a firm decides what businesses it wants to be in, the next question is **how much it should own and control itself**. That is where vertical integration, partnerships, outsourcing, and strategic control become important.
## Vertical integration
Vertical integration happens when a firm owns stages of its own value chain.
### Backward integration
The firm owns or controls its inputs.
- Example logic: secure supply, protect quality, lower dependence on suppliers
### Forward integration
The firm owns or controls distribution or customer-facing channels.
- Example logic: improve customer access, control brand experience, capture more value downstream
## Why firms choose vertical integration
Possible benefits:
- More control over quality and timing
- Easier differentiation
- Lower transaction costs
- Better protection of proprietary resources or technology
- Economies of scale in linked operations
- Less dependence on outside firms
## Risks and tradeoffs
Possible downsides:
- Higher **administrative costs** from coordinating more units internally
- Less strategic flexibility if the market changes
- Greater risk of inefficiency or complacency
- Technology or processes may become outdated
A useful decision rule:
> Vertical integration makes more sense when transaction costs from dealing with outside firms are higher than the administrative costs of doing the activity internally.
## Alternatives to full ownership
Sometimes owning more is not the best answer.
### Outsourcing
Let an outside firm perform the activity.
- Good when flexibility matters more than control
- Good when outside specialists can do it better or cheaper
### Spot contracts
Use simple market purchases for needed inputs or services.
- Good when inputs are standardized and switching is easy
### Partnerships and alliances
These fall between simple contracts and full mergers.
Examples include:
- preferred supplier arrangements
- strategic alliances
- strategic business partnering
- joint ventures
- mergers and acquisitions at the highest end of combination
The key question is not just “Should we grow?” but also “Should we **own**, **partner**, or **buy externally**?”
## Internationalization
At the corporate level, firms often expand internationally to:
- find new markets
- achieve economies of scale
- take advantage of location-specific resources or cost conditions
Internationalization can support growth, but it also increases coordination demands. Leaders must manage differences in market conditions, local needs, and execution systems.
## Intended, emergent, and realized strategy
This part matters because strategy is rarely carried out exactly as planned.
### Intended strategy
What leaders originally plan.
### Deliberate strategy
The part of the intended plan that is actually implemented as designed.
### Emergent strategy
New patterns and opportunities that develop while the firm is operating.
### Unrealized strategy
Parts of the original plan that never happen.
### Realized strategy
What the firm actually ends up doing in practice.
This means strong leaders do two things at once:
- keep the organization aligned with a clear plan
- stay alert to useful learning and change
## Why this matters for leadership
1. Strong corporate-level strategy requires:
	- **Conceptual skill** to see fit, tradeoffs, and long-term direction
	- **Judgment** to avoid expansion for its own sake
	- **Communication skill** to explain why the portfolio of businesses makes sense
2. A leader at this level must do more than pick a strategy. They also have to:
	- align structure with strategy
	- align people and incentives with strategy
	- build information and control systems
	- explain changes clearly so people understand what is happening and why
	That is where leadership and speaking skill matter. A weak explanation can make a good strategy fail in execution.
## Entrepreneurship angle
1. For a founder, this concept shows up in questions like:
	- Should we build our own logistics or use a third party?
	- Should we distribute directly or through partners?
	- Should we enter a new city ourselves or with a local partner?
	- What part of the original plan should we keep, and what should we adapt based on what the market is teaching us?
2. A startup founder usually begins with a single-business strategy. As the venture grows, the founder faces corporate-level questions:
	- Should we launch adjacent products?
	- Should we acquire a complementary business?
	- Should we partner instead of owning?
	- Are we expanding because it creates advantage, or just because growth looks exciting?
## Speaking tip
When talking about diversification in class or on an exam, use this sentence frame:
> A firm should diversify only when the new business passes the attractiveness, cost-of-entry, and better-off tests, and when the combined businesses can create more value together than apart.
- A strong class explanation sounds like this:
> The decision is not only whether the firm should expand, but whether the value comes best from ownership, partnership, or market contracting. The best option depends on control needs, flexibility, coordination costs, and learning from execution.
## Quick memory line
> Intended strategy is the plan. Realized strategy is the result. Emergent strategy is what the real world teaches along the way.
> Related diversification shares resources. Unrelated diversification shares capital.
