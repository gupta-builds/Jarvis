# Concept - Rational decision making vs bounded rationality
## Core idea
Decision making is often taught as if people first gather all the facts, compare every option carefully, and then choose the best one.
That is the **rational model**.

Real life is messier.
Managers usually decide with limited information, limited time, limited attention, and limited certainty.
That is **bounded rationality**.

The most important insight is not that the rational model is wrong.
It is that the rational model is an ideal standard, while bounded rationality describes the real conditions under which most decisions are made.

## 1. The rational model: what strong decision structure looks like
The rational model follows six steps:
1. define the problem
2. identify criteria
3. weight the criteria
4. generate alternatives
5. evaluate alternatives
6. choose the optimal option

This model is useful because it forces discipline.
It slows impulsive thinking and helps people avoid random choice.

It is strongest when:
- the problem is clear
- criteria are stable
- alternatives are known
- enough time exists
- evidence can actually be compared

In these situations, the model improves clarity and consistency.

## 2. Why the rational model breaks down in practice
Real decisions rarely happen in such clean conditions.

Managers often face:
- incomplete information
- misleading information
- unclear tradeoffs
- shifting preferences
- political pressures
- time pressure
- uncertainty about outcomes

So even when a manager wants to be systematic, the situation itself puts limits on full rationality.

This is why the lecture’s “assumptions vs limitations” slide is important.
It shows that weak decisions often come not from carelessness, but from the fact that reality does not cooperate with the ideal model.

## 3. Bounded rationality
Bounded rationality means people aim for reason, but reason operates inside constraints.

These constraints include:
- what the person knows
- how much the person can process
- how much time the person has
- how much ambiguity the situation contains

This makes bounded rationality more realistic than the pure rational model.

A boundedly rational manager does not always ask:
- “What is the best possible answer in theory?”

They often ask:
- “What is a workable answer given the information and time available?”

That difference matters.

## 4. Satisficing
Because people cannot always optimize, they often **satisfice**.

Satisficing means selecting an option that is acceptable and sufficient rather than perfect.

This is not always bad.
Sometimes satisficing is responsible:
- when the decision is reversible
- when delay is costly
- when information quality will not improve much
- when the decision is routine

But satisficing becomes weak when:
- important alternatives were ignored too early
- the “good enough” standard is too low
- the person uses satisficing as an excuse to avoid careful thought

So the key question is not:
- “Did I optimize?”

The better question is:
- “Was the level of analysis appropriate for the stakes?”

## 5. Rational structure still matters under constraints
Even when full rationality is impossible, the rational steps are still useful as a checklist.

A manager can still ask:
- Did we define the real problem?
- What criteria matter most?
- What are we assuming?
- What options did we fail to consider?
- What evidence supports the current favorite?
- What would make us change our mind?

This means the rational model still helps, even when the final decision is made under bounded rationality.

## 6. Connection to leadership
Strong leaders are not the people who always sound decisive.
Strong leaders are often the people who:
- define the issue better than others
- keep the criteria visible
- resist jumping too fast to one option
- know when enough analysis is enough
- recognize when the team is using speed as a substitute for thought

A leader must balance:
- analysis
- timing
- confidence
- openness to revision

That is much harder than simply “being decisive.”

## 7. Connection to entrepreneurship
Entrepreneurs work under bounded rationality almost all the time.

Why?
- markets are uncertain
- customers are not fully understood yet
- time and cash are limited
- data arrives unevenly
- the future is not stable

This means founders must constantly decide:
- what deserves deep analysis
- what deserves a quick test
- when to commit
- when to wait
- when “good enough” is good enough for now

The best founders do not confuse speed with wisdom.
They use structured thinking even when conditions are uncertain.

## 8. Speaking tip
When explaining a decision in class, internship work, or an interview, use this order:
1. define the problem
2. identify the criteria
3. explain the main constraint
4. compare alternatives briefly
5. justify why the final option was appropriate under the conditions

That format sounds thoughtful and mature because it shows process, not just opinion.

## 9. Quick comparison
### Rational decision making
- assumes clarity
- aims for the optimal choice
- works best in stable, analyzable conditions

### Bounded rationality
- accepts human and situational limits
- aims for a workable choice
- fits uncertainty, complexity, and time pressure

## Quick memory line
> Rationality is the ideal. Bounded rationality is the real setting. Good managers know how to work between them.

# Concept - Cognitive bias, innovation bias, and de-biasing decisions
## Core idea
Bias is not just a mistake.
Bias is a predictable pattern in judgment.

People use shortcuts because the world is too complex to process fully.
Those shortcuts can help speed.
They can also distort what we notice, how we interpret evidence, and which options feel acceptable.

This becomes even more serious in innovation and group decision making because teams can mistake familiarity, authority, or momentum for quality.

## 1. What cognitive bias really means
A cognitive bias is a systematic tendency to think in a skewed way.

It is “systematic” because it is not random.
The same kinds of distortions keep showing up:
- we notice confirming evidence
- we trust first numbers too much
- we prefer the familiar
- we avoid admitting losses
- we judge by presentation rather than substance

The danger is that biased decisions often feel reasonable while they are happening.

## 2. Biases from the lecture
### Availability bias
We rely too much on what comes to mind quickly.
Recent, emotional, vivid, or memorable examples feel more important than they really are.

### Representativeness bias
We judge by resemblance.
If something looks like our stereotype of a category, we assume it belongs there.

### Anchoring bias
We rely too much on the first piece of information.
A first number, first idea, or first estimate quietly shapes later judgment.

### Confirmation bias
We search for support rather than a fair test.
We prefer evidence that keeps our original view intact.

### Escalation of commitment
We keep investing because we already invested.
Past effort starts controlling present judgment.

### Status quo bias
We stick with the familiar because change feels riskier than staying put.

### Framing
Equivalent facts produce different choices depending on wording or presentation.

## 3. Innovation-specific biases that deepen the problem
Innovation work is especially vulnerable because it includes:
- uncertainty
- identity
- creativity
- politics
- weak early evidence
- pressure to show progress

That setting invites extra biases.

### Conformity bias
People move toward the group view, even when their private judgment differs.

### Authority bias
The senior person’s opinion gets extra weight, even when it is weaker.

### Ambiguity bias
Teams prefer options with clearer outcomes over options with uncertain but possibly greater upside.

### Bandwagon bias
Ideas become attractive because others seem to support them.

### Projection bias
Teams assume future customers will want what the current team prefers.

### Pro-innovation bias
Teams assume “new” means “better.”
Novelty itself becomes persuasive.

### Feature-positive effect
Teams focus on attractive benefits and underweight negative effects, missing risks, or gaps in evidence.

These biases are especially common when teams are choosing concepts, building pitches, or deciding which features deserve development. 
They often push teams toward what feels safe, approved, or exciting rather than what is actually strongest. :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

## 4. Why bias is dangerous in groups
A person alone can be biased.
A group can make that bias stronger.

Why?
- social approval matters
- disagreement feels risky
- authority shapes discussion
- silence gets mistaken for agreement
- early momentum becomes self-reinforcing

This is why some bad ideas survive not because everyone believed them, but because too few people challenged them at the right time.

## 5. Emotional side of bias
Many biases are not just information errors.
They are also emotionally protective.

For example:
- status quo bias protects comfort
- escalation protects ego
- confirmation protects identity
- ambiguity bias protects certainty
- conformity protects belonging

That means de-biasing is not only about “more data.”
It is also about creating conditions where people can tolerate discomfort, uncertainty, and correction.

## 6. Signs bias is shaping the room
A team should become alert when it hears statements like:
- “That’s how we’ve always done it”
- “We already know what customers want”
- “That idea is too crazy”
- “The CEO needs to approve it first”
- “Let’s just do what the others are doing”
- “Nobody would buy it”
- “It’s too uncertain”

These comments often signal that judgment is narrowing before proper evaluation happens. :contentReference[oaicite:13]{index=13}

## 7. How to de-bias decisions
De-biasing does not mean becoming perfectly neutral.
It means building habits that reduce predictable distortion.

### A. Slow down key moments
Do not slow every decision.
Slow the high-stakes moments:
- defining the problem
- choosing criteria
- selecting finalists
- deciding whether to continue or exit

### B. Ask disconfirming questions
Instead of asking:
- “Why do we like this?”

also ask:
- “What would make this wrong?”
- “What evidence are we ignoring?”
- “What would a critic say?”

### C. Separate idea quality from who said it
This reduces authority bias and conformity bias.

### D. Use independent first judgments
Have people assess an idea privately before group discussion.
This reduces bandwagon effects.

### E. Test assumptions, not just enthusiasm
A strong concept is not the one people like most in a meeting.
It is the one that survives contact with evidence.

### F. Revisit sunk costs explicitly
Ask:
- “If we had not already invested in this, would we still choose it now?”

### G. Watch the frame
Before agreeing, restate the same issue in another way.
If the conclusion changes too much, framing may be driving the choice.

### H. Invite outside perspective
People close to a project often cannot see their own tunnel vision clearly.

## 8. Leadership link
A leader reduces bias by shaping process, not just making the final call.

Good leaders:
- ask better questions
- protect dissent
- prevent early closure
- distinguish confidence from evidence
- refuse to let status replace reasoning

The leader’s job is not to remove all uncertainty.
It is to keep uncertainty from being hidden behind false certainty.

## 9. Entrepreneurship link
Founders are especially vulnerable to bias because they are close to the idea emotionally.
That can create:
- confirmation bias around customer feedback
- escalation of commitment after time and effort
- projection bias about user needs
- feature-positive thinking during product design
- ambiguity avoidance when a risky pivot may be necessary

This is why strong founders test, revise, and sometimes walk away.

## 10. Speaking tip
One of the best speaking habits in decision-heavy settings is to name the possible bias directly but calmly.

Examples:
- “I think we may be anchoring on the first number.”
- “We may be giving too much weight to the initial idea.”
- “Can we test whether this is evidence or confirmation bias?”
- “Are we choosing this because it is best, or because it feels safest?”

That kind of language raises the quality of discussion without sounding aggressive.

## Quick memory line
> Bias narrows thought. Better process reopens it.
