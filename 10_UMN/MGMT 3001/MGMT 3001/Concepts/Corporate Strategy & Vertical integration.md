# Corporate Strategy & Vertical Integration

## Core idea
Corporate-level strategy asks the broadest strategic question:
> **What businesses should the firm be in, and how should those businesses fit together?**

This is distinct from business-level strategy, which asks *how* to compete inside *one* industry. Corporate strategy is about the **portfolio** — which industries to enter, exit, and how to create value across them.

---

## Three major corporate-level decisions

| Decision type | The question |
|--------------|-------------|
| **Scope** | Which industries or markets will we compete in? |
| **Organizational design** | How do we coordinate resources across different business units? |
| **Ownership** | Should we fully own these activities, or partner, outsource, or ally? |

**Corporate advantage** is achieved when the firm creates more value by owning these businesses together than they would generate as independent entities. Without a real corporate advantage, the whole is *less* than the sum of its parts.

---

## Diversification strategy

**Diversification** = engaging in several different businesses to create more value together than they would create separately.

### BCG Growth-Share Matrix (portfolio planning tool)

The Boston Consulting Group matrix maps businesses by **market growth rate** and **relative market share**:

| Quadrant | Market Growth | Market Share | Strategic logic |
|----------|--------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Stars** | High | High | Invest heavily to maintain position; will become cash cows when growth slows |
| **Cash Cows** | Low | High | Generate steady cash; fund stars and question marks |
| **Question Marks** | High | Low | Risky; must decide: invest to make them Stars, or exit |
| **Dogs** | Low | Low | Typically candidates for divestiture (selling off) |

### Why firms diversify:
- **Synergy** — combining businesses to create cost savings or capability advantages that neither could achieve alone
- **Growth** — finding new revenue opportunities beyond the core business
- **Risk management** — reducing dependence on a single market or product
- **Economies of scope** — costs are lower when activities are shared across businesses
- **International expansion** — entering new geographic markets
- **Personal gain** — sometimes managers pursue diversification for their own compensation or prestige (a governance risk)

---

## Three approaches to diversification

### 1. Single-product strategy
- Focus entirely on one product in one market
- Deepens expertise and core competence
- Highest vulnerability if that market shifts

### 2. Related diversification
- Own multiple businesses that **share tangible or intangible resources**
- Goal: **economies of scope** — costs of two businesses together are less than operating each separately
- Example: Disney owns theme parks, films, merchandise, and streaming — all connected to the same entertainment brand and characters

**Economies of scope** arise from:
- Shared distribution networks
- Shared manufacturing or technology
- Shared brand or customer relationships
- Shared managerial expertise across similar businesses

### 3. Unrelated diversification
- Own businesses with **little operational connection**
- Goal: **financial economies** — spread capital across many units to drive profitability through financial discipline rather than shared resources
- Example: A conglomerate owning insurance, real estate, and consumer goods
- **Research finding:** Performance typically follows an **inverted U-shape** — related diversification improves performance, but moving into increasingly unrelated businesses tends to destroy value

---

## The diversification test — three prongs

Before entering any new business, ask:

| Test | Question |
|------|---------|
| **Industry attractiveness test** | Is the industry's underlying structure favorable for high returns? (Use Porter's Five Forces) |
| **Cost of entry test** | Is the cost to enter the industry lower than the expected benefit? |
| **Better-off test** | Will the new business provide a competitive advantage that wouldn't exist without the parent firm? |

**Additional ownership test:** Does the firm actually need to *own* this business, or would an alliance, partnership, or contract achieve the same benefit with less commitment and risk?

## Intended, Emergent, and Realized Strategy

Strategy rarely unfolds exactly as planned. Three concepts explain the gap between plan and reality:

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Intended strategy** | What managers deliberately planned to do | A five-year growth plan approved by the board |
| **Emergent strategy** | Unplanned patterns that develop through adaptation and learning | Stumbling into a new market because a customer asked for something unexpected |
| **Realized strategy** | The actual strategy the firm ends up executing — a combination of intended + emergent | The hybrid of what was planned and what actually worked |

**Exam point:** No firm executes its intended strategy perfectly. What matters is recognizing emergent patterns early, learning from them, and incorporating them deliberately. Realized strategy = intended − abandoned + emergent.

## Vertical integration

**Vertical integration** = owning business units that produce the firm's inputs OR handle its outputs, rather than buying/selling through the market.

### Backward integration
Owning or controlling **upstream inputs** — suppliers, raw materials, components.
- Example: A candy company buying a sugar plantation
- Benefit: secures supply, controls quality, may reduce cost

### Forward integration
Owning or controlling **downstream distribution** — channels, retail, customer-facing stages.
- Example: Apple owning Apple Stores rather than selling only through third-party retailers
- Benefit: controls the customer experience, captures more margin

---

## When does vertical integration make sense?

The decision hinges on comparing two types of costs:

| Cost type | Definition |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Transaction costs** | The cost of buying from outside — contracts, negotiation, monitoring, risk of hold-up |
| **Administrative costs** | The cost of managing the activity internally — coordination, overhead, loss of market discipline |

**Decision rule:** Integrate vertically when **transaction costs > administrative costs** (i.e., it's more expensive to rely on the market than to manage it internally).

### Benefits of vertical integration:
- More control over quality and delivery
- Lower transaction costs and reduced risk of supply disruption
- Easier coordination across the value chain
- Protection of proprietary technologies or processes
- Capture more of the value chain margin

### Costs of vertical integration:
- Higher administrative overhead
- Less flexibility (locked into internal processes)
- Risk of inefficiency when internal suppliers face no competitive pressure
- Risk of getting locked into outdated technology
- Reduced ability to pivot if the market shifts

---

## Alternatives to vertical integration

Firms don't have to own everything. Alternatives:

| Alternative | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Outsourcing** | Contract with an outside firm for activities previously done internally (e.g., outsourcing IT or customer service) |
| **Spot contracts** | Purchase commodities at market price — no long-term commitment |
| **Strategic alliances** | Collaborate with a partner without merging; share knowledge or markets |
| **Joint ventures** | Create a new shared entity with a partner to pursue a specific opportunity |
| **Preferred supplier arrangements** | Long-term exclusive or near-exclusive relationships with key suppliers |

## Internationalization

Firms expand internationally for several reasons:
- **New markets** — grow revenues beyond a saturated home market
- **Economies of scale** — spread fixed costs over more units globally
- **Location advantages** — access to lower-cost labor, raw materials, or specialized talent
- **Portfolio diversification** — reduce dependence on a single country's economic cycle

**Strategy implementation in international contexts requires:**
- Alignment of organizational structure with international scope
- HR systems that can function across cultures
- Information and control systems that work across time zones and regulatory environments
