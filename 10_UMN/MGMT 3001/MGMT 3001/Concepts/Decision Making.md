# Decision Making

## Core idea
Decision making is described in the textbook as the "heart of activity in every business." The study guide asks you to understand both the **ideal** (rational model) and the **reality** (bounded rationality, heuristics, bias) — and the gap between them.

---

## The Rational Decision-Making Process

The rational model provides a structured, step-by-step process for reaching the **optimal** decision. It assumes managers have complete information, clear preferences, and enough time to evaluate every alternative.

**Six steps:**

| Step | What happens |
|------|-------------|
| **1. Define the problem or opportunity** | Clearly state the organizational issue or goal to be addressed |
| **2. Identify criteria and objectives** | Determine what a good outcome would look like (e.g., cut costs, grow revenue) |
| **3. Weight the criteria** | Rank criteria by importance — not all objectives matter equally |
| **4. Generate alternatives** | Brainstorm a full set of possible courses of action |
| **5. Evaluate each alternative** | Rate how well each alternative achieves each weighted criterion |
| **6. Choose the optimal decision** | Calculate the "weighted score" across all criteria and select the highest-scoring option |

**What the rational model is good for:**
- Provides a useful benchmark for careful thinking
- Forces criteria to be made explicit before alternatives are considered
- Reduces the risk of anchoring on the first solution that comes to mind

---

## Assumptions of the Rational Model — and why they fail

The rational model assumes:
- The problem is clearly defined
- All alternatives are known
- Preferences are stable and consistent
- Full information is available
- There is no time pressure
- The decision maker is objective

**Reality:** Managers face incomplete information, multiple competing goals, time pressure, and cognitive limits that make pure rationality impossible.

---

## Bounded Rationality and Satisficing

**Bounded rationality** (Simon): Managers *intend* to be rational, but are limited by:
- Incomplete or uncertain information
- Limited cognitive processing ability
- Time pressure and competing demands
- Conflicting organizational goals

Because of these constraints, managers typically **satisfice** — they choose the first option that meets minimum acceptable criteria ("good enough"), rather than searching indefinitely for the mathematically optimal solution.

**Important nuance:** Satisficing is not simply being lazy. In a fast-moving business environment, spending weeks searching for the perfect answer while the market moves is often worse than making a good decision quickly.

---

## Conditions of Decision Making

| Condition | What is known | What is uncertain |
|-----------|--------------|------------------|
| **Certainty** | All information is available; outcomes are predictable | Nothing — ideal but rare |
| **Risk** | Goals and alternatives are clear | Exact outcomes of each option are probabilistic |
| **Uncertainty** | Goals may be clear | Alternatives and their likely outcomes are incomplete or unknown |
| **Ambiguity** | Even goals may be unclear | Both alternatives and outcomes are vague or shifting |

Most managerial decisions occur under **risk or uncertainty**.

---

## Intuitive Decision Making and "Thin-Slicing"

When speed is critical, managers often use **intuitive decision making** — rapid judgments based on pattern recognition from past experience.

**Thin-slicing** (Malcolm Gladwell): The ability to find patterns from narrow slices of experience and make rapid, often accurate, snap judgments.

This works when:
- The manager has deep relevant experience
- Time pressure is extreme
- The situation matches patterns from prior experience

This fails when:
- The situation is genuinely novel
- The manager's experience is in a different domain
- The pattern being recognized is a stereotype rather than a real signal

## Heuristics and Biases

**Heuristics** are mental shortcuts — rules of thumb that save cognitive effort. They are efficient but introduce systematic errors called **biases**.

### The three primary heuristics:

**1. Availability Heuristic**
- People judge the probability of an event by how **easily examples come to mind**
- Vivid, recent, or emotionally intense events feel more common than they statistically are
- Example: After a plane crash in the news, people overestimate the risk of flying

**2. Representativeness Heuristic**
- People judge by how closely a case **resembles an existing stereotype or category**
- Leads to overlooking base rates and actual statistical probabilities
- Example: Assuming a well-dressed, articulate person is more competent regardless of their actual track record

**3. Adjustment / Anchoring Heuristic**
- People start with an **initial reference point (anchor)** and adjust from it — but typically don't adjust enough
- The anchor biases the final estimate even when it is arbitrary
- Example: A salary negotiation starting at $60K will likely settle closer to $60K than one starting at $90K, even if the "fair" salary is somewhere in between

---

### Other critical biases for the exam:

| Bias | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Confirmation bias** | Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs; ignoring contradictory evidence | A manager believes a project is on track and dismisses warning signs |
| **Escalation of commitment** | Continuing to invest in a failing course of action to justify prior investments (sunk cost fallacy) | Pouring more money into a failing product because "we've already spent so much" |
| **Status quo bias** | Favoring the current state and resisting change even when change would be beneficial | Sticking with an outdated vendor relationship because switching feels risky |
| **Framing** | Responding differently to the same information depending on how it is presented | "90% survival rate" vs. "10% death rate" feel different even though they're identical |

**Framing detail:** People are generally **risk-averse when choices are framed as gains** (e.g., saving lives) and **risk-seeking when framed as losses** (e.g., preventing deaths). The presentation shape the risk tolerance even when the underlying facts are the same.

## Emotions and Social Situations

Decision quality is shaped by more than information:

**Emotions:**
- Good moods make people more optimistic and more willing to take risks
- Fear and anxiety push toward excessive risk-aversion
- Anger can increase confidence beyond what evidence warrants

**Social situations:**
- **Asch conformity experiments:** People often agree with the stated majority opinion even when they can see it is factually wrong — to avoid social rejection
- **Appropriateness framework:** Rather than calculating utility, people ask "What does a person like me do in this situation?" — decisions reflect identity and social norms as much as pure analysis
- **Groupthink:** In high-cohesion groups, the pressure to agree can suppress critical evaluation of alternatives

## Four Models of Organizational Decision Making

| Model | Core assumption | When it fits |
|-------|----------------|-------------|
| **Classical model** | Managers can identify the optimal decision through rational analysis | Decisions with clear goals, good information, limited alternatives |
| **Administrative model** | Managers satisfice using bounded rationality and intuition | Most real-world managerial decisions |
| **Political model** | Decisions emerge from bargaining and coalition-building among managers with different goals | High-stakes decisions with multiple stakeholders and conflicting interests |
| **Garbage Can model** | Problems, solutions, and participants flow randomly; solutions may precede problems | Highly ambiguous, loosely organized settings |

**Political model exam point:** Most big organizational decisions involve managers who disagree. "The right answer" is insufficient — you also need to build coalitions, manage stakeholders, and negotiate.
## Programmed vs. Non-programmed Decisions

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Programmed** | Recurring decisions with established rules or procedures | Reordering supplies when inventory falls below a threshold |
| **Non-programmed** | Novel, unstructured, or poorly defined situations requiring unique judgment | Deciding whether to acquire a competitor |

**Why this matters:** Managers waste cognitive resources by treating non-programmed decisions as if they were programmed. Novel situations require deliberate analysis, not autopilot.

---

## Improving Decision Quality

Managers can reduce bias and improve outcomes by:

- **Slowing down high-stakes choices** — most bias appears in fast, automatic thinking
- **Making criteria explicit before** evaluating options (prevents post-hoc rationalization)
- **Seeking disconfirming evidence** — actively looking for information that challenges the preferred option
- **Taking an outsider's view** — imagining how someone with no stake in the decision would evaluate it
- **Pre-mortem analysis** — before committing, imagine the decision has failed and ask why
- **Using statistical models** where available to discipline intuitive estimates
- **Building in accountability** — decisions that must be explained to others are more careful
