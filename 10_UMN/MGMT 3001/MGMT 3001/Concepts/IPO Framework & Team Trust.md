# IPO Framework & Team Trust

## Core idea
The IPO framework explains team effectiveness by tracing how **Inputs** shape **Processes and States**, which produce **Outputs**. A team succeeds when it is designed well, develops healthy internal conditions, and uses productive interaction processes — not just when it has talented members.

---

## Teams vs. Work Groups — the foundational distinction

| Feature | Work Group | Team |
|---------|-----------|------|
| Leadership | Clearly focused single leader | Shared leadership roles |
| Accountability | Individual only | Individual AND mutual |
| Purpose | Mirrors the organization's broad mission | Specific to the team |
| Work products | Individual deliverables | Collective deliverables |
| Task type | Simpler, more independent | Complex, interdependent |
| Decision making | Leader decides | Team decides jointly |

**The key distinction:** Teams add **interdependence** — members must rely on each other and integrate their work. A work group can share space or supervisor; a team shares outcome.

---

## When are teams appropriate?

Teams are NOT always the right structure. Use them when:

**Task complexity is high:**
- Large amount of information to process
- Significant uncertainty about outcomes
- Multiple specialized skills required
- Too complex for one person to master

**Task interdependence is high:**
- Members must actively coordinate their work
- Output requires integrating different expertise
- Example: Product development requiring marketing, engineering, and manufacturing expertise simultaneously

When tasks are simple and independent, teams add coordination overhead without adding value — use a work group instead.

---

## Types of teams

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Manager-led (vertical)** | Manager defines goals and methods; team follows chain of command |
| **Manager-led (cross-functional / horizontal)** | Members from same level but different departments |
| **Self-directed** | Team determines its own objectives and methods; high empowerment but requires strong norms |
| **Advisory** | Provide recommendations without implementation authority |
| **Task force / project team** | Temporary; formed for a specific purpose |
| **Virtual (geographically distributed)** | Members dispersed across locations; rely on electronic communication |
| **Collocated** | Members work physically close together; rich face-to-face communication |

**Collocated vs. virtual comparison:**

| Factor | Collocated | Virtual |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| Communication richness | High (body language, spontaneous interaction) | Lower (text, video; non-verbal cues lost) |
| Trust building | Easier and faster | Requires intentional effort |
| Coordination challenges | Lower | Higher |
| Flexibility | Lower | Higher |

## The IPO Framework

The IPO model traces the full causal path of team effectiveness.

---

### INPUTS — what goes into the team before work begins

**Goal and purpose**
- Clear, challenging, and meaningful goals drive higher effort and focus
- Shared understanding of purpose creates identity

**Structure**
- Size: smaller teams coordinate more easily; larger teams have more resources but face coordination costs
- Role clarity: who does what, who has authority over what

**People (composition)**
- Right mix of **technical skills** (can they solve the problem?) and **interpersonal skills** (can they work together?)
- Diversity: brings broader perspectives and creative solutions over time, but creates short-term friction and identity challenges

**Resources and tools**
- Physical tools, information, budget, technology

---

### STATES — internal team conditions that develop over time

**Norms**
Expected behaviors established by the team. Should be set early and explicitly. Categories:
- **Meeting norms** — when, how often, attendance expectations
- **Working norms** — deadlines, work quality standards, accountability
- **Communication norms** — how to share information, how to express disagreement
- **Leadership norms** — how decisions are made, who has final say
- **Consideration norms** — how to treat each other respectfully

**Team identity**
The degree to which members see themselves as part of a shared "we" rather than isolated individuals.

**Trust**
Belief that teammates will follow through, act with good intentions, and not exploit vulnerability.

**Psychological safety**
A climate where members believe it is safe to:
- Speak up with ideas
- Ask questions without being judged
- Admit mistakes without punishment
- Give and receive candid feedback

**Without psychological safety**, teams hide weakness, avoid real issues, fake confidence, and suppress information — all of which harm decision quality.

**Efficacy**
Shared belief that the team can succeed. High-efficacy teams persist longer and achieve more.

**Empowerment**
Shared sense that the team has real authority, voice, and ownership over its work.

**Cohesion**
Attraction to the group — wanting to stay and contribute. Helps commitment, but too much can produce groupthink.

---

### PROCESSES — how members interact to get work done

**Coordination**
Synchronizing effort so members' contributions fit together without redundancy or gaps.

**Participation**
All members contributing, not just the most confident or senior voices. The **common information effect** is a major risk: teams often only discuss information that everyone already knows, failing to surface unique individual knowledge.

**Peer monitoring and feedback**
Members tracking each other's progress and giving honest input. Requires psychological safety.

**Conflict management**
- **Task (cognitive) conflict** — disagreement about ideas, approaches, priorities → **improves decision quality when managed well**
- **Relationship (affective) conflict** — personal tensions, interpersonal friction → **harms performance and trust**

The goal is to encourage task conflict while minimizing relationship conflict. They often bleed into each other, which requires strong norms and safety.

**Backup behavior**
Members helping each other when someone falls behind or needs support — only possible in high-trust, high-safety teams.

**Blocking behaviors to avoid:**
- **Social loafing** — reducing effort when in a group because individual contribution is less visible
- **Dominating** — one or two members monopolizing discussion
- **Fault-finding** — criticizing without contributing

---

### OUTPUTS — what the team produces

**Three conditions for team effectiveness:**

| Condition | Definition |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Meaningful results** | Tangible outcomes that meet the quality standards of those receiving the work |
| **Member satisfaction** | Individuals feel their contributions are valued and experience personal growth |
| **Future capacity (viability)** | The team's ability to work together again is enhanced — trust and skills are built, not depleted |

A team that produces good output but burns out its members has failed condition 3. A team where members are satisfied but produce nothing has failed condition 1.

## Tuckman's Five Stages of Team Development

Teams typically evolve through a predictable sequence:

| Stage | What happens |
|-------|-------------|
| **1. Forming** | Members are polite and exploratory; defining the task, learning about each other |
| **2. Storming** | Conflict emerges over roles, priorities, and interpersonal styles — this is normal and necessary |
| **3. Norming** | The group resolves conflict and establishes shared standards and cohesion |
| **4. Performing** | Members integrate their differences and work efficiently toward the goal |
| **5. Adjourning** | The team disbands after the task; reflects on experience and outcomes |

**Midpoint transition:** Many teams undergo a significant behavioral shift at the halfway point toward their deadline — abandoning old approaches and recommitting to finishing.

**Exam point:** Storming is not a sign of failure — it is a necessary developmental step. Teams that skip storming often have unresolved conflict that erupts later.

---

## Boundary Management

Teams must also manage their **relationship with external stakeholders**.

**Three types of boundary stance:**

| Stance | Description | Risk |
|--------|-------------|------|
| **Informing** | Minimal outsider interaction; rely on internal knowledge | May miss critical external feedback |
| **Parading** | Passively promote their work to external groups | May get praise without useful input |
| **Probing** | Actively engage outsiders; constantly revise based on external needs | Most demanding but most adaptive |

---

## Trust and Psychological Safety — deeper look

| Teams with trust and safety | Teams without trust and safety |
|----------------------------|-------------------------------|
| Ask for help | Hide weakness |
| Admit mistakes | Avoid accountability |
| Give honest feedback | Suppress disagreement |
| Stay candid under pressure | Fake confidence |
| Surface real problems | Paper over conflict |

**Why this matters for managers:**
The team leader's behavior is the primary determinant of psychological safety. Leaders who punish mistakes, shoot down ideas, or show favoritism destroy safety quickly. Leaders who model vulnerability, reward honesty, and respond calmly to bad news build it.
