## Chapter 1 — Management Foundations
**See:** [[Concepts/Modern Management|Modern Management Theories]] | [[Concepts/Management vs Leadership|Management vs Leadership]] | [[Textbook/Chapter - 1|Chapter - 1]]

### Key management philosophies — what are they and what problem do they solve?

| Theory | Core figure(s) | Key idea | Limitation |
|--------|---------------|----------|-----------|
| Scientific Management | Taylor, Gantt, Gilbreths | Efficiency through studied, standardized work methods + incentives | Dehumanizing; workers can underperform ("ratchet") |
| Bureaucracy | Max Weber | Formal rules, chain of command, merit-based system for fairness + scale | Rigidity; rules replace judgment |
| Human Relations View | Mayo, Follett, McGregor | People and social factors drive performance — attention, participation, motivation matter | Can underemphasize structure and constraints |
| Quantitative / Mgmt Science | Various | Data, models, and MIS improve decisions | Only as good as the data and model |
| Systems Thinking | Various | Organizations are interconnected — changes ripple | Hard to operationalize |
| Contingency View | Various | No one best way — fit between org and context drives effectiveness | Doesn't always prescribe a clear action |

### Theory X vs Theory Y

| Theory X | Theory Y |
|----------|----------|
| Workers are lazy; avoid responsibility | Workers are not inherently lazy; can be self-directed |
| Need close supervision and strict rules | Need autonomy, resources, and meaningful work |
| Motivation through coercion and punishment | Motivation through empowerment and participation |

### Leadership vs Management

| Management | Leadership |
|-----------|-----------|
| Plan, organize, staff, control | Set direction, align, motivate, inspire |
| Creates order and consistency | Creates movement and change |
| "Doing things right" | "Doing the right things" |

Both are needed — neither replaces the other.

## Chapter 12 — Leadership Styles
**See:** [[Concepts/Leadership Styles, EQ & Self-Awareness|Leadership Styles, EQ & Self-Awareness]] | [[Textbook/Chapter - 12|Chapter - 12]]

### Task vs Relationship orientation
- **Task-oriented:** planning, role clarity, monitoring performance
- **Relationship-oriented:** support, development, trust-building, recognition
- These are **independent dimensions**, not opposites

### Managerial Grid — four key styles
- Impoverished (low/low) → minimal effort
- Country Club (high relationship, low task) → pleasant, poor performance
- Produce or Perish (high task, low relationship) → results + burnout
- **Team Leader (high/high)** → strongest general pattern

### Transformational vs Transactional

| Transactional | Transformational |
|--------------|-----------------|
| Exchanges, rewards, standards | Vision, inspiration, individual growth |
| Contingent reward + MBE | Idealized influence, motivation, stimulation, consideration |
| Good for stability and routine | Good for change and commitment |

### Emotional intelligence (EQ) — four domains
1. Self-awareness — knowing your emotions and patterns
2. Self-management — regulating reactions
3. Social awareness — reading others accurately
4. Relationship management — influencing, coaching, resolving conflict

**Signals of high EQ:** reads the room, regulates before reacting, listens actively, adapts tone, challenges without humiliating

### House's Path–Goal Theory
The leader's job is to **make the path from effort to valued reward clearer and more achievable** by supplementing what is missing in the task or follower:
- Directive style → use when task is ambiguous
- Supportive style → use when work is stressful
- Participative style → use when followers value autonomy and are skilled
- Achievement-oriented → use when followers want to grow and excel

## Chapter 13 — Personality, Locus of Control, Resilience
**See:** [[Concepts/Personality, Locus of Control & Resilience|Personality, Locus of Control & Resilience]] | [[Textbook/Chapter - 13|Chapter - 13]]

### Big Five Personality Traits

| Trait | Low | High |
|-------|-----|------|
| Extraversion | Reserved, solitary | Sociable, assertive |
| Openness | Pragmatic, cautious | Creative, unconventional |
| Conscientiousness | Flexible, spontaneous | Organized, persistent, reliable |
| Agreeableness | Competitive, skeptical | Cooperative, compassionate |
| Emotional Stability | Reactive, anxious | Calm, methodical under pressure |

No profile guarantees success — traits shape default tendencies, not destiny.

### Locus of Control

| Internal | External |
|---------|---------|
| Effort and action shape outcomes | Luck and outside forces shape outcomes |
| Proactive, self-motivated | Prefers structure and direction |
| Comfortable with autonomy | May need more guidance |

### Managing difficult situations
- Pause before reacting
- Name the emotion to reduce its intensity
- Seek the other person's perspective
- Focus on behavior, not identity
- Stay composed — leader emotions are contagious

### Resilience markers
- Faces reality directly
- Maintains meaning through adversity
- Focuses on what is controllable
- Breaks problems into manageable parts
- Learns from failure rather than being defined by it

## Chapter 5 — Business-Level Strategy
**See:** [[Concepts/Business-Level Strategy & Sustainable Advantage|Business-Level Strategy & Sustainable Advantage]] | [[Textbook/Chapter - 5|Chapter - 5]]

### Mission, Vision, Goals, Plans hierarchy
- **Mission** — why the org exists
- **Vision** — what the desired future looks like
- **Goals** — specific desired outcomes (measurable)
- **Plans** — concrete actions and resource allocations

### Sustainable competitive advantage — VRIN
Valuable + Rare + Inimitable + Non-substitutable

### Strategic management process
1. Current-state evaluation (Who, What, How?)
2. External analysis (Porter's 5 Forces)
3. Internal analysis (RBV, Value Chain)
4. SWOT (bridge internal ↔ external)
5. Strategy formulation (choose generic strategy)
6. Implementation and control

### Porter's Five Forces
Threat of new entrants | Supplier power | Buyer power | Threat of substitutes | Rivalry

### Generic strategies
- Cost leadership → lowest cost, broad market
- Differentiation → uniqueness customers will pay premium for
- Focus → narrow segment (cost or differentiation focus)
- **Stuck in the middle** = trying everything, mastering nothing

## Chapter 6 — Corporate-Level Strategy
**See:** [[Concepts/Corporate Strategy & Vertical integration|Corporate Strategy & Vertical Integration]] | [[Textbook/Chapter - 6|Chapter - 6]]

### Types of diversification
- **Single business** → deepest expertise, highest market risk
- **Related diversification** → shared resources → economies of scope → typically strongest performance
- **Unrelated diversification** → financial economies → weaker performance at extremes

### Diversification test (three prongs)
1. Is the industry attractive?
2. Is the cost of entry reasonable?
3. Will the firm be better off?

### Vertical integration
- **Backward** = own inputs/suppliers
- **Forward** = own distribution/channels
- Integrate when **transaction costs > administrative costs**

### Intended → Emergent → Realized strategy
Plans meet reality; emergent patterns + intended elements = what actually gets done

## Chapter 15 — Decision Making
**See:** [[Concepts/Decision Making|Decision Making]] | [[10_UMN/MGMT 3001/Textbook/Chapter - 15|Chapter - 15]]

### Six-step rational process
Define problem → identify criteria → weight criteria → generate alternatives → evaluate → choose optimal

### Bounded rationality and satisficing
Managers try to be rational but are limited by time, information, and cognition → satisfice with "good enough"

### Major heuristics and biases — exam favorites
- **Availability** — judge by what's vivid or easy to recall
- **Representativeness** — judge by stereotype match
- **Anchoring** — over-rely on an initial reference point
- **Confirmation bias** — seek supporting evidence, ignore contradictions
- **Escalation of commitment** — continue a failing course because of sunk cost
- **Status quo bias** — resist change even when change is better
- **Framing** — same facts feel different depending on how they're presented (gain vs. loss framing)

### Conditions of decision making
Certainty → Risk → Uncertainty → Ambiguity (in order of increasing difficulty)

## Chapter 17 — Teams
**See:** [[Concepts/IPO Framework & Team Trust|IPO Framework & Team Trust]] | [[Textbook/Chapter - 17|Chapter - 17]]

### IPO Framework
- **Inputs:** goal, structure, people, resources
- **States:** norms, identity, trust, psychological safety, efficacy, empowerment, cohesion
- **Processes:** coordination, participation, peer monitoring, conflict management, backup behavior
- **Outputs:** meaningful results, member satisfaction, future viability

### Tuckman's five stages
Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing → Adjourning

### Types of conflict
- **Task conflict** → disagreement about ideas → can improve quality
- **Relationship conflict** → personal tension → harms performance

### Common team failures
Social loafing | Domination | Groupthink | Weak norms | Unclear roles

### Three conditions for team effectiveness
Meaningful results + Member satisfaction + Future capacity (viability)

## Chapter 8 — Organizational Culture
**See:** [[Concepts/Organizational Culture|Organizational Culture]] | [[Textbook/Chapter - 8|Chapter - 8]]

### Three levels of culture
1. **Artifacts** — visible symbols, rituals, stories, office layout
2. **Espoused values** — what the org says it values
3. **Underlying assumptions** — deep, unconscious beliefs that drive behavior

### Who builds culture
- **Founders** — initial imprint
- **Leaders** — ongoing reinforcement through attention, reactions, resource allocation, role modeling
- **Teams** — routines that become embedded assumptions

### Cultural strength dimensions
High agreement + High intensity = **Strong culture**

### Socialization tools
Formal statements | Physical space | Reward systems | Stories and myths | Hiring and selection | Onboarding
