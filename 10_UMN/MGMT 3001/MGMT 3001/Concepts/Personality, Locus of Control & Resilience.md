# Personality, Locus of Control & Resilience

## Core idea
Chapter 13 argues that effective leadership starts with **knowing yourself**. A leader who doesn't understand their own personality, assumptions, and default reactions cannot consistently adapt to different followers and situations.

Self-awareness is the foundation of interpersonal effectiveness.

---

## Why self-awareness matters
A leader's **default style** — the approach they revert to under pressure — is driven by:
- Personality traits
- Values and beliefs
- Past experiences
- Skills and abilities

Understanding your defaults helps you **stretch intentionally** without losing authenticity. If you stretch too far outside your core values, followers perceive inauthenticity, which erodes trust.

---

## Forms of intelligence
The textbook broadens intelligence beyond IQ:

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **IQ / Analytical** | Processing information, reasoning, academic tasks | Logical problem solving |
| **Practical intelligence** | Applying knowledge to real-world situations (common sense) | Navigating office politics |
| **Creative intelligence** | Generating novel ideas, seeing new solutions | Product innovation |
| **Emotional intelligence (EQ)** | Recognizing and managing emotions in self and others | Defusing a tense meeting |

**Multiple intelligences (Gardner):** People show different types of strength — linguistic, mathematical-logical, spatial, kinesthetic, musical, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalist. No single measure captures all.

**Management implication:** Academic intelligence (IQ) predicts school performance but is an incomplete predictor of leadership effectiveness. Practical and emotional intelligence often matter more in organizational settings.

## The Big Five Personality Traits
**Framework from McCrae and Costa.** Each person's personality can be broken down into five factors, each on a sliding scale.

| Trait | Low End | High End | Management relevance |
|-------|---------|----------|---------------------|
| **Extraversion** | Reserved, prefers to work alone, introverted | Sociable, assertive, energetic, prefers interaction | High extraversion often suits sales, public-facing, and high-communication roles |
| **Openness to Experience** | Pragmatic, cautious, avoids ambiguity | Creative, imaginative, risk-tolerant, unconventional | High openness suits innovation, strategy, and change roles |
| **Conscientiousness** | Flexible, spontaneous, loosely structured | Organized, persistent, disciplined, detail-oriented | High conscientiousness predicts task follow-through and reliability |
| **Agreeableness** | Competitive, challenging, skeptical | Cooperative, compassionate, trusting, harmonious | High agreeableness supports collaborative work; very high levels can make conflict avoidance a problem |
| **Emotional Stability** (low neuroticism) | Reactive, anxious, excitable under stress | Calm, confident, methodical even in crisis | High emotional stability is critical during crises and high-pressure situations |

**Key exam points:**
- No single Big Five profile guarantees leadership success
- The traits shape *how* someone tends to lead and build relationships, not whether they can
- Knowing your own Big Five profile helps you identify blind spots and growth areas

**Application to conflict:**
Different personalities create natural friction. Someone high in conscientiousness may clash with someone low in conscientiousness over deadlines. Someone high in agreeableness may avoid the direct feedback that a disagreeable colleague values. Self-awareness helps you manage these gaps.

## Locus of Control
**Definition:** The extent to which a person believes they can influence or control the outcomes of events.

Outcomes are attributed to four causes:
1. **Personal ability** — internal, stable
2. **Effort** — internal, unstable (can vary situation to situation)
3. **Task difficulty** — external, stable
4. **Luck or chance** — external, unstable

### Internals
- Believe that their own actions, effort, and decisions shape outcomes
- Tend to be more self-motivated and proactive
- More comfortable with delegation and participative decision making
- More likely to take initiative and persist through adversity
- Prefer more autonomy and less supervision

### Externals
- Believe outcomes are primarily shaped by luck, circumstance, or other people
- Tend to prefer more structure and direction from others
- May be less persistent when things go wrong (attribute failure to outside forces)
- May seek more feedback and reassurance

**Exam application:**
- Internals often respond well to Theory Y management and empowerment
- Externals may benefit from more directive leadership (Path–Goal: directive style)
- Knowing a follower's locus of control helps a leader choose the right approach

## Emotional Intelligence (EQ) — detailed breakdown
*(Also covered in [[Concepts/Leadership Styles, EQ & Self-Awareness|Leadership Styles, EQ & Self-Awareness]] — see that note for full exam detail)*

The four domains:

1. **Self-awareness** — recognizing your emotions, patterns, and triggers in real time
2. **Self-management** — regulating emotions so they serve rather than derail your goals
3. **Social awareness (empathy)** — reading others' emotional states and team dynamics accurately
4. **Relationship management** — influencing, inspiring, coaching, and navigating conflict effectively

Emotionally intelligent leaders are more adaptable because they make conscious choices about their style rather than defaulting to habit.

## Managing Adversity and Resilience
Leadership inherently involves adversity — setbacks, failures, ambiguity, and pressure. The chapter distinguishes leaders by how they respond to difficulty.

**Resilient leaders:**
- **Face reality directly** — don't minimize or deny problems
- **Maintain a sense of meaning** — connect setbacks to a larger purpose
- **Keep a sense of control** — focus on what they *can* influence rather than catastrophizing about what they cannot
- **Break problems into parts** — make large, overwhelming challenges manageable
- **Persist and adapt** — stay in motion rather than freezing
- **Learn from failure** — treat adversity as data, not identity

**Low resilience patterns:**
- Avoidance — refusing to confront the problem
- Catastrophizing — treating every setback as permanent and total
- Externalization — always attributing problems to outside forces (external locus)
- Collapse — inability to function under sustained pressure

**Why this matters for managers:**
Managers with low resilience model anxiety and avoidance to their teams. Resilient managers model steadiness, which reduces team anxiety and enables better problem-solving during crises.

## Self-Monitoring
**Definition:** The ability to read cues from social situations and adjust one's behavior accordingly.

| Level | Behavior | Leadership implication |
|-------|----------|----------------------|
| **High self-monitors** | Read the environment, adapt style to fit context, behave differently in different settings | More versatile; can lead very different kinds of people and situations effectively |
| **Low self-monitors** | Behave consistently regardless of context; prioritize authenticity over adaptation | More predictable and perceived as genuine, but may struggle in varied or multicultural settings |

**Important nuance:** High self-monitoring is not the same as being fake. It is the strategic use of social awareness to communicate more effectively with different people.

---

## Managing difficult situations
The chapter frames difficult situations as opportunities to apply self-awareness and emotional intelligence.

**Key tactics:**
1. **Pause before reacting** — your default emotional response under stress may not be the best leadership choice
2. **Name the emotion** — research shows labeling emotions reduces their intensity
3. **Seek perspective** — ask what the other person is experiencing, not just what you are
4. **Focus on behavior, not identity** — give feedback on actions, not character
5. **Regulate the room** — leaders' emotional states are contagious; stay composed to keep the group functional
6. **Know your triggers** — pre-identifying what pulls you toward ineffective reactions helps you catch them earlier
