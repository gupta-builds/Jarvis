# Week 5 — Leading Teams, IPO Framework & Conflict

## What you must be able to do
- Define a team and distinguish it clearly from a work group.
- Explain when teams are appropriate (task complexity + interdependence).
- Distinguish major team types by structure and authority.
- Use the full IPO framework to analyze what causes team success or failure.
- Define and explain all key team states: norms, identity, trust, psychological safety, efficacy, empowerment, cohesion.
- Distinguish task conflict from relationship conflict — and explain their opposite effects.
- Explain social loafing and groupthink and how to prevent each.
- Apply Tuckman's five stages of team development.
- State the three conditions for team effectiveness.

## Key ideas
- A team requires interdependence and shared accountability — not just shared space.
- Good teams depend on process design, not just talent.
- Team climate (safety, trust, norms) matters as much as technical skill.
- Productive disagreement improves teams; personal conflict damages them.
- Effective teams must produce results AND keep members healthy for future collaboration.

## Lecture

### 1. What makes a team a team — vs. a work group

**Work group:**
- Clearly focused single leader
- Individual accountability only
- Purpose mirrors the broader organization
- Independent tasks → individual work products
- Simpler, more routine work

**Team:**
- Shared leadership roles
- Individual AND mutual accountability
- Purpose specific to the team
- Interdependent tasks → collective work products
- Complex tasks requiring integrated expertise

**The critical distinction:** Teams add **interdependence** — members must rely on each other and integrate their work into a joint outcome. Without that, it's just a work group with a shared manager.

---

### 2. When are teams the right structure?

Teams are NOT always better. Use them when BOTH factors are high:

**Task complexity is high:**
- Too much information for one person to process
- Significant outcome uncertainty
- Multiple specialized skills required simultaneously
- Too many sub-tasks for one person to master

**Task interdependence is high:**
- Members must actively coordinate
- Output genuinely requires integrating different expertise
- Example: New product development requires marketing, engineering, design, and manufacturing to collaborate — not just report separately

When tasks are simple and independent, teams add overhead without value — use a work group or individual assignment instead.

---

### 3. Types of teams — six main categories

| Type | Key characteristics |
|------|-------------------|
| **Manager-led (vertical)** | Manager defines goals and methods; formal chain of command |
| **Manager-led (cross-functional / horizontal)** | Same hierarchical level, different departments — integrates diverse expertise |
| **Self-directed** | Team determines its own goals and methods; high empowerment but requires strong norms and maturity |
| **Advisory** | Provides recommendations without implementation authority |
| **Task force / project team** | Temporary; formed for a specific deliverable; disbands afterward |
| **Virtual / geographically distributed** | Members dispersed; rely on electronic communication tools |

**Collocated vs. virtual:**

| Feature | Collocated | Virtual |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Communication | Rich; body language, tone, spontaneous interaction | Lean; text and video; non-verbals lost |
| Trust building | Faster; natural social interaction | Requires intentional effort |
| Coordination | Lower cost | Higher cost; more misunderstanding risk |
| Flexibility | Lower | Higher |

---

### 4. The IPO Framework — full breakdown

#### INPUTS — what goes in before work starts

**Goal and purpose**
- Clear, meaningful, challenging goals drive higher engagement
- Shared understanding of purpose creates team identity

**Structure**
- Size: smaller = easier coordination; larger = more resources but coordination costs rise
- Role clarity: who owns what

**Composition (people)**
- Right mix of **technical skills** (can they solve it?) AND **interpersonal skills** (can they work together?)
- Diversity brings broader perspectives over the long term but creates friction in the short term

**Resources and tools**
- Budget, technology, information access, physical environment

---

#### STATES — internal conditions that develop as the team works

**Norms** — expected behaviors; must be established early and explicitly

Five norm categories the textbook identifies:
- **Meeting norms** — when, how often, who attends, how decisions are made in the room
- **Working norms** — deadlines, work quality standards, accountability mechanisms
- **Communication norms** — how to share information, how to express disagreement constructively
- **Leadership norms** — how decisions get made, who has final say
- **Consideration norms** — how members treat each other with respect

**Identity** — "We" feeling; members see themselves as part of something shared

**Trust** — confidence that teammates will follow through and act with good intentions

**Psychological safety**
> The belief that it is safe to speak up, ask questions, admit mistakes, and give candid feedback without fear of judgment or punishment.

Without safety:
- Members hide weakness and avoid admitting errors
- Real issues get suppressed and workarounds multiply
- Feedback disappears and problems fester
- Groupthink becomes likely

**Efficacy** — shared belief that the team can succeed; high efficacy teams persist longer and achieve more

**Empowerment** — shared sense of real authority, voice, and ownership over the team's work

**Cohesion** — attraction to the group; desire to remain and contribute. Healthy cohesion drives commitment. Excessive cohesion drives groupthink.

---

#### PROCESSES — how members interact during work

**Coordination** — synchronizing effort so members' contributions fit together without redundancy or gaps

**Participation** — all members contributing, not just the loudest voices

⚠️ **Common information effect:** Teams tend to discuss only information everyone already knows — failing to surface unique individual knowledge. This is one of the most costly team process failures.

**Peer monitoring and feedback** — tracking each other's progress and giving honest, specific input; requires psychological safety

**Conflict management:**
- **Task (cognitive) conflict** — disagreement about ideas, approaches, priorities → **improves decision quality** when properly managed
- **Relationship (affective) conflict** — personal tension, interpersonal friction → **consistently harms** performance and trust

*They often bleed into each other.* A task disagreement can become personal if the team lacks safety and norms for constructive debate.

**Blocking behaviors to prevent:**
- **Social loafing** — reducing effort when in a group because individual contribution is less visible (more common in large groups with unclear individual accountability)
- **Dominating** — one or two voices monopolizing discussion, blocking others' input
- **Fault-finding** — criticizing without contributing alternatives

**Backup behavior** — members helping each other when someone falls behind; only possible in high-trust, high-safety environments

---

#### OUTPUTS — what the team produces

**Three conditions for team effectiveness (all three must be met):**

| Condition | Definition |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Meaningful results** | Tangible outcomes meeting the standards of those receiving the work |
| **Member satisfaction** | Individuals feel valued, grow, and find the work worthwhile |
| **Future capacity (viability)** | The team's ability to work together again is *enhanced*, not depleted |

A team that produces great output but burns out its members fails condition 3. A team where everyone is satisfied but produces nothing fails condition 1.

---

### 5. Tuckman's Five Stages of Team Development

Teams typically move through a predictable sequence:

| Stage | What happens | Leadership priority |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Forming** | Polite exploration; defining the task; sizing up teammates | Set clear goals and norms |
| **Storming** | Conflict over roles, priorities, working styles | Facilitate constructive resolution; don't suppress |
| **Norming** | Shared standards emerge; cohesion builds | Reinforce healthy norms; maintain openness |
| **Performing** | Integrated work; differences bridged; roles settled | Enable autonomy; remove obstacles |
| **Adjourning** | Task complete; reflect on what was learned | Celebrate and extract lessons |

**Key insight about Storming:** This stage is normal and necessary. Teams that skip storming often have unresolved conflict that erupts at the worst possible moment (usually near the deadline). The goal isn't to eliminate conflict — it's to make it productive.

**Midpoint transition:** Many teams experience a behavioral shift at the halfway point toward their deadline — abandoning approaches that aren't working and recommitting to finishing. Effective teams recognize this transition and use it productively.

---

### 6. Trust, safety, and groupthink — connecting the states

**Groupthink** occurs when cohesion and conformity pressure suppress critical evaluation:
- The group wants harmony more than good decisions
- Dissenters self-censor to avoid being seen as negative
- The team collectively rationalizes weak decisions
- Illusion of invulnerability — "we're too good to fail"

**Prevention:**
- Designate a "devil's advocate" role
- Invite dissent explicitly in team norms
- Leaders should withhold their own opinions initially to avoid anchoring
- Create structured processes (e.g., pre-mortem analysis) to surface concerns

**Social loafing prevention:**
- Make individual contributions visible and measurable
- Keep teams small enough that everyone's contribution is clearly identifiable
- Build clear individual accountability into team norms
- Ensure each member has a meaningful, non-replaceable role

---

### 7. Boundary management — how teams interact with the outside

| Stance | Description |
|--------|------------|
| **Informing** | Minimal outsider interaction; relies on internal knowledge |
| **Parading** | Passive; promotes work to external groups without soliciting input |
| **Probing** | Active; constantly engages outsiders and revises based on their needs |

**Probing is most adaptive** but also most demanding. Teams with probing orientations tend to produce work that better fits real organizational needs.

IPO framework::Inputs (design, people, resources) → States and Processes (norms, trust, safety, coordination) → Outputs (results, satisfaction, viability).
Team norms::Shared behavioral expectations covering meetings, work, communication, leadership, and respect — must be set early.
Psychological safety::A team climate where members believe they can speak up, ask questions, admit mistakes, and give candid feedback without fear of punishment.
Task conflict::Disagreement about ideas, approaches, or priorities — can improve decision quality when managed constructively.
Relationship conflict::Personal tension and interpersonal friction — consistently harms trust and team performance.
Social loafing::Reduced individual effort when in a group, often because individual contributions are less visible.
Groupthink::Poor group decisions caused by conformity pressure, desire for harmony, and suppression of critical evaluation.
Tuckman's stages::Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing → Adjourning — the typical team development sequence.
Three conditions for team effectiveness::Meaningful results + Member satisfaction + Future capacity (viability to work together again).
