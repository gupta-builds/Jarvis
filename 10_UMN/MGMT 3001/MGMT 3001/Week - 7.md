# Week 7 — Organizational Culture: Levels, Formation & Change

## What you must be able to do
- Define organizational culture precisely and explain why it matters.
- Distinguish artifacts, espoused values, and underlying assumptions — know all three levels deeply.
- Explain how culture affects organizational performance (both positive and negative effects).
- Explain who builds culture and how — roles of founders, leaders, and teams.
- Identify the main mechanisms through which culture spreads and is reinforced.
- Explain what makes culture strong vs. weak, and what subcultures are.
- Explain why culture change is extremely difficult and what it actually requires.

## Key ideas
- Culture teaches people what is normal, rewarded, and risky — without anyone saying so explicitly.
- Visible symbols are only the surface; the most powerful layer is the deepest and least visible.
- What an organization says it values is not always what it actually rewards.
- Strong cultures can improve alignment and performance — but can also create rigidity and ethical blind spots.
- Culture spreads through repeated signals, not slogans.

## Lecture

### 1. Why culture matters — the opening claim
The lecture opens with a strong assertion: culture is **hard to determine, hard to imitate, and extremely powerful** in shaping behavior. In some organizations, culture exerts more influence over behavior than any formal strategy, policy, or management system.

Why? Because culture operates **continuously** — not just when managers are present or when formal systems are checked. Culture shapes:
- How people handle situations that fall outside the formal rules
- Whether people speak up or stay quiet
- What ambition looks like (drive results? build relationships? stay safe?)
- What happens to people who challenge the status quo

This makes culture a potential source of **sustainable competitive advantage** — hard to observe from outside and nearly impossible to copy.

---

### 2. Defining organizational culture precisely

**Textbook definition:** Culture is a **pattern of shared basic assumptions** that a group learns as it solves its problems. These assumptions work well enough to be considered valid, and are taught to new members as the **correct way to perceive, think, and feel** about organizational challenges.

Five lenses for reading a culture:
| Lens | What to observe |
|------|----------------|
| **Values and philosophy** | What does the company say it stands for? What is its stated purpose? |
| **Group boundaries** | What makes someone a cultural insider? What is expected? |
| **Power structure** | Who advances? How is status earned, kept, and lost? |
| **Work norms** | What does a "good day's work" look like? What gets sanctioned? |
| **Reward and punishment** | What behavior gets celebrated? What gets ignored or punished? |

The gap between the stated answer and the observable reality on each lens reveals the **true culture**.

---

### 3. The three levels of culture — exam essential

#### Level 1: Artifacts (Visible)
The observable surface — what a newcomer can see, hear, and feel:
- Physical layout (open office vs. private offices; casual vs. formal)
- Dress code and communication style
- Company logos, slogans, language, and acronyms
- Ceremonies, rituals, and celebrations
- Stories and myths that circulate about founders or heroes
- How meetings are run; who speaks first; how conflict surfaces

**Critical exam point:** Artifacts are the *most visible* but the *most ambiguous*. Two companies can have identical open-plan offices and mean completely different things by them. You can see an artifact without understanding what it signifies.

#### Level 2: Espoused Values and Beliefs (Known)
The meanings and principles that members attach to artifacts:
- Stated company values (e.g., "We value transparency and innovation")
- What leaders say they believe about customers, employees, and success
- Formal mission statements and codes of conduct

**Critical exam point:** Espoused values may or may not match actual behavior. The gap between what an org *says* it values and what it *actually* rewards is where culture problems hide. This gap is the most common source of employee cynicism.

#### Level 3: Underlying Assumptions (Deeply Embedded)
The deepest level — unconscious beliefs that drive behavior without being questioned:
- "Around here, challenging leadership damages your career" (even if the stated value is "speak your truth")
- "Real commitment means 60+ hour weeks" (even if flexibility is an official policy)
- "Sales always wins over compliance" (even if an ethics function exists)

**Critical exam point:** These assumptions feel like *reality*, not culture. They are the most powerful drivers of behavior and the hardest to change — because they are no longer consciously held as choices.

---

### 4. How culture affects performance — a two-sided story

#### Benefits of strong culture:
- **Goal alignment** — shared assumptions create consistent behavior without constant monitoring
- **Role clarity** — members know what's expected even in novel situations
- **Faster coordination** — less negotiation needed when everyone shares assumptions
- **Stronger commitment** — members feel connected to something meaningful
- **Moral compass** — culture is often the primary determinant of ethical behavior, more than formal compliance programs

#### Risks of strong culture:
- **Suppresses dissent** — disagreement is seen as disloyalty, not problem-solving
- **Resists change** — deeply embedded assumptions make adaptation painful
- **Ethical blind spots** — a culture that rewards "winning" can normalize unethical shortcuts
- **Subculture conflict** — different units develop incompatible assumptions and struggle to collaborate

---

### 5. Cultural strength — four combinations

| Combination | Agreement | Intensity | Result |
|-------------|-----------|-----------|--------|
| **Strong** | High | High | Unified, consistent behavior |
| **Passive** | High | Low | Know the values; don't act on them |
| **Competing forces** | Low | High | Different groups fighting over different values |
| **Weak** | Low | Low | No clear cultural direction |

---

### 6. Who builds culture — three actors

#### Founders
Founders imprint their assumptions from the very beginning through:
- What they choose to prioritize and ignore
- How they handle the first conflicts and crises
- Who they hire, fire, and promote in the earliest days
- Their own behavior as a template others observe and mimic

The founding assumptions often persist long after the founders are gone — embedded in routines and stories.

#### Organizational leaders
Leaders are the most powerful **ongoing** shapers of culture. Culture is transmitted through:

| Leader behavior | Cultural signal sent |
|----------------|---------------------|
| **What they pay attention to and measure** | "This is what matters here" |
| **How they react to crises** | "These are our true priorities when values are tested" |
| **How they allocate resources** | "Money follows actual priorities" |
| **Role modeling** | "Behavior that looks like mine gets rewarded" |
| **Who they hire, promote, and exit** | "This is who belongs here" |

**The most powerful cultural signal:** How a leader behaves during a *crisis* — when following official values is costly or difficult. That behavior reveals the true underlying assumptions.

#### Teams
When teams repeatedly solve problems the same way, their methods become **organizational routines**. Once applied without questioning, those routines become embedded cultural assumptions at the team and unit level.

---

### 7. Strong culture examples from lecture
The lecture uses companies like Zappos, L.L. Bean, Warby Parker, and Adobe to illustrate strong culture elements:

**Elements that signal strong, healthy culture:**
- Clear mission and values that are lived, not just posted
- High participation in goal-setting and decision making
- Transparency about performance, challenges, and direction
- Consistent celebration of wins and recognition of contributions
- Accessible leadership — not hidden behind hierarchy
- Alignment between stated culture and business outcomes

**Four features of cultures that support organizational success:**
1. **Adaptable** — responsive to environmental change
2. **Employee involvement focused** — members feel ownership and voice
3. **Mission-driven** — clear purpose beyond profit
4. **Consistent** — values are applied uniformly, not selectively

### 8. How culture spreads — socialization tools

**Socialization** is how new members learn what behavior is expected and rewarded.

| Tool | How it works |
|------|-------------|
| **Formal statements** | Credos, values documents, onboarding materials communicate explicit expectations |
| **Physical space** | Office layout and design communicate hierarchy vs. openness, focus vs. collaboration |
| **Reward systems** | What gets recognized and compensated signals what is truly valued |
| **Stories and myths** | Parables about organizational heroes teach what the culture rewards in action |
| **Recruiting and selection** | Hiring for "cultural fit" through behavioral interviews |
| **Onboarding** | Early structured experiences that transmit assumptions before other habits form |

**Three-stage organizational commitment:**
1. **Compliance** — "I follow the rules in exchange for pay"
2. **Identification** — "I identify with this group and want to belong"
3. **Internalization** — "My values and the organization's values are genuinely aligned"

Internalized employees act like owners — they go beyond their job description because they believe in the mission.

---

### 9. Culture in critical moments

**Joining a new firm:**
New members must quickly decode which artifacts reflect true values and which are aspirational. The fastest decoder: watch what happens when someone violates an implicit norm.

**Mergers and acquisitions:**
- Research shows **90% of mergers fail to meet expectations**
- Cultural clash is one of the primary causes
- Before acquiring, leaders must evaluate both cultures and decide: whose culture dominates? Which elements from each should survive? Is genuine merging into a new culture possible (rare) or will one absorb the other?

---

### 10. Culture change — why it's so hard

**Culture change requires far more than:**
- A new set of stated values
- New slogans or posters
- A company-wide training session
- A new leader who talks differently

**Culture change requires:**
- Sustained leadership behavior change over *years*, not months
- Changes to *systems* (rewards, promotions, measurement) — not just language
- Repeated new experiences that create new assumptions
- Consistent role modeling from the top — leaders who preach new values but embody old ones undermine every initiative

**Why most culture programs fail:** They target artifacts and espoused values without touching underlying assumptions or the structural systems that reinforce them. You can rename values and redecorate offices without changing a single assumption.
