# Chapter - 1
## Big picture: what this course is training you to do
The course roadmap includes topics like management theory, leadership, strategy, decision making, teams, culture, change, power, conflict/negotiation, ethics, and global management.
A useful way to think about it:
- **Strategy** = where we want to compete and how we plan to win.
- **Management** = how we coordinate people + resources to execute.
- **Leadership** = how we influence and motivate people through change.
>Strategy chooses the game.  
>Management builds the system to play it well.  
>Leadership gets people to commit and adapt.
## VUCA: the environment modern managers deal with
**VUCA** is a way to describe why managing today feels hard: the outside world changes fast and isn’t always predictable.
1. *V* = Volatility (things change fast)
	- Example: your main supplier suddenly increases prices or shuts down.
2. *U* = Uncertainty (you don’t know what the change means yet)
	- Example: a competitor launches a product - will customers actually switch?
3. *C* = Complexity (many connected parts)
	- Example: growth affects hiring, customer support, product quality, and cash flow all at once.
4. *A* = Ambiguity (cause-effect is unclear)
	- Example: marketing results are mixed; you can’t tell which channel caused the growth.
**Manager move in VUCA:** use short cycles of learning - small tests, fast feedback, and quick adjustments. (That’s basically how early-stage startups survive.)
## The “3 pillars” of management (core framework)
The chapter frames management around **three interdependent pillars**:  
1. **Strategic positioning**
2. **Organizational design**  
3. **Individual leadership**
>These pillars move together. Changing one usually forces changes in the others.
### 2.1 Strategic positioning (where/how the business competes)
This perspective involves understanding the **environmental landscape** and determining how a business should compete to align its resources for success. Think: “What are we offering, to whom, and why would they choose us?”
>Strategy is a set of choices, not a wish.
>For entrepreneurship: define your target customer, problem, and value proposition.
### 2.2 Organizational design (how we set up the work)
Once a strategy is set, the organization must be **structured to optimize resources**. This includes:
- Roles and responsibilities  
- Processes (how work happens)
- Culture (what behaviors get rewarded)
- Hiring + performance practices
>Design turns strategy into repeatable action.
>For entrepreneurship: even a 3-person team needs clarity on **who owns what** and **how decisions get made**.
### 2.3 Individual leadership (how we move people toward the goal)
Since an organization is a collection of individuals, leadership is required to **coordinate and motivate diverse people** to work toward a common goal. Results depend on motivation, communication, conflict handling, and alignment.
## Management vs. leadership (not the same, both needed)
1. *Management (execution + coordination)*: 
	Management is **working with and through a group of people to accomplish a goal efficiently and effectively**. Managers focus on **planning, budgeting, organizing, staffing, and problem-solving**.
2. *Leadership (change + direction)*:
	Leadership is about **driving change and innovation** by setting direction and motivating people.
A quick way to remember:
- **Leaders**: set direction, align people, motivate/inspire.
- **Managers**: plan/budget, organize/staff, control/problem-solve.
> **Entrepreneurship link:** founders usually do *both* (especially early on). You’re setting direction while also building the systems to deliver.
## Skills by level (what matters as you move up)
The course emphasizes different skills depending on your role:
1. *Technical skills*
	- Know-how in a specific area (tools, tasks, methods).
	- Most important for entry-level and frontline roles.
2. *Interpersonal skills*
	- Communicating, collaborating, resolving conflict.
	- Important at every level.
3. *Conceptual skills*
	- Big-picture thinking, strategy, understanding systems and tradeoffs.
	- Most important for top leadership roles.
>As you move up, your job shifts from “doing tasks” to “designing how tasks get done.”
>**Entrepreneurship link:** early on you need technical + interpersonal; as you scale you must strengthen conceptual skills fast.
## Where management ideas came from (major perspectives)
The lecture frames “modern management theories” as several perspectives, not one single truth.
### 5.1 Classical perspective: Scientific management
*Goal*: improve efficiency by redesigning work using systematic study (like time and motion). The lecture lists ways to raise efficiency:
1. Study the job and create better methods  
2. Match workers to jobs
3. Train standard methods
4. Plan work and reduce interruptions
5. Use performance incentives
**Limitations** mentioned:
- Specialized jobs can become boring
- Managers may not share the gains
- Workers might underperform on purpose (“ratcheting”)
### 5.2 Classical perspective: Bureaucracy (Max Weber)
Bureaucracy is a formal system designed for efficiency and effectiveness. Key elements listed in the *lecture*:
- Qualification based hiring, merit based promotion
- Chain of command, division of labor
- Impartial rules, written records
- Managers separate from owners
Textbook definition adds: clear tasks, strict hierarchy/decision rights, standardized rules, and separating planning (upper levels) from execution (lower levels).
### 5.3 Humanistic (human relations) perspective
The Hawthorne Studies showed productivity increased regardless of lighting changes, people responded to attention and the social setting. Mary Parker Follett emphasized:
- Workers should help analyze jobs for improvement
- If workers know the task, they should have control
- Informal groups matter inside organizations
### 5.4 Quantitative (management science) perspective
Uses quantitative techniques to maximize resources, including operations research and **MIS** (*management information systems*).
### 5.5 Systems thinking
Organizations are linked parts (marketing, finance, accounting, etc.) and they receive inputs from other systems. The key question is how parts affect each other.
### 5.6 Contingency view (depends on the situation)
Emerging in the 1960s, this view argues that there is **no "one-size-fits-all" structure**. Instead, an effective organizational structure depends on the **fit or alignment** between the organization and its specific environment,. The *lecture* contrasts:
- “One best way” vs “Every situation is unique”
The textbook explains the contingent view as: effective structure depends on fit/alignment between the organization and its environment.
>Good management is matching the approach to the situation.
## How people think about the purpose of business (changed over time)
The textbook summarizes shifts in what business should focus on:
- **Managerial view** (up to 1960s): Saw the firm as a mechanism for *converting raw materials into products* for sale. Focus on production and direct relationships (suppliers/customers/owners/employees).
- **Shareholder view** (1960s–1980s): Held that the primary job of top managers was to produce the *highest possible stock market valuation* of the firm's assets. Focus on financial performance.
- **Stakeholder view** (since 1990s): A framework that identifies and analyzes *multiple groups that interact with the firm* and attempts to satisfy their diverse needs. Focus on serving multiple constituencies.
## Stakeholder approach (super important for modern firms)
A **stakeholder** is any group or individual who can affect, or is affected by, an organization’s purpose.
### 7.1 Stakeholder mapping (4-step process)
According to **stakeholder theory**, a stakeholder is **any group or individual who can affect or is affected by** the achievement of an organization's purpose.
1. Map relationships  
2. Identify subsets  
3. Determine stakes  
4. Define connections
> Stakeholder mapping is basically “who matters, why they matter, and how they connect.”
> **Entrepreneurship link:** do this for your startup idea: Customers, suppliers, employees/cofounders, platform partners, local community, regulators, etc.
### 7.2 Stakeholder management processes (how firms actually do it)
The chapter describes processes like:
- Strategic review process (leaders + managers review progress and incorporate stakeholder analysis)
- Environmental scanning (watch trends/events that may affect the firm in the future)
- Scenario building (imagine outcomes when events/stakeholders interact)
- Contingency planning (prepare for alternative futures)
- Trend analysis (track variables to anticipate change) 
## Contextual intelligence + managing uncertainty
Managers face varying levels of uncertainty based on the **number of contextual factors** and the **rate of change** within those factors.
### Contextual intelligence
Defined as understanding how environmental factors affect a firm and knowing how to react or influence them.
### Managing uncertainty (common mistake patterns)
- Underestimate uncertainty → don’t prepare enough
- Overestimate uncertainty → “paralysis” (no action because you feel you can’t influence anything)
>Contextual intelligence = “read the environment and respond without freezing.”
>**Entrepreneurship link:** your job is to reduce uncertainty with: customer interviews, prototypes, small experiments, clear metrics
## Theory X vs Theory Y (manager assumptions about people)
The lecture frames these as two different assumptions leaders can make about workers.
### Theory X (control-heavy assumption)
- People dislike work; must be monitored/pressured to perform.
### Theory Y (trust + autonomy assumption)
- People want to do well; will accept responsibility when given autonomy and meaningful rewards.
The lecture gives a simple comparison:
- Theory Y style: encourages team input and explains why their voice matters
- Theory X style: demands completion and monitors compliance.
>**Practical takeaway:** your assumption changes your leadership style, team culture, and whether people feel safe contributing ideas.
