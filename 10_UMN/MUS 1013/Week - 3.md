### In Class Assignment
**Form:** Both versions use a **12-bar blues** foundation and fit a **simple verse-chorus** setup where the hook (“You ain’t nothin’ but a hound dog…”) functions like the chorus that keeps returning.
**Differences:** Big Mama Thornton’s original sounds more like blues/R&B: rougher vocal timbre, bluesy guitar responses, and a looser band feel, with lyrics that come from hokum-blues style double meanings.  
Elvis’s 1956 version is more rockabilly/rock-and-roll: a tighter, more driving groove, a more “pop single” arrangement (drums and backup vocals are obvious), and lyric/attitude shifts that make it feel less adult and more mainstream.
1. Form: simple verse chorus built on a 12-bar blues framework. A fresh look at those first rhythm and blues tracks shows how often they leaned on a 12-bar blues pattern underneath. Instead of just talking in general, the writer points to "Hound Dog" by Thornton as an example when tracing where early rock lyrics came from. Plain truth is this tune runs on a loop of classic blues changes.
2. Differences between the original and the cover
	- Style/genre emphasis: Blues and R&B shape Thornton's work, especially how she performs and writes lyrics. Her approach lives inside that playful, sly storytelling line.
	- Lyrics and “tone”: That book talks about hokum blues, which helps make sense of Thornton's lyrics - lines like “snoopin", "round my door,” “wag your tail,” or “feed you.” When heard through that lens, they carry hints meant for grown-ups. Context shifts how those words land. What sounds playful might not be so innocent after all. Funny how it matches what comes through the speakers. Thornton's take lands like a sharp jab, laced with attitude. Elvis, on the other hand, slides into something flashier, built for crowds and lights.
	- Beat feel/energy: That little extra drive in the rhythm shows up when pop versions cover R&B songs. A livelier pulse replaces the calm groove of the earlier track. White artists speed things just enough to make them bounce. The mood shifts without changing notes. Energy rises where ease once lived. Folks often hear it this way when comparing Thornton’s version to Elvis’s - his comes across as tauter, pushing forward with more urgency.
