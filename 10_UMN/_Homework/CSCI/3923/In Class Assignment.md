### Drop In Canvas Assignment
1. *What is the purpose of an ethics class? is it possible to learn or teach ethics?*:
	Thinking through tough choices is what an ethics course helps with, particularly when it comes to artificial intelligence. Instead of offering solutions, it builds your ability to handle uncertain situations. Since technology evolves quickly, pausing to reflect becomes essential. Real lives are affected in serious ways, like unfair treatment in lending decisions or being watched without consent. Questioning things from the start makes a difference. Who ends up hurt? What role do the builders play? That kind of reflection shapes better outcomes. Who gets to decide what's fair in this situation anyway? Hidden costs always show up later, one way or another. Could a person pick up ethics by being taught? Not exactly, though guiding someone through moral thinking helps. Picture walking them step by step - naming who gets affected, questioning beliefs buried under the surface, shaping strong arguments, putting systems in place to prevent harm. With AI, it isn’t about gut feelings, it shows up as structure, rules, shared responsibility. Think of unesco’s take: fairness matters, people must stay central, openness counts, decisions should be traceable, dignity stays protected. Trust flows through the actions take on artificial intelligence, where systems must honor basic freedoms and shared societal beliefs. Running parallel, NIST built a hands on guide not just theory, that walks step by step through spotting and handling dangers at every stage of AI development. 
	Yep, locking down AI might actually make sense before things go sideways. Think of the EU’s AI Act like a filter: it blocks sketchy applications while setting rules for those that could cause real harm. Taking an AI ethics course helps you spot problems early, long before anyone needs to rush in with fixes.
2. *Is Data Objective? Are Algorithms objective? Is AI Objective? Why? Why not?*:
	What seems solid on paper still flows from decisions made by people, choices about participation, naming and what not. Leave some voices out, let assumptions color tags, then call the result truth? That slant slips straight into the system. Researchers at MIT put it plainly. When training data carries bias, the model inherits limits along with lessons. Its reach narrows before it even begins. What seems like cold logic often hides human choices underneath. Goals built into systems reflect priorities chosen by people, not neutral facts. Math might work perfectly, yet still serve biased aims. Accuracy can crowd out safety, just as profit may override fairness. Code does what it's told, nothing more. 
	Because of similar reasons plus others, AI lacks objectivity. Shaped not only by data but also by choices in design, how it gets used matters too. The environment where systems run plays a role alongside those interacting with them. unescos's advice on ethical AI emphasizes justice, basic rights, protection measures. That stance alone shows detached, impartial artificial intelligence does not emerge naturally. Truth sits ahead like a target, never something claimed outright. Getting there means digging into how data gets gathered, checking results on different people, then standing by what happens when things go live.

## Week - 2
### Drop in canvas Assignment
1. *Prompt 1*: add another law to Kranzberg’s list of laws.
	Law: The greater a system’s reach and impact, the stronger the duty to explain it, test it for harm, and provide a real way to challenge its decisions.
	Why it fits: Connects directly to algorithmic scoring, automated decisions, and real-world harm. Let's refer to when social media was launched, it was just supposed to a means to communicate with people across the world. Soon it turned out to expose nudity to children, dopamine addiction and I can keep going on. So when software like such are released, it's best to spend a lot of time testing and debating whether this is what is needed for 7 biliion people.
2. *Prompt 2*: Tuskegee research
	This happens often in technology: damage gets labeled as advancement. Rather than asking permission, firms gather personal details or test changes on people, claiming better results follow. It also appears in A/B testing, where platforms run experiments on users. What they do is change what people see in feeds, ads, or notifications to increase engagement, without clear consent(cookies). With artificial intelligence, systems learn from huge datasets, sometimes unfair or protected by law. While leaders insist it's essential for growth, regardless of who suffers. 
3. *Prompt 3*: Therac-25 research
	Someone down the line might hit the button, yet blame rarely belongs there alone. Decisions stack, poor planning today becomes failure tomorrow. Fault travels like a crack in glass, branching from one weak point to another. Ownership spreads wide, though it must land somewhere real. Not everyone shares equal fault but all involved bear some part. Clear lines prevent excuses when things break apart. A single team must own safety, so confusion fades when choices get made. Decisions show up in written records, leaving a trail others can follow. Someone outside the group checks the work now and then, just to be sure. Close calls get shared openly, not brushed aside. High risk tools wait for release until they pass strict tests plus constant watch afterward.
4. *Prompt 4*: Facebook
	Understanding your data matters today. When sites gather information, folks deserve to know exactly what gets taken, plus the reasons behind it. Who sees it later must be clear too. Length of storage? That detail often goes missing. Hidden dangers in services need light. Hitting agree rarely counts when pages stretch on, fuzzy and built for blind approval. Imagine smaller messages written like normal speech. Choices about private details need clear permission each time. Studies or tests? Separate approval needed there too. Turning things down should be painless, no penalties hiding behind buttons. Back out fully if you want, anytime at all.
## Week - 3
### [[10_UMN/CSCI 3923/Week - 3#Lecture|Drop in canvas Assignment]]
1. Privacy matters even when someone did nothing wrong. Because rights guard everyone, not just those accused of crimes. Government power needs boundaries so it cannot overreach easily. A key reason the Fourth Amendment exists is to stop random searches, is that fairness demands proof before intrusion. Warrants backed by real evidence serve that purpose. When authorities watch without rules, fear grows among ordinary people. Certain communities often feel that pressure more sharply. Wrong numbers lead to wrong choices, people get hurt by mistakes hidden inside code. Machines guess, humans trust, outcomes break lives without warning. A single flaw in logic treats someone unjusticely, yet there is no way to fight back. When decisions come from black boxes, fairness gets lost even if you followed every rule. Silent judgments pile up where questions aren’t allowed. Watched messages, movements, queries these make some hold back, even when they’ve done nothing wrong. Laws shield us not just from guilt but from control without limits. What matters isn’t suspicion. It’s who holds the leash.
2. A tool needing shared information happens to be Google Maps (Apple Maps fits here too). Getting where you need to go becomes smoother when your position is shared. Since apps know your spot, directions stay on point without guesswork. Traffic alerts pop up the moment congestion builds ahead. When delays hit, new paths appear before you’re stuck. If an accident blocks the road, the system guides you around it quietly. In emergencies, help finds you faster because your location sends automatically. Efficiency grows simply by letting systems respond in real time. Home addresses, job sites, places of worship, these spots trace lives. Because location trails show habits like gym trips or doctor visits, they expose more than intended. Stored too long, those records might slip into databases beyond your control. Law officers could ask for them without warning. Companies may pass them to partners who weren’t part of the original deal. Picture this. Location access works just during active navigation, stops when you close the app. Stored data sticks around only as long as it must. Sharing details with advertisers never happens unless someone actively agrees first. Removing personal records takes one step, no hurdles. Background monitoring runs only if turned on deliberately, not automatically. Shutting down tracking feels effortless, built right into settings.
3. 1. Sometimes I understand how the school handles my information. Running things like courses, scores, internet access, security, and funding support clearly needs personal details. Yet how long records stay on file often feels unclear. Access by staff members varies without clear patterns. Information moving between internal platforms lacks transparency. Sharing with external companies, those providing educational software, exam monitoring, or online storage isn’t always explained. What happens behind those processes remains partly hidden.
	2. Do I actually finish reading the Terms of Service or privacy notices? Rarely. Lengthy wording plus confusing language make most students skip ahead, access to courses hinges on clicking agree. True understanding slips away when approval comes by default.
	3. Giving up privacy just to learn - does that make sense? Fairness comes into question here. Enrollment details, scores, entry to school buildings some info needs recording. Yet signing up for class should not mean agreeing to constant monitoring. Balance appears only when information gathered stays small, purpose narrow. Clear explanations matter. Protection must be strong. Students deserve actual control: chances to say no, ways to erase data, rules blocking outside companies from using personal records freely. What counts is respect, not compliance.
4. Without handing over much of your private life, can you still be part of today’s world? Hardly at all. Daily routines like logging into school systems or checking bank balances leave traces behind. Using a phone often means handing over details, whether wanted or not. Skipping some online spaces makes a difference too. Yet staying active in modern life almost always requires giving something up front. Most tools demand acceptance before allowing entry. Staying offline entirely just pushes someone to the edges. Fundamental right, privacy might shape fairer systems, yet some treat it like a favor earned through sacrifice. Could fairness exist without guarded boundaries? Often lost when ease becomes the louder deal. What if privacy were truly guaranteed? When access to essentials like jobs or medical care depends on surrendering personal data, saying "no" becomes impossible. Those already vulnerable face the sharpest trade offs, forced consent isn’t freedom. Systems built on mandatory exposure quietly erode fairness. Least protected often bear the cost. Who must answer when things go wrong, the builders of tools or those who set the rules? Could it be both, depending on how power shifts moment by moment. Start by collecting less user information, tech firms can still operate without hoarding every detail. Clarity matters explain how data gets used instead of burying it in pages nobody reads. Sharing? Only when necessary, never casual or unchecked. Give people actual choices, not just checkboxes that pretend to offer control but let them leave, erase traces, see what’s stored. When systems fail or decisions vanish behind code, somebody has to answer, make companies face consequences instead of vanishing into fine print.

## Week - 6
### Drop in Assignment
1. Using extra power for new gadgets isn’t clearly right or wrong. What matters is where that energy comes from, along with who gains and who suffers. Today, tiny digital habits seem innocent enough - yet when multiplied across millions of searches, massive server farms appear, chewing through vast amounts of juice. This flood of consumption turns into an environmental weight we can’t ignore. Given most grids still burn coal and gas, the toll hides behind sleek screens, unseen by users flipping open laptops. So under today’s conditions, labeling this trend harmful fits better than pretending otherwise. While machines speed up green power and smarter systems, the real aim isn’t halting progress. It’s shifting its fuel source. Who does what counts: people can toss less, firms must trim server pollution while building leaner tools, meanwhile officials ought to shape rules and networks that lift renewable energy into view, not keep damage out of sight.
2. A single phone and a laptop - that’s what sticks out when I look back at the past half decade. Five years passed before I ever owned a mobile device, while this computer arrived roughly three seasons ago. Earlier still stood an older desktop machine, televisions filled rooms long before any of them showed up. Run it through something like an electronic waste estimator, and suddenly power use feels less central than the full journey - how raw elements are pulled from earth, shaped into gadgets, moved across oceans, only to vanish later after being swapped out. What sits heavy is how easily forgotten those outdated items become, particularly tiny things: cords coiled in drawers, worn-out cells, plugs without purpose, little listening pieces that disappear one by one. It turns out fairness isn’t built into tech updates. Just saying people should upgrade wisely ignores that fixes, secure recycling, or long-lasting gear aren’t within reach for all. What makes sense to me? Hold on to gadgets longer. Fix them if you can. Think hard before tossing, don’t act like swapping devices is routine.
3. Maybe tech gadgets ought to show their real cost to people and planet, yet figuring that out exactly feels messy. Still, here’s why backing transparency makes sense - right now damage hides in plain sight: mining disasters, factory risks, pollution from factories, discarded electronics dumped on vulnerable communities. This mirrors older stories of injustice: scatter the pain far enough, keep it unseen, suddenly responsibility fades. Show those consequences front and center? Users may pause before upgrading yearly, firms might build tougher, fixable, reusable gear. Sure, some fear flawed markets or weak budget models taking over - but even so, honest signals around true tolls can lift norms, improve recycling networks, awaken understanding about what’s actually in our pockets.
4. Rich countries churn out heaps of gadgets per person, so their role in global e-waste stands out. Since these places profit heavily from fast, low-cost production lines, ignoring what happens later feels off balance. Much like we’ve seen in past examples, pain often lands on those with little say, while gains stay tucked safely away. Constant new models bring convenience northward, yet smokestacks and sickness tend to gather somewhere else. The pattern repeats - advantage clusters one place, fallout spreads to another. Truth is, fair solutions come from rules such as making manufacturers pay for collecting and processing used goods. Better repair rights for devices play a role, just as tight oversight on where old electronics go stops shady disposal under the label of reuse. People tossing gadgets out bear some duty, though those shaping products and moving them worldwide should answer most. The Basel system gives structure, however muscle comes only when checks tighten, data improves, and local systems actually handle waste where it starts.
