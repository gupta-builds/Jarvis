Human Brains Are Not Unique?

True, people do stand out, mainly since their brains carry a bigger front section, particularly the outer part that handles thinking. Still, it is worth noting that nothing inside is entirely new compared to other animals; every backbone creature runs on similar mental blueprints. While size shifts things, the pieces themselves? They follow age-old patterns found across species. Parts of the brain tied to the topic have specific roles, these functions back up my point. Each region handles distinct tasks, linking directly to the argument made earlier. Their involvement isn’t random, it matches the behavior or response discussed. What happens inside them helps explain why the claim makes sense
1. Telencephalon (cerebrum/forebrain): Despite its modest look, the telencephalon handles tasks such as sorting through what the senses detect, picking up new skills, storing experiences, organizing steps ahead, and making choices layered with thought. When labs ran side-by-side checks on animals with backbones, a pattern stuck out. The proportion of brain taken by the telencephalon shifted noticeably. Take the drawings examined - carp, frog, snake, pigeon, rabbit and you’d spot right away it takes up more room in creatures like rabbits than in ones like carp, this lines up neatly with the notion that backbone species vary mostly in how much mental real estate fuels advanced thinking.
2. Cerebellum (hindbrain): Balance and smooth motion rely heavily on the cerebellum. Flight demands sharp timing, so it fits that pigeons show a notably big cerebellum in scans. When we look closer, differences in brain part sizes link clearly to an animal’s daily survival tasks. Though built from similar pieces, vertebrate brains adjust proportions based on lifestyle. What stands out is how need shapes structure over time.
3. Medulla oblongata/brainstem (hindbrain): Breathing, along with heartbeat regulation, falls under the control of the medulla. All vertebrates share this trait survival demands it, so their brains contain a brainstem area. Though species differ wildly, that core piece remains unchanged.

Lab results include exact figures backing the findings Looking at our sheep dissection numbers, the brain inside the sheep turned out to be: ~60% forebrain, ~55% telencephalon (cerebrum). This suggests in animals such as sheep, over fifty percent of the brain's space belongs to the front part(telencephalon) matching what earlier vertebrate charts showed: compared with many backboned creatures, mammals usually pack more brain into that region. Being mammals too, people follow this general layout yet go further, assigning an unusually high share of neural mass to their outer brain layer. A trend visible across species reaches its peak here. That day, the sheep showed up in the notes with these details:

This suggests big brains by themselves do not reveal how unique an animal might be. What matters more lies in how brain tissue divides among areas - say, telencephalon compared to brainstem, then links to behavior. Humans appear distinct not because they possess a brain, rather due to enlarged forebrains enabling advanced thinking.

Conclusion: Brains in people stand out mostly by size, not structure. Just like other backboned animals, human brains split into three main sections - front, middle, rear. Handling basics such as keeping us alive, moving muscles smoothly, noticing surroundings. What makes our species different? A big chunk of brain tissue piles up in the front area, especially the outer layer, fueling deep thinking, long-term choices, problem-solving on the fly. Observations from sheep show similar layout - about 60 percent in the front zone, half of total mass in its upper part. When comparing across animals, shifts in these zones appear. Larger front areas often link to smarter habits, while bigger balance centers tie to demands like flying. So, even though blueprints match, emphasis changes per creature. Structure stays familiar, yet scale tilts one way or another.
# Post Lab - 7
**Hypothesis:** If kidney-transplanted mice are treated with the new bacterial compound at a dose that is not toxic, then they will show a lower immune response (less rejection) to the donated kidney than transplanted mice that do not receive the compound (because the compound suppresses parts of the immune response involved in rejection). This is a _strong_ hypothesis because it is testable and falsifiable and clearly connects a variable (drug vs no drug, and dose) to a measurable outcome (immune response / rejection markers). 
**Replacement:** Before using large numbers of mice, the researcher should screen the compound in non-animal systems—like cultured human immune cells (or mouse immune cells) and kidney tissue models—to look for obvious toxicity and whether immune activation decreases, and use computer/in vitro dose–response work to narrow a realistic dose range. This replaces some animal testing while still answering the early “does it look toxic / does it affect immune signaling?” questions. 
**Reduction:** Instead of “100 mice for toxicity,” start with a small, stepwise dose-escalation pilot (with careful controls) and use a basic power/sample-size plan so only the minimum number of animals needed are used; then only proceed to a larger transplant study if the pilot shows an effect worth testing. Also, collect multiple endpoints from the same animals (blood markers + behavior/weight + pathology) so fewer total animals answer more questions. 
**Refinement:** The current plan has high risk of unnecessary suffering (very high doses, euthanizing all animals just to “look for toxicity,” and a brand-new surgeon doing transplants). Refine by using humane endpoints (stop/withdraw if severe weight loss, poor grooming, lethargy), daily monitoring, and appropriate anesthesia/analgesia for surgeries; have an experienced surgeon or veterinarian train/supervise the student, and practice first on simulations/cadavers or non-survival practice to reduce failed surgeries and pain. Where possible, use non-lethal monitoring (bloodwork, imaging) and only euthanize when scientifically necessary, since animal studies are expected to minimize pain/distress and be reviewed/approved under IACUC standards that weigh benefit vs harm. (This redesign also fits the ethics ideas you’ve been using in class: pushing benefits while limiting harms—beneficence vs nonmaleficence.).

### Ethical Section
1. Autonomy - “People choose the light they’re in.”: 
	- Expect folks to know right away which lighting setup they’re in. Staying duration gets spelled out too. A brief stress check marks one part of tracking. Heart rate shows up as a quiet body signal we watch. Saliva tests for cortisol might join, only when already planned. Each piece stays open, nothing hidden.
	- If someone wants to leave, they are free to do so at any moment - no consequences apply. Skipping a question is also allowed whenever needed.
	- Light might shift sleep patterns and impact stress levels, so folks taking part will be told about it. Testing near bedtime won’t happen - keeps rest undisturbed.
2. Beneficence - “This should help real people in real rooms.”
	- Flooded spaces like classrooms, offices, and dorms meet us every day with artificial skies overhead. Studies point out that how bright it is, along with the shade of light, shifts how people feel inside. When lights burn too harsh or too cold in work areas, tension builds - recovery slows down as a result.
	- Few realize light choices might ease tension during study or work. A shift in brightness could help - no need to overhaul daily habits. Some setups quietly reduce strain, fitting into routines as they are. Changes stay subtle, yet effects add up over time. Simple swaps may matter more than expected.
3. Non-maleficence - “No one should leave worse than they arrived.”
	Headaches might show up. Eye strain can sneak in too. Some people feel uneasy during use. Trouble sleeping tends to follow exposure to bright, blue-heavy lighting after dark - timing plays a role here.
	Fewer risks show up when we keep things brief, hold meetings only during daylight hours. Taking pauses whenever needed helps, so does letting people step out right away if they want. Anyone with a past of migraines or trouble with bright lights won’t join.
	A person's cortisol level might show how their body handles stress - we see the number as part of a pattern, nothing more. When light hits the eyes early, it tweaks when cortisol rises, which is why routines stay short and steady each time.
4. Justice - “The study should be fair, and the benefits should be shareable.”
	Hiring reaches beyond a single field of study - sometimes pulling from overlooked corners. Flexibility matters most when routines are tight, so effort lands where it won’t stretch anyone too thin.
	Not every light works the same for all - bodies react differently, sleep times shift. Because of that, findings are handled with care, never stretched too far.
	A fresh idea might come up, yet we’ll look at practical ways forward - better light doesn’t need deep pockets to happen.
5. Privacy & data integrity (supports non-maleficence + justice)
	- From time to time, we gather just what’s necessary - then strip away personal details before sharing anything. Results appear strictly as summaries across groups, never tied to individuals.
	- One person's stress score won’t be shared - findings help spot trends instead of tagging individuals.
