## My Response
What happened during the Arab Spring varied across the four nations, yet grasping the reasons reveals deeper insights than just knowing what unfolded. 

Tunisia stands out most clearly, yet demonstrations by themselves fall short of explaining why. On December 10, 2010, in Sidi Bouzid, a twenty-six-year-old street seller named Mohamed Bouazizi lit himself on fire following police seizure of his wares. That act sparked unrest across the nation until President Zine el Abidine Ben Ali - ruling for twenty-three years - departed in January 2011. Functioning civic bodies existed there: the UGTT, a group uniting workers; an organized bar association; a league defending human rights; along with a coalition representing business owners. As tensions rose after Ben Ali's fall in 2013 - amid growing clashes between opposing groups nearing civil unrest - four key organizations stepped forward, forming what became known as "the quartet," guiding talks with the governing Ennahda movement toward a constitutional path. What made Tunisia different wasn’t mass protests alone but established bodies capable of steering change, rather than merely removing authority.

By mid-2012, Egypt seemed stable - then shifted sharply. In February 2011, mass demonstrations at Cairo’s Tahrir Square ended Hosni Mubarak’s three-decade rule. Economic hardship stood out: over half the people were younger than twenty-five; daily income below two dollars affected more than four in ten. That year, the Freedom and Justice Party - the Muslim Brotherhood's political branch - took 45 percent of parliamentary seats, followed by Muhammad Morsi claiming the presidency in June. As of 2021, about six out of every ten people in Egypt struggled on little or no income, while official figures hinted at nearly sixty thousand individuals detained for their political views. Though hopes were high back then, power remained tightly controlled by similar forces behind the scenes.

Twelve lives lost when Qaddafi’s troops opened fire sparked unrest in Libya. By February 20 that year, resistance had taken hold in Benghazi. With NATO airstrikes backing them, opposition fighters pushed into Tripoli come August. Power crumbled further, ending ten months in, when Qaddafi fell, captured then killed in October. Without independent courts, political parties vanished; civil society groups disappeared alongside any real national military force. As Jones states clearly: "Ultimately, despite Qaddafi’s clear abuses and shortcomings, removing him amid the Arab Spring ultimately harmed Libya by unraveling central control, sparking internal conflict, enabling rampant bloodshed, and leaving lasting disorder across politics and daily life" (Jones, p. 190). Present-day Libya hosts competing administrations - one based in Tripoli, another operating from Benghazi - while unified voting has not occurred since 2011.

Protests erupted across Algeria in December 2010, sparked by rising food costs, joblessness, and graft. Despite lacking revolutionary aims, people filled streets calling for President Abdelaziz Bouteflika to step down. In response, authorities applied selective crackdowns alongside swift financial measures. Price caps were placed on staple goods while billions flowed into welfare hikes, back pay, and state funding - roughly $23 billion overall. Only after another surge of demonstrations did Bouteflika leave office - in April 2019. Although protests spread from the Arab Spring reached Algeria, they failed to shift its core political structure.

Clearly, the trend emerges. In Tunisia, existing civic frameworks helped steer change smoothly. Not so in Libya, where years of dismantling public structures left nothing to support a shift. Egypt did have power anchored in its armed forces - yet that strength served self-preservation more than democratic progress. As for Algeria, surveillance and control networks acted early, dampening dissent before momentum built. After a dictator goes, the real challenge begins. Whether things improve hinges mostly on which structures remain ready to take his place.

The Arab Spring played out differently in each of these four countries, and understanding why tells you more than the events themselves.

**Tunisia** is the clearest success, but protests alone don't explain it. Mohamed Bouazizi, a twenty-six-year-old street vendor, set himself on fire on December 10, 2010 in Sidi Bouzid to protest police confiscation of his goods. His death triggered nationwide protests that forced President Zine el Abidine Ben Ali — in power for twenty-three years — to flee in January 2011. What made Tunisia different was what happened after. The country had functioning civil institutions: the UGTT (general labor federation), a Lawyers' Order, a Human Rights League, and an Employers' Union. When the post-Ben Ali transition grew dangerous in 2013 — with rival factions pushing toward civil conflict — these four organizations emerged as "the quartet" and negotiated a constitutional roadmap with the ruling Ennahda party. Parliament passed a new constitution in January 2014 guaranteeing freedom of speech and assembly. Elections followed in October; Ennahda lost and accepted the result. The quartet won the Nobel Peace Prize in 2015. Tunisia worked because it had institutions that could manage a political transition, not just crowds that could topple a dictator.

**Egypt** looked like a success through mid-2012 and then reversed completely. Hosni Mubarak's thirty-year regime fell in February 2011 after massive protests in Cairo's Tahrir Square. The scale of economic grievance was stark: 51% of Egypt's population was under twenty-five, and more than 40% lived on less than $2 per day. The Muslim Brotherhood's political wing (FJP) won legislative elections with 45% of seats, and Muhammad Morsi won the presidency in June 2012. But Defense Minister Abdel Fattah al-Sisi — appointed by Morsi himself — used renewed street protests in June 2013 to oust Morsi, banned the Muslim Brotherhood, and consolidated military control. By 2021, roughly 60% of Egyptians lived at or below the poverty line and the government reportedly held an estimated 60,000 political prisoners. Egypt's Arab Spring replaced one military-backed authoritarian regime with another.

**Libya** is the clearest failure. Protests began in February 2011 after Qaddafi's forces fired on demonstrators, killing twelve. Rebels seized Benghazi on February 20. The UN imposed a no-fly zone in March; NATO air support helped rebel forces drive Qaddafi out of Tripoli by August, and he was captured and killed in October 2011. But the problem wasn't Qaddafi — it was the forty-two years he spent destroying every institution that could challenge him: no independent courts, no political parties, no civil society organizations, no unified national army. When he fell, there was nothing to govern with. Jones puts it plainly: "In sum, for all of Qaddafi's many indisputable faults and failings, his overthrow during the Arab Spring has definitely been a net loss for Libya as it led to a breakdown of centralized governance, civil war, widespread violence, and continuing political and social instability" (Jones, p. 190). Libya today has two rival governments — one in Tripoli, one in Benghazi — and no unified elections since 2011.

**Algeria** never had a revolution. Massive protests broke out in December 2010 demanding President Abdelaziz Bouteflika's resignation over food prices, corruption, and unemployment. The government survived by combining targeted repression with immediate economic concessions: price controls on basic foods and $23 billion in public grants, retroactive salary payments, and benefit increases. The protests subsided. Bouteflika stayed in power until April 2019, when a second protest wave finally forced him out. His Prime Minister then won the December 2019 elections. The Arab Spring brought pressure to Algeria but no systemic change.

The pattern is clear. Tunisia succeeded because civil institutions existed to manage the transition. Libya failed because Qaddafi spent decades eliminating those institutions. Egypt had strong institutions — the military — but those institutions used the transition to protect their own entrenched position, not build democracy. Algeria's security apparatus was sophisticated enough to contain protests before they could grow into a full revolution. Removing a dictator is the easier part. What happens next depends almost entirely on what institutions already exist to fill the space he leaves.

Question**:** Do you think the Arab Spring failed in some countries because the old governments were too powerful, or because the new leaders were not ready to govern?

### My Reply

It’s not quite either/or — the better frame is institutional. Tunisia succeeded not because Ben Ali was especially weak or because new leaders were especially capable, but because the UGTT, a Lawyers’ Order, and other civil organizations already existed to mediate the transition. Libya is the clearest counter-example: Qaddafi spent forty-two years destroying any institution that could challenge him, so when rebel groups won militarily, there was no framework to govern within. Egypt had strong institutions — the military — but those institutions used the transition to protect their own economic and political position, not build democracy. The problem in Egypt wasn’t that new leaders were unready; it’s that the military was never going to allow genuine change. Institutional infrastructure, not leader quality or regime strength alone, determined the outcome.
