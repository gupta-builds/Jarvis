Mathea Armstrong posted Jun 12, 2026 6:20 PM

The Arab Spring is a topic that feels confusing at first because every country had a different experience. Before learning more about it, I thought it was just one big protest movement across the Middle East and North Africa. Now I understand that each country has its own problems, leaders, and results. Algeria, Libya, Tunisia, and Egypt all had people who wanted change, but not all of them ended up with the kind of freedom or stability they hoped for.

Tunisia is usually seen as the country where the Arab Spring began. One of the most important people was Mohamed Bouazizi. He was a young street vendor who set himself on fire in 2010 after being mistreated by officials. His death showed how angry people were about corruption, poverty, and unfair treatment. Protesters, students, lawyers, and labor groups helped push the movement forward. The major labor organization, the UGTT, also played an important role. Their protests forced President Zine El Abidine Ben Ali to leave power in 2011. Tunisia was the most successful of these countries because it actually created a new constitution and held elections. Even though Tunisia still had economic problems, it came the closest to building a real democracy. Jeffrey Jones explains that after the Arab Spring, Tunisia still struggled because unemployment “continued and even worsened to some degree.” This quote stands out to me because it shows that removing a dictator does not automatically fix people’s lives.

Egypt also had a huge uprising in 2011. The main people and groups involved were young activists, the April 6 Youth Movement, the “We Are All Khaled Said” Facebook group, and thousands of regular Egyptians who gathered in Tahrir Square. Their main target was President Hosni Mubarak, who had ruled Egypt for about thirty years. At first, Egypt looked successful because Mubarak stepped down. Mohamed Morsi and the Muslim Brotherhood later won elections. However, that success did not last. In 2013, the military removed Morsi from power. Abdel Fattah el-Sisi eventually became president. I would say Egypt’s Arab Spring was only partly successful. It removed Mubarak, but it did not create lasting democracy.

Libya’s Arab Spring became much more violent. The main opposition groups were anti-Gaddafi rebels, local militias, and the National Transitional Council. They wanted to remove Muammar Gaddafi, who had ruled Libya since 1969. With help from NATO, the rebels defeated Gaddafi’s government, and Gaddafi was killed in 2011. At first, this seemed like a victory. However, Libya fell into civil war and chaos after he was gone. Different militias and rival governments fought for power. Because of that, I do not think Libya was truly successful. The dictator was removed, but the country did not become stable.

Algeria had protests too, but the government survived the Arab Spring in 2011. The main groups included opposition parties, civil society groups, and the National Coordination for Change and Democracy. Many Algerians were upset about unemployment, high food prices, corruption, and President Abdelaziz Bouteflika’s long rule. The government responded by using police pressure, promising reforms, increasing spending, and ending the state of emergency. These actions helped stop the protests from growing into a full revolution. Algeria was not very successful during the Arab Spring because Bouteflika stayed in power until 2019, when a later protest movement finally forced him out.

Overall, Tunisia was the most successful country from this group. Egypt had early success, but the military took control again. Libya removed Gaddafi, but the country became unstable. Algeria avoided a full revolution in 2011, so its results were limited. To me, the Arab Spring shows that people can be brave enough to demand change, but building a better government afterward is much harder. A revolution can remove a leader, but it takes strong institutions, fair elections, and economic improvement to make the change last.

Question**:** Do you think the Arab Spring failed in some countries because the old governments were too powerful, or because the new leaders were not ready to govern?
