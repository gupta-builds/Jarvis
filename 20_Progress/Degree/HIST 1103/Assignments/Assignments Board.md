## Writing workflow (established Week 1 — do not skip steps)

1. **Read the textbook chapters** listed for this assignment
2. **Check classmate posts** — see what country/topic has been claimed (max 5 per country on claiming assignments)
3. **Draft the content** with specific facts, numbers, dates from the textbook
4. **Write in Anant's voice** — see Human Voice Rules below
5. **Weave the textbook quote** verbatim, with page number, inside a paragraph — never as a standalone sentence
6. **End with a question** — must be the last line, relevant to your argument
7. **Check the checklist above** — every box — before posting anything
8. **Post in Discussion tab** (typed text, no attachment), then upload .docx to Assignments dropbox

## Human voice rules

These rules come from Anant's actual human-written versions in Week 1. They are the gold standard for what passes AI detection and sounds authentic.

- **Short sentences.** Not every sentence, but the rhythm alternates short and medium. No 40-word sentences.
- **Dashes** — used to sharpen a point or add a quick aside without a full clause.
- **Specific numbers over vague claims.** "17%" not "a small percentage." "1947" not "around independence." "2–7 million deaths" not "many people died."
- **Personal only where real.** India background, CS major at UMN, cricket — one personal paragraph per assignment. Not running commentary.
- **No filler openers.** Delete: "It is important to note that," "This shows us that," "Overall, we can see," "In conclusion." Start sentences with the actual point.
- **No AI transitions.** "The connection between these two events is clearly..." = AI slop. Make the connection directly in the next sentence.
- **No restating what you just said.** If the previous sentence established a point, move on.
- **Target 500–520 words** for standard assignments. Assignment 6 (4-part) should go 800–1000.
- **If it's over 600 words, start cutting.** Cut sentences that repeat what surrounding text already shows. Never add content during cutting.

## Mistakes never repeat (from Week 1)

1. **Paraphrasing a quote and calling it verbatim.** Assignment 1 draft put a paraphrase in quotation marks. The quote must be word-for-word from the textbook. Open the PDF and confirm before writing the citation.
2. **Posting before the assignment is complete.** Practice assignment was posted without the quote and question. Write the complete final version FIRST. Then post. Discussion posts cannot be cleanly edited after classmates have read them.
3. **Adding the question as a separate reply.** The question for classmates must be the last line of the original post — not a follow-up reply.
4. **First draft over 700 words = AI slop.** Assignments 2 and 3 first drafts came in at 700–830 words. If it's over 600, cut aggressively. Cut, do not rewrite.

**File:** [[Assignment - 4]]  
**Due:** Sat Jun 13, 11:30 PM  
**Textbook:** Read chapters on Southeast Asia (Vietnam, Cambodia, Indonesia)

### The prompt
Discuss the evolution of **Vietnam** (required) and **one other country** — Cambodia or Indonesia — from roughly 1960 to 2020. Identify and discuss at least **2 events or people for each country** (4 total). The professor says to expect this to run longer than 400 words.

### What classmates have already covered
- **Mathea Armstrong** chose Vietnam + Indonesia. Events: Vietnam War/Fall of Saigon (1975), Doi Moi reforms (1986), Sukarno, Suharto/1965 coup/mass killings.

### Recommendation: Choose Cambodia
Cambodia is uncovered by any classmate. Zero overlap risk. The Khmer Rouge case is one of the most documented genocides of the 20th century — specific numbers, clear timeline, strong argument.

**Vietnam (required) — 2 events to discuss:**
- Ho Chi Minh / the North Vietnamese independence struggle (context for everything that follows)
- Fall of Saigon, April 30, 1975 — end of the Vietnam War, reunification under communist control, renamed Socialist Republic of Vietnam 1976
- OR Doi Moi reforms (1986) — Vietnam's economic opening; private business, foreign investment, shift away from rigid planned economy

**Cambodia — 2 events/people:**
- **Pol Pot / Khmer Rouge (1975–1979)** — Year Zero, evacuation of Phnom Penh, abolition of money/schools/religion, 1.5–2 million deaths = ~25% of Cambodia's population. One of the worst genocides per capita in history.
- **Vietnamese invasion (1978) / UN-backed 1993 elections** — Vietnam invaded, ended the genocide, installed People's Republic of Kampuchea. Khmer Rouge kept the UN seat until 1993. First free elections held under UNTAC in 1993.

### Approach
Longer is fine here — professor explicitly said so. 600–700 words is appropriate. The Cambodia case is brutal and specific. Numbers carry this argument.

## Assignment 5 — Middle East: One Country, 1900–Present
**File:** [[Assignment - 5]]  
**Due:** Sat Jun 13, 11:30 PM  
**Textbook:** Read chapter on the Middle East (Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Syria)

### The prompt
Choose one country: **Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, or Syria.** Sketch its journey from 1900 to the present. Identify the pressures (external imperialist, internal fundamentalist, or Baathist interests). Give at least **3 examples.** Max 5 people per country.

### What's been claimed
- **Iraq: 2 people claimed** (April Mazzucco + 1 other). 3 spots remain. Still available but not ideal.
- No other claims seen yet for Iran, Kuwait, Syria.

### Recommendation: Choose Iran
Iran gives the widest range of specific examples across all three pressure types. The professor also explicitly referenced current US-Israel-Iran conflict in the prompt, signaling this is on his mind.

**3 examples structured around pressure types:**

1. **Mossadegh / CIA coup 1953** — external imperialist pressure. British oil interests (Anglo-Iranian Oil Company, nationalized by Mossadegh in 1951) + CIA/MI6 Operation Ajax toppled a democratically elected PM to reinstall the Shah and protect Western oil access.
2. **Islamic Revolution 1979 / Khomeini** — internal fundamentalist pressure. Decades of SAVAK repression + Shah's forced westernization (White Revolution) created a popular revolution that replaced a secular monarchy with a theocracy overnight.
3. **Iran-Iraq War 1980–1988** — external pressure from Baathist Iraq + US involvement. Saddam attacked while Iran was weak post-revolution. US backed Iraq (provided intelligence, weapons). ~500,000 dead on both sides.

**Optional 4th example if needed:**
- Nuclear program (2000s–present) — external pressure from US/Israel; internal hardliner agenda; repeated sanctions.

### Approach
One country, three clear examples, ~500 words. Pick one pressure type as the dominant lens or argue that multiple pressures converged on the same country. The professor's framing ("external imperialist, internal fundamentalist, Baathist") should show up in your writing.

## Assignment 6 — Israel/Palestine: 4-Part Assignment
**File:** [[Assignment - 6]]  
**Due:** Sat Jun 13, 11:30 PM  
**Textbook:** Read chapter on Israel/Palestine and the Middle East  
**⚠️ This is the longest assignment of the week — professor says "go well over the word count minimum."**

### The prompt (all 4 parts are required)

**Part 1:** What are 2 issues (one Arab perspective, one Israeli) causing the heart of the disagreement today? NOT religion. Look at 19th/early 20th century history.

**Part 2:** Choose one: 1948 War, Suez Canal Crisis 1956, 6 Day War 1967, 1973 War. Discuss general causes from both sides.

**Part 3:** One peace effort since the 1980s. State its goals, whether a treaty was signed, why it failed.

**Part 4:** 4 contemporary events across the Middle East (Egypt to Iran, not Turkey) over the last 20 years. For each: does it stabilize or destabilize the region, and why?

**Extra credit:** Post a question in "Questions and Other Info → Questions" subforum. Free point.

### Recommended choices

**Part 1 — 2 core issues:**
- Arab/Palestinian perspective: self-determination and displacement. The 1948 Nakba — 700,000 Palestinians fled or were expelled — and ongoing Israeli settlement expansion in the West Bank since 1967 leave no path to a Palestinian state.
- Israeli perspective: security and the right to exist. Attacked in 1948, 1967, 1973 by Arab coalition states. Hamas in Gaza, Hezbollah in Lebanon — both with explicit destruction-of-Israel in their founding charters. Israel's security argument is grounded in documented existential threats, not paranoia.

**Part 2 — Choose: 6 Day War (1967)**
- Clearest causes from both sides. Israel: preemptive strike based on Egypt closing Strait of Tiran (act of war) + troop buildup + Jordan/Syria mobilizing. Arab coalition: Egypt/Jordan/Syria wanted to reverse 1948. Israel tripled its territory in 6 days — Sinai, Gaza, West Bank, Golan Heights. Changes everything that comes after.

**Part 3 — Choose: Oslo Accords (1993)**
- Rabin + Arafat. Goals: PLO recognized Israel, Israel recognized PLO, Palestinian Authority created, Israeli withdrawal from parts of Gaza/West Bank. No final status treaty. Why it failed: Israeli settlement expansion continued despite negotiations; Rabin assassinated November 1995; Palestinian factions split (Hamas vs. Fatah); Camp David 2000 collapsed when Clinton's final-status proposal was rejected; Second Intifada 2000–2005.

**Part 4 — 4 contemporary events:**
1. Arab Spring 2011 — destabilizing (Egypt, Libya, Syria fell into chaos; Muslim Brotherhood briefly in power in Egypt; Syrian civil war killed 500,000+)
2. ISIS rise 2014 — destabilizing (seized territory across Iraq and Syria; exported terrorism globally)
3. Abraham Accords 2020 — stabilizing (UAE, Bahrain, Morocco, Sudan normalized with Israel; first Arab-Israeli normalization since Jordan 1994)
4. Hamas attack October 7, 2023 / Gaza War — massively destabilizing (deadliest day for Jews since the Holocaust; Israeli military response in Gaza; regional escalation with Hezbollah, Houthi attacks on shipping, Iran missile exchanges)

### Approach
Structure the post clearly by part (Part 1, Part 2, etc.). Target 800–1000 words — this is the one assignment where longer is right. The professor wants historical thinking applied to current events. Take a clear position supported by evidence; don't hedge every sentence.

## Assignment 7 — Arab Spring: North Africa
**File:** [[Assignment - 7]]  
**Due:** Sat Jun 13, 11:30 PM  
**Textbook:** Read chapter on North Africa / Arab Spring

### The prompt
Look at **Algeria, Libya, Tunisia, and Egypt.** Identify the main individuals or organizations responsible for the Arab Spring in each country. Which nations were successful and which were not? Explain.

### What Mathea Armstrong already covered
Covered all four countries. Key points she hit:
- Tunisia: Bouazizi, UGTT, Ben Ali fled, Tunisia "most successful," used Jones quote on unemployment continuing
- Egypt: April 6 Youth Movement, Tahrir Square, Mubarak fell → Morsi → Sisi. "Partly successful."
- Libya: Anti-Gaddafi rebels, NATO, Gaddafi killed, civil war. "Not successful."
- Algeria: opposition parties, government used police + spending. Bouteflika survived until 2019.

### What to cover differently (add depth, not overlap)
- **Tunisia:** Add Rachid Ghannouchi / Ennahda party winning first elections (2011). UGTT as a negotiating institution between Islamists and secularists — not just protesters. Tunisia's 2014 constitution included women's rights, religious freedom. This is WHY Tunisia succeeded: it had organized institutions capable of governing post-revolution.
- **Egypt:** The military (SCAF — Supreme Council of Armed Forces) managed Mubarak's exit and never actually left. They let Morsi govern, waited for him to fail, then removed him. Sisi is SCAF. Egypt's revolution didn't fail — it was captured by the same institution that was always there.
- **Libya:** Gaddafi deliberately destroyed all civil institutions over 42 years — no political parties, no civil society, no bureaucracy. When he fell, there was nothing to govern with. The NTC (National Transitional Council) had no institutional base. Militia fragmentation was structural, not accidental.
- **Algeria:** The DRS (intelligence/security service) was more sophisticated than any neighboring state. The government outflanked protests with targeted concessions + repression before they could escalate. Bouteflika survived because the security state was intact.

### Success/failure framework

| Country | Dictator removed? | Stable democracy? | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tunisia | ✅ Ben Ali fled Jan 2011 | ✅ (fragile, backslid 2021) | Successful |
| Egypt | ✅ Mubarak fell Feb 2011 | ❌ military took back control 2013 | Partial / failed |
| Libya | ✅ Gaddafi killed Oct 2011 | ❌ civil war, two rival governments | Failed |
| Algeria | ❌ Bouteflika survived until 2019 | ❌ | Not successful in 2011 |

### Approach
All four countries are required — this isn't a choose-one. Cover each, but unequally: spend more words on why Tunisia succeeded (institutions) and why Libya failed (no institutions) — the contrast makes the argument. ~600–700 words is right.
