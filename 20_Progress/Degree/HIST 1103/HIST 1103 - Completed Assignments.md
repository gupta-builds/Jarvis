# HIST 1103 — Completed Assignments

This is the source of truth for every completed assignment in this course. Each entry documents the topic chosen, the argument made, the textbook quote used (with page number), the classmate reply written, and any mistakes or lessons learned. Read this at the start of every new session before writing anything.

---

## How This Course Works (Operating Rules)

### The two-submission system
Every assignment requires two separate actions:
1. **Discussion tab** — type the text directly into the reply box. No file attachment. No attachments ever in the discussion.
2. **Assignments tab dropbox** — upload the file (.docx, .pdf, .rtf, or .doc). This is what Turnitin scans.

Both are required. Missing either = no credit.

### The non-negotiable checklist
Before submitting anything, every box must be checked. One missed item = zero credit, no exceptions:
- [ ] 400+ words (textbook quote does NOT count toward this)
- [ ] Posted as text in the Discussion tab (no attachment)
- [ ] File uploaded to correct Assignments dropbox
- [ ] One textbook quote, **verbatim**, with page number — e.g. "..." (Jones, p. X)
- [ ] Question for a classmate is the **last line** of the post
- [ ] Turnitin ≤ 15% (check immediately after dropbox upload)
- [ ] Classmate reply posted in the discussion by **Sunday 11:30 PM**

### The textbook quote rule — the most important rule to get right
- The quote must be **verbatim** — word for word from the textbook. Not paraphrased. Not summarized.
- It must include the **page number** in the citation: (Jones, p. X)
- It does **not** count toward the 400-word minimum
- It should be woven into the argument, not dropped in as a standalone sentence
- The professor explicitly said: avoid "On page X, Jones says..." as padding. The quote is required; the formulaic citation style is not.

### Writing voice for this course
The professor values data, specifics, and a genuine argument — not polished AI prose. Anant's writing style for this course:
- Direct sentences, often short. Dashes used frequently.
- Personal angle where authentic (India background, CS major)
- No filler phrases, no transitions that say nothing
- Specific numbers and dates over vague claims
- Human, compressed, concrete

### Working with AI on assignments
The correct workflow established this session:
1. AI drafts content based on textbook research
2. A more human voice is applied (rewrite to match Anant's style)
3. AI cuts to target length — **only cut, do not add new content**
4. Final check against the 7-item checklist before posting

The professor's AI policy: AI beyond grammar help = plagiarism = immediate course failure. The work must be Anant's own. AI is used for research and drafting, not for final submission text.

### Classmate replies
- ~100 words, no hard minimum
- Post as a reply to the classmate's specific thread so it lands with their post
- Due Sunday 11:30 PM (discussion only — not uploaded to dropbox)
- Must directly answer their question with a real position and reasoning

## Mistakes Made — Never Repeat

### Practice Assignment
**What went wrong:**
1. The discussion post was submitted without a textbook quote or page number
2. The question for classmates was added as a **separate reply** after the fact, not at the end of the original post
3. The .docx uploaded to the dropbox had the Jones p.38 quote and question included, making it slightly different from the discussion post

**What this means for future assignments:**
- Write the complete, correct version FIRST — quote woven in, question as the last line — THEN post it in the discussion
- Never post first and patch afterward. The discussion post cannot be cleanly edited.
- The file uploaded to the dropbox should be the corrected/complete version if the discussion post has errors. For graded assignments, get it right in the discussion from the start.

### Assignment 1 — First submission
**What was caught before posting:**
- The first draft used a paraphrased version of the Jones quote in quotation marks — presenting a paraphrase as a direct quote. This would have been a misquote.
- Fixed to verbatim: "In elections held in Hungary in November 1945, communists received only 17% of the vote; in Romania, 28%; in Czechoslovakia, 38%" (Jones, p. 13)

### Assignment 1 — Eastern Europe Post-WWII Democratic Collapse
**Prompt:** Choose three Eastern European countries that moved from democracy to single-party rule. Identify the leader responsible for each. Discuss one country's takeover in detail.
**Countries chosen:**
- Czechoslovakia → Klement Gottwald
- Hungary → Mátyás Rákosi
- Poland → Bolesław Bierut

**Detailed country: Czechoslovakia / Gottwald**
**Argument:** Czechoslovakia is the clearest example of how the Soviet blueprint worked in practice. The Communist Party had 38% in genuine 1946 elections — the highest communist vote share in any free Eastern European election. Gottwald used that democratic legitimacy as cover. In February 1948, when non-communist ministers resigned expecting Beneš to call new elections, Gottwald moved immediately: organized workers' militias, packed the cabinet, and exploited the presence of Soviet troops in neighboring countries. Beneš capitulated. From there: police → military → courts → press → show trials. Jan Masaryk found dead beneath his window in March 1948. By June 1948, Gottwald was president of a single-party state.

**Textbook quote:** Jones, p. 13 — "Subsequent coups or unfair elections brought the communist parties to power throughout Eastern Europe by 1948, and each of them (with the notable exception of Yugoslavia) became puppet states of the Soviet Union."
**Supporting data from textbook:** Hungary communists got 17% in Nov 1945 elections; Romania 28%; Czechoslovakia 38% (Jones, p. 13).
**Question asked:** "Given that Czechoslovakia's Communist Party won 38% in a genuine election before the 1948 coup, at what point does using democratic legitimacy to dismantle democracy become something more than just politics — and does the method of takeover matter if the result is the same?"
**Reply written:** To April Mazzucco's question about whether she'd fight for or against communism — answered from Anant's perspective: against it, but reasoning is about individual agency and India's 1947 experience, not religion. ~100 words.

### Assignment 2 — East/South Asia: Two Topics Across Two Eras
**Prompt:** Choose a country from the list. Pick one topic from 1945–1975 and one from 1995–2025 (Person or Event each). Describe both, connect them, explain why you picked them.
**Country claimed:** India (1 of 3 max claimed; Tommy Thorn was the only prior claim)
**Topics chosen:**
- 1945–1975: **Jawaharlal Nehru** (Person) — India's first PM, 1947–1964
- 1995–2025: **India's Information Technology Revolution** (Event) — 1995–2025

**Argument:** Nehru made two decisions that had nothing to do with technology but made India's IT revolution possible fifty years later: (1) keeping English for higher education despite pressure to switch to Hindi — giving Indian engineers a direct working language with US/UK firms; (2) founding five IITs (1951–1959) with international partnerships (Kanpur/US consortium, Madras/West Germany, Bombay/USSR). When Manmohan Singh's 1991 economic liberalization dismantled the License Raj, the talent Nehru had been building for four decades had a global market to compete in. Jones's quote on p.38 directly links the two periods.

**Textbook quote:** Jones, p. 38 — "In the twenty-first century India's large well-educated, English-speaking urban population has put it in a strong position to flourish amid globalization in technology and telecommunications industries among others."
**Personal angle used:** Anant studies CS in the US. The IITs shaped the country he grew up in. His being in a CS program follows the same pipeline Nehru built.
**Question asked:** "Did Nehru's non-alignment during the Cold War ultimately help or hurt India's path to the IT boom — or would closer US ties from the start have accelerated things by decades?"
**Classmate replied to:** None identified yet (reply due Sun Jun 7)
**Writing lesson:** The first draft was ~700 words — too long. Cut to ~520 by removing filler bridge paragraphs and redundant sentences. Rule: cut only, do not add. Keep every phrase that carries real information; remove anything that restates something the surrounding text already shows.

### Assignment 3 — East/South Asia: Same Format, Opposite Region
**Prompt:** Same structure as Assignment 2, but must choose from the opposite region. Since India was chosen (South Asia), must now choose from East Asia: China, Japan, Korea, or Pacific Rim Countries.
**Country claimed:** China (2 of 3 max already claimed by Tom Chi and Tommy Thorn — one spot remaining)
**Topics chosen:**
- 1945–1975: **The Cultural Revolution (1966–1976)** (Event)
- 1995–2025: **China's emergence as a global economic power** (Event)

**Argument:** The Cultural Revolution is where China's economic rise actually starts — not because Mao intended it, but because the devastation of his ideology (2–7 million deaths; closed universities; exiled administrators; worst famine in history under the preceding Great Leap Forward at 20–30 million dead) made it politically impossible for anyone afterward to argue for a return to ideological governance. Deng Xiaoping — personally targeted by Mao, labeled a "capitalist roader," son permanently disabled by Red Guards — emerged from that wreckage and implemented the pragmatic reforms that drove everything after. His famous line captures the entire shift: "It doesn't matter if a cat is black or white, so long as it catches mice" (Jones, p. 68). The 1991 Special Economic Zones and WTO accession (2001) unlocked talent and capacity that had been building since the 1980s reforms. By 2010, China surpassed Japan as the world's second largest economy.

**Textbook quote:** Jones, p. 68 — "It doesn't matter if a cat is black or white, so long as it catches mice." (Deng Xiaoping's famous saying, as recorded by Jones.)
**Supporting data from textbook:** Great Leap Forward famine: 20–30 million deaths (Jones, p. 65). Cultural Revolution deaths: 2–7 million (Jones, p. 67). By 2004 Walmart sourced 80% of goods from China (Jones, p. 71). China surpassed Japan as world's 2nd largest economy in 2010 (Jones, p. 71).
**Personal angle used:** Anant studies CS. Lenovo (world's largest laptop producer) bought IBM's PC division in 2004 — a company that didn't exist before Deng's reforms. The US-China tech competition is directly relevant to anyone entering computing.
**Question asked:** "Can wealth grow while voices stay silent? What happens when expectations shift, when new generations start asking for more?"
**Classmate reply written:** To Mathea Armstrong's Korea question ("Would Korea have united without US/Soviet division?") — answered that probably not, because Korean resistance movements were already split between communist-aligned and conservative western-leaning factions before 1945. Foreign powers accelerated and hardened the divide but the internal tensions were pre-existing. ~100 words.
**Writing lesson:** Human-voice version came in at ~830 words — too long. Cut to ~520. Same rule: only cut, preserve every sentence that carries information. The philosophical ending was pure filler; kept only the two questions from it.
