# HIST 1103 — Syllabus (plain-language)
This is the full official syllabus rewritten in simpler words. Nothing is dropped. When two rules in the syllabus seem to disagree, both versions are kept here so you can check with the instructor.

There are no special arrangements for vacations or changed due dates because of work schedules. If you cannot keep up, the instructor says you should not take the class. Read the whole syllabus carefully.

## Course description
A survey of contemporary world history after World War II. The class covers major historical events, processes, and transformations and how they shaped the modern world. Likely topics: the Cold War, Korean War, Vietnam Conflict, Eastern European uprisings, Communist China, the Cuban Missile Crisis and Bay of Pigs, the Middle East (Israel & the PLO, Iran/Iraq), South Asia (India & Pakistan), Africa, Latin America, international terrorism, and the New World Order.
## Textbook
*Contemporary World History Since 1945* by Jeffrey W. Jones. Published by Routledge, 2025.
## Course goals
1. To think.
2. To learn the historical process and how to apply it across world history.
3. To learn and be able to discuss world issues and how they affect the development of history.
## Grading policy
- The course is worth **1000 total points**.
- Grades use a straight percentage scale (no curve):
| Grade | Percentage |
|---|---|
| A | 91% and above |
| B | 81-90% |
| C | 71-80% |
| D | 61-70% |
| F | 60% and below |
- Weighting: exams are **80%** of the grade (Midterm 30%, Final 50%); assignments/participation are **20%**.
- Assignments and exams can be made up only with the instructor's prior consent.
- Late exams lose **10 percentage points per day late**, and **10 percentage points for every 5% above the 15% Turnitin similarity limit**. Example: an exam 3 days late loses 30 points, so the most it can earn is 70 (a 95 turned in 3 days late becomes a 65). Points are subtracted after the exam is scored. No exceptions.
- Always turn work in even if it is late — any credit beats zero credit.
- Exam grades are returned only in the **"Feedback"** area of the dropbox submission (nowhere else), so you have to open the feedback to see your grade.
## Plagiarism and cheating
- Any student who plagiarizes or cheats fails the class immediately, and the instructor pursues all available disciplinary action through the college.
- Always cite every source (website, book, journal, etc.). Ask before it becomes a problem.
- Every assignment and exam is checked with Turnitin through D2L. If your similarity score is over 15%, you must lower it to 15% or below **before** the due date and time.
- No more than 15% of your writing may come from another source (quotes or paraphrasing).
- The instructor may ask to meet over Zoom to discuss your work and your study/research/writing process before assigning a final grade for that piece. He intends to meet with everyone at least once.
## Expectations
*Student is expected to:*
- Read the assigned reading before doing the weekly assignments.
- Read at least some of other students' posts (about 40% is adequate).
- Be ready to discuss the week's material or comment on others' posts.
- Turn in all assignments on or before their due dates.
- **AI rule:** any use of AI to write exams or assignments — other than help with grammar — counts as plagiarism and results in immediate course failure.
- Do **not** use Wikipedia or any wiki as a source. You may mine a wiki article's bibliography for real sources, but then check those sources yourself.
- Read the syllabus completely.
*Instructor will:*
- Grade exams in a timely manner.
- Keep graded material and any conferences confidential.
- Be available for appointments.
- Give extra help as needed.
*Ungraded discussion:* the instructor may post discussion questions in the "Questions and Other Info" forum. Responding is optional and ungraded, but visible effort here can nudge a final grade up if you are right on the edge between two letter grades.

## Common Course Outline (official catalog record)
- **A. Prefix/Number/Title:** HIST 1103 Contemporary World History. MnTC: Goal Areas 5C & 8.
- **B. Credits:** 3.
- **C. Prerequisites (need a C or better):** None.
- **D. Recommended:** one year of U.S. and World History in high school.
- **E. Catalog description:** a historical analysis of the contemporary world in global perspective, from the aftermath of WWII to the present. Topics include WWII and the Peace, Cold War containment, global social and economic patterns, neo-imperialism, decolonization, peace initiatives, local and international views of global conditions and events, and global terrorism.
- **F. Course info:** 3 classroom hours/week; 0 lab; 0 clinical; 0 other; no additional fee. (Degree-requirement and certificate fields in the form were left effectively blank / "no degree".)
- **G. Learner outcomes:** by the end of the course a student can —
	1. Critically evaluate factual accuracy and sources while showing historical knowledge and understanding.
	2. Describe and discuss the sociopolitical and socioeconomic sides of various states in the contemporary era, from informed perspectives, with appreciation and tolerance of diversity.
	3. Analyze global problems, patterns, events, people, and themes to develop workable solutions and become an active participant in democracy at local, state, national, and global levels.
	4. Take an active part in discussions on core Contemporary World History issues — problems, peoples, historic forces, patterns of global interdependence, current political/economic/social issues, and cross-cultural differences that block understanding.
	5. Show understanding of what world peace and world citizenship mean and why they are foundational to the United Nations.
- **H. Major areas of content:** (1) the end of WWII; (2) containment and the Cold War; (3) global social, economic, and political patterns; (4) regional issues and conflict; (5) a new era of anti-imperialism and decolonization.
- **I. MnTC Goal Areas 5C & 8:** Area 5 — History and the Social and Behavioral Sciences; Area 8 — Global Perspective. (The competency-to-outcome mapping table in the PDF is garbled; the readable parts tie learner outcomes 4, 5, 6, 7 to these goal areas.)
- **J. Credit for prior learning:** available via a course-specific exam or portfolio review (the PDF text here is partly garbled).
- **K. Proposed implementation date:** Spring 2019.
- **L. Review cycle:** the outline is reviewed about every three years.
