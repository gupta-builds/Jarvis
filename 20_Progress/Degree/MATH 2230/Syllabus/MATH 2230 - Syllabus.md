## Course description
A calculus-based study of probability and statistics. Topics: descriptive statistics, general probability, probability distributions, sampling, normal and binomial distributions, interval estimation and hypothesis testing, chi-squared and t-tests, two-sample inference, analysis of variance, correlation, and linear regression. Students also learn to use a statistical software package. Concepts are introduced at a more advanced level than in MATH 1114.
## Prerequisite
**MATH 1401** with a grade of C or better, or equivalent.
## Major areas of course content
1. Descriptive statistics
2. Probability and counting principles
3. Mathematical expectation
4. Discrete probability distributions
5. Density functions and continuous probability distributions
6. Sampling distributions and the Central Limit Theorem
7. Estimation and confidence intervals
8. Correlation and regression
9. Hypothesis testing
10. Analysis of variance

## Learner outcomes
By the end of the course a successful student can:
1. Calculate and analyze measures of center and spread: mean, median, mode, range, variance, standard deviation, percentiles, and z-scores.
2. Build and read visual data summaries: frequency tables, histograms, box plots, stem-and-leaf plots.
3. Understand core probability ideas: combinatorics, independence, complementary events, conditional probability, and Bayes' theorem.
4. Find probabilities using the addition and multiplication rules, the fundamental counting principle, permutations, combinations, and sample spaces.
5. Understand random variables and probability distributions; build a distribution for a random variable; tell discrete from continuous distributions.
6. Understand mathematical expectation; compute mean, variance, and covariance of random variables.
7. Compute and interpret expected values, variances, and probabilities for discrete distributions — binomial, geometric, negative binomial, hypergeometric, Poisson — emphasizing binomial and Poisson.
8. Identify density functions and use calculus to find their mean, variance, and probabilities.
9. Compute and interpret expected values, variances, and probabilities for continuous distributions — uniform, gamma, exponential, chi-squared — emphasizing the normal distribution.
10. Apply the Central Limit Theorem to describe sampling distributions and compute probabilities.
11. Build and interpret confidence intervals for population means, proportions, and variances.
12. Create, run, and interpret hypothesis tests for population means, proportions, and variances.
13. Run chi-square tests of goodness of fit, independence, and homogeneity.
14. Read scatter plots for form, direction, and strength; compute and interpret the correlation coefficient for linear bivariate data.
15. Use linear regression to study correlation of paired data and make predictions.
16. Perform analysis of variance.
17. Use statistical software to get descriptive statistics, display data, find probabilities, do basic inference, and run linear correlation and regression analysis.
