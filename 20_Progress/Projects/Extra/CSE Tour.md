## The Tour
### Opening Talk
- Hi everyone, welcome to the University of Minnesota Twin Cities and to CSE. My name is Anant, and I’ll be taking you through part of the engineering and science side of campus today. I’ll show you a mix of academic buildings, student support spaces, research spaces, and places students actually use day to day. As we go, feel free to ask questions at any point. I’m happy to talk about classes, student life, research, internships, support resources, or just what it’s like being here.
- University of Minnesota Twin Cities was founded in 1851, sits along the Mississippi River -> East bank & West bank. Is one of only five universities in the country with engineering, medicine, law, veterinary medicine, and agriculture all on one campus. That creates a lot of opportunities for collaboration across fields.
### (Stop: 1) Lind Hall – ISyE (Industrial and Systems Engineering) & Computer Science Building
- Student Support Services
- All students are required to meet with their academic advisor each semester.
#### Main talk track
- We’re ==starting== here at **Lind Hall**, which is a really useful place to begin because it highlights something families usually care a lot about: *support*. ==Student Support Services on 1st floor==
- This building connects a few important things for CSE students: *academics*, **advising**, office hours, *study space*, and *career support*.
- One big point I always share is that **all CSE students are expected to meet with their academic advisor regularly**, and advising here is not just for *registration*. Students use it for degree planning, figuring out major requirements, handling schedule issues, and getting help during the *transition* into college. That support focus is built into the college experience here.
- The **CSE Career Center** is also a huge resource. Students can get help with *resumes*, cover letters, *internship* search prep, and *career fair* readiness. The college runs career fairs in both fall and spring, so there are repeated chances to connect with *216 employers*.
> Move to second floor through the stairs.
- This building is also tied to **Industrial and Systems Engineering**, and it’s near spaces used by **computer science and data science** students for classes, advising, and office hours. Your “*home building*” matters more as you move deeper into your major, but no one stays in just one building all four years.
- **Taylor Center**, which includes a *computer lab*, *printing*, *monitors*, and *study space*. It’s one of those practical places students end up using a lot. Free printing for CSE Students.
- ==CompSci, Data Sci office hours on 3rd floor==.
1. ==Ask the group==: Are any of you already pretty set on a major, or are you still deciding between a few areas?
2. ==If you need extra time==
	- Support spaces like this matter because first-year students often take shared foundation classes(Math, Phy, etc.) before narrowing in on more specialized courses later.
> *Transition*
	- From here, we’re heading to a space that gives a very *different* side of ==CSE - less advising and more making, building, and hands-on project work==.
## Stop 2: Mechanical Engineering – Maker Space/Anderson Labs/CSE Shop
### Main talk track
- This is one of my favorite stops because it shows that engineering here is not just theory on a whiteboard.
- ==Anderson student labs: 2 on campus==.
- The **Maker Space** gives *24/7 CSE students access* to equipment after they complete the required safety training. It’s designed so students can actually build and test ideas, whether that’s for class projects, student groups, or personal projects.
- Equipment like **3D printers, 4 laser cutters, and workbench tools**, and students get an allotment of **3D-printing filament at no extra cost**. That makes the space much easier to actually use, instead of feeling like something students only look at.
- A nice way to describe this building is that it turns ideas into physical things. Students design, prototype, fix, test, and repeat.
> Turn around and move to Mechie
- This is also a good place to talk about *student organizations*. The University says there are **more than 1,000 student groups and organizations** across campus, so students can find technical groups, identity-based communities, hobby groups, design teams, and professional organizations pretty quickly.
- If you want a more engineering-specific example, spaces like this are used for ==design competitions, robotics projects, club builds, and workshops== hosted by student organizations.
- These are *discussion* classrooms that you see on your left and right with study spaces as we move down to *CSE Maker Space*. Feel free to ask me any questions as we move along.
- As we moved down, we changed from Lind hall to mechanical engineering through gopher tunnels through the campus. These are signs that are attached all over Conley inside buildings for students to travel through tunnels in hard winter weather.
- The CSE Shop is a *fully staffed  9,000 square foot fabrication and support space* that helps with machining, welding, assembly, light fabrication, and inspection. That means students are on a campus where serious research and serious making are happening right alongside undergraduate coursework.
- *Welding* - The most common material we weld is ==stainless steel==, but we also have capabilities for welding *aluminum* and other metals and do assembly. 
- Mechanical engineering research labs are also nearby, which gives you a good opening to talk about undergraduate research. At the U, undergrads can get involved in research through faculty labs, and the **Undergraduate Research Opportunities Program, or UROP**, provides support for students working with faculty mentors. The University’s Office of Undergraduate Research says UROP funds students for research projects under faculty mentorship.
> Go out from ME and to front of Akerman
1. *Ask the group*
	- Who here likes the idea of hands-on projects - building, prototyping, or design work?
2. *If you need extra time*
	- Spaces like this are especially useful because CSE students often learn best by doing: designing something, seeing what fails, and then improving it. 
> *Transition*
	- Next we’re heading to **Akerman Hall**, which is home to aerospace-related spaces and gives a good sense of what a department “home building” can feel like.
## Stop 3: Akerman Hall – Aerospace and Collaborative Department Space
### Main talk track
- This is **Akerman Hall**, one of the home spaces tied to aerospace and mechanics-related work.
- I usually explain the idea of a **home building** here: *every major* tends to have a building where you’ll find more faculty offices, *department spaces*, study areas, and upper-level classes. You won’t live in one building forever, but as you move through your major, you’ll spend more time in the spaces most connected to it.
- Research groups and specific student groups like NASA
- The **Gary Balas Atrium** is a nice example of the kind of open, collaborative layout students use for meeting up, working on assignments, or talking through projects on whiteboards.
- This building also connects to *aerospace lab work*, including instructional and research spaces such as **low-speed wind tunnels** focusing on the design, development, testing and production of aircraft. Also used in coursework and projects such as *Flight Dynamics and Control* (AEM 4303), *Aeromechanics Lab* (AEM 4602), and *Senior Design* (AEM 4333), as well as through various *student groups*. 
- This is a good stop to tell students that one of the nice things about CSE is that even when programs are large, the departments themselves usually create smaller communities through their buildings, faculty interactions, labs, and student groups.
1. *Ask the group*:
	- Is anyone here interested in aerospace, flight, space systems, or anything design-heavy?
2. *If you need extra time*
	- Mention that collaborative spaces matter because students spend a lot of time between classes doing homework in groups, preparing lab reports, and meeting with project teams.
3. *Transition*:
	- Next we move from large-scale structures to some of the smallest-scale science happening on campus.
## Stop 4: Physics and Nanotechnology (PAN) – Research, Study Space, and Precision Environments
### Main talk track
- A fun line to use here is: **this building tends to have great study space because there are no regular classrooms in it**. It feels quieter and more research-oriented.
- PAN houses part of the **Minnesota Nano Center**, including clean-room environments where researchers fabricate and test tiny materials and devices. In those spaces, even a tiny dust particle can matter. This is the *second clean room* at the UMN, we have another one.
- This building supports *highly controlled environments*, including *temperature*, *humidity*, and *vibration* control, which matters for sensitive physics and biophysics research.
- If you want a fun outreach example, this is a good place to mention **Physics Force**, the University’s long-running science demonstration program. The School of Physics and Astronomy says it received a **2025 Societal Impact Award**, which is a nice way to show that science here is not only about labs, but also public engagement and teaching.
- This is also another place where you can mention *UROP*. Students interested in physics, materials, biophysics, or nanotechnology can often connect with faculty research pretty early, especially if they are proactive. The University notes that UROP supports undergraduates working with faculty mentors and can connect students with existing or self-designed projects.
- ==Speak about your UROP experience==.
1. *Ask the group*: Change the question
	- Is anyone here more interested in physics, materials, semiconductors, nanotech, or research-heavy science?
2. *If you need extra time*
	- You can say this is one of those buildings that gives students a sense of what a major research university feels like day to day: quieter, specialized, and full of work happening behind the scenes.
3. *Transition*
	- Next we head to the largest-scale science based research taking place at the UMN. One of the most interesting buildings.
## Stop 5: Civil Engineering – Underground Layout / MAST / Infrastructure Focus
### Main talk track
- Go to the lift and press the floor 1, take a right and exit through another right. 
- This building is memorable right away because it’s **mostly underground**.
- One fun fact people remember is that the floors feel *reversed*: the top is floor 1, and the numbering goes *downward*. There’s also *no traditional fifth floor* because of the ==limestone layer== in the site. That gives the building its unusual structure.
- Civil, environmental, and geo-engineering are all about designing, understanding, and protecting the systems people depend on every day - buildings, roads, bridges, water systems, environmental systems, and earth materials.
- This is also a *strong research stop*. Concrete testing, structural stress testing, and framing techniques.
- **MAST(Multi-Axial Subassemblage Testing)Laboratory is the largest test system of its kind in the world**, and it is used to study how large structural components respond to earthquakes, hurricanes, and other extreme forces.
- Research here is not abstract for the sake of being abstract - a lot of it directly connects to *public safety, infrastructure, and resilience*.
- Student groups like **Concrete Canoe**: where they build a canoe and race it made from concrete, show that even a major that sounds very serious and infrastructure-focused still has *fun*, creative *team projects*.
1. *Ask the group*
	- When you hear civil engineering, do you mostly think bridges and buildings, or environment and water systems?
2. *If you need extra time*
	- Add that the design lab here gives students another place to prototype and build, even though the building is best known for research.
3. *Transition*
	- Next we head to a stop that usually gets a lot of attention from students interested in computer science, robotics, electrical engineering, and hardware.
## Stop 6: Keller Hall – Computer Science, Computer Engineering, Electrical Engineering
### Main talk track
- This is **Keller Hall**, and for a lot of visitors this is one of the most anticipated stops because it connects to computer science, computer engineering, and electrical engineering.
- A fun opening detail is that people often say the building looks like a **floppy disk from above**. It’s one of those campus trivia facts people tend to remember. Your notes also point out that the ground level feels unusual here because of the layout.
- This is a good place to clarify something families ask a lot: **computer science students are part of CSE here**, so even if students picture software as separate from engineering, at Minnesota it sits inside a broader engineering and science environment.
- Your notes mention the **Undergraduate Robotics Laboratory**, which is a great thing to point out because it makes the building feel more dynamic than just “a CS building.” The official notes describe projects in robotics, virtual reality, and graphics, with students working under faculty guidance.
- If you want a quick practical detail, the **ECE Depot** is a good one: students can get electronic components for projects and class work, which is the kind of resource that matters a lot once students are actually in labs and design courses.
- I’d also keep your note about the floating study areas and TA office hours, because it helps families picture what student support looks like inside a technical building rather than only in an advising office.
- For students interested in robotics, embedded systems, hardware, or computing, this stop helps show that there are both classroom and project pathways here.
1. *Ask the group*
	- Who here is interested in computer science, robotics, AI, hardware, or electrical engineering?
2. *If you need extra time*:
	- You can mention that CS and engineering students still use multiple buildings, libraries, and shared support spaces, so no one’s whole college life stays inside Keller.
3. *Transition*: 
	- We’ll keep moving, but from here the tour starts to connect technical spaces with the broader student experience — tutoring, study space, and places students spend time outside class.
## Stop 7: Tate Hall – Physics, Astronomy, Earth Sciences
### Main talk track
- **Tate Hall** is useful to mention because it reflects the reality that many CSE students share core science classes, especially early on.
- Physics tutoring is a strong point to mention here. Your notes describe support from students or graduate students who know the material well, and that’s the kind of thing first-year students often end up really appreciating.
- This is also a good place to normalize large intro classes. Yes, some first-year classes are big — calculus, chemistry, introductory physics — but those are balanced out by office hours, tutoring, labs, discussion sections, and smaller upper-level experiences.
- Tate also includes research and teaching labs, plus the historic observatory with its telescope used for public viewing events.
1. *Ask the group*
	- Are any of you excited about astronomy or physics, or are those more “supporting classes” for your planned major?
2. *Transition*
	- From here we head toward one of the most useful all-purpose student spaces on the route: Walter Library.
## Stop 8: Walter Library – Study Support, Tutoring, Makerspaces, AI
### Main talk track
- **Walter Library** is a great stop because almost everyone can picture themselves using it, no matter their major.
- Historically, it’s one of the iconic academic buildings on campus. The University Libraries say Walter first opened in **1924**, and it’s known for its owl-themed architecture and details. The library’s own building history page says there are **225 owls** throughout the building. That’s a fun fact people actually remember.
- On the practical side, Walter includes computing resources, free printing, study areas, and tutoring-related support. The official tour notes mention the **Tutoring and Academic Success Center**, where students can get peer support across a large number of subject areas at no extra cost.
- Your draft also mentions the **Toaster** and makerspace resources in the building. The Libraries describe Walter’s makerspace as a free-to-use, student-run makerspace with tools and materials available to students from any major.
- A strong modern detail to add here is the **CSE AI Makerspace** in Walter. The College of Science and Engineering describes it as an open collaborative lab and study environment where students can engage with AI concepts and technologies, use computing resources, do projects, and attend workshops.
- That’s a helpful thing to say out loud because families often hear “AI” and assume it’s only for advanced students. The framing here should be: students can enter at different experience levels and still find workshops, support, and exploratory resources.
1. *Ask the group*
	- Are you the kind of person who studies best in a quiet library, a group space, or somewhere with a lot going on?
2. *If you need extra time*
	- You can do a quick owl fact or mention the café and the general role of Walter as a go-to East Bank study spot. The owl count and the 1924 opening usually land well with families.
3. *Transition*
	- Our last main stop is **Fraser Hall**, which is a good example of how the University designs teaching spaces around collaboration, not just lectures.
## Stop 9: Fraser Hall – Chemistry, Tutoring, Modern Teaching Labs
### Main talk track
- **Fraser Hall** is one of the strongest examples on the tour of how teaching spaces are intentionally designed for student learning and teamwork.
- Right near the entrance, it’s worth pointing out the contrast between historic and newer campus architecture. Your notes mention that part of the brick side connects to the site’s older law school history, which helps visitors see how campus has developed over time.
- On the first level, there’s student gathering space and chemistry support. Your draft mentions **walk-in tutoring**, which is a great practical detail because it shows that help is built into the physical space, not hidden somewhere students have to search for.
- The second-level undergraduate chemistry labs are especially nice to talk about because they reflect how the University teaches science in a more collaborative format. Students work in teams, run experiments, then return to collaboration space to interpret results and discuss what happened.
- That’s useful to point out because many families still picture college science as a professor talking for an hour and students copying notes. Fraser is a better example of active learning, lab teamwork, and presentation practice.
- If your group has the energy and time, the upper levels and art installation detail are a nice closing touch. Your notes mention the float glass artwork by **Dyani White Hawk**, which gives the building a strong visual finish.
1. *Ask the group*
	- For those of you interested in science, do you prefer lecture-style learning or labs and team-based problem solving?
2. *If you need extra time*
	- Mention that spaces like this help students practice communication too, not just content mastery, because they often need to explain methods, results, and reasoning.
3. Closing transition
	- That wraps up the main route, but I’m happy to stay for questions about majors, research, internships, student life, or what it’s actually like to be a CSE student here.
