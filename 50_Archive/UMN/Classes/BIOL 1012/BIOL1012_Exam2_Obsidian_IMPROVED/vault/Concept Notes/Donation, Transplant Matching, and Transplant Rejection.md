# Donation, Transplant Matching, and Transplant Rejection

## Donations 1 — Key Definitions

### Organ Donor
- A person whose organ or tissue is **provided** for transplantation.
- Can be **living** (e.g., one kidney) or **deceased** (e.g., heart).

### Organ Recipient
- A person who **receives** the donated organ.

### Organ Rejection
- When the recipient's **immune system recognizes the transplanted organ as foreign** and mounts an immune attack against it.
- Rejection = the immune system correctly identifying non-self tissue.
- Not a failure of the immune system — it is doing exactly what it is designed to do.

---

## Donations 2 — How the Immune System Rejects a Transplanted Organ

- Every person's cells display **surface antigens** (including HLA antigens and blood group antigens) determined by their genetics.
- A transplanted organ carries the **donor's antigens**, which may differ from the recipient's.
- The recipient's adaptive immune cells recognize the foreign antigens → **T cells and B cells activate**.
- **Cytotoxic T cells** directly attack donor cells displaying foreign antigens.
- **B cells/antibodies** may also attack blood vessels and tissue of the donated organ.
- **Inflammation** damages the organ further.
- Result: **the organ is damaged or destroyed** (rejected).

### Connection to Central Dogma
- The **central dogma** (DNA → RNA → Protein) determines which antigen proteins are made.
- Each person's unique DNA produces unique surface proteins → unique antigen profile.
- The more different the donor's antigen profile, the higher the rejection risk.

## Donations 3 — Steps in Blood and Organ Donation

### Organ Donation Process
1. **Recipient identified** — patient placed on a **national waiting list**.
2. **Recipient tested** — blood type (ABO) and **HLA antigen matching** performed.
3. **Donor identified** — living or deceased; matched based on: organ size, geographic proximity, genetics (ABO type, HLA), and time sensitivity.
4. **Pre-transplant treatment** — recipient receives preparation treatment.
5. **Transplant surgery** performed.
6. **Anti-rejection drugs (immunosuppressants)** prescribed — taken for the **rest of the recipient's life** to suppress immune attack on the organ.
7. **Possible rejection** — even with treatment, rejection can still occur; if it does, the process may restart.

### Why Blood Type Matters
- Donor and recipient blood types must be **compatible**.
- Blood type mismatch = immune system may attack transfused blood or transplanted organ that carries incompatible antigens.
- Blood type O is the universal donor (no A or B antigens to trigger rejection); AB is the universal recipient.

### Why HLA Matching Matters
- **HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen)** molecules are immune identity markers on cell surfaces.
- Better HLA matching = fewer foreign antigens = lower rejection risk.
- Perfect HLA match is ideal but not always possible (usually found within families).

### Blood Donation Process
1. Donor meets **eligibility requirements** (no illness, meets health criteria).
2. Blood is **collected**.
3. Blood is **separated** into: RBCs, platelets, plasma. **White blood cells are removed** to reduce immune problems in the recipient.
4. Blood is **ABO and Rh typed**.
5. Blood is **screened for ~10 diseases** (viruses and bacteria).
6. Components are **stored** (storage time varies by component).
7. Appropriate components are **transfused** into the patient who needs them.

### What "Finding a Match" Means
- Donor and recipient are compatible enough that **rejection risk is reduced**.
- Key compatibility factors: **blood type** and **HLA antigens**.
- Does NOT mean genetically identical — just compatible enough for safer transplantation.

### Anti-Rejection Therapy (Immunosuppression)
- Drugs **suppress immune activity** to prevent the recipient's immune system from attacking the organ.
- **Trade-off:** less rejection risk, but greater **infection risk** because overall immune activity is suppressed.
- Recipients are often more vulnerable to infections they would normally fight off easily.

## Donations 4 — The Four Pillars of Biomedical Ethics

Developed by Thomas Beauchamp and James Childress in *Principles of Biomedical Ethics* (1985).

### Autonomy
- "Self-rule" — a person's ability to make **informed decisions for themselves**.
- Requires understanding risks, benefits, and options.
- Tied to **consent**.
- Vulnerable persons = those who may be unable to consent or may be coerced.

### Beneficence
- **Doing the most good** possible for an individual or for society.
- Ask: does this decision improve outcomes, save lives, or advance health?

### Non-Maleficence
- **"Do no harm"** — avoid actions that cause unnecessary or avoidable damage.
- Ask: does this decision cause harm? Could harm be prevented?

### Justice
- **Treating everyone fairly** (not the criminal justice system).
- Ask: are burdens and benefits distributed fairly? Are all patients treated equally?

## Transplant Ethics Case Studies

### Case 1: Non-Paternity Discovered During Donor Testing
**Scenario:** Blood type testing for transplant compatibility reveals that a potential donor (daughter) is not biologically related to the patient (the recipient/father).

**Key ethical arguments from class:**
- **Autonomy upheld → share information:** Donors need accurate information to make a truly informed decision about whether to donate. Hiding relevant facts undermines consent.
- **Non-maleficence potentially violated → sharing information:** Revealing non-paternity could severely damage family relationships and cause psychological harm.
- **Autonomy upheld → only tell if donor asks:** Leaves the choice to the donor — respects self-determination.

> There is no single "correct" answer — the exam expects you to **identify and articulate the pillar-based reasoning**, not to pick one right option.

### Case 2: Convicted Felon Seeking Heart Transplant
**Scenario:** A convict with congestive heart failure (likely to die without transplant) is being evaluated. Investigation reveals he has been convicted (twice) of child sexual abuse.

**Key ethical arguments from class:**
- **Justice upheld → provide transplant:** All patients deserve equal treatment. He is a person too, and may even be wrongly convicted.
- **Beneficence upheld → provide transplant:** Transplant gives him a chance to survive and rejoin society.
- **Non-maleficence violated → deny transplant:** Denying care will likely cause his death.
- **Autonomy upheld → case-by-case:** Gives the ethics committee autonomy to consider all specifics.

### Case 3: Transplant Tourism / Unethical Donor Practices
**Scenario:** Patients receive transplants abroad in countries where **organ donor victimization** (coercion, organ trafficking) is suspected, then return seeking post-transplant care.

**Key ethical arguments from class:**
- **Beneficence upheld → provide care:** The patient in front of you needs good medical care, regardless of how they got the organ.
- **Non-maleficence violated → provide care:** Post-transplant care supports the patient, but may indirectly legitimize unethical transplant operations.
- **Autonomy upheld → physician discretion:** Allows individual physicians to decide based on their conscience and the specifics of the case.

## Additional Ethics Topics (for Exam)

### Informed Consent vs. Specific Consent vs. General Consent

| Type | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Informed consent** | Participant knows what the study is about and understands risks/benefits before agreeing |
| **Specific consent** | Participant agrees to data/tissue use for *specific* questions, *specific* time period, by *specific* researchers |
| **General consent** | Broader permission for future unspecified use — gives researchers more flexibility, participant less control |

### Henrietta Lacks Case
- Tissue was taken and used for research **without any consent**.
- Violated: **Autonomy** (no consent obtained), **Justice** (she was a Black woman in a segregated hospital, an already vulnerable population), **Beneficence** (research generated enormous benefit but she and her family received none), **Non-maleficence** (her privacy and family's wellbeing were harmed).
