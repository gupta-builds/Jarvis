# Vaccines and Vaccine Ethics

## Vaccines 1 — Components of a Vaccine

### Antigen (Active Ingredient)
The component that **triggers the immune response**. Can be:

- **Weakened or inactivated pathogen** that cannot cause disease in healthy people.
  - Example: some flu vaccines — weakened flu virus grown in eggs or cells, then collected.
- **Isolated proteins or other antigens** from the pathogen.
  - Example: HPV vaccine and J&J COVID vaccine — genes for pathogen proteins inserted into nonpathogenic microbes, which produce the protein antigen.
- **mRNA encoding an antigen** — delivered into human cells, which then translate it into the antigen protein.
  - Example: Pfizer and Moderna COVID vaccines — mRNA produced in a lab reactor (no virus or bacteria); human cells make the protein; adaptive immune system recognizes it as an antigen.

### Adjuvants
- Molecules (e.g., aluminum salts) that **stimulate innate immune responses**.
- Strengthen vaccine effectiveness by amplifying the immune reaction.

### Preservatives and Buffers
- Prevent contamination; keep vaccine **stable and effective**.

### Lipids
- Present **only in mRNA vaccines**.
- Fatty acid macromolecules that help transport mRNA **into human cells** (mRNA cannot enter cells on its own).

## Vaccines 2 — First Immune Response to a Vaccine

### Innate Response (occurs first)
- Adjuvants and the act of injection trigger innate defenses.
- Common **"side effects"** = signs of innate immune activity:
  - **Pain, redness, swelling** at injection site (inflammatory response)
  - **Fever** (hypothalamus adjusts temperature)
  - **Fatigue** (immune system using significant energy)
  - Rare: allergic reactions

> These side effects are EVIDENCE that the immune system is working — not evidence of harm from the vaccine.

### Adaptive Response ("first response" without real infection)
- All **4 steps of the adaptive immune response** occur:
  1. **Encounter** — lymphocytes encounter the vaccine antigen.
  2. **Activate** — clonal expansion of matching B and T cells.
  3. **Attack** — antibodies are produced; cytotoxic T cells may be primed. Attack step is **limited** because there is no actual pathogen to eliminate.
  4. **Memory** — **memory B cells and memory T cells** are generated.
- This is a **primary response** — without the person getting sick.

---

## Vaccines 3 — Why the First Real Infection Behaves Like a Secondary Response

- The vaccine acts as a **safe "first exposure"** to pathogen antigens.
- The primary adaptive response to the vaccine creates **memory B and T cells**.
- When the real pathogen enters the body later:
  - Memory cells recognize the antigen immediately.
  - They respond **as if it's a second exposure** → **secondary response** (faster, stronger, higher antibody levels).
- This provides **protection against severe disease** even on the very first encounter with the real pathogen.

## Vaccines 4 — Vaccination Rates, Transmission, and Infection Rates

### Basic Relationship
- As **vaccination rate ↑**:
  - Disease **transmission rate ↓**
  - **Infection rate ↓**

### Why?
- More vaccinated individuals = fewer susceptible hosts.
- Pathogen spreads less efficiently → transmission slows.
- Even unvaccinated individuals gain some protection because they are less likely to encounter an infected person.

### Herd Immunity
- When enough of a population is immune (via vaccination or prior infection), disease spread becomes controlled.
- **Herd Immunity Threshold (HIT):** the percentage of the population that must be immune to prevent uncontrolled spread.

### R₀ — Reproductive Number
- **R₀** = number of people each infected person will infect in a fully susceptible population.
- Higher R₀ = more contagious → requires higher vaccination rate to stop spread.

| Disease | Approx. R₀ | Herd Immunity Threshold |
|---------|-----------|------------------------|
| **Delta variant COVID-19** | 5–7 | ~80–87.5% |
| **Measles** | ~20 | ~95% |

> As the fraction of the population with immunity decreases linearly, disease rates **increase exponentially**.

## Vaccine Case Studies (Graphic Novels)

### Case 1: Smallpox — Edward Jenner
- **Hypothesis:** cowpox infection protects against smallpox.
- **Test:** deliberately infected James Phipps (a child) with cowpox, then later with smallpox.
- **Ethical analysis:**
  - *Non-maleficence:* potentially violated — child was exposed to real cowpox and then smallpox.
  - *Beneficence:* could be argued — if it worked, huge public health benefit.
  - Modern standards would not permit this without rigorous prior data and consent.

### Case 2: Polio — Jonas Salk
- **Formaldehyde** in Salk's vaccine: used to **kill** (inactivate) the poliovirus so it cannot cause disease but still contains antigens that trigger immunity.
- A virus must be **killed or weakened** before use as a vaccine because a live virulent virus would cause the disease instead of preventing it.
- **Salk did not patent his vaccine** — chosen to maximize global access and benefit (beneficence, justice).

### Case 3: MMR — Wakefield Fraud
- **Andrew Wakefield's 1998 Lancet paper:** falsely claimed a connection between the MMR vaccine and autism.
- Problems with the study:
  - Only 12 children included; **no control group**.
  - Data was **fabricated**.
  - Funded by lawyers seeking evidence against vaccine manufacturers.
  - At least **25 subsequent studies** found NO connection between MMR and autism.
- **Consequences:**
  - Widespread vaccine hesitancy.
  - Measles outbreaks — including in **Minneapolis's Somali community** in 2017 after Wakefield specifically targeted that community (2010–2011).
  - Confirmed measles cases in 30+ states as of 2026.
- **Ethical pillars violated:**
  - *Non-maleficence* — fabricated data led directly to harm (vaccine refusal → disease outbreaks).
  - *Beneficence* — undermined public health.
  - *Autonomy* — people cannot make good decisions based on false information.
  - *Justice* — communities were disproportionately harmed.

### Correlation vs. Causation
- **Correlation:** two things occur together (e.g., children diagnosed with autism have also been vaccinated).
- **Causation:** one thing directly causes another.
- The fact that some vaccinated children have autism does **NOT** mean vaccination causes autism — both are common in children at similar ages. Correlation ≠ causation.

## HPV Vaccine Trial Data (Figure 7)

- Study: patients ages 11–23 given HPV vaccine or placebo at months 0, 2, and 6.
- Groups: HPV+ (already infected) and HPV- (never infected), each split into vaccine and placebo.

### Key findings:
- **HPV- patients with vaccine:** ~1000x more antibodies at 3 months vs. HPV- with placebo; levels remained elevated throughout the trial.
- **HPV+ patients with vaccine:** also showed increased antibody response vs. HPV+ placebo — behaved as a **secondary response** (already had prior exposure).
- **Placebo groups:** antibody levels stayed flat or very low.
- Conclusion: vaccine was **effective** at generating an immune response in both HPV+ and HPV- patients.

## Vaccines 5 — Applying the Four Pillars to Vaccine Ethics

| Pillar | Application to Vaccines |
|--------|------------------------|
| **Autonomy** | People should receive understandable information; mandatory vaccination debates often center on autonomy |
| **Beneficence** | Vaccines prevent disease, complications, and death; research should aim for safety and efficacy |
| **Non-maleficence** | Harms must be minimized; rigorous testing matters; fabricated data (Wakefield) violates this |
| **Justice** | Fair access to vaccines; patents, cost, and distribution affect justice; certain communities must not be unfairly targeted |
