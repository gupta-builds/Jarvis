# Week 2 — Leadership Styles, Emotional Intelligence & Self-Awareness

## What you must be able to do
- Explain leadership as a process, not merely a role or title.
- Distinguish leadership emergence from leadership effectiveness — these are different things.
- Compare task-oriented and relationship-oriented leadership and explain when each matters.
- Use the Managerial Grid to classify leadership styles by name and behavior.
- Distinguish transformational from transactional leadership and explain when each is appropriate.
- Explain Leader–Member Exchange (LMX) theory and its implications for fairness and performance.
- Apply House's Path–Goal theory to a scenario: match leader style to follower and task conditions.
- Identify the four domains of emotional intelligence and recognize signals of high vs. low EQ.
- Explain the Big Five personality traits and their relevance to leadership style.
- Distinguish internal from external locus of control and explain how each affects management approach.

## Key ideas
- Leadership can be formal or informal — position is helpful but not required.
- The person who *looks* like the leader is not always the most effective leader.
- Strong leaders balance task and relationship demands — not just one at a time.
- Effective leadership depends on follower needs and situation (contingency applies here too).
- Emotional intelligence and self-awareness allow leaders to choose their approach rather than defaulting to habit.

## Lecture

### 1. Leadership is a process, not a position
Leadership is about **influencing others to mobilize effort toward goals** — regardless of where that influence comes from. This means:
- A senior manager who holds a title but fails to inspire is not leading effectively
- A junior employee who shapes how a team thinks and makes decisions is exercising real leadership
- Positional authority is *one* source of influence — but not the only one
- Leadership can be **formal** (comes from a designated role) or **informal** (earned through influence, credibility, and relationships)

**Born or made?** The research suggests both traits and skills matter:
- **Traits** (innate personal characteristics) — self-confidence, drive to take initiative, high levels of physical energy, motivation to complete tasks. These help explain who tends to *emerge* as a leader
- **Skills** (learned capabilities) — cognitive skills, technical skills, interpersonal skills. These can be deliberately developed
- Neither alone guarantees effectiveness — situational variables matter too. Leadership only emerges when an opportunity for leadership arises (e.g. war, crisis, absence of another leader)

**Critical flaw in pure trait theory:** People who possess leadership traits don't always become leaders. Traits are not destiny — they are tendencies.

---

### 2. Leadership emergence vs. leadership effectiveness — a critical distinction

| Concept | Definition | Associated with |
|---------|-----------|----------------|
| **Emergence** | Who gets *recognized* as a leader in a group | Confidence, visibility, interpersonal savvy, self-promotion |
| **Effectiveness** | Who actually *builds performance* and develops people | Building strong teams, sustainable results, developing talent |

**Why this matters on exams:** A charismatic, confident person may emerge as leader but fail to produce actual results. A less visible person may quietly be the most effective leader in the room. Don't confuse the two.

**Emergent leadership traits (from lecture):** Creative thinking, influence, self-management, adaptability, team-orientation. Emergent leaders arise naturally, self-identify based on their ability to make decisions and achieve results, and inspire others on their own without formal authority.

---

### 3. Task-oriented vs. relationship-oriented leadership

**Task-oriented behaviors:**
- Short-term planning and goal-setting
- Clarifying roles and expectations
- Monitoring work progress
- Correcting deviations from standard

**Relationship-oriented behaviors:**
- Emotional support and active listening
- Coaching and developing subordinates
- Recognizing and celebrating good work
- Building trust and team morale

**Key insight:** These are **two independent dimensions** — a leader can be high or low on both simultaneously. The best general approach (the Managerial Grid "Team Leader") is high on both.

---

### 4. The Managerial Grid — four quadrant styles

Maps **concern for production** (task) vs. **concern for people** (relationship):

| Style | Task | Relationship | Result |
|-------|------|-------------|--------|
| **Impoverished** | Low | Low | Minimal effort; disengaged team |
| **Country Club** | Low | High | Pleasant but low performance |
| **Produce or Perish** | High | Low | Short-term results, long-term burnout |
| **Team Leader** | High | High | Sustained high performance + commitment |

**Country Club (High Relationship, Low Task):**
- Focused on happiness, little focus on performance → happy employees, harmonious environment
- Advantages: strong focus on people → higher employee retention; low performance expectations → lower stress
- Disadvantages: leads to low or no performance/output; lack of performance can undermine job security

**Impoverished (Low Task, Low Relationship):**
- Very little concern for people or production; leader sees no need for team to perform
- Advantages: None
- Disadvantages: low/no performance, low engagement and motivation, sets a poor example

**Produce or Perish (High Task, Low Relationship):**
- Manager focuses entirely on production at an extreme level
- Advantages: high production output; quick decisions (leader makes all decisions); high clarity of expectations
- Disadvantages: high employee turnover; low initiative/creativity/engagement; productivity suffers long-term

**Team Leader (High Relationship, High Task) — Most Effective:**
- Leaders show commitment to empowering staff AND productivity
- Motivate workers to operate as a team while focusing on accomplishments
- This is the target style in most organizational contexts

**Exam tip:** The team leader style is the strongest *general* pattern. But the contingency view reminds us that situational factors (task complexity, follower maturity) might call for different emphases at different moments.

### 5. Transformational vs. Transactional leadership

#### Transactional
Uses exchanges to get work done. Most common day-to-day management style.

Mechanisms:
- **Contingent reward** — "If you hit the target, you get the bonus"
- **Management-by-exception (active)** — Constantly monitor and correct deviations
- **Management-by-exception (passive)** — Only act after a problem has occurred

**Best for:** Stable environments, clear roles, routine tasks, where maintaining standards is the priority.

#### Transformational (the "4 I's")
Raises performance by connecting people to vision and meaning, not just reward.

1. **Idealized Influence** — Leader becomes a trusted role model; "walks the talk"
2. **Inspirational Motivation** — Articulates a compelling future that followers emotionally invest in
3. **Intellectual Stimulation** — Challenges followers to question assumptions and think innovatively
4. **Individualized Consideration** — Treats each follower as an individual; coaches and develops personally

**Best for:** Change, innovation, building commitment, raising performance beyond minimum expectations.

**Clean contrast for exams:**
- Transactional = exchange-based stability
- Transformational = meaning-based inspiration

### 6. Charismatic leadership
Charismatic leaders create strong emotional attachment through:
- Extreme self-confidence
- Personal risk-taking for the cause
- Powerful imagery and symbolism
- A compelling sense of mission

**Dark side of charisma:** Can become narcissistic or exploitative. Followers grant charisma — it is partly socially constructed, not solely an innate trait.

---

### 7. Leader–Member Exchange (LMX)
Leaders build **different-quality relationships** with different followers:

- **In-group:** High trust, high autonomy, challenging assignments, more support and opportunity
- **Out-group:** More formal, rule-based, transactional relationship

**Relationship life cycle:**
Stranger (formal) → Acquaintance (testing, role-making) → Mature Partnership (transformational, strong mutual trust)

**Fairness concern:** If in-group membership is influenced by demographic similarity or personal liking rather than performance, LMX dynamics can create inequity and undermine team cohesion.

---

### 8. House's Path–Goal Theory
The leader's central job: **make the path from effort to valued outcome as clear and achievable as possible** by providing what followers are missing.

Four leader styles and when to use them:

| Style | Best when |
|-------|----------|
| **Directive** | Task is ambiguous; follower needs structure and clarity |
| **Supportive** | Work is stressful or demanding; follower needs emotional support |
| **Participative** | Follower is skilled and values autonomy and input |
| **Achievement-oriented** | Follower is motivated to grow; task benefits from high goals |

**Core exam logic:** Leaders supplement what is missing. If the task is already crystal-clear and the follower is already confident, adding more direction adds nothing. If the path is murky, directive behavior removes the ambiguity.

### 9. Emotional Intelligence (EQ) — four domains

| Domain | What it involves |
|--------|----------------|
| **Self-awareness** | Recognizing your emotions, triggers, and patterns in real time |
| **Self-management** | Regulating emotions so they inform rather than control your behavior |
| **Social awareness** | Reading others' emotional states and team dynamics accurately |
| **Relationship management** | Influencing, coaching, inspiring, and navigating conflict through emotional insight |

**Hallmarks of Emotional Intelligence (from lecture):**
- Concern for socially appropriate action
- Sensitivity to social cues
- Ability to self-control / self-monitor

**Why it matters:** Surveys have found that emotional intelligence influences 58% of success.

**Real-world leaders with high EQ:**
- **Howard Schultz (Starbucks):** Returned to run the company out of "love" for its people. Invested in very generous healthcare benefits — a classic people-oriented move demonstrating high EQ and individualized consideration
- **Indra Nooyi (Pepsi CEO):** Philosophy of "Performance with Purpose." Wanted employees to see their roles as a "calling." Known for singing in hallways and walking barefoot in the office — created psychological safety and authentic connection

**Signals of high EQ in a leader:**
- Reads the room before speaking
- Regulates reactions before escalating conflict
- Listens deeply instead of waiting to talk
- Adapts tone and approach to the individual
- Challenges others without humiliating them
- Responds to mistakes with coaching, not punishment

**Signals of low EQ:**
- Defensive under feedback
- Misreads team morale
- Lets frustration damage relationships
- Applies the same rigid style regardless of context

### 10. Chapter 13 — knowing yourself as a leader

#### Big Five personality traits

| Trait | Low | High | Leadership relevance |
|-------|-----|------|---------------------|
| **Extraversion** | Reserved | Sociable, assertive | High → suits public-facing, high-communication roles |
| **Openness** | Pragmatic | Creative, imaginative | High → suits innovation and change leadership |
| **Conscientiousness** | Flexible, spontaneous | Organized, persistent | High → predicts task follow-through and reliability |
| **Agreeableness** | Competitive | Cooperative, compassionate | High → aids collaboration; very high can cause conflict avoidance |
| **Emotional Stability** | Reactive, anxious | Calm under pressure | High → critical for crisis leadership |

**Which Big Five trait predicts business success most?** → **High Conscientiousness** — meta-analysis shows it is the strongest predictor of work performance across a wide range of jobs.

**Career applications by trait (from lecture):**
- **High Openness** → flight attendant, artist, fashion designer, graphic designer, lawyer, chief diversity officer
- **Low Openness** → data-driven careers, conventional roles with familiar, structured environments
- **High Conscientiousness** → physician, accountant, sales manager, fundraiser (methodic planning, low risk)
- **Low Conscientiousness** → artist, sales rep, firefighter, call center (spontaneity, unpredictable demands)
- **High Extraversion** → cosmetologist, teacher, sales manager, event planner (lots of conversation)
- **Low Extraversion** → lab technician, freelance writer, engineer, mechanic, programmer (works with data/animals, alone)
- **High Agreeableness** → human resources, teaching, nursing, counseling (building connections, positive contributions)
- **Low Agreeableness** → accountant, engineer, scientist (logical, objective, no emotional connections required)
- **High Neuroticism** → florist, yoga instructor, artist, writer (low change, low stress, self-expression)
- **Low Neuroticism** → social worker, nurse (optimistic, thrive in stressful/challenging situations)

Traits shape default tendencies — they don't determine destiny.

#### Self-Monitoring — the "Social Chameleon"
Self-monitoring is the extent to which one attends to social cues and attempts to adapt behavior to impact their outward image.

- High self-monitors are **"social chameleons"** — flexible and skillful in adapting their expressive behaviors to the context
- Useful in job searching and interviewing — allows tailoring presentation to each environment
- Related to greater occupational success — but comes with a price: stress-related illness and psychological distress can result from sustained self-monitoring
- **Exam note:** One of the most important individual differences in a work context, alongside conscientiousness and learning orientation

#### Three Components of Strengths
The lecture defines strengths as combining three things:
1. **Personality** — natural, enduring inner characteristics and tendencies (see Big Five)
2. **Knowledge and Expertise** — facts, data, and lessons learned from experience
3. **Skills** — behavioral competencies and the ability to conduct specific activities/tasks

#### Goal Orientation
**Definition:** The belief or attitude toward learning and performance that influences how you learn and engage with challenges.
- **Learning-oriented** → focus on mastering skills regardless of setbacks; strongly predictive of performance
- **Performance-oriented** → focus on demonstrating competence relative to others
- Learning orientation is also strongly predictive of job performance across jobs

#### Locus of control

| Internal | External |
|---------|---------|
| Effort and action shape outcomes | Luck and outside forces shape outcomes |
| More proactive, self-motivated | May prefer structure and direction |
| Comfortable with autonomy | May need more guidance |

**Management implication:** Path–Goal directive style tends to fit externals better; participative style tends to fit internals better.

#### Resilience and managing adversity
Resilient leaders:
- Face reality directly (don't minimize or deny)
- Maintain a sense of meaning and purpose
- Focus on what they can control
- Break problems into manageable parts
- Persist and learn from failure

**Why this matters:** Leaders set the emotional tone. A resilient leader models steadiness during crisis; an anxious leader amplifies team uncertainty.

---

## Key summary from lecture
- Ability has multiple components: cognitive and emotional — the relative importance depends on the specific job
- Employees' personality can be described by multiple traits; traits impact natural talents/strengths and liabilities
- Professionals improve careers by understanding their own personality traits and managing how they affect behavior at work (expressed or constrained)
- Most important individual differences in a work context: **self-monitoring**, **learning orientation**, and **conscientiousness** — all are strongly predictive of job performance across a range of jobs

## Flashcards
#cards/MGMT
Leadership emergence::Who gets recognized as a leader in a group — associated with confidence, visibility, and interpersonal savvy.
Leadership effectiveness::Who actually builds performance and develops people — associated with results, talent development, and sustainable teams.
Managerial Grid::Maps concern for production vs concern for people. Team Leader (high/high) is the strongest general pattern.
Transactional leadership::Uses exchanges, rewards, and management-by-exception to maintain stability and meet standards.
Transformational leadership::Uses idealized influence, inspirational motivation, intellectual stimulation, and individualized consideration to raise commitment and performance.

Emotional intelligence::Self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, and relationship management — ability to recognize and manage emotions in self and relationships.
Internal locus of control::Belief that personal effort and action determine outcomes — associated with proactiveness, autonomy, and self-motivation.
External locus of control::Belief that luck and outside forces determine outcomes — associated with preference for structure and direction.
Big Five personality traits::Extraversion, openness, conscientiousness, agreeableness, and emotional stability — five dimensions that describe personality on sliding scales.
