# Week 1 - Management Foundations & Historical Perspectives
## What you must be able to do
- Explain the three pillars of management: strategy, organizational design, and individual leadership.
- Distinguish management from leadership using specific behaviors — not just definitions.
- Identify each major historical management perspective, the problem it was designed to solve, and its key characteristics.
- Explain Theory X and Theory Y assumptions and give an example of each in action.
- Explain why the contingency view matters and why "one best way" thinking is insufficient.
- Connect VUCA to why modern management requires contextual intelligence.
## Key ideas
- Management is about getting results through people efficiently and effectively.
- Leadership is about direction, alignment, and motivation — especially during change.
- Management theory changed because business problems changed. Each theory answered a real gap.
- There is no one best way to manage — **fit** between approach and context is what matters.
- Modern managers must think about stakeholders and environmental uncertainty, not just internal efficiency.

## Lecture
### 1. Course roadmap - three connected lenses
The opening lecture frames MGMT 3001 around three interconnected ideas:
- **Management foundations** - how organizations coordinate work
- **Strategic thinking** - how organizations decide where to compete and how
- **Leadership in action** - how individuals move people toward goals
These lenses cannot be separated. Strategy without organizational design stays abstract. Organizational design without leadership lacks human commitment. Leadership without strategy can be inspiring but misdirected.
### 2. Management vs. Leadership — side-by-side
**Managers** create order and consistency:
- Plan and budget
- Organize and staff
- Control and problem-solve
**Leaders** create direction and movement:
- Set direction
- Align people
- Motivate and inspire
**Historical examples from lecture:**
- **George Washington:** Set direction (vision for America), aligned people (commanded the Continental Army), motivated and inspired (personal conviction held the cause together)
- **Martin Luther King Jr.:** Set direction (nonviolent advancement of civil rights), aligned people (organized protests and coalitions), motivated and inspired (words and moral authority rather than positional power)
Neither was an "organizational manager" in the traditional sense — but both demonstrate the full scope of leadership: vision, alignment, and inspiration.
**Critical nuance:** Both skills are needed. An organization with only leadership has inspiring vision but poor execution. An organization with only management has efficient systems pointed in the wrong direction.
### 3. Historical perspectives on management — full breakdown
#### Scientific Management (Classical Perspective)
**Key figures:** Frederick Taylor, Henry Gantt, the Gilbreths
**Problem:** How do we make work more efficient?
**Answer:** Systematically study how work is done, identify the best method, standardize it, and link pay to performance.
**Taylor's five steps:**
1. Study the job and codify the best method
2. Match workers to jobs by skill
3. Train workers in the standard method
4. Plan work and eliminate interruptions
5. Establish performance-based incentives
**Gantt's contributions:** Gantt chart (still used today in project management), task-based bonus system, training and development focus.
**Where scientific management survives today:** McDonald's kitchen operations, Amazon warehouse workflow, airline crew scheduling, call center design.
**Limitations:**
- Specialized jobs become boring and demoralizing
- Managers often don't share productivity gains with workers
- Workers deliberately underperform to protect their output standard ("ratcheting")
- Treats workers as interchangeable machine parts
#### Bureaucracy (Classical Perspective)
**Key figure:** Max Weber
**Problem:** How do large organizations stay consistent, fair, and scalable without relying on personal relationships or arbitrary power?
**Answer:** A formal system built on qualification-based hiring, merit promotion, chain of command, division of labor, impartial rules, written records, and professional (non-owner) management.
**Seven elements of bureaucratic organizations:**
1. Qualification-based hiring
2. Merit-based promotion
3. Chain of command
4. Division of labor
5. Impartial application of rules
6. Recorded in writing
7. Managers separate from owners
**Weber was reacting against:** Monarchies and family empires where authority came from birth, not competence.
**Strengths:** Creates consistency, fairness, and accountability at scale.
**Limitations:** Can become rigid, slow to adapt, and substitute rule-following for good judgment.
#### Human Relations / Humanistic Perspective
**Key figures:** Elton Mayo (Hawthorne Studies), Mary Parker Follett, Douglas McGregor
**Problem:** Why don't workers always respond to better physical conditions and pay the way classical theory predicts?
**Answer:** Social factors — attention, belonging, participation, recognition — powerfully drive behavior.
**The Hawthorne Studies (1924–1932):**
- At Western Electric, researchers changed lighting to measure productivity
- Productivity rose regardless of whether lighting went up or down
- Conclusion: Workers responded to the **attention** of being studied, not to the physical change
- This exposed the limits of purely mechanistic views of motivation
**Mary Parker Follett:** Workers who have knowledge of a task should have control over it. Suggested managers and workers co-analyze jobs for improvement.
**Douglas McGregor — Theory X and Theory Y:**

| Theory X                            | Theory Y                                                     |
| ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Workers are lazy, dislike work      | Workers are not inherently lazy                              |
| Managers must closely supervise     | Workers will self-direct given proper environment            |
| Strict rules and punishments needed | Managers should decentralize authority and provide resources |
**Theory X example:** "Complete the survey by Wednesday — I will monitor your responses."
**Theory Y example:** "I've completed it — your input shapes our future, I encourage you to do the same."
The manager's assumption shapes every interaction: how they design work, how they communicate, whether people feel trusted or policed.
#### Quantitative / Management Science Perspective
**Problem:** How do we make better decisions about complex operations and resource allocation problems?
**Answer:** Apply mathematical models, operations research, and management information systems (MIS) to management decisions.
**Still used today for:** airline scheduling, supply chain optimization, financial forecasting, inventory management.
#### Systems Thinking
**Problem:** How do we see the whole organization rather than just individual parts?
**Key idea:** Organizations are interconnected systems. A change in marketing affects operations, which affects finance, which affects hiring, which circles back to marketing. Optimizing one part in isolation can harm the whole.
#### Contingency View
**Problem:** Why does the "best practice" that works at one company fail at another?
**Key idea:** There is **no single best way** to manage every organization. Effective management depends on **fit** between the approach and the specific context (task, environment, people, strategy).
**Three positions compared:**
- **Universalist:** There is one best way — apply it everywhere
- **Case view:** Every situation is completely unique — no generalizations possible
- **Contingency view:** Situations follow patterns — match the right approach to each type
This is the most reusable exam framework in the course. When a question asks "what should a manager do?" — the correct frame is: *it depends on the fit.*
### 4. VUCA — why modern management is hard
**VUCA** describes the modern business environment:
- **Volatility** — things change fast and unpredictably (supplier shutdowns, rapid market shifts)
- **Uncertainty** — you don't know what a change means yet (competitor launches — will customers switch?)
- **Complexity** — many connected parts (growth affects hiring, support, quality, and cash flow simultaneously)
- **Ambiguity** — cause-effect is unclear (mixed marketing results — which channel actually drove growth?)
**Manager's response to VUCA:** Use short learning cycles — small tests, fast feedback, quick adjustments. Avoid the twin failure modes: underestimating uncertainty (not preparing) or overestimating it (paralysis).
### 5. The stakeholder view — who modern firms serve
Business purpose has shifted over time:
- **Managerial view** (pre-1960s): Firm converts inputs to outputs for direct stakeholders
- **Shareholder view** (1960s–1980s): Firm maximizes stock price
- **Stakeholder view** (since 1990s): Firm serves multiple constituencies who can affect or be affected by its activities
A **stakeholder** is any group or individual who can affect, or is affected by, an organization's purpose.
