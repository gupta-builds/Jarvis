# Index Fund Investing

> Distilled from [[Reddit]] and [[Stocks]]

## Deep Dive

### One-Sentence Version

Index fund investing means buying the whole market through low-cost funds like VOO or SPY, using dollar-cost averaging, and holding for years — it beats most active strategies over long horizons.

### What It Is

A passive investment strategy built on four principles:
1. **Diversification** — spread risk across hundreds of companies through one fund (S&P 500 ETF holds ~500 stocks)
2. **Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)** — invest a fixed amount regularly regardless of price, reducing timing risk
3. **Low fees** — index funds charge 0.03-0.10% expense ratios vs 1-2% for active funds. Over 30 years, high fees can eat 25% of wealth
4. **Time horizon** — only invest money you won't need for 5+ years. The market rewards patience, not speculation

### Why It Matters

For a CS student building long-term wealth, index investing is the highest-return-per-hour-of-effort strategy. It requires no stock picking, no market timing, and no daily monitoring. The compounding math is real: $200/month at 10% average annual return for 30 years becomes ~$400K. Starting early matters more than starting with a lot.

### Real Example

A sensible beginner path:
1. Build emergency savings (3-6 months of expenses in high-yield savings)
2. Kill high-interest debt (anything above 7%)
3. Open a brokerage account (Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard)
4. Set up automatic monthly investment into VOO or a total market ETF
5. Ignore short-term noise. Check quarterly at most.

The Reddit beginner guide's 12-month action plan: Month 1 open account + fund emergency savings, Month 2 first ETF investment + automate, Months 3-6 learn one concept weekly, Year 1+ gradually add individual stocks but keep them under 20% of total portfolio.

### Contrast With

- **Index investing vs stock picking**: Index investing buys the whole market. Stock picking tries to beat the market by choosing winners. Most professional fund managers fail to beat the index over 10+ years.
- **Investing vs trading**: Investing is buying and holding for years. Trading is buying and selling frequently. Trading requires more skill, more time, and more emotional discipline — and most retail traders lose money.
- **Dollar-cost averaging vs lump sum**: DCA reduces timing risk by spreading purchases. Lump sum investing has slightly higher expected returns (because markets trend up), but DCA is psychologically easier and protects against buying at a peak.
