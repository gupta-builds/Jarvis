# Interview Preparation

> Distilled from [[ABB Interview Prep]], [[Interview Questions]], and mentorship notes

## Deep Dive

### One-Sentence Version

Interview preparation is about having concrete stories, technical depth you can demonstrate live, and practiced answers that connect your experience to the role — not memorized scripts.

### What It Is

Three pillars for technical internship interviews:
1. **Technical demonstration** — show you can build, debug, and explain real systems (not just recite definitions)
2. **Behavioral stories** — STAR-format narratives grounded in actual project work (BOOM observability, portfolio RAG chatbot, Learning Tracker)
3. **Role-specific preparation** — research the company's stack, prepare questions that show genuine interest, practice elevator pitch

### Why It Matters

The gap between "I know this concept" and "I can explain it under pressure with a concrete example" is where most candidates fail. Interview prep is the bridge between vault knowledge and career leverage. The ABB interview showed this: talking about AI in a technical manner (embeddings, RAG pipelines, data flow) impressed the interviewer more than generic answers.

### Real Example

For the ABB AI Data Analytics interview, three talking pillars worked:
1. **Data pipelines** from BOOM — Rust-based logging middleware that captures system events and transforms them into structured data streams
2. **AI systems** from Arc + RAG chatbot — embeddings, vector database, semantic search, retrieval-augmented generation
3. **Generative AI experimentation** — hosting local LLMs, prompt pipelines, integrating AI features into applications

The elevator pitch connected all three: "I build AI systems where data pipelines and real-world applications come together."

### Contrast With

- **Memorized answers vs practiced stories**: Memorized answers sound generic. Practiced stories adapt to the question because you know the underlying experience deeply enough to reshape it.
- **Technical breadth vs depth**: Breadth gets you past the screen. Depth gets you the offer. For each project, know the architecture, one failure you debugged, and one design decision you made.
- **Company research vs generic interest**: "I admire your innovation" is empty. "Your team uses React/GraphQL/AWS and I've built similar stacks" shows you did the work.
